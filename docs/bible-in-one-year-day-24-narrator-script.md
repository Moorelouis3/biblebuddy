# Bible In One Year - Day 24 Narrator Script

## Day 24 - Passover and Deliverance

**Reading:** Exodus 9 through 12  
**Bible text:** King James Version, Exodus 9-12  
**Audio style:** Narrator teaching with Scripture reading  

## Opening

Welcome to Day 24 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are reading Exodus 9 through 12.

This is one of the most important turning points in the whole Old Testament.

For several chapters, Pharaoh has resisted the word of the LORD.

He has seen the Nile struck.

He has seen frogs, lice, and flies.

He has watched Egypt get shaken again and again.

And still, his heart remains hard.

But in today's reading, the confrontation moves toward its final night.

The plagues become heavier.

Livestock die.

Boils break out.

Hail falls with fire.

Locusts eat what remains.

Darkness covers Egypt.

And then God announces the final plague: the death of the firstborn.

But the center of today's reading is not only judgment.

The center is Passover.

Before Israel walks out of Egypt, God gives them a meal.

He gives them a lamb.

He gives them blood on the doorposts.

He gives them unleavened bread.

He gives them bitter herbs.

He gives them a night they must remember for generations.

That means God is not only removing Israel from slavery.

He is forming them into a redeemed people.

He is teaching them how to remember rescue.

He is teaching them that deliverance comes through His power, His mercy, and His provided protection.

So as we walk through Exodus 9 through 12, listen for three big themes.

First, God makes a distinction.

The plagues are not random chaos. The LORD knows who belongs to Him.

Second, Pharaoh's false repentance keeps appearing.

He admits wrong when pressure is high, but when relief comes, his heart hardens again.

And third, God gives rescue through the blood of the lamb.

This is where Passover begins.

And once you understand Passover, you begin to understand a major pattern that runs through the rest of the Bible.

Judgment is real.

Mercy is provided.

The lamb matters.

The blood marks the place of safety.

And God's people walk out free.

## Exodus 9:1-7

**Scripture**

Then the LORD said unto Moses, Go in unto Pharaoh, and tell him, Thus saith the LORD God of the Hebrews, Let my people go, that they may serve me.

For if thou refuse to let them go, and wilt hold them still,

Behold, the hand of the LORD is upon thy cattle which is in the field, upon the horses, upon the asses, upon the camels, upon the oxen, and upon the sheep.

And the LORD shall sever between the cattle of Israel and the cattle of Egypt: and there shall nothing die of all that is the children's of Israel.

And Pharaoh sent, and, behold, there was not one of the cattle of the Israelites dead.

And the heart of Pharaoh was hardened, and he did not let the people go.

**Narrator**

The fifth plague reaches into Egypt's economy.

Livestock in the ancient world were not just animals in a field.

They meant food.

They meant transportation.

They meant farming.

They meant wealth.

They meant sacrifice.

They meant military strength.

So when God strikes the livestock of Egypt, He is touching everyday survival.

But notice the phrase:

The LORD shall sever between the cattle of Israel and the cattle of Egypt.

That word sever means God is making a distinction.

This is not a natural disaster spinning out of control.

This is not random disease.

The LORD is showing that He rules with precision.

Egypt's livestock are struck.

Israel's livestock are protected.

And Pharaoh even checks.

He sends people to investigate.

They find that not one of Israel's animals has died.

That is important.

Pharaoh does not lack evidence.

He has facts in front of him.

But facts alone do not soften a heart that wants control more than truth.

This is one of the sobering lessons in the plague story.

Some people think, If God would only show me more, I would believe.

But Pharaoh keeps seeing more.

And he keeps hardening.

The problem is not that God has been unclear.

The problem is that Pharaoh does not want to surrender.

## Exodus 9:8-12

**Scripture**

And the LORD said unto Moses and unto Aaron, Take to you handfuls of ashes of the furnace, and let Moses sprinkle it toward the heaven in the sight of Pharaoh.

And it shall become small dust in all the land of Egypt, and shall be a boil breaking forth with blains upon man, and upon beast, throughout all the land of Egypt.

And the magicians could not stand before Moses because of the boils; for the boil was upon the magicians, and upon all the Egyptians.

And the LORD hardened the heart of Pharaoh, and he hearkened not unto them.

**Narrator**

This plague begins with ashes from the furnace.

That detail matters.

Israel has been crushed by forced labor.

They have been making bricks.

They have been living under a system that used their bodies to build Egypt's power.

So when Moses takes ashes from the furnace and scatters them toward heaven, the image is powerful.

Something connected to Egypt's oppression becomes a sign of judgment against Egypt itself.

The ashes become dust.

The dust becomes boils.

And the boils break out on people and animals.

Earlier, the magicians tried to imitate God's signs.

They copied what they could.

They stood in Pharaoh's court as symbols of Egypt's spiritual confidence.

But now they cannot even stand before Moses.

That sentence is not accidental.

The magicians cannot stand.

Egypt's religious experts are publicly humiliated.

The powers Pharaoh trusted are covered in the same judgment as everyone else.

This is not only physical pain.

It is the collapse of Egypt's spiritual pride.

The LORD is showing that there is no hidden power behind Pharaoh strong enough to resist Him.

## Exodus 9:13-35

**Scripture**

And the LORD said unto Moses, Rise up early in the morning, and stand before Pharaoh, and say unto him, Thus saith the LORD God of the Hebrews, Let my people go, that they may serve me.

For I will at this time send all my plagues upon thine heart, and upon thy servants, and upon thy people.

And in very deed for this cause have I raised thee up, for to shew in thee my power; and that my name may be declared throughout all the earth.

He that feared the word of the LORD among the servants of Pharaoh made his servants and his cattle flee into the houses.

And he that regarded not the word of the LORD left his servants and his cattle in the field.

So there was hail, and fire mingled with the hail, very grievous.

And Pharaoh sent, and called for Moses and Aaron, and said unto them, I have sinned this time: the LORD is righteous, and I and my people are wicked.

And when Pharaoh saw that the rain and the hail and the thunders were ceased, he sinned yet more, and hardened his heart.

**Narrator**

Before the hail falls, God explains what is happening.

He tells Pharaoh that His power will be shown so that His name may be declared throughout all the earth.

That means the Exodus is bigger than Egypt.

It is bigger than one king.

It is bigger than one generation.

God is revealing His name in history.

Israel will tell this story.

Nations will hear this story.

Future generations will remember that the LORD broke Pharaoh's power and delivered His people.

But there is another detail we should not miss.

God gives a warning.

He tells Egypt that hail is coming.

And any Egyptian who fears the word of the LORD can bring servants and livestock indoors.

That is mercy inside judgment.

Some Egyptians listen.

Some Egyptians fear the word of the LORD.

Others ignore it.

That means even in Egypt, the issue is no longer only ethnicity or location.

The question is:

Who will take God's word seriously?

The hail falls.

Fire flashes in the storm.

Crops are destroyed.

People and animals left in the field are struck.

But Goshen is spared again.

Then Pharaoh says something that sounds spiritual.

I have sinned.

The LORD is righteous.

I and my people are wicked.

On the surface, that sounds like repentance.

But when the hail stops, Pharaoh hardens his heart again.

So Exodus teaches us the difference between regret and repentance.

Regret wants consequences to stop.

Repentance surrenders to God.

Pharaoh wants relief.

He does not want the LORD.

And that difference matters.

## Exodus 10:1-20

**Scripture**

And the LORD said unto Moses, Go in unto Pharaoh: for I have hardened his heart, and the heart of his servants, that I might shew these my signs before him:

And that thou mayest tell in the ears of thy son, and of thy son's son, what things I have wrought in Egypt.

And Moses and Aaron came in unto Pharaoh, and said unto him, Thus saith the LORD God of the Hebrews, How long wilt thou refuse to humble thyself before me?

And the locusts went up over all the land of Egypt, and rested in all the coasts of Egypt.

And they did eat every herb of the land, and all the fruit of the trees which the hail had left.

Then Pharaoh called for Moses and Aaron in haste; and he said, I have sinned against the LORD your God, and against you.

But the LORD hardened Pharaoh's heart, so that he would not let the children of Israel go.

**Narrator**

The locust plague begins with a purpose statement.

God says these signs are meant to be told to children and grandchildren.

That is huge.

The plagues are not only punishment on Egypt.

They become testimony for Israel.

The children who are not yet born will need to know what God did.

They will need to know that freedom did not come from Pharaoh's kindness.

They will need to know that Israel did not rescue itself.

They will need to know that the LORD personally confronted Egypt.

So God is already building memory into the story.

Then Moses asks Pharaoh:

How long wilt thou refuse to humble thyself before me?

That is the real issue.

Pharaoh's problem is not lack of information.

It is refusal to humble himself.

The locusts come.

They cover the land.

They eat what the hail left behind.

Egypt's food supply is being stripped layer by layer.

Even Pharaoh's servants start to panic.

They ask how long Moses will be a snare to Egypt.

They can see what Pharaoh refuses to admit.

His pride is destroying the nation.

Then Pharaoh confesses again.

I have sinned.

But again, he asks for relief more than surrender.

He wants this death removed from him.

And once the locusts are gone, the hardness remains.

Exodus is showing us how dangerous it is when a person only turns to God because pain is high.

The test of repentance is not only what someone says during the storm.

It is what remains when the storm is gone.

## Exodus 10:21-29

**Scripture**

And the LORD said unto Moses, Stretch out thine hand toward heaven, that there may be darkness over the land of Egypt, even darkness which may be felt.

And there was a thick darkness in all the land of Egypt three days:

They saw not one another, neither rose any from his place for three days: but all the children of Israel had light in their dwellings.

And Pharaoh called unto Moses, and said, Go ye, serve the LORD; only let your flocks and your herds be stayed.

And Moses said, Thou must give us also sacrifices and burnt offerings, that we may sacrifice unto the LORD our God.

**Narrator**

The ninth plague is darkness.

But this is not ordinary nighttime.

Exodus says it is darkness that may be felt.

It is thick.

Heavy.

Oppressive.

For three days, Egypt sits in darkness.

People cannot see one another.

They do not rise from their places.

And that matters in Egypt.

Egypt was a land that honored the sun.

Light, order, and visible power were central to how Egypt understood itself.

But now the LORD covers the land in darkness.

The gods of Egypt cannot give light.

Pharaoh cannot command light.

Egypt's confidence goes dark.

But all the children of Israel have light in their dwellings.

That contrast is beautiful.

Darkness in Egypt.

Light in Israel's homes.

The LORD is still making a distinction.

Then Pharaoh tries another compromise.

The people can go, but the flocks and herds must stay behind.

That sounds like progress, but it is still control.

If the animals stay, Pharaoh still has leverage.

If the animals stay, Israel's worship is limited by Pharaoh's terms.

Moses refuses.

He says they do not know what they must offer until they arrive.

Israel's worship belongs fully to the LORD.

Not partly to Pharaoh.

Not negotiated by Egypt.

Not limited by the old slave master.

That is still a word for us.

Freedom is not letting bondage decide how much of your life God gets.

The LORD does not rescue half a person.

He claims the whole life.

## Exodus 11:1-10

**Scripture**

And the LORD said unto Moses, Yet will I bring one plague more upon Pharaoh, and upon Egypt; afterwards he will let you go hence.

And Moses said, Thus saith the LORD, About midnight will I go out into the midst of Egypt:

And all the firstborn in the land of Egypt shall die, from the firstborn of Pharaoh that sitteth upon his throne, even unto the firstborn of the maidservant.

But against any of the children of Israel shall not a dog move his tongue, against man or beast: that ye may know how that the LORD doth put a difference between the Egyptians and Israel.

**Narrator**

Exodus 11 is quieter than the chapters around it, but the quiet feels heavy.

God announces one final plague.

The death of the firstborn.

This judgment will reach from Pharaoh's throne to the lowest servant.

No part of Egypt's social ladder is outside the reach of God's justice.

And we need to remember the larger story.

At the beginning of Exodus, Pharaoh commanded Hebrew sons to be thrown into the river.

He tried to destroy Israel's children.

Now judgment comes upon Egypt's firstborn.

This is not random cruelty.

This is divine justice answering Pharaoh's violence.

But again, God says He will put a difference between Egypt and Israel.

Israel will be spared.

Not because they are stronger.

Not because they are naturally better.

Not because they deserve mercy more than anyone else.

They will be spared because God marks out His people through His provided way.

And before the final plague falls, God tells Israel how to shelter under that mercy.

That brings us to Passover.

## Exodus 12:1-20

**Scripture**

And the LORD spake unto Moses and Aaron in the land of Egypt, saying,

This month shall be unto you the beginning of months: it shall be the first month of the year to you.

In the tenth day of this month they shall take to them every man a lamb, according to the house of their fathers, a lamb for an house.

Your lamb shall be without blemish, a male of the first year.

And they shall take of the blood, and strike it on the two side posts and on the upper door post of the houses.

And they shall eat the flesh in that night, roast with fire, and unleavened bread; and with bitter herbs they shall eat it.

And thus shall ye eat it; with your loins girded, your shoes on your feet, and your staff in your hand.

**Narrator**

Before Israel leaves Egypt, God gives them a calendar.

That may sound small, but it is not.

God says this month will be the beginning of months for them.

Their year now begins with redemption.

Their time is reorganized around rescue.

That means Israel's identity will not begin with slavery.

It will begin with the night God brought them out.

Then God gives instructions for the lamb.

Each household takes a lamb.

The lamb must be without blemish.

Not leftovers.

Not whatever is unwanted.

A whole, clean, set-apart lamb.

The lamb is killed.

Its blood is placed on the doorposts and the upper doorpost.

The doorway becomes the boundary of shelter.

Inside the house, the family eats the meal.

Roasted lamb.

Unleavened bread.

Bitter herbs.

And each part teaches memory.

The lamb teaches substitution and rescue.

The blood marks the house.

The unleavened bread teaches haste because there will be no time to wait for dough to rise.

The bitter herbs teach the bitterness of slavery.

God is teaching through taste.

Through smell.

Through family.

Through a doorway.

Through a meal.

And He tells them to eat dressed for departure.

Loins girded.

Shoes on feet.

Staff in hand.

This is not a slow dinner for people settling in.

This is a meal for people ready to move.

Passover is faith at the table with shoes on.

Israel eats because God has spoken.

They prepare because deliverance is coming.

## Exodus 12:21-32

**Scripture**

Then Moses called for all the elders of Israel, and said unto them, Draw out and take you a lamb according to your families, and kill the passover.

And ye shall take a bunch of hyssop, and dip it in the blood that is in the bason, and strike the lintel and the two side posts with the blood.

For the LORD will pass through to smite the Egyptians; and when he seeth the blood upon the lintel, and on the two side posts, the LORD will pass over the door.

And it came to pass, that at midnight the LORD smote all the firstborn in the land of Egypt.

And there was a great cry in Egypt; for there was not a house where there was not one dead.

And Pharaoh rose up in the night, and said, Rise up, and get you forth from among my people.

**Narrator**

Now the instructions become action.

Moses calls the elders.

Each family takes the lamb.

The blood is placed on the lintel and the doorposts.

And the people stay inside.

The house is safe because it is marked by the blood.

That is one of the clearest rescue pictures in the Old Testament.

The people inside are not standing at the door explaining why they deserve to live.

They are not comparing themselves to Egypt.

They are not trusting family reputation.

They are sheltered under the sign God provided.

When the LORD sees the blood, He passes over the door.

At midnight, judgment falls.

And there is a great cry in Egypt.

The story does not make this light.

This is devastating.

From Pharaoh's house to prison houses, Egypt is touched by death.

The king who said he would not let Israel go now calls Moses and Aaron in the night.

And the command he resisted finally comes out of his own mouth:

Rise up.

Get you forth.

Serve the LORD.

Pharaoh cannot keep what belongs to God.

The hand that held Israel in bondage is forced open.

And the people are sent out.

## Exodus 12:33-42

**Scripture**

And the Egyptians were urgent upon the people, that they might send them out of the land in haste.

And the people took their dough before it was leavened, their kneadingtroughs being bound up in their clothes upon their shoulders.

And the children of Israel journeyed from Rameses to Succoth, about six hundred thousand on foot that were men, beside children.

And a mixed multitude went up also with them; and flocks, and herds, even very much cattle.

Now the sojourning of the children of Israel, who dwelt in Egypt, was four hundred and thirty years.

It is a night to be much observed unto the LORD for bringing them out from the land of Egypt.

**Narrator**

The Egyptians urge Israel to leave quickly.

This is not a secret escape.

This is a public departure.

The same Egypt that enslaved them is now pressing them to go.

Israel leaves with dough that has not risen.

They leave with kneading troughs wrapped in their clothes.

They leave with children.

They leave with animals.

They leave with silver and gold.

And Exodus says a mixed multitude goes with them.

That is an important detail.

Not everyone leaving Egypt is ethnically Israelite.

Some outsiders go with Israel.

God's deliverance is already drawing others near to His people.

Then the text mentions four hundred and thirty years.

That number makes the night feel even heavier.

This is not a small delay finally ending.

This is generations of waiting.

Generations of tears.

Generations of bondage.

Generations of people who died before seeing the answer.

But God did not forget.

The night of Passover becomes a night to be much observed.

Israel must remember it.

Parents must tell children.

Children must tell their children.

The story of rescue must become part of the people's bones.

Because freedom without memory is dangerous.

If Israel forgets who brought them out, they will misunderstand who they are.

## Exodus 12:43-51

**Scripture**

And the LORD said unto Moses and Aaron, This is the ordinance of the passover: There shall no stranger eat thereof:

But every man's servant that is bought for money, when thou hast circumcised him, then shall he eat thereof.

One law shall be to him that is homeborn, and unto the stranger that sojourneth among you.

And it came to pass the selfsame day, that the LORD did bring the children of Israel out of the land of Egypt by their armies.

**Narrator**

After the drama of the Exodus night, the chapter ends with instructions about the Passover meal.

At first, that may feel like the story slows down.

But this matters.

Passover is not just a meal about leaving Egypt.

It is a covenant meal.

It belongs to a people marked as the LORD's.

That is why circumcision appears here.

Circumcision was the covenant sign given to Abraham's household in Genesis.

So Exodus is connecting Passover back to covenant belonging.

Outsiders are not hated.

They can come in.

But they come in through covenant belonging.

Grace has a doorway.

The meal is open to the foreigner who joins himself to the LORD's people, but it is not casual.

It is holy memory.

It is worship.

It is identity.

Then the chapter ends with a powerful sentence.

The LORD did bring the children of Israel out of the land of Egypt by their armies.

Think about that.

They were slaves.

Brickmakers.

Oppressed people.

People whose sons had been targeted.

People whose labor had been stolen.

But now they are described like ordered hosts.

The LORD is not only freeing them from Pharaoh.

He is forming them under His own command.

They are no longer Pharaoh's labor force.

They are the LORD's redeemed people.

## Closing

Day 24 is heavy, but it is beautiful.

It shows us judgment.

It shows us mercy.

It shows us warning.

It shows us false repentance.

It shows us distinction.

It shows us the blood of the lamb.

And it shows us slaves walking out of bondage because God kept His word.

Pharaoh said no again and again.

But Pharaoh's no was never stronger than God's promise.

Egypt had power.

But Egypt's power was never stronger than the LORD's hand.

Israel had waited for generations.

But the waiting did not mean God had forgotten.

And Passover teaches us that God's rescue is not vague.

It is specific.

A lamb.

A doorway.

Blood.

A meal.

Shoes on feet.

Staff in hand.

A people ready to leave.

Later in Scripture, this Passover pattern will become one of the great ways the Bible helps us understand Jesus.

The New Testament will call Christ our Passover.

That does not erase Exodus.

It shows how deeply Exodus was already teaching redemption.

God's people are delivered from bondage through blood God provides.

So as you sit with today's reading, remember this:

The LORD knows how to make a distinction.

He knows how to judge evil.

He knows how to shelter His people.

He knows how to turn a night of fear into a night of deliverance.

And He knows how to bring His people out.

Not empty.

Not forgotten.

Not still owned by Pharaoh.

But redeemed.

Marked by mercy.

And moving forward under His hand.
