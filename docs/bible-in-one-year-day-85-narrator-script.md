# Day 85 Narrator Script

## Opening

Welcome to Day 85 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are reading First Kings 12 through 15.

The title for today is The Kingdom Divides.

This day shows the kingdom splitting after Solomon.

Rehoboam answers the people roughly.

Israel rebels against the house of David.

Jeroboam becomes king over the northern tribes.

But Jeroboam quickly creates false worship with golden calves.

A man of God cries against the altar at Bethel.

The word of the LORD proves true.

Judah also struggles under Rehoboam.

Kings rise and fall, and the repeated question is simple: will the king walk in the way of the LORD?

## First Kings 12

First Kings 12 begins with Rehoboam going to Shechem, where all Israel comes to make him king.

Jeroboam returns from Egypt.

The people ask Rehoboam to lighten the heavy yoke Solomon placed on them.

If he will serve them and answer them well, they will serve him.

Rehoboam asks for three days.

The old men advise him to be a servant to the people that day, speak good words, and they will be his servants forever.

But Rehoboam forsakes the counsel of the old men.

He asks the young men who grew up with him.

They tell him to answer harshly.

Rehoboam tells the people his little finger will be thicker than his father's loins.

His father chastised them with whips; he will chastise them with scorpions.

The king answers roughly.

The people say, What portion have we in David?

Israel rebels against the house of David.

Rehoboam sends Adoram, who is over tribute, and Israel stones him.

Rehoboam flees to Jerusalem.

He gathers Judah and Benjamin to fight, but the word of God comes through Shemaiah.

They must not fight their brothers.

This thing is from the LORD.

They listen and return.

Jeroboam then fears that if the people go to Jerusalem to sacrifice, their hearts will return to Rehoboam.

So he makes two calves of gold and says, Behold thy gods, O Israel, which brought thee up out of Egypt.

He sets one in Bethel and one in Dan.

He makes houses of high places, makes priests from people who are not Levites, and creates his own feast.

The political division becomes a worship disaster.

Jeroboam uses religion to protect power.

## First Kings 13

First Kings 13 opens with a man of God from Judah coming to Bethel by the word of the LORD.

Jeroboam stands by the altar to burn incense.

The man of God cries against the altar.

He says a child named Josiah will be born to David's house, and priests of the high places will be offered on that altar.

He gives a sign: the altar will be rent, and ashes poured out.

Jeroboam stretches out his hand, telling them to lay hold on the prophet.

His hand dries up so he cannot pull it back.

The altar is rent, and ashes pour out according to the sign.

Jeroboam asks the man of God to pray for him.

The man prays, and the king's hand is restored.

The king invites him home for refreshment and reward.

The man refuses because the word of the LORD commanded him not to eat bread, drink water, or return the same way.

But an old prophet in Bethel deceives him.

He claims an angel spoke by the word of the LORD and told him to bring the man back.

The man of God returns and eats.

Then the word of the LORD comes, judging his disobedience.

On the way home, a lion kills him, but does not eat the body or harm the donkey.

The old prophet buries him and tells his sons to bury him beside the man of God.

The chapter ends by saying Jeroboam does not return from his evil way.

This chapter is sobering.

Even a true messenger must keep obeying the word of the LORD.

Religious-sounding lies must be tested against what God has commanded.

## First Kings 14

First Kings 14 begins with Jeroboam's son Abijah becoming sick.

Jeroboam tells his wife to disguise herself and go to Ahijah the prophet.

Ahijah is old and cannot see, but the LORD tells him she is coming.

When she arrives, Ahijah calls her the wife of Jeroboam.

Disguise cannot hide anything from the LORD.

Ahijah announces judgment.

God exalted Jeroboam, but Jeroboam did evil above all before him and made other gods and molten images.

The house of Jeroboam will be cut off.

The child will die, but he is the only one of Jeroboam's house who will come to the grave because some good thing toward the LORD was found in him.

Jeroboam's wife returns, and the child dies as she comes to the threshold.

The chapter then turns to Rehoboam in Judah.

Judah does evil in the sight of the LORD.

They provoke Him with high places, images, groves, and abominations.

Shishak king of Egypt comes up against Jerusalem and takes away the treasures of the house of the LORD and the king's house.

Rehoboam makes shields of brass in place of the gold shields.

The glory has diminished.

The chapter ends with Rehoboam's death and Abijam reigning.

## First Kings 15

First Kings 15 gives the reigns of Abijam, Asa, Nadab, and Baasha.

Abijam walks in the sins of his father.

His heart is not perfect with the LORD.

Yet for David's sake, the LORD gives him a lamp in Jerusalem.

That phrase matters.

Judah's hope rests on God's covenant mercy, not the king's perfection.

Then Asa reigns.

Asa does what is right in the eyes of the LORD, as David his father did.

He removes sodomites, puts away idols, and removes Maachah from being queen because she made an idol in a grove.

Asa's heart is perfect with the LORD all his days.

Still, the high places are not removed.

The chapter is honest about reform that is real but not complete.

Then the story turns to Israel.

Nadab, Jeroboam's son, reigns and does evil, walking in his father's way.

Baasha conspires against him, kills him, and destroys the house of Jeroboam according to the word of the LORD.

There is war between Asa and Baasha.

The divided kingdom is now marked by instability, reform, idolatry, judgment, and conflict.

## Full Lesson Review

Day 85 shows the kingdom dividing and worship breaking.

Rehoboam rejects wise counsel.

Jeroboam creates golden calves.

A man of God warns Bethel.

Jeroboam refuses to repent.

Ahijah announces judgment.

Judah also falls into sin.

Asa brings real reform, though not complete reform.

The day teaches that bad leadership wounds worship and that idolatry is never a harmless political tool.

## Key Connections

First, Rehoboam shows the danger of prideful leadership that refuses servant wisdom.

Second, Jeroboam shows the danger of shaping worship to protect power.

Third, the man of God shows the seriousness of obeying the word of the LORD.

Fourth, Asa shows that reform is possible even in a dark period.

## Slow Walk Through the Day

Begin with Rehoboam at Shechem.

The people come with a real burden.

Solomon's kingdom had glory, but it also placed a heavy yoke on the people.

They ask Rehoboam for relief.

The old men advise him to serve the people that day and speak good words.

That counsel is not weakness.

It is wisdom.

True authority can serve without becoming small.

But Rehoboam rejects the counsel of the old men.

He listens to the young men who grew up with him.

Their answer is harsh, proud, and performative.

Rehoboam threatens heavier burdens.

His little finger will be thicker than his father's loins.

Solomon chastised with whips.

Rehoboam will chastise with scorpions.

The words are cruel.

The kingdom fractures.

The people cry, What portion have we in David?

This is the tearing that had been announced because of Solomon's sin.

Rehoboam is responsible for his foolishness, but the text also says this thing is from the LORD.

That means God's judgment is working through human pride.

Rehoboam then prepares for war, but Shemaiah brings the word of God.

They must not fight their brothers.

Judah and Benjamin listen and return.

That moment matters.

Even in the divided kingdom, obedience to God's word prevents further bloodshed.

Then Jeroboam faces his own test.

He fears the people's hearts will return to Jerusalem if they go there to worship.

His fear becomes false religion.

He makes two calves of gold.

He borrows language from the Exodus and says, Behold thy gods, O Israel, which brought thee up out of Egypt.

This is not creative worship.

It is covenant betrayal.

Jeroboam changes the place of worship, the priesthood, and the feast.

He uses worship as a tool to secure political control.

That is why his sin becomes such a repeated standard in the books of Kings.

He teaches Israel to sin.

Now listen to First Kings 13.

A man of God comes from Judah by the word of the LORD.

He cries against the altar at Bethel.

The altar will be torn.

The ashes will pour out.

A future king named Josiah will judge that false worship.

This prophecy reaches far beyond the moment.

The word of the LORD sees what Jeroboam cannot control.

When Jeroboam stretches out his hand against the prophet, his hand dries up.

The altar is torn exactly as the sign said.

Jeroboam asks for prayer, and his hand is restored.

But the king still does not repent from his evil way.

That is sobering.

A person can receive mercy and still refuse repentance.

The man of God refuses the king's invitation because God commanded him not to eat, drink, or return the same way.

Then an old prophet deceives him.

The old prophet claims an angel gave a new word.

But the new claim contradicts the command the man of God already received.

The lesson is serious.

Religious language cannot cancel God's clear word.

Even a true messenger must obey.

The lion killing the man of God is strange and severe, but the details show divine judgment.

The lion does not eat the body.

The donkey is unharmed.

This is not normal animal hunger.

It is a sign.

The word of the LORD stands.

Now slow down with First Kings 14.

Jeroboam's son is sick.

Jeroboam sends his wife in disguise to Ahijah.

He wants prophetic help without honest repentance.

The disguise fails before she enters the room because the LORD tells Ahijah.

Human costume cannot hide from divine sight.

Ahijah announces judgment on Jeroboam's house.

God had lifted Jeroboam up, but Jeroboam made other gods and cast the LORD behind his back.

That phrase is vivid.

To cast the LORD behind the back means to treat Him as unwanted and disregarded.

The child will die, yet he alone will be buried because some good thing toward the LORD is found in him.

The chapter then turns to Judah.

Judah also does evil.

High places, images, groves, and abominations fill the land.

Shishak of Egypt comes and takes treasures from the house of the LORD and the king's house.

Gold shields are replaced with brass shields.

That image tells a story.

The glory is fading.

The kingdom still has forms, guards, palace routines, and shields, but the gold has been replaced.

Sin diminishes what looked strong.

Now listen to First Kings 15.

Abijam walks in the sins of his father.

His heart is not perfect with the LORD.

Yet for David's sake, the LORD gives him a lamp in Jerusalem.

That phrase is covenant mercy.

Judah continues not because every king is faithful, but because the LORD keeps His promise to David.

Then Asa reigns.

Asa does right in the eyes of the LORD.

He removes public wickedness.

He puts away idols.

He removes Maachah from being queen because of her idol.

That detail matters because reform must reach even painful family and palace places.

Asa's heart is perfect with the LORD all his days.

Yet the high places are not removed.

The Bible gives a balanced picture.

Asa's reform is real.

It is also incomplete.

Then Israel's throne continues in violence.

Nadab walks in Jeroboam's sin.

Baasha kills him and destroys Jeroboam's house according to the word of the LORD.

The northern kingdom is unstable because false worship has poisoned its foundation.

## Guided Listening Reflection

Day 85 teaches that worship and leadership cannot be separated.

Rehoboam's leadership breaks the people.

Jeroboam's fear corrupts worship.

The man of God warns the altar.

Ahijah warns Jeroboam's house.

Asa reforms Judah.

Every chapter asks whether power will listen to the LORD.

The listener should especially watch Jeroboam.

His sin is not that he stops being religious.

His sin is that he invents religion to serve his fear.

He wants people to worship in a way that keeps his throne safe.

That is why the golden calves are so dangerous.

They are political strategy dressed in worship language.

False worship often promises security, convenience, and control.

But it leads people away from the LORD.

Rehoboam also teaches a hard lesson.

Harshness can lose what humility might have preserved.

He had a moment to serve.

He chose pride.

His words became a fracture line in the kingdom.

The old men's counsel was simple, but Rehoboam did not have the heart to receive it.

Asa gives some hope.

He shows that reform is possible after decline.

He does not fix everything, but he does act.

He removes what must be removed.

He honors the LORD in a dark period.

That should encourage the listener not to despise real reform because it is not yet complete.

The day ends with conflict, but not without mercy.

The lamp in Jerusalem still burns for David's sake.

The LORD's word still exposes false altars.

The LORD still raises reforming kings.

The divided kingdom is painful, but God has not lost His voice.

## Final Guided Recap

Before the closing, gather the main lessons one more time.

First Kings 12 shows a king losing the people through pride.

Rehoboam could have spoken good words.

He could have served the people and won their loyalty.

Instead he performs strength through cruelty.

The kingdom divides under the weight of foolish leadership.

Yet when Judah prepares to fight Israel, the word of the LORD stops them.

Even after division, obedience can prevent further destruction.

Jeroboam then shows a different kind of failure.

He is not merely harsh.

He is afraid.

His fear tells him that obedience is too risky.

So he makes worship convenient and politically useful.

Bethel and Dan become religious anchors for a kingdom trying to secure itself without trusting the LORD.

First Kings 13 shows that God's word confronts false worship.

The altar at Bethel is not safe just because a king stands beside it.

The man of God cries against it.

The sign happens.

Jeroboam's hand is dried and restored.

Still, Jeroboam does not turn.

The man of God is then judged for disobeying the word he had received.

That makes the chapter deeply sobering.

The word of the LORD must be obeyed by kings, prophets, and ordinary people alike.

First Kings 14 shows that disguises cannot fool God.

Jeroboam's wife may hide her identity from people, but not from the LORD.

Ahijah announces judgment, and the child dies as the word said.

Judah's decline under Rehoboam shows that the south is also in danger.

The gold shields taken by Shishak and replaced with brass become an image of lost glory.

First Kings 15 gives both warning and hope.

Abijam continues the sins of his father, but the LORD gives a lamp in Jerusalem for David's sake.

Asa then brings real reform.

He removes idols and confronts corruption even in the royal family.

His reform is not perfect, but it is faithful.

Meanwhile Israel's throne continues in sin and bloodshed.

So Day 85 is a day of division, false worship, prophetic warning, and partial reform.

The main question is not whether a nation can sound religious.

The question is whether king and people will listen to the LORD.

## Prayerful Landing

Let this day end with careful discernment.

Rehoboam sounded strong, but his strength was foolish.

Jeroboam sounded practical, but his practicality became idolatry.

The old prophet sounded spiritual, but his words contradicted the command of the LORD.

Asa's reform was imperfect, but it was real.

The listener needs discernment for all of that.

Not every hard word is wisdom.

Not every religious object is worship.

Not every spiritual claim is true.

Not every incomplete reform is worthless.

Day 85 teaches us to measure leadership, worship, prophecy, and reform by the word of the LORD.

The divided kingdom begins in pain, but God's word keeps speaking.

It stops a civil war.

It confronts an altar.

It exposes a disguise.

It judges a dynasty.

It preserves a lamp for David.

It honors reform in Asa.

So even when the kingdom is divided, the LORD is not silent.

That is the hope under the warning.

## Closing

As you finish Day 85, remember the split kingdom and the golden calves.

The people asked for relief, but received harshness.

Jeroboam wanted security, but created sin.

The only safe path for king and people is still the same: listen to the LORD and walk in His ways.
