# Bible In One Year - Day 20 Narrator Script

## Day 20 - Jacob Blesses Joseph's Sons

**Reading:** Genesis 47 and 48  
**Bible text:** King James Version, Genesis 47-48  
**Audio style:** Narrator teaching with Scripture reading  

## Opening

Welcome to Day 20 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are reading Genesis 47 and 48.

At this point in the story, Joseph has revealed himself to his brothers.

Jacob has heard the news that Joseph is alive.

The family has packed up and moved down into Egypt.

And God has already met Jacob on the road and told him not to be afraid.

That promise matters because Egypt is not just another location on a map.

Egypt is powerful.

Egypt is wealthy.

Egypt is foreign.

Egypt has food when the rest of the region is starving.

And for Jacob's family, Egypt is both a place of rescue and a place they must not mistake for home.

That is one of the tensions in today's reading.

God is going to preserve the family in Egypt.

But God's promise is still tied to the land He spoke about to Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob.

So Genesis 47 and 48 are not only about settling in.

They are about remembering.

They are about provision.

They are about famine.

They are about old age.

They are about blessing the next generation.

And they are about faith that still looks toward God's promise, even when the family is living far from the land.

Picture Jacob now.

He is an old man.

He has lived through exile, deception, family conflict, Rachel's death, Joseph's disappearance, years of grief, and now a shocking reunion.

He has seen more sorrow than many people could carry.

But he has also seen God keep him.

And near the end of his life, Jacob is still thinking about God's promise.

That is what makes Day 20 so important.

The story slows down so we can watch an old man pass faith forward.

## Genesis 47:1-12

**Scripture**

Then Joseph came and told Pharaoh, and said, My father and my brethren, and their flocks, and their herds, and all that they have, are come out of the land of Canaan.

And he took some of his brethren, even five men, and presented them unto Pharaoh.

And Pharaoh said unto his brethren, What is your occupation?

And they said unto Pharaoh, Thy servants are shepherds, both we, and also our fathers.

And Pharaoh spake unto Joseph, saying, Thy father and thy brethren are come unto thee:

The land of Egypt is before thee; in the best of the land make thy father and brethren to dwell.

And Joseph brought in Jacob his father, and set him before Pharaoh: and Jacob blessed Pharaoh.

And Pharaoh said unto Jacob, How old art thou?

And Jacob said unto Pharaoh, The days of the years of my pilgrimage are an hundred and thirty years: few and evil have the days of the years of my life been.

And Joseph nourished his father, and his brethren, and all his father's household, with bread.

**Narrator**

Genesis 47 begins with Joseph bringing his family before Pharaoh.

That might sound like a simple administrative detail, but slow down and picture the scene.

Joseph's brothers are shepherds from Canaan.

They are not Egyptian nobles.

They are not diplomats.

They are not merchants with power.

They are famine refugees with animals, families, and need.

And now they are standing before the most powerful ruler in the region.

Joseph does not hide who they are.

When Pharaoh asks about their occupation, they say plainly, Thy servants are shepherds.

That matters because Genesis already told us that shepherds were despised by Egyptians.

So the family enters Egypt with provision, but also with a clear identity.

They are not becoming Egyptian.

They are being preserved in Egypt.

There is a difference.

Pharaoh gives them Goshen, described as the best of the land for their needs.

Goshen gives them room for flocks.

It gives them food and safety.

It also gives them space to remain distinct.

That will matter later.

The family of promise is inside Egypt, but God is not letting them dissolve into Egypt.

Then Jacob enters before Pharaoh.

And something surprising happens.

Jacob blesses Pharaoh.

Think about that.

Pharaoh has wealth.

Pharaoh has land.

Pharaoh has power.

Pharaoh has grain.

Jacob is an old shepherd who had to leave Canaan because of famine.

But Jacob blesses Pharaoh because Jacob carries the covenant promise of God.

In worldly terms, Pharaoh looks greater.

In covenant terms, Jacob carries something Pharaoh does not.

God told Abraham that through his family blessing would come to the nations.

And here, in a small way, that promise is already visible.

The hungry old man blesses the king.

Then Pharaoh asks Jacob his age.

Jacob answers with one of the most honest lines in Genesis.

The days of the years of my pilgrimage are one hundred and thirty years.

Few and evil have the days of the years of my life been.

Jacob is not saying God failed him.

He is telling the truth about the road.

His life has been a pilgrimage.

Not settled.

Not simple.

Not clean.

Jacob has been the deceiver and the deceived.

He fled from Esau.

He was mistreated by Laban.

He lost Rachel.

He thought Joseph was dead.

His family was full of rivalry, grief, and sin.

So when Jacob says his years have been few and evil, he is not being dramatic.

He is speaking like an old man who knows faith does not erase the cost of the journey.

And yet, right there in Egypt, Joseph nourishes him.

The son Jacob thought was dead is now feeding the family.

The boy who was sold away from home has become the one God uses to preserve home.

That is grace.

## Genesis 47:13-26

**Scripture**

And there was no bread in all the land; for the famine was very sore, so that the land of Egypt and all the land of Canaan fainted by reason of the famine.

And Joseph gathered up all the money that was found in the land of Egypt, and in the land of Canaan, for the corn which they bought.

And when money failed in the land of Egypt, and in the land of Canaan, all the Egyptians came unto Joseph, and said, Give us bread: for why should we die in thy presence?

And Joseph gave them bread in exchange for horses, and for the flocks, and for the cattle of the herds.

And they said, Buy us and our land for bread, and we and our land will be servants unto Pharaoh: and give us seed, that we may live, and not die.

Then Joseph said unto the people, Behold, I have bought you this day and your land for Pharaoh: lo, here is seed for you, and ye shall sow the land.

And they said, Thou hast saved our lives.

**Narrator**

The next section pulls us back into the severity of the famine.

Sometimes when we read the Joseph story, we remember the dreams.

We remember the pit.

We remember the prison.

We remember Joseph rising to power.

But we can forget how desperate the famine really was.

Genesis says there was no bread in all the land.

Egypt and Canaan fainted because of the famine.

That is strong language.

This is not a mild shortage.

This is a regional collapse.

People are running out of food.

Then they run out of money.

Then they trade livestock.

Then they offer land and labor.

Modern readers often feel tension in this passage.

And honestly, that is understandable.

The people end up as servants to Pharaoh.

Land is transferred.

Power is centralized.

The scene feels heavy.

Genesis does not stop and answer every question we might have about economics, politics, or policy.

But it does show us what the people themselves say.

They say to Joseph, Thou hast saved our lives.

That is the emphasis of the passage.

Joseph is preserving life during a deadly famine.

His leadership is not romantic.

It is not simple.

It is crisis administration in a broken world.

He stores grain before the famine.

He distributes grain during the famine.

He manages resources as normal systems break down.

And then notice the detail at the end.

Joseph gives them seed.

That is important.

Seed means the future is still being considered.

If he only gives them bread, they can eat today.

But seed means they can plant tomorrow.

Joseph is not only trying to keep people alive for one more meal.

He is trying to help the land produce again when the famine pressure changes.

That is what wise leadership does.

It sees the emergency.

But it also thinks about the next season.

And in the wider story, this continues the pattern of Joseph's life.

God gave Joseph wisdom not only so Joseph would be lifted up, but so many people would be preserved.

The gift was never only for Joseph.

The gift was for life.

## Genesis 47:27-31

**Scripture**

And Israel dwelt in the land of Egypt, in the country of Goshen; and they had possessions therein, and grew, and multiplied exceedingly.

And Jacob lived in the land of Egypt seventeen years: so the whole age of Jacob was an hundred forty and seven years.

And the time drew nigh that Israel must die: and he called his son Joseph, and said unto him, If now I have found grace in thy sight, put, I pray thee, thy hand under my thigh, and deal kindly and truly with me; bury me not, I pray thee, in Egypt.

But I will lie with my fathers, and thou shalt carry me out of Egypt, and bury me in their buryingplace.

And he said, I will do as thou hast said.

And Israel bowed himself upon the bed's head.

**Narrator**

Now Genesis gives us a quiet but important contrast.

Jacob's family is doing well in Goshen.

They have possessions.

They are growing.

They are multiplying exceedingly.

That language should make us think about God's promise.

God told Jacob in Genesis 46 that He would make him a great nation in Egypt.

And now we begin to see that promise taking shape.

The family is no longer shrinking under famine.

They are being preserved.

They are growing.

But Jacob knows something.

Egypt is not the final home.

So when the time of his death draws near, he calls Joseph and makes a request.

Bury me not in Egypt.

Carry me out of Egypt.

Bury me with my fathers.

This is not only sentiment.

It is not just Jacob saying, I want to be buried near family because that feels meaningful.

It is that, but it is more than that.

This is faith.

Jacob is saying with his burial instructions that God's promise still stands.

Canaan still matters.

The cave Abraham bought still matters.

The land promised to Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob still matters.

Jacob is living in Egypt.

He is being fed in Egypt.

His family is growing in Egypt.

But he refuses to let Egypt become the final word over his identity.

That is a powerful lesson.

Sometimes God provides in a place that is not permanent.

And wisdom knows how to receive the provision without forgetting the promise.

Jacob's body will become a testimony.

When he dies, he wants to be carried back to the land.

Even in death, he is pointing the family toward God's word.

And then he worships.

Israel bows himself upon the bed's head.

He is weak.

He is near the end.

But he worships.

Old age has not erased faith.

Pain has not erased faith.

Egypt has not erased faith.

Jacob still belongs to the God who promised.

## Genesis 48:1-7

**Scripture**

And it came to pass after these things, that one told Joseph, Behold, thy father is sick.

And he took with him his two sons, Manasseh and Ephraim.

And one told Jacob, and said, Behold, thy son Joseph cometh unto thee: and Israel strengthened himself, and sat upon the bed.

And Jacob said unto Joseph, God Almighty appeared unto me at Luz in the land of Canaan, and blessed me,

And said unto me, Behold, I will make thee fruitful, and multiply thee, and I will make of thee a multitude of people.

And now thy two sons, Ephraim and Manasseh, which were born unto thee in the land of Egypt before I came unto thee into Egypt, are mine.

And as for me, when I came from Padan, Rachel died by me in the land of Canaan.

**Narrator**

Genesis 48 begins with Joseph hearing that Jacob is sick.

So Joseph brings his two sons, Manasseh and Ephraim.

This is a family moment.

But it is also a covenant moment.

Joseph is not only bringing grandchildren to see their grandfather.

He is bringing the next generation to the man who carries the promise of Abraham and Isaac.

Jacob strengthens himself and sits up on the bed.

That detail is tender.

He is old.

He is weak.

But he gathers strength because blessing the next generation matters.

Then Jacob begins with memory.

God Almighty appeared unto me at Luz.

Luz is Bethel.

This takes us back to Genesis 28.

Jacob was not strong then.

He was running.

He was afraid.

He had left home because Esau wanted to kill him.

He slept with a stone for a pillow.

And there, in that lonely place, God appeared to him.

God promised land.

God promised descendants.

God promised presence.

God promised to bring him back.

Now, near the end of his life, Jacob is still holding that promise.

That is important.

The promise God spoke on a lonely road becomes the promise Jacob repeats at a deathbed.

Faith remembers.

Then Jacob does something major.

He says Ephraim and Manasseh are mine.

In other words, Jacob adopts Joseph's two sons into his own inheritance structure.

They will be counted like Reuben and Simeon.

This is why Joseph receives a double portion in Israel.

There is no tribe simply called Joseph in the same way there is a tribe of Judah or Benjamin.

Instead, Joseph's inheritance comes through Ephraim and Manasseh.

Jacob is pulling these boys, born in Egypt, into the covenant family identity.

That matters.

They were born in a foreign land.

Their mother was Egyptian.

Their father ruled in Egypt.

But Jacob says they belong in the promise.

Then he remembers Rachel.

That might feel sudden, but it is deeply human.

Jacob is blessing Joseph's sons, and he remembers Joseph's mother.

Rachel's death is still with him.

Promise and grief sit together in the same speech.

And that is often how faith feels.

God's promises are real.

But the losses are real too.

Jacob does not have to erase Rachel's death in order to bless the next generation.

He carries both.

## Genesis 48:8-16

**Scripture**

And Israel beheld Joseph's sons, and said, Who are these?

And Joseph said unto his father, They are my sons, whom God hath given me in this place.

And he said, Bring them, I pray thee, unto me, and I will bless them.

Now the eyes of Israel were dim for age, so that he could not see.

And he brought them near unto him; and he kissed them, and embraced them.

And Israel said unto Joseph, I had not thought to see thy face: and, lo, God hath shewed me also thy seed.

And he blessed Joseph, and said, God, before whom my fathers Abraham and Isaac did walk, the God which fed me all my life long unto this day,

The Angel which redeemed me from all evil, bless the lads.

**Narrator**

Jacob sees Joseph's sons, though his eyesight is weak.

That detail should make Bible readers remember Isaac.

Isaac's eyes were dim when Jacob deceived him in Genesis 27.

But this scene is different.

There is weakness here, but not deception.

There is old age here, but not trickery.

Jacob asks who the boys are, and Joseph says, They are my sons, whom God hath given me in this place.

That phrase is beautiful.

In this place.

Egypt was the place of Joseph's slavery.

Egypt was the place of false accusation.

Egypt was the place of prison.

But Egypt also became the place where God gave Joseph sons.

That does not erase the suffering.

But it shows that God can give gifts in places we never would have chosen.

Then Jacob brings them near.

He kisses them.

He embraces them.

And he says to Joseph, I had not thought to see thy face, and now God has shown me your children also.

This is one of the sweetest lines in Genesis.

Jacob thought Joseph was dead.

For years, he lived with that grief.

He did not expect to see Joseph's face again.

But now he sees Joseph.

And more than Joseph.

He sees Joseph's sons.

Sometimes God's mercy does not only restore what we thought was lost.

Sometimes He lets us see fruit beyond what we thought possible.

Then Jacob blesses them.

Listen to how he describes God.

The God before whom my fathers Abraham and Isaac did walk.

The God which fed me all my life long unto this day.

The Angel which redeemed me from all evil.

This is not cold theology.

This is personal testimony.

Jacob is saying, This is the God of my fathers, yes.

But He is also the God who shepherded me.

The word translated fed has the idea of shepherding.

Jacob, the shepherd, says God has shepherded him.

Through exile.

Through fear.

Through Laban.

Through loss.

Through wrestling.

Through return.

Through grief.

Through Egypt.

God has shepherded him all his life long.

And Jacob calls Him the One who redeemed him from all evil.

That is a huge statement.

Jacob's life was not easy.

He was not protected from every painful thing.

But as he looks back, he can say God rescued him.

God carried him.

God kept him.

And that is the blessing he wants over the boys.

Not merely wealth.

Not merely status.

Not merely Egyptian success.

He wants them blessed by the God who shepherds and redeems.

## Genesis 48:17-22

**Scripture**

And when Joseph saw that his father laid his right hand upon the head of Ephraim, it displeased him.

And Joseph said unto his father, Not so, my father: for this is the firstborn; put thy right hand upon his head.

And his father refused, and said, I know it, my son, I know it.

He also shall become a people, and he also shall be great: but truly his younger brother shall be greater than he.

And he blessed them that day, saying, In thee shall Israel bless, saying, God make thee as Ephraim and as Manasseh.

And he set Ephraim before Manasseh.

And Israel said unto Joseph, Behold, I die: but God shall be with you, and bring you again unto the land of your fathers.

**Narrator**

Now comes the famous crossed-hands moment.

Joseph positions his sons carefully.

Manasseh is the firstborn, so Joseph expects Jacob's right hand, the hand of greater blessing, to rest on Manasseh.

Ephraim is younger, so Joseph expects the lesser position for him.

That was normal.

That was expected.

That was how family order usually worked.

But Jacob crosses his hands.

His right hand goes on Ephraim.

His left hand goes on Manasseh.

Joseph is displeased and tries to correct him.

Not so, my father.

This is the firstborn.

But Jacob says, I know it, my son, I know it.

That line matters.

Jacob is not confused.

His eyesight is weak, but his discernment is not.

The crossed hands are intentional.

Jacob knows exactly what he is doing.

And this moment fits a pattern we have seen throughout Genesis.

God often moves His promise in ways that overturn normal human expectation.

Isaac is chosen, not Ishmael.

Jacob is chosen, not Esau.

Joseph is lifted above his older brothers.

And now Ephraim is set before Manasseh.

Genesis keeps teaching us that blessing is not controlled by human custom.

Birth order matters in the culture.

But birth order does not control God.

Family expectation matters to people.

But family expectation does not control God's promise.

God is free to bless according to His wisdom.

That can be uncomfortable because we like predictable systems.

We like to know who should come first.

We like order we can manage.

But grace often crosses its hands.

Grace often surprises.

Grace often chooses in ways that remind us the promise belongs to God.

Then Jacob speaks to Joseph.

Behold, I die.

But God shall be with you.

And bring you again unto the land of your fathers.

That is the end of the chapter, and it is full of hope.

Jacob is dying.

But God is not.

Jacob's voice will soon be silent.

But God's promise will keep speaking.

Joseph may be powerful in Egypt.

But the future of the family is not Egypt.

God will be with you.

God will bring you again.

Jacob's last words in this chapter are not centered on Pharaoh, famine, or Egyptian security.

They are centered on God's presence and God's promised return.

## Closing

Day 20 is quieter than some of the chapters around it, but it is deeply important.

Genesis 47 and 48 show us what faith looks like after the dramatic reveal.

Joseph has already wept.

The brothers have already been reconciled.

Jacob has already come down to Egypt.

Now the question is, how will the family live in this new place?

And what will they remember?

They receive Goshen.

They receive bread.

They receive protection.

They multiply.

But Jacob keeps saying with his words and choices:

Egypt is not our final home.

Bury me with my fathers.

Remember the promise.

Bring the next generation into the covenant story.

Trust the God who shepherds.

Trust the God who redeems.

Trust the God who blesses in surprising ways.

And trust the God who will bring you again to the land of your fathers.

That is the heart of this day.

God can provide in temporary places.

God can preserve families through famine.

God can turn an old man's grief into blessing.

God can give more mercy than someone expected to see.

And God can carry His promise into the next generation, even when the family is living far from home.

Jacob's life was not easy.

He says so himself.

Few and evil have my days been.

But near the end, he can also say God has shepherded me all my life long.

Both are true.

The road was hard.

And God was faithful.

That is a word many people need.

Faith does not mean pretending the years were painless.

Faith means looking back over the years, with all their wounds and surprises, and still being able to say:

God fed me.

God redeemed me.

God was with me.

And God is not finished with the promise.

Jacob dies soon.

But the blessing continues.

The family is in Egypt.

But the promise is still alive.

And God will be with them until He brings them home.
