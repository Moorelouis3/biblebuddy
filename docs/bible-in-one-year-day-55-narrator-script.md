# Bible in One Year - Day 55 Narrator Script

**Reading:** Deuteronomy 30-33

## Opening

Welcome to Day 55 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are reading Deuteronomy chapters 30 through 33.

The theme is choose life and receive Moses' blessing.

Moses is almost at the end of his ministry.

He has retold the journey.

He has repeated the law.

He has warned Israel about blessing and curse.

Now he brings the covenant choice close to the heart.

Life is not hidden from them.

The LORD has spoken.

The people must love Him, obey His voice, and cling to Him.

Then Moses prepares Joshua, teaches Israel a song, and blesses the tribes.

These chapters feel like final words.

They are urgent.

They are tender.

They are serious.

And they keep pointing beyond Moses to the LORD who will remain with His people.

## Deuteronomy 30 verses 1 through 20

**Scripture**

Moses says that if Israel is scattered because of disobedience and then returns to the LORD, the LORD will have compassion and gather them again.

The LORD will circumcise their heart so they may love Him.

Moses says the commandment is not hidden or far away.

It is near, in their mouth and in their heart, that they may do it.

He sets before them life and good, death and evil.

He calls heaven and earth to witness and says, choose life.

**Narrator**

Deuteronomy 30 begins with hope after warning.

Moses has already spoken about blessing and curse.

He has warned Israel that rebellion will bring scattering.

But judgment is not the last word for the repentant.

If Israel returns to the LORD with all their heart and soul, the LORD will have compassion.

That matters.

The covenant is serious, but the LORD is merciful.

He does not treat repentance as meaningless.

He promises to gather His people again.

Then Moses says the LORD will circumcise their heart.

This is one of the deepest phrases in Deuteronomy.

Circumcision was the outward covenant sign.

But Moses says the heart itself needs God's work.

Israel does not only need new circumstances.

They need changed hearts.

They need love for the LORD from the inside.

That is why this chapter is not shallow.

It does not say, just try harder and everything will be fine.

It says the LORD must deal with the heart.

Then Moses says this commandment is not hidden from thee.

He also says it is not far off.

Israel does not need someone to climb into heaven to bring God's word down.

They do not need someone to cross the sea to bring it back.

The word is very near.

It is in their mouth and in their heart.

That means God has made His will known.

The problem is not that Israel lacks revelation.

The question is whether they will receive and obey what God has spoken.

For a beginner, this helps explain why Deuteronomy keeps repeating memory, teaching, and hearing.

God's people are not being asked to guess.

They are being called to respond.

Moses then sets two paths in front of Israel.

Life and good.

Death and evil.

Blessing and curse.

This is covenant clarity.

The choice is not vague.

Loving the LORD, walking in His ways, and keeping His commandments leads toward life.

Turning away leads toward death.

Then Moses says, choose life.

That phrase is short, but it carries the force of the whole chapter.

Choose life does not mean choose whatever feels alive in the moment.

It means choose the LORD.

It means love Him.

It means obey His voice.

It means cleave to Him.

Moses even says that the LORD is thy life.

That is the center of the passage.

Life is not finally found in the land by itself.

Life is not finally found in safety, harvest, family, or victory by themselves.

The LORD Himself is Israel's life.

The land matters because God promised it.

Obedience matters because God commanded it.

But the deepest gift is belonging to the LORD.

So Day 55 begins with a final appeal.

Do not treat God's word as far away.

Do not treat obedience as impossible to understand.

Do not treat life as something separate from God.

The word is near.

The choice is clear.

The LORD is merciful.

The people must return, love, obey, and live.

## Deuteronomy 31 verses 1 through 30

**Scripture**

Moses tells Israel that he is one hundred twenty years old and will not cross the Jordan.

He says the LORD will go before them, and Joshua will lead them.

Moses charges Joshua to be strong and of a good courage.

The law is written and given to the priests and elders.

It must be read every seven years at the feast of tabernacles.

The LORD tells Moses that Israel will turn away after his death, so Moses must write a song as a witness.

**Narrator**

Deuteronomy 31 begins with leadership transition.

Moses speaks plainly.

I am an hundred and twenty years old this day.

I can no more go out and come in.

He also says the LORD has told him that he will not go over Jordan.

This is a sobering moment.

Moses has led Israel for decades.

He stood before Pharaoh.

He stretched out his hand over the sea.

He went up Sinai.

He interceded when Israel sinned.

He carried the burden of a difficult people through the wilderness.

But he will not lead them into the land.

That could feel like the story is weakening.

But Moses does not point Israel to himself.

He says the LORD thy God, he will go over before thee.

That sentence is the comfort.

Moses will not cross.

But the LORD will go before them.

Joshua will lead, but Joshua is not the ultimate foundation.

The LORD is.

This is why Moses tells Joshua to be strong and of a good courage.

Courage is not based on Joshua's personality.

Courage is based on God's presence.

The LORD will not fail thee, nor forsake thee.

That phrase matters because leadership will change, but covenant faithfulness will not.

Then Moses writes the law and gives it to the priests and elders.

The word must not disappear when Moses dies.

The law must be preserved.

It must be read publicly.

Men, women, children, and strangers must hear it.

That detail is important.

The covenant word belongs in the ears of the whole community.

Children must hear.

Strangers living among them must hear.

The people must learn to fear the LORD and observe His words.

This is one of the clearest pictures of Scripture shaping a people after a leader is gone.

Moses will die.

But the written word remains.

The people will need repeated hearing.

They cannot live on one memory of one sermon.

They must hear again.

They must teach again.

They must gather again.

Then the chapter becomes painful.

The LORD tells Moses that after his death, Israel will turn after other gods.

The warning is not vague.

God sees the danger ahead.

So Moses must write a song.

The song will be a witness.

That means the song will testify when Israel forgets.

For a beginner, that may sound strange.

Why a song?

Because songs stay with people.

They can be memorized.

They can be repeated.

They can travel from one generation to another.

The song will carry truth into Israel's memory.

It will remind them of the LORD's faithfulness and their danger of rebellion.

Deuteronomy 31 therefore teaches three things at once.

Leadership changes.

God's word remains.

Memory must be taught before forgetfulness takes over.

Moses is not pretending Israel's future will be easy.

He is preparing them with courage, Scripture, and witness.

That is faithful leadership.

He does not make the people depend on his presence forever.

He points them to the LORD who goes before them.

He points them to Joshua who must lead under God.

And he points them to the written word that must be heard by every generation.

## Deuteronomy 32 verses 1 through 52

**Scripture**

Moses speaks the words of a song in the ears of Israel.

He calls heaven and earth to hear.

The LORD is called the Rock.

His work is perfect, and all His ways are judgment.

The song remembers how the LORD found Israel, led him, instructed him, and kept him.

It warns that Israel will forget God, grow fat, and provoke Him with strange gods.

The LORD will judge, but He will also vindicate His people and show that there is no god with Him.

Moses tells Israel to set their hearts unto all the words.

Then the LORD tells Moses to go up Mount Nebo and see the land before he dies.

**Narrator**

Deuteronomy 32 is the song of Moses.

It begins with heaven and earth as witnesses.

Give ear, O ye heavens.

Hear, O earth.

The song is not casual poetry.

It is covenant testimony.

Moses wants the creation itself to hear the witness against Israel.

Then he says, my doctrine shall drop as the rain.

That is a beautiful picture.

The teaching is meant to fall like rain and settle like dew.

God's word is not dry information.

It gives life.

It refreshes.

It nourishes.

Then Moses names the LORD as the Rock.

The Rock means stability, faithfulness, and strength.

His work is perfect.

All His ways are judgment.

A God of truth and without iniquity.

Just and right is He.

That opening is important because the song will speak honestly about Israel's failure.

Before Israel's sin is described, God's character is declared.

The problem is not that the LORD was unstable.

The problem is not that His way was crooked.

The LORD is faithful.

Israel is the one who corrupts itself.

The song remembers God's care.

He found Israel in a desert land.

He led him about.

He instructed him.

He kept him as the apple of His eye.

That phrase is tender.

It shows careful protection.

The song compares the LORD to an eagle stirring up her nest, fluttering over her young, spreading abroad her wings, and bearing them.

This is not distant leadership.

The LORD carried Israel.

He provided.

He gave abundance.

But then the song warns that abundance can become dangerous.

Jeshurun waxed fat, and kicked.

That means Israel grew full and rebelled.

The blessings that should have led to worship became the setting for forgetfulness.

They lightly esteemed the Rock of their salvation.

That is one of the great tragedies in the song.

The Rock saved them, but they treated Him as light.

They provoked Him with strange gods.

They sacrificed to devils, not to God.

They forgot the God who formed them.

Moses is putting future rebellion into song so Israel cannot say they were never warned.

The song also teaches judgment.

The LORD will hide His face.

He will provoke them with a foolish nation.

He will bring consequences for rebellion.

But the song does not end by making idols look powerful.

The LORD says, see now that I, even I, am he, and there is no god with me.

He kills and makes alive.

He wounds and heals.

No one can deliver out of His hand.

That means the LORD alone rules judgment and mercy.

The false gods are nothing beside Him.

At the end, Moses tells the people to set your hearts unto all the words.

That phrase is important.

He does not say, admire the song.

He says, set your hearts.

Take these words seriously.

Command your children to observe them.

For it is not a vain thing for you.

It is your life.

That line brings us back to Deuteronomy 30.

The word is near.

The LORD is their life.

These words are not empty.

They are life for the people.

Then the chapter ends with Moses being told to go up Mount Nebo.

He will see the land.

He will die there.

The reason is repeated: he trespassed at Meribah-kadesh and did not sanctify the LORD among the children of Israel.

This is painful, but it is not random.

Moses is honored, but Moses is still accountable.

The servant of the LORD must also obey the LORD.

Deuteronomy 32 therefore gives Israel a song that tells the truth.

God is faithful.

Israel is forgetful.

Idols are false.

Judgment is real.

Mercy belongs to the LORD.

The word is not empty.

It is life.

## Deuteronomy 33 verses 1 through 29

**Scripture**

Moses blesses the children of Israel before his death.

He speaks of the LORD coming from Sinai.

He blesses the tribes by name.

He speaks over Reuben, Judah, Levi, Benjamin, Joseph, Zebulun, Issachar, Gad, Dan, Naphtali, and Asher.

The chapter ends by saying there is none like unto the God of Jeshurun.

The eternal God is thy refuge, and underneath are the everlasting arms.

**Narrator**

Deuteronomy 33 gives Moses' blessing.

After warning, song, and covenant choice, Moses blesses the tribes.

This matters because Moses is not only a preacher of warning.

He is also a shepherd speaking blessing before he dies.

The chapter begins with the LORD coming from Sinai.

This roots the blessing in covenant revelation.

The God who blesses Israel is the God who spoke at Sinai.

He came with holy majesty.

He loved the people.

All His saints are in His hand.

They sit down at His feet.

They receive His words.

That picture is tender and strong at the same time.

The holy God gives words to His people.

Then Moses blesses tribe by tribe.

Reuben is blessed with life.

Judah is blessed with help against enemies.

Levi is connected to teaching, priestly service, and covenant loyalty.

Benjamin is described as beloved of the LORD, dwelling in safety.

Joseph receives rich language of fruitfulness, land, and favor.

Zebulun and Issachar are blessed in their going out and their tents.

Gad is spoken of with strength.

Dan is compared to a lion's whelp.

Naphtali is satisfied with favor.

Asher is blessed with strength for his days.

Some details are difficult for modern readers because they are tribal blessings tied to Israel's future.

But the big idea is clear.

Moses is placing the tribes under the care, purpose, and help of the LORD.

He is not giving empty compliments.

He is speaking covenant blessing over real tribes with real futures.

The ending is one of the most beautiful parts of Deuteronomy.

There is none like unto the God of Jeshurun.

Jeshurun is a poetic name for Israel.

Moses says no one is like Israel's God.

He rides upon the heaven in thy help.

The eternal God is thy refuge.

Underneath are the everlasting arms.

Those words are strong comfort.

Israel is about to lose Moses.

They are about to enter the land.

They will face enemies, temptations, failures, and fears.

But underneath them are not Moses' arms.

Underneath them are the everlasting arms of God.

The eternal God is their refuge.

That means their security is deeper than any human leader.

Moses can bless them because God will remain.

The chapter ends by calling Israel happy, or blessed.

Who is like unto thee, O people saved by the LORD?

That phrase explains Israel's identity.

They are saved by the LORD.

Their help and shield are not their own greatness.

Their future depends on the God who saves.

Day 55 therefore closes with blessing after warning.

Moses has called Israel to choose life.

He has charged Joshua.

He has written the law.

He has taught the song.

He has warned them that the word is not empty.

And now he blesses them by pointing to the eternal God.

This is a fitting near-ending to Moses' work.

Moses will not be Israel's refuge forever.

The LORD will.

Moses will not carry them into every battle.

The everlasting arms will.

Moses will not be the source of their life.

The LORD is their life.

Before we close Day 55, listen to the whole movement one more time.

Deuteronomy 30 brings the covenant to the heart.

The word is not far away.

The command is not hidden.

The LORD has made His will known.

The question is whether Israel will return, love, obey, and cling to Him.

Choose life is not a slogan.

It is a call to belong to the LORD with the whole heart.

Deuteronomy 31 prepares Israel for life after Moses.

That is a major transition.

Moses will not cross Jordan.

But the LORD will go before them.

Joshua must be strong and courageous, but his courage rests on God's presence.

The law must be read again and again because the people will need more than memory of Moses.

They need the written word of the LORD.

Deuteronomy 32 gives them a song because God knows people forget.

The song tells the truth in a form Israel can carry.

The LORD is the Rock.

His work is perfect.

Israel is the one who forgets, grows proud, and turns aside.

Yet the song also says there is no god beside the LORD.

He wounds and heals.

He kills and makes alive.

He alone has final power.

Deuteronomy 33 gives blessing.

Moses speaks tribe by tribe, but the final comfort is not in the tribes themselves.

The final comfort is the eternal God.

Underneath are the everlasting arms.

That phrase is a beautiful answer to the fear of losing Moses.

Moses' arms are not underneath Israel.

God's arms are.

That is why Day 55 is such a strong ending movement.

The people are called to choose life, but they are not asked to walk into the future without God.

The LORD is their life.

The LORD goes before them.

The LORD gives His word.

The LORD gives witness.

The LORD gives blessing.

The LORD is their refuge.

So Day 55 gives us one of the clearest handoffs in the Bible.

The people are not handed from Moses into emptiness.

They are handed from Moses into the care of the LORD.

They are handed the written law.

They are handed a song of witness.

They are handed Joshua's leadership.

They are handed tribal blessing.

And above all, they are handed the same covenant God who has carried them from Egypt to this moment.

That is why the command choose life is full of hope.

The life set before Israel is not vague optimism.

It is life with the LORD who speaks, gathers, forgives, leads, warns, blesses, and holds His people underneath with everlasting arms.

One more detail is worth holding carefully.

Moses is not ending by making Israel admire Moses.

He is ending by making Israel trust the LORD.

That is faithful leadership.

Moses has been central to the story, but he is not the source of the promise.

He has spoken God's word, but he is not the God who spoke.

He has led the people through the wilderness, but he is not the one who can finally give them life.

So his final words keep turning Israel's eyes away from human dependence and toward covenant trust.

This helps us understand the emotional shape of the chapter.

There is sadness because Moses is leaving.

There is seriousness because Israel has been warned.

There is urgency because the people must choose life.

There is courage because Joshua is charged.

There is memory because the song will testify.

And there is comfort because the eternal God is still underneath His people.

That is why the phrase everlasting arms matters so much.

Arms carry.

Arms protect.

Arms hold up the weak.

Moses' arms once grew tired in battle, and others had to hold them up.

But God's arms are everlasting.

They do not weaken.

They do not disappear when Moses dies.

They do not fail when Israel faces the land.

For a beginner, this makes the end of Moses' ministry easier to understand.

The Bible honors Moses deeply, but it never lets Moses replace the LORD.

The story can move forward because God remains.

The people can choose life because the LORD is their life.

Joshua can be courageous because the LORD goes before him.

Israel can enter the future because underneath are the everlasting arms.

This also keeps Moses' blessing from becoming sentimental.

The blessing is strong because it rests on who God is.

Israel is not blessed because the future will be easy.

They are blessed because the LORD is their help.

They are blessed because the LORD is their shield.

They are blessed because the eternal God does not die when Moses dies.

That is the hope at the edge of the land.

The people must choose life, but the life they choose is not an idea.

The LORD Himself is their life.

They must hear the song, but the song points beyond itself to the faithful Rock.

They must receive the blessing, but the blessing rests on the God who carries His people.

## Closing

That is Day 55.

Deuteronomy 30 through 33 brings Moses' final ministry close to its end.

The word is near.

The choice is clear.

Joshua is charged.

The law is written.

The song is taught.

The tribes are blessed.

And the LORD remains the life, refuge, help, and shield of His people.

Choose life means cling to Him.
