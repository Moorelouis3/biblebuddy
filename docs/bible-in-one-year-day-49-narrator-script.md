# Bible in One Year - Day 49 Narrator Script

**Reading:** Deuteronomy 6-9

## Opening

Welcome to Day 49 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are reading Deuteronomy chapters 6 through 9.

The theme is love God and remember grace.

This reading includes one of the most important calls in Scripture: love the LORD thy God with all thine heart, and with all thy soul, and with all thy might.

## Deuteronomy 6 verses 1 through 25

**Scripture**

Moses commands Israel to fear the LORD and keep His commandments.

He says, Hear, O Israel: The LORD our God is one LORD.

Israel must love the LORD with all the heart, soul, and might.

These words must be taught diligently to children and remembered in daily life.

Moses warns them not to forget the LORD when they enjoy houses, wells, vineyards, and olive trees they did not build.

**Narrator**

This chapter is central to the whole Bible.

Hear, O Israel is a call to listen with covenant loyalty.

The LORD is one.

Israel must love Him wholly.

The heart belongs to Him.

The soul belongs to Him.

Their strength belongs to Him.

Love for God is not meant to stay in a private moment.

It enters the home.

It shapes children.

It follows the people when they sit, walk, lie down, and rise up.

God wants whole-life love.

Deuteronomy 6 contains the Shema, one of the central confessions of Israel's faith.

Hear, O Israel means more than letting sound enter the ears.

It means listen with loyalty.

The LORD our God is one LORD means Israel belongs to the one true God.

He is not one god among many gods competing for attention.

He alone is the LORD.

That truth leads directly into love.

Israel must love the LORD with all the heart, soul, and might.

Heart means the inner life.

Soul means the whole living person.

Might means strength, energy, and resources.

The point is total love.

God does not want a small religious corner of Israel's life.

He wants the whole person.

That love must be taught diligently to children.

Diligently means carefully, repeatedly, and intentionally.

Parents must talk about God's words when they sit, walk, lie down, and rise up.

That means discipleship belongs in ordinary life.

The home becomes a place of teaching.

Daily rhythms become reminders of covenant loyalty.

Moses also warns about prosperity.

Houses, wells, vineyards, and olive trees can become spiritually dangerous if they make Israel forget the LORD.

The danger is not that the gifts are bad.

The danger is forgetting the Giver.

So Deuteronomy 6 teaches love, teaching, memory, and warning all together.

## Deuteronomy 7 verses 1 through 26

**Scripture**

Moses warns Israel not to make covenant with the nations of Canaan or serve their gods.

Israel is a holy people unto the LORD.

The LORD chose them because He loved them and kept the oath He swore to their fathers.

He will keep covenant and mercy with those who love Him and keep His commandments.

**Narrator**

Israel is chosen by grace.

Moses makes that clear.

God did not choose them because they were the biggest nation.

He loved them.

He kept His oath.

That grace gives Israel a holy identity.

They must not blend into the idolatry of the land.

Grace does not make obedience unnecessary.

Grace tells Israel who they are and why their loyalty matters.

Deuteronomy 7 explains election by grace in very simple terms.

Israel is a holy people, meaning they are set apart for the LORD.

But Moses makes clear that they were not chosen because they were the greatest or strongest nation.

They were few.

God chose them because He loved them and because He kept the oath He swore to their fathers.

That protects Israel from pride.

Their identity begins with God's love, not their greatness.

The command not to make covenant with the idolatrous nations is tied to that identity.

Israel belongs to the LORD, so they must not bind themselves to gods that are not gods.

The warning about intermarriage in this context is about covenant loyalty.

The danger is being turned away to serve other gods.

God's love calls for exclusive allegiance.

Moses also tells Israel that the LORD keeps covenant and mercy with those who love Him and keep His commandments.

Love and obedience belong together.

Grace does not erase loyalty.

It creates a people who know whom they belong to.

The chapter also tells Israel not to fear the nations.

They must remember what the LORD did to Pharaoh and Egypt.

Past deliverance becomes courage for future obedience.

The God who rescued them before can keep them as they enter the land.

## Deuteronomy 8 verses 1 through 20

**Scripture**

Moses tells Israel to remember the way the LORD led them forty years in the wilderness.

God humbled them, tested them, and fed them with manna.

Man does not live by bread only, but by every word that proceeds out of the mouth of the LORD.

Moses warns that when they prosper in the good land, they must not forget the LORD.

**Narrator**

The wilderness was not meaningless.

God used it to humble and teach His people.

Manna taught dependence.

Every day, Israel had to receive bread from God.

But prosperity will bring a new test.

When they have houses, herds, silver, and gold, they may think their own power made them rich.

Moses says remember.

The God who fed you in scarcity is also the God who gives strength in abundance.

Deuteronomy 8 explains the purpose of the wilderness.

Moses says God humbled Israel and proved them.

To prove means to test what is in the heart.

The wilderness showed whether Israel would keep God's commandments when life was difficult.

Manna was part of that lesson.

God fed them with bread they did not know.

They had to receive daily provision from His hand.

The phrase man doth not live by bread only is one of the most important lines in Scripture.

Bread is necessary, but bread is not ultimate.

Human life depends on the word of the LORD.

That means God's command, promise, and provision are deeper than physical food.

Jesus later quotes this verse when tempted in the wilderness.

So Deuteronomy 8 connects Israel's wilderness testing with a larger biblical theme.

God teaches His people dependence.

Prosperity will bring another test.

When Israel has eaten and is full, when houses are built, when herds multiply, when silver and gold increase, the heart may be lifted up.

They may say, My power and the might of mine hand hath gotten me this wealth.

Moses says remember the LORD.

He gives power to get wealth.

Scarcity tests trust.

Abundance tests memory.

Both require dependence on God.

## Deuteronomy 9 verses 1 through 29

**Scripture**

Moses tells Israel they are crossing Jordan to possess nations greater and mightier than themselves.

But they must not say the LORD gave them the land because of their own righteousness.

Moses reminds them of their rebellion, especially the golden calf.

He remembers interceding before the LORD for the people.

**Narrator**

Deuteronomy 9 attacks pride before it grows.

Israel will win battles, but they must not rewrite the story.

The land is not a reward for their perfect righteousness.

Moses reminds them of the golden calf because it proves the point.

They have been stubborn.

They have needed mercy.

They have survived because God kept His promise.

This chapter teaches humility.

The story of Israel is grace, not self-congratulation.

Deuteronomy 9 attacks self-righteousness before Israel can settle into it.

Moses says they are crossing over to face nations greater and mightier than themselves.

The cities are great and fenced up to heaven.

The people are strong.

The sons of Anak are known as giants.

But the LORD goes before Israel as a consuming fire.

Victory will come from Him.

Then Moses gives the warning: do not say it is because of my righteousness.

That phrase matters because success can easily rewrite memory.

Israel might win and then assume they deserved the land.

Moses says no.

The land is not proof of Israel's perfection.

It is connected to God's promise to Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, and to judgment on the wickedness of the nations.

Then Moses reminds them of the golden calf.

That memory is painful on purpose.

At Horeb, while Moses was receiving the covenant tablets, Israel made an idol.

They broke covenant almost immediately.

Moses shattered the tablets, interceded, fasted, and pleaded for the people.

This proves they are not receiving the land because they have been righteous and easy to lead.

They have been stubborn.

They have needed mercy.

Moses' intercession reminds the reader that Israel survived because the LORD kept His promise.

Before we close Day 49, hold the whole day together.

Deuteronomy 6 calls Israel to love the LORD with the whole heart.

Deuteronomy 7 says Israel was chosen by grace, not greatness.

Deuteronomy 8 says the wilderness taught dependence and prosperity must not create forgetfulness.

Deuteronomy 9 says the land is not earned by Israel's righteousness.

That means the covenant life is love, memory, humility, and grace.

For beginners, this is one of the clearest sections in the Old Testament.

God wants more than outward religion.

He wants love that fills the home.

He wants children taught with care.

He wants people who remember grace in prosperity.

He wants obedience without pride.

He wants Israel to know that everything good comes from Him.

Day 49 speaks strongly because the land will test Israel in a new way.

Need tested them in the wilderness.

Fullness will test them in Canaan.

Moses is preparing them not to forget God when life gets good.

That is a lesson every reader can understand.

Blessing should lead to love, not pride.

Provision should lead to gratitude, not forgetfulness.

Victory should lead to humility, not self-congratulation.

The LORD is the center from beginning to end.

Before we close, let us slow down over the teaching flow of this day.

Deuteronomy 6 begins with love.

That matters because many people think the Old Testament is only about outward rules.

But Moses begins the heart of Israel's daily life with love for the LORD.

Hear, O Israel, the LORD our God is one LORD.

This is a confession of loyalty.

Israel is not to divide worship among many gods.

They belong to the one true God.

Then Moses says to love the LORD with all the heart, all the soul, and all the might.

That means love for God is not supposed to be small or occasional.

It is whole-person love.

The heart is the inner life.

The soul is the life of the person.

The might is strength, effort, and capacity.

In simple words, God is not asking for a leftover corner of Israel's life.

He is calling for all of them.

Then Moses talks about teaching children.

This is not classroom-only teaching.

The words are to be spoken in the house.

They are to be spoken on the way.

They are to be spoken when lying down and rising up.

That means God's word belongs in ordinary routines.

Faith is not only for the sanctuary.

It belongs at the table, on the road, at bedtime, and at the beginning of the day.

For Bible Buddy readers, this is one of the easiest places to see the purpose of daily Scripture.

God's word shapes people through repeated attention.

Then Moses warns Israel about houses they did not build, wells they did not dig, and vineyards they did not plant.

These are pictures of blessing.

Israel is about to receive good things.

But good things can become spiritually dangerous when people forget the Giver.

Moses is not against blessing.

He is against forgetfulness.

That is why he says they must not forget the LORD who brought them out of Egypt.

The Exodus must stay in their memory even when life becomes comfortable.

Deuteronomy 7 then explains why Israel was chosen.

They were not chosen because they were the greatest people.

They were chosen because the LORD loved them and kept the oath He swore to their fathers.

That keeps Israel humble.

Election is not a trophy for greatness.

It is grace.

God's choice begins with His love, His promise, and His faithfulness.

Moses also warns against covenant mixing with the nations around them.

This is not about ordinary dislike of other people.

The issue is worship.

Israel must not be pulled into idolatry.

Marriage alliances in that world could join families, gods, customs, and political loyalties.

Moses warns that such unions could turn Israel's heart away from the LORD.

That is why the command is serious.

The LORD is preserving worship.

He is protecting the covenant.

He is guarding the people from spiritual compromise.

Deuteronomy 8 then explains the wilderness.

The wilderness was not wasted time.

It was training.

God humbled Israel.

He let them hunger.

He fed them with manna.

He taught them that man does not live by bread only.

That does not mean bread is unimportant.

God gave them bread from heaven.

It means physical food is not enough if people live apart from God's word.

Israel needed food for the body and trust in the LORD for the soul.

The manna taught daily dependence.

They could not store up independence from God.

They had to receive daily provision.

They had to trust God's instruction.

They had to learn that survival came from Him.

Then Moses warns about prosperity again.

When they eat and are full, build good houses, and see wealth increase, they must not say their own power got them these things.

That warning is very practical.

Need can make people cry out to God.

Comfort can make people forget God.

The land will test Israel differently than the wilderness did.

The wilderness tested hunger.

The land will test fullness.

Both tests reveal the heart.

Deuteronomy 9 then removes self-righteousness.

Moses says Israel must not think they receive the land because of their own righteousness.

He points back to the golden calf.

That was one of Israel's greatest failures.

While Moses was receiving the covenant tablets, the people made an idol.

They broke the covenant almost as soon as it was given.

That memory is not included to shame them without purpose.

It is included to humble them with truth.

They need to know that mercy, not merit, has carried them.

They need to know that God's promise, not their perfection, has preserved them.

They need to know that Moses had to intercede because their sin was real.

This helps readers understand the Bible's larger story.

God's people need grace from the beginning.

They are called to obedience, but they are not saved by pretending they have never failed.

The LORD keeps covenant.

The LORD shows mercy.

The LORD corrects pride.

Day 49 therefore has a strong heart message.

Love God fully.

Teach His word daily.

Remember Him in blessing.

Receive election as grace.

Treat prosperity as a gift.

Learn from the wilderness.

Do not claim righteousness you do not have.

Trust the God who keeps His promise.

That is why this day has so much weight.

It teaches the spiritual posture Israel must carry into the land.

They must enter as loved people, taught people, humbled people, and remembering people.

One more thing is worth noticing before the final summary.

Day 49 keeps joining the heart and the home.

Moses does not separate private love for God from public obedience.

He does not separate worship from parenting.

He does not separate memory from daily habits.

The command to teach children shows that faith must become understandable.

Children are not expected to absorb covenant life by accident.

They must hear the story.

They must hear the commands.

They must see the connection between God's rescue and the way the family lives.

That helps Bible Buddy readers understand why simple teaching matters.

The Bible is deep, but it is meant to be taught plainly.

The next generation needs words they can understand.

They need parents and leaders who can explain why the LORD is worthy of love.

They need to know why idols are dangerous.

They need to know why blessing should lead to gratitude.

They need to know why pride is spiritually dangerous.

The wilderness lesson also becomes clearer when we connect Deuteronomy 8 and 9.

In chapter 8, Moses says the wilderness humbled Israel.

In chapter 9, Moses reminds them that they were stubborn.

Those two ideas belong together.

God used the wilderness to expose what was already in the heart.

Hunger showed whether they trusted Him.

Manna showed whether they would receive daily provision His way.

Delay showed whether they would wait.

Abundance would later show whether they would remember.

The golden calf showed that even rescued people can turn quickly when their hearts are not settled on the LORD.

That is why Moses speaks with such urgency.

He is not trying to make Israel feel hopeless.

He is trying to make them honest.

Honest people can receive mercy.

Proud people rewrite the story and call themselves righteous.

Moses will not allow that.

The land is gift.

The promise is grace.

The covenant is serious.

The LORD is faithful.

Israel's future depends on remembering those truths.

So Day 49 is not only about avoiding idols.

It is about becoming the kind of people who remember rightly when life changes.

Remember in the house.

Remember on the road.

Remember when hungry.

Remember when full.

Remember after victory.

Remember after failure.

Remember that the LORD loved first.

That is the heart of this reading.

So when the listener finishes Day 49, the point should be clear.

God is not only preparing Israel to cross a border.

He is preparing them to become a people who love, remember, teach, obey, and stay humble after the blessing arrives.

Before we close, listen again to the emotional center of these chapters.

Deuteronomy 6 begins with love.

Thou shalt love the LORD thy God is not a small command added beside other rules.

It is the heart of covenant life.

Moses wants Israel's obedience to come from belonging, not from empty routine.

That is why the words must be in the heart.

That is why they must be taught to children.

That is why they must be spoken in the house, on the road, when lying down, and when rising up.

The covenant is meant to shape ordinary time.

Faithfulness is not supposed to appear only at the sanctuary.

It is supposed to be heard in family conversations.

It is supposed to guide decisions during the day.

It is supposed to be remembered before rest and when the day begins.

Then Deuteronomy 7 shows that Israel's love for God is grounded in God's love first.

The LORD did not choose Israel because they were more impressive than other nations.

He loved them because of His promise.

That keeps obedience from becoming pride.

The people are not allowed to say, God chose us because we were naturally better.

They must say, God loved us, kept His oath, and redeemed us by His power.

That is grace.

Then Deuteronomy 8 warns about a danger that comes after grace.

The danger is forgetting.

When hunger is gone, when houses are full, when flocks multiply, and when the land is good, the heart can quietly lift itself up.

Moses knows prosperity can become spiritually dangerous if memory disappears.

So he tells them to remember the wilderness and remember the manna.

The manna taught daily dependence.

The good land must not erase that lesson.

Then Deuteronomy 9 removes another kind of pride.

Israel must not say, the LORD brought me into this land because of my righteousness.

Moses tells the truth about the golden calf because grace needs honest memory.

The people were stubborn.

They failed badly.

Yet the LORD remained faithful to His promise.

That is why Day 49 matters so much.

It teaches love without pride.

It teaches obedience without self-congratulation.

It teaches memory without pretending the past was cleaner than it was.

It teaches grace without turning grace into laziness.

The LORD loved first.

The people must love Him back with the whole heart.

That is also why Moses keeps bringing children into the picture.

The next generation will not automatically remember Egypt, manna, or the golden calf just because their parents lived through those moments.

They must be taught.

They must hear the story.

They must learn why the commandments matter.

They must understand that obedience is not detached from rescue.

The parents must speak the words in ordinary places because ordinary places are where forgetfulness grows.

The home can become a place of memory.

The road can become a place of teaching.

The table can become a place of gratitude.

The full pantry can become a place of worship instead of pride.

That is the practical force of Day 49.

Love the LORD.

Remember His grace.

Teach the children.

Stay humble when the blessing arrives.

## Closing

That is Day 49.

Deuteronomy 6 through 9 calls Israel to love God fully, teach His words diligently, remember His grace, and refuse pride.

The heart of covenant life is not forgetful religion.

It is love shaped by memory.
