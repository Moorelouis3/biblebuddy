# Bible In One Year - Day 32 Narrator Script

## Day 32 - Offerings and Atonement

**Reading:** Leviticus 1 through 4
**Bible text:** King James Version, Leviticus 1-4
**Audio style:** Narrator teaching with Scripture reading

## Opening

Welcome to Day 32 of Bible in One Year.

Today we begin the book of Leviticus.

We are reading Leviticus 1 through 4.

That may sound intimidating.

For many readers, Leviticus feels like the place where the Bible suddenly becomes difficult.

Animals.

Blood.

Altars.

Priests.

Flour.

Oil.

Salt.

Fire.

Offerings.

But Leviticus is not random ritual.

It begins exactly where Exodus ended.

Exodus ended with the glory of the LORD filling the tabernacle.

God came to dwell among His people.

That is beautiful.

But it also raises a serious question:

How can sinful people live near a holy God?

That is what Leviticus begins to answer.

The God who fills the tabernacle now speaks from the tabernacle.

And He teaches Israel the way near.

So today we will see four major offerings.

The burnt offering.

The grain offering.

The peace offering.

And the sin offering.

These chapters teach surrender, gratitude, fellowship, cleansing, atonement, and forgiveness.

The details are heavy because access to God is holy.

But the details are also merciful because God is providing a way for His people to come near.

## Leviticus 1:1-4

**Scripture**

And the LORD called unto Moses, and spake unto him out of the tabernacle of the congregation.

Speak unto the children of Israel, and say unto them, If any man of you bring an offering unto the LORD.

If his offering be a burnt sacrifice of the herd, let him offer a male without blemish.

And he shall put his hand upon the head of the burnt offering; and it shall be accepted for him to make atonement for him.

**Narrator**

Leviticus begins with God calling to Moses.

But notice where God speaks from.

From the tabernacle.

Exodus ended with God's glory filling the tent.

Now Leviticus begins with God's voice coming from the tent.

That means Leviticus is not disconnected from the story.

It is the next step.

God has come near, and now He teaches His people how to approach Him.

The burnt offering begins with an animal without blemish.

The worshiper brings it willingly.

Then he lays his hand on the animal's head.

That action matters.

It shows identification.

The offering is connected to the worshiper.

And the text says it is accepted for him to make atonement.

Atonement means the offering deals with guilt and makes a way for the worshiper to stand before God.

Already, Leviticus is teaching that nearness to God is a gift.

It is not casual.

It comes through the way God provides.

## Leviticus 1:5-9

**Scripture**

And he shall kill the bullock before the LORD.

And the priests, Aaron's sons, shall bring the blood, and sprinkle the blood round about upon the altar.

And the priest shall burn all on the altar, to be a burnt sacrifice, an offering made by fire, of a sweet savour unto the LORD.

**Narrator**

This section is heavy.

The animal is killed.

The blood is handled by the priests.

The pieces are arranged on the altar.

And the whole offering is burned.

That is why it is called a burnt offering.

The entire offering goes up to God.

This teaches surrender.

Not a small part.

Not leftovers.

The whole offering rises to the LORD.

The phrase sweet savour means the offering is accepted according to God's instruction.

It does not mean God enjoys death in a shallow way.

It means the worship is received because it is brought the way God commanded.

Leviticus wants us to feel both truths:

Sin is serious.

And God provides a way near.

## Leviticus 1:10-17

**Scripture**

And if his offering be of the flocks, namely, of the sheep, or of the goats, for a burnt sacrifice.

And if the burnt sacrifice for his offering to the LORD be of fowls, then he shall bring his offering of turtledoves, or of young pigeons.

And the priest shall bring it unto the altar.

And the priest shall burn it upon the altar: it is a burnt sacrifice, an offering made by fire, of a sweet savour unto the LORD.

**Narrator**

The burnt offering can come from the herd, the flock, or birds.

That matters because not every Israelite has the same resources.

Some can bring cattle.

Some can bring sheep or goats.

Some can bring birds.

But the poor are not shut out from worship.

God provides a way for different people to come near.

The offering still matters.

The altar still matters.

Atonement still matters.

But access is not limited only to the wealthy.

Leviticus is serious, but it is not heartless.

The holy God makes room for His people to approach Him through the offering He commands.

## Leviticus 2:1-3

**Scripture**

And when any will offer a meat offering unto the LORD, his offering shall be of fine flour.

And he shall pour oil upon it, and put frankincense thereon.

And the priest shall burn the memorial of it upon the altar, to be an offering made by fire, of a sweet savour unto the LORD.

**Narrator**

Leviticus 2 describes the grain offering.

The KJV calls it a meat offering, but here meat means food, not animal flesh.

This offering comes from the fruit of the ground.

Fine flour.

Oil.

Frankincense.

A memorial portion burned on the altar.

The grain offering teaches gratitude and dedication.

Israel gives back to God from daily provision.

The field, the mill, the kitchen, and the altar are connected.

Worship is not only dramatic sacrifice.

It also includes ordinary provision offered back to the Lord with care.

## Leviticus 2:4-10

**Scripture**

And if thou bring an oblation of a meat offering baken in the oven, it shall be unleavened cakes of fine flour mingled with oil.

And if thy oblation be a meat offering baken in a pan, it shall be of fine flour unleavened, mingled with oil.

And the priest shall burn the memorial of it upon the altar.

**Narrator**

Now the grain offering appears in prepared forms.

Baked in an oven.

Made in a pan.

Prepared in pieces with oil.

This brings worship close to ordinary life.

Leviticus is not only talking about animals in the courtyard.

It is also talking about food, labor, preparation, and gratitude.

The worshiper brings something carefully prepared.

And again, a memorial portion is burned to the LORD.

That phrase means the part represents the whole gift before God.

The rest belongs to Aaron and his sons.

So the offering supports priestly service while honoring the LORD as the giver of provision.

## Leviticus 2:11-16

**Scripture**

No meat offering, which ye shall bring unto the LORD, shall be made with leaven.

Neither shall ye suffer the salt of the covenant of thy God to be lacking from thy meat offering.

With all thine offerings thou shalt offer salt.

And if thou offer a meat offering of thy firstfruits unto the LORD.

**Narrator**

This section gives important boundaries for the grain offering.

No leaven is burned on the altar.

No honey is burned on the altar.

But salt must be included.

Leaven spreads and changes what it touches.

Honey can ferment.

Salt preserves and seasons.

So salt becomes a sign of covenant faithfulness.

The phrase salt of the covenant teaches that Israel's worship is tied to loyal relationship with the LORD.

The offering is not just a religious object.

It belongs inside covenant life.

Then firstfruits are mentioned.

That means the first yield of provision can be brought to God.

Before Israel treats provision as personal possession, they learn to give thanks to the giver.

## Leviticus 3:1-5

**Scripture**

And if his oblation be a sacrifice of peace offering.

And he shall lay his hand upon the head of his offering, and kill it at the door of the tabernacle of the congregation.

And Aaron's sons the priests shall sprinkle the blood upon the altar round about.

And the priest shall burn it upon the altar.

**Narrator**

Leviticus 3 introduces the peace offering.

Peace here means more than the absence of conflict.

It points toward wholeness, well-being, fellowship, and life in right relationship before God.

The worshiper lays a hand on the animal's head, just as in the burnt offering.

The blood is sprinkled on the altar.

And the fat portions are burned to the LORD.

This offering teaches that sacrifice is not only about guilt being removed.

It is also about fellowship being enjoyed.

God provides a way for His people to have peace before Him.

## Leviticus 3:6-11

**Scripture**

And if his offering for a sacrifice of peace offering unto the LORD be of the flock.

And he shall lay his hand upon the head of his offering.

And the priest shall burn it upon the altar: it is the food of the offering made by fire unto the LORD.

**Narrator**

The peace offering can come from the flock.

Again, the worshiper lays a hand on the offering.

Again, the priest handles the blood and the altar.

And again, the rich fat portions are given to the LORD.

That detail matters.

The fat was considered a rich, valuable portion.

Leviticus teaches Israel not to treat the best casually.

The best belongs to the LORD.

This is not because God is needy.

It is because worship trains the heart.

Israel learns that fellowship with God includes honor, gratitude, and reverence.

## Leviticus 3:12-17

**Scripture**

And if his offering be a goat, then he shall offer it before the LORD.

And the priest shall burn them upon the altar.

It shall be a perpetual statute for your generations throughout all your dwellings, that ye eat neither fat nor blood.

**Narrator**

The chapter ends with a lasting instruction.

Israel must not eat fat or blood.

The fat belongs to the LORD.

The blood is tied to life and atonement.

This will become even clearer later in Leviticus.

For now, the point is that Israel must learn reverence even in what they eat.

Life with God reaches the table.

It reaches habits.

It reaches ordinary patterns.

The peace offering teaches fellowship, but fellowship is not careless.

The people live before a holy God.

## Leviticus 4:1-12

**Scripture**

And the LORD spake unto Moses, saying, If a soul shall sin through ignorance against any of the commandments of the LORD.

If the priest that is anointed do sin according to the sin of the people, then let him bring for his sin a young bullock without blemish unto the LORD for a sin offering.

And the priest shall dip his finger in the blood, and sprinkle of the blood seven times before the LORD, before the vail of the sanctuary.

**Narrator**

Leviticus 4 introduces the sin offering.

This chapter focuses especially on sin through ignorance, or unintentional sin.

That is important.

Modern people often think, I did not mean to, so it should not matter.

Leviticus teaches something deeper.

Intentions matter, but they do not erase guilt.

Sin still pollutes.

Sin still damages worship.

Sin still needs atonement.

The first case is the anointed priest.

That means spiritual leaders are not above atonement.

If the priest sins, his sin affects more than himself because he represents the people near holy things.

Blood is brought into holy space.

That shows how serious priestly sin is.

God provides mercy, but He does not pretend the sin is harmless.

## Leviticus 4:13-21

**Scripture**

And if the whole congregation of Israel sin through ignorance, and the thing be hid from the eyes of the assembly.

When the sin, which they have sinned against it, is known, then the congregation shall offer a young bullock for the sin.

And the elders of the congregation shall lay their hands upon the head of the bullock before the LORD.

And the priest shall make an atonement for them, and it shall be forgiven them.

**Narrator**

Now the whole congregation is addressed.

A community can become guilty together.

That is a serious thought.

Sin is not only private.

It can become shared, normalized, hidden, or carried by a whole people.

The elders lay hands on the bull because they represent the congregation.

The priest makes atonement.

And then comes the beautiful line:

It shall be forgiven them.

Leviticus is not only exposing guilt.

It is showing the mercy God provides.

Even corporate guilt can be brought before God through the way He commands.

## Leviticus 4:22-26

**Scripture**

When a ruler hath sinned, and done somewhat through ignorance against any of the commandments of the LORD his God.

Or if his sin, wherein he hath sinned, come to his knowledge.

He shall bring his offering, a kid of the goats, a male without blemish.

And the priest shall make an atonement for him as concerning his sin, and it shall be forgiven him.

**Narrator**

Next Leviticus speaks to a ruler.

Leadership does not place someone above God's holiness.

A ruler can sin.

A ruler can become guilty.

A ruler must respond when the sin becomes known.

The phrase come to his knowledge matters.

Awareness should lead to confession and atonement, not image management.

The leader brings the offering.

The priest makes atonement.

And the promise is repeated:

It shall be forgiven him.

God provides a path back for leaders too.

But the path is not denial.

It is atonement.

## Leviticus 4:27-35

**Scripture**

And if any one of the common people sin through ignorance.

Or if his sin, which he hath sinned, come to his knowledge.

Then he shall bring his offering.

And the priest shall make an atonement for his sin that he hath committed, and it shall be forgiven him.

**Narrator**

The final case is the ordinary person.

That matters because atonement is not only for priests, leaders, or the whole congregation.

Every person needs mercy.

Every ordinary life is lived before God's holiness.

The worshiper lays a hand on the offering.

The priest makes atonement.

And again the promise appears:

It shall be forgiven him.

That repeated line is one of the beautiful gifts inside Leviticus.

The book is serious because sin is serious.

But it is also merciful because God provides forgiveness.

Leviticus 1 through 4 teaches that access to God is not invented by people.

It is provided by the LORD.

## Closing Reflection

Day 32 teaches us how to begin reading Leviticus.

Do not read it like random ancient ceremony.

Read it as life near God's presence.

The holy God has come to dwell among His people.

And now He teaches them the way near.

The burnt offering teaches surrender and atonement.

The grain offering teaches gratitude and dedication.

The peace offering teaches fellowship and wholeness.

The sin offering teaches cleansing, confession, and forgiveness.

Priests need atonement.

Communities need atonement.

Leaders need atonement.

Ordinary people need atonement.

And God provides it.

So Leviticus begins with holiness, yes.

But it also begins with mercy.

The God who dwells among His people gives His people a way to come near.
