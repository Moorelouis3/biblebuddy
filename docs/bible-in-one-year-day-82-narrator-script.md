# Day 82 Narrator Script

## Opening

Welcome to Day 82 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are reading Second Samuel 24 and First Kings 1 through 3.

The title for today is David's Census and Solomon's Wisdom.

This day moves from David's final failure in Second Samuel to Solomon's early reign in First Kings.

David numbers the people and brings judgment on Israel.

The plague stops at the threshingfloor of Araunah.

David buys the place and offers sacrifice.

Then David grows old.

Adonijah tries to seize the throne.

Bathsheba and Nathan intervene.

Solomon is anointed king.

David gives Solomon final charges.

Solomon asks the LORD for wisdom.

The day shows transition, judgment, mercy, succession, and the need for a listening heart.

## Second Samuel 24

Second Samuel 24 tells of David numbering Israel and Judah.

David commands Joab to go through the tribes and count the people.

Joab questions the command.

Even Joab can see danger in David's desire.

But the king's word prevails.

The count is taken.

Afterward David's heart smites him.

He confesses that he has sinned greatly and asks the LORD to take away his iniquity.

The prophet Gad comes to David with three options: famine, fleeing before enemies, or pestilence.

David says he is in a great strait.

He chooses to fall into the hand of the LORD, because His mercies are great, rather than into the hand of man.

The LORD sends pestilence, and many die.

When the angel stretches his hand upon Jerusalem to destroy it, the LORD repents Him of the evil and says, It is enough.

The angel is by the threshingfloor of Araunah the Jebusite.

David sees the angel and says he has sinned, but asks what the sheep have done.

Gad tells David to rear an altar to the LORD.

Araunah offers the place, oxen, and instruments freely.

David refuses to offer to the LORD that which costs him nothing.

He buys the threshingfloor and oxen.

He offers burnt offerings and peace offerings.

The LORD is entreated for the land, and the plague is stayed.

This place will matter later for the temple.

Judgment and mercy meet at the place of sacrifice.

## First Kings 1

First Kings opens with David old and stricken in years.

The kingdom is moving toward transition.

Adonijah, David's son, exalts himself and says, I will be king.

He prepares chariots, horsemen, and fifty men to run before him.

The text notes that David had not displeased him at any time by asking why he had done so.

Adonijah's ambition grows in a space where correction has been weak.

He gathers Joab and Abiathar.

But Zadok, Benaiah, Nathan, Shimei, Rei, and David's mighty men are not with him.

Nathan goes to Bathsheba.

He tells her to go to David and remind him of his oath that Solomon would reign.

Bathsheba comes to the king.

Nathan confirms the danger.

David acts.

He swears that Solomon shall reign after him and sit upon his throne.

David commands Zadok, Nathan, and Benaiah to set Solomon on his own mule, bring him to Gihon, anoint him, blow the trumpet, and say, God save king Solomon.

Solomon is anointed.

The people rejoice with great joy, so that the earth rends with the sound.

Adonijah and his guests hear the noise.

Jonathan, Abiathar's son, reports that David has made Solomon king.

Adonijah's guests fear and rise up.

Adonijah catches hold of the horns of the altar.

Solomon shows restraint.

If Adonijah shows himself worthy, not a hair of him will fall.

But if wickedness is found in him, he will die.

Solomon sends him home.

The chapter shows Solomon's throne established by David's word, prophetic action, priestly anointing, and public joy.

## First Kings 2

First Kings 2 gives David's charge to Solomon.

David tells him to be strong and show himself a man.

He must keep the charge of the LORD, walk in His ways, keep His statutes, commandments, judgments, and testimonies written in the law of Moses.

The throne is not only political.

It is covenantal.

David also gives Solomon instructions concerning Joab, Barzillai's sons, and Shimei.

Then David dies and is buried in the city of David.

Solomon sits on the throne, and his kingdom is established greatly.

Adonijah then asks Bathsheba to request Abishag for him as wife.

Solomon sees the political danger.

Adonijah had already tried to take the throne.

This request is not innocent.

Solomon orders Adonijah's death.

Abiathar is removed from the priesthood, fulfilling the word concerning Eli's house.

Joab flees to the altar but is executed for the innocent blood he shed.

Shimei is confined to Jerusalem and warned not to cross the brook Kidron.

After three years, Shimei leaves to retrieve servants.

Solomon holds him accountable, and Shimei is put to death.

The chapter ends with the kingdom established in Solomon's hand.

This is a hard chapter, but it shows the throne being secured and unresolved bloodguilt being addressed.

## First Kings 3

First Kings 3 begins with Solomon making affinity with Pharaoh and loving the LORD.

The chapter also notes that the people sacrifice in high places because no house has yet been built for the name of the LORD.

Solomon goes to Gibeon to sacrifice.

The LORD appears to him in a dream and says, Ask what I shall give thee.

Solomon answers with humility.

He remembers God's mercy to David.

He says he is but a little child and does not know how to go out or come in.

He asks for an understanding heart to judge God's people, to discern between good and bad.

The speech pleases the LORD.

God gives Solomon a wise and understanding heart.

He also gives riches and honor.

If Solomon walks in God's ways, God will lengthen his days.

Then the chapter shows Solomon's wisdom in action.

Two women come with one living child and one dead child.

Each claims the living child.

Solomon calls for a sword and says to divide the child.

The true mother pleads for the child to live, even if given to the other woman.

Solomon gives the child to her.

All Israel hears and fears the king, because they see the wisdom of God is in him to do judgment.

## Full Lesson Review

Day 82 moves from David's last public failure to Solomon's first public wisdom.

David's census brings judgment, but the plague stops at the place of sacrifice.

Adonijah tries to seize the throne, but Solomon is anointed.

David charges Solomon to walk in the LORD's ways.

Solomon asks for wisdom, not selfish greatness.

The LORD gives wisdom, riches, and honor.

The kingdom begins with promise, but also with warning.

Solomon must keep walking with the LORD.

## Key Connections

First, David's census shows the danger of pride and misplaced security.

Second, Araunah's threshingfloor shows costly worship and mercy after judgment.

Third, Adonijah shows ambition trying to crown itself.

Fourth, Solomon's prayer shows the kind of heart a king needs: a listening heart.

## Slow Walk Through the Day

Begin with David's census.

The command to number the people looks simple on the surface, but the chapter shows that something is wrong in David's heart.

Joab questions the plan.

That matters because Joab is not usually the moral center of the story.

When even Joab senses danger, the reader should pause.

David seems to be measuring the kingdom as if numbers can give him safety.

He wants to know the strength of Israel and Judah.

The problem is not arithmetic.

The problem is trust.

The king who sang that the LORD is his rock now acts as if his confidence may rest in the size of his fighting force.

After the count, David's heart smites him.

That phrase is important.

David is not confronted first by a public crowd.

His own heart is struck with conviction.

He confesses that he has sinned greatly.

David's greatness is never that he is sinless.

His greatness is that when the LORD exposes him, he does not keep defending the sin forever.

Gad gives David three judgments.

David chooses to fall into the hand of the LORD because His mercies are great.

This is not a small line.

David knows judgment is coming, yet he still knows God's mercy is better than man's cruelty.

The plague is terrible.

The death is real.

Then the angel reaches Jerusalem, and the LORD says, It is enough.

Judgment stops by mercy.

Araunah's threshingfloor becomes the place where sacrifice is offered and the plague is stayed.

David refuses to worship with what costs him nothing.

That line should shape the listener.

Worship is not a leftover handed to God after convenience has been protected.

David buys the place.

He offers burnt offerings and peace offerings.

The land is entreated.

This chapter closes Second Samuel with sin, judgment, sacrifice, and mercy all meeting in one place.

Now move into First Kings 1.

David is old.

The kingdom is vulnerable.

Adonijah says, I will be king.

That sentence sounds like self-crowning.

He gathers the symbols of rule before he receives the right to rule.

Chariots, horsemen, runners, guests, and a public feast all create momentum.

But the text says David had not displeased him at any time by asking why he acted this way.

Weak correction has consequences.

Adonijah's ambition has grown without challenge.

Nathan and Bathsheba move wisely.

They do not start a private rebellion.

They appeal to David's oath.

The throne must be settled according to promise, not according to Adonijah's appetite.

Solomon is set on David's mule.

He is anointed by Zadok.

Nathan is present.

The trumpet sounds.

The people rejoice.

The earth rends with the sound.

This is public confirmation.

Adonijah's feast collapses because the true king has been announced.

Solomon shows measured mercy at first.

Adonijah is sent home under warning.

The kingdom begins with restraint, but the danger is not gone.

Now listen to First Kings 2.

David charges Solomon to keep the way of the LORD.

This is the foundation of kingship.

Before Solomon deals with enemies, offices, or court politics, he must walk in God's ways.

The law of Moses stands above the throne.

The king is not free to invent righteousness.

He must keep statutes, commandments, judgments, and testimonies.

Then David speaks about Joab, Barzillai's sons, and Shimei.

This chapter can feel severe, but it is about the throne being established in a world where bloodguilt and rebellion are still alive.

Joab's violence has never been fully answered.

Abiathar is removed, fulfilling the word concerning Eli's house.

Adonijah's request for Abishag is recognized as another reach for royal claim.

Solomon sees the political meaning beneath the polite request.

Shimei is given boundaries and later violates them.

The repeated line is that the kingdom is established.

Solomon's throne is not established by sentiment.

It is established through covenant charge, judgment, discipline, and the removal of threats.

Now listen to First Kings 3.

Solomon loves the LORD, but the chapter also gives early tension.

He makes affinity with Pharaoh.

The people sacrifice in high places because the temple is not yet built.

The story is promising, but not simple.

At Gibeon, the LORD asks Solomon what He shall give him.

Solomon remembers mercy to David.

He calls himself a little child.

He admits that he does not know how to go out or come in.

That humility is the doorway to wisdom.

Solomon does not ask first for long life, riches, or the life of his enemies.

He asks for an understanding heart to judge God's people.

The phrase understanding heart means a listening heart.

Solomon wants the inner ability to hear, discern, and judge rightly.

God is pleased with that request.

The famous case with the two women then shows wisdom in action.

Solomon's judgment uncovers the true mother by revealing love.

The true mother would rather lose her claim than see the child die.

Wisdom does more than know facts.

Wisdom discerns hearts.

All Israel hears and fears because the wisdom of God is in the king to do judgment.

## Guided Listening Reflection

Day 82 is a transition day, but it is also a heart day.

David's heart is struck after the census.

Adonijah's heart is lifted up by ambition.

Solomon asks for a listening heart.

Watch how the heart shapes the kingdom.

When David seeks security in numbers, the people suffer.

When Adonijah seeks the throne for himself, the kingdom trembles.

When Solomon asks for wisdom, the people receive justice.

The listener should also notice that mercy is not casual.

The plague stops, but sacrifice is offered.

Adonijah is spared, but only under a real warning.

Solomon receives wisdom, but he is still called to walk in God's ways.

Mercy does not mean nothing matters.

Mercy brings people back under the LORD's rule.

The threshingfloor is especially important.

It is a place of judgment stopped and worship offered.

It points forward because this place will connect to the temple story.

The Bible is already preparing the listener to see that sacrifice, mercy, and worship belong together.

By the end of the day, David has died and Solomon has begun.

One generation passes.

Another generation receives the charge.

The question remains the same.

Will the king listen to the LORD?

Will the king govern God's people with wisdom?

Will power be submitted to covenant?

Day 82 teaches that the throne needs more than inheritance.

It needs humility, obedience, justice, and a heart that asks God for what it cannot produce by itself.

## Final Guided Recap

Before the closing, trace the movement again.

Second Samuel 24 begins with a king counting strength and ends with a king buying a place of sacrifice.

That movement is the lesson.

David begins by looking at human numbers.

He ends by falling before divine mercy.

The census exposes a heart looking for security in what can be measured.

The altar shows worship that costs something.

The plague is not treated lightly, and the mercy is not cheap.

David's confession matters because he does not call his sin a mistake only.

He says he has sinned greatly.

He knows the people have suffered under his failure.

Then First Kings 1 shows a throne being contested.

Adonijah wants royal status without waiting for the appointed word.

His feast looks powerful until the real anointing happens at Gihon.

The sound of the people's joy exposes the weakness of self-exaltation.

Solomon's first response to Adonijah is restrained, which shows that strength does not always need to strike immediately.

First Kings 2 shows that transition is not sentimental.

David's charge to Solomon is first covenantal.

Solomon must walk in the LORD's ways.

Only after that does David speak about unresolved dangers in the kingdom.

The chapter is severe because the kingdom is being established in a world where old violence and fresh ambition are still active.

First Kings 3 shows the new king at his best.

Solomon knows he is small before the task.

He asks for an understanding heart.

That request is beautiful because it puts God's people at the center.

Solomon wants wisdom so he can judge rightly.

The case of the two women then proves that God has answered.

Wisdom protects life.

Wisdom discerns love.

Wisdom brings fear and honor to the throne.

So Day 82 moves from David's costly altar to Solomon's listening heart.

The throne is preserved by mercy, established through charge, and blessed through wisdom from the LORD.

## Prayerful Landing

Let the end of this day press one question into the heart.

Where do I look for security?

David looked at numbers and had to be brought back to mercy.

Adonijah looked at symbols of power and was exposed by the true anointing.

Solomon looked at the size of the task and asked God for wisdom.

That is the path the listener should remember.

The safest person in these chapters is not the strongest, loudest, oldest, or most ambitious person.

The safest person is the one who knows he needs the LORD.

David needs mercy.

Solomon needs wisdom.

The people need justice.

The kingdom needs covenant faithfulness.

Day 82 teaches us not to despise that need.

It is better to ask God for what we lack than to pretend we can carry holy responsibility in our own strength.

## Closing

As you finish Day 82, remember Solomon's request.

He asks for wisdom to govern God's people.

The throne needs more than power.

It needs a heart that listens to the LORD.
