# Bible In One Year - Day 10 Exact WEB Narrator Script

## Day 10 - Covenant Through Isaac

**Reading:** Genesis 25, 26, and 27  
**Bible text:** World English Bible, Genesis 25-27  
**Goal:** Read the full Scripture in order, pause after natural sections, explain what is happening in clear modern language, and help the listener follow the story while driving, walking, working, or listening in the background.

## Estimated Length

**Estimated script word count:** about 5,800-6,300 words  
**Estimated spoken time:** about 30-38 minutes at a calm narrator pace  
**Estimated audio runtime with light pauses/music bed:** about 34-42 minutes

This follows the Day 1 style: Scripture first, then a short explanation. The narrator should sound like a guide sitting beside the listener, not like a preacher giving a sermon.

## Production Direction

Treat this as an audio Bible drama and story companion, but do not say that style out loud in the script. Start by carrying the emotional weight of the previous day into the new day, like the next scene in one connected story. Read the WEB Scripture with reverence, then explain what is happening in plain modern language that a sixth grader can follow without talking down to them.

The narrator should sound warm, grounded, cinematic, and close to the listener. Keep the explanations conversational, not preachy. Give useful nuggets: words that need explaining, cultural context, family pressure, repeated patterns, and how the scene connects to real life. Let tension, grief, fear, favoritism, deception, and hope feel alive, but do not overact.

## Opening

Yesterday, we watched Abraham's story move through joy, grief, testing, and legacy.

Isaac was born after years of waiting. Sarah laughed with joy, and the promise finally had a child you could hold. But then Sarah died, and Abraham became a widower. He bought a burial place in the land God had promised him, which means even in grief, he was still holding on to God's word.

Then Abraham faced one of the hardest tests in the whole Bible. God told him to take Isaac, the son of promise, up Mount Moriah. Abraham obeyed, Isaac was spared, and God provided the ram. The promise survived the test.

And by the end of Day 9, Abraham knew something important: Isaac could not carry the promise alone.

So Abraham sent his servant back to his family line to find a wife for Isaac. The servant prayed. Rebekah appeared at the well. She showed kindness, courage, and willingness. And when the question came, she said yes.

Day 9 ended with Rebekah entering Isaac's life. Isaac received her, loved her, and was comforted after his mother's death.

But that leaves a new question hanging over the story.

Abraham is old. Sarah is gone. Isaac has a wife. The promise is ready to move into the next generation.

But will this family trust God with it?

Will Isaac carry the promise well?

Will Rebekah's children walk in faith?

Or will the same old patterns of fear, favoritism, and control start shaping the future?

Welcome to Day 10 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are reading Genesis 25, 26, and 27.

Genesis 25 closes Abraham's life and introduces the twins. Genesis 26 shows Isaac trying to live under God's promise in famine, fear, conflict, and pressure. Genesis 27 takes us inside a family breaking apart around favoritism, deception, and blessing.

Abraham dies. Isaac becomes the next covenant heir. Rebekah has twins. Jacob and Esau are already struggling before they are even born. Isaac faces famine. Isaac repeats some of Abraham's fear. Wells get fought over. Favoritism grows inside the family. And by the end of Day 10, Jacob receives the blessing through deception, Esau is crushed, and the family is fractured.

This is not a perfect family story.

That matters.

The Bible is not pretending that God's plan moves forward through clean, polished people who always make wise choices. God's promise moves forward through real families, real fear, real jealousy, real shortcuts, and real consequences.

As we read today, listen for three big ideas.

First: God's promise continues after Abraham.

Second: what parents pass down is not only faith, but also patterns.

Third: God can keep His promise, but that does not mean our bad choices stop hurting people.

So let's step into the next scene of the story.

Genesis chapter 25, verses 1 through 6.

## Genesis 25:1-6

**Scripture**

1. Abraham took another wife, and her name was Keturah.

2. She bore him Zimran, Jokshan, Medan, Midian, Ishbak, and Shuah.

3. Jokshan became the father of Sheba, and Dedan. The sons of Dedan were Asshurim, Letushim, and Leummim.

4. The sons of Midian were Ephah, Epher, Hanoch, Abida, and Eldaah. All these were the children of Keturah.

5. Abraham gave all that he had to Isaac,

6. but Abraham gave gifts to the sons of Abraham's concubines. While he still lived, he sent them away from Isaac his son, eastward, to the east country.

**Narrator**

Genesis starts Day 10 by showing us that Abraham's family is bigger than Isaac. Abraham has other children through Keturah, and those children become family lines of their own.

But verse 5 is the key: Abraham gave all that he had to Isaac.

That does not mean the other sons had no value. Abraham gives them gifts. But the covenant line, the promise God made back in Genesis 12, continues through Isaac.

So the Bible is narrowing the camera. Many descendants come from Abraham, but the covenant story is going through Isaac.

## Genesis 25:7-11

**Scripture**

7. These are the days of the years of Abraham's life which he lived: one hundred seventy-five years.

8. Abraham gave up his spirit, and died at a good old age, an old man, and full of years, and was gathered to his people.

9. Isaac and Ishmael, his sons, buried him in the cave of Machpelah, in the field of Ephron, the son of Zohar the Hittite, which is near Mamre,

10. the field which Abraham purchased from the children of Heth. Abraham was buried there with Sarah, his wife.

11. After the death of Abraham, God blessed Isaac, his son. Isaac lived by Beer Lahai Roi.

**Narrator**

Abraham dies at one hundred seventy-five years old. The phrase "full of years" means his life was complete. Not perfect, but full.

And notice something quiet but powerful: Isaac and Ishmael bury him together.

These two sons had a complicated story. Ishmael had been sent away. Isaac carried the covenant promise. But at Abraham's burial, they stand together.

Then verse 11 tells us the main handoff: after Abraham's death, God blessed Isaac.

Abraham is gone, but God is not gone. The promise is not buried with Abraham. It continues.

## Genesis 25:12-18

**Scripture**

12. Now this is the history of the generations of Ishmael, Abraham's son, whom Hagar the Egyptian, Sarah's servant, bore to Abraham.

13. These are the names of the sons of Ishmael, by their names, according to the order of their birth: the firstborn of Ishmael, Nebaioth, then Kedar, Adbeel, Mibsam,

14. Mishma, Dumah, Massa,

15. Hadad, Tema, Jetur, Naphish, and Kedemah.

16. These are the sons of Ishmael, and these are their names, by their villages, and by their encampments: twelve princes, according to their nations.

17. These are the years of the life of Ishmael: one hundred thirty-seven years. He gave up his spirit and died, and was gathered to his people.

18. They lived from Havilah to Shur that is before Egypt, as you go toward Assyria. He lived opposite all his relatives.

**Narrator**

Before Genesis moves fully into Isaac, it pauses to honor Ishmael's line.

This matters because God had promised Hagar and Abraham that Ishmael would also become a great people. And here we see it: twelve princes come from him.

So even though Ishmael is not the covenant line, he is not forgotten by God.

That is an important Bible nugget: not being the main character in one part of the story does not mean you are invisible to God. Ishmael's life still matters. His descendants still matter. God keeps His word to him too.

## Genesis 25:19-23

**Scripture**

19. This is the history of the generations of Isaac, Abraham's son. Abraham became the father of Isaac.

20. Isaac was forty years old when he took Rebekah, the daughter of Bethuel the Syrian of Paddan Aram, the sister of Laban the Syrian, to be his wife.

21. Isaac entreated Yahweh for his wife, because she was barren. Yahweh was entreated by him, and Rebekah his wife conceived.

22. The children struggled together within her. She said, "If it is like this, why do I live?" She went to inquire of Yahweh.

23. Yahweh said to her,

"Two nations are in your womb.

Two peoples will be separated from your body.

The one people will be stronger than the other people.

The elder will serve the younger."

**Narrator**

Now Isaac's story begins with a familiar pain: barrenness.

Sarah had waited for a child. Now Rebekah is waiting too. But Isaac prays, and God answers.

Then the pregnancy itself becomes intense. The twins are struggling inside Rebekah, and she asks, basically, "Why is this happening to me?"

God tells her that this is bigger than two babies. Two nations are in her womb.

And then comes the reversal: the elder will serve the younger.

In that culture, the older son usually had priority. But God is already showing that His plan will not simply follow human expectations.

## Genesis 25:24-28

**Scripture**

24. When her days to be delivered were fulfilled, behold, there were twins in her womb.

25. The first came out red all over, like a hairy garment. They named him Esau.

26. After that, his brother came out, and his hand had hold on Esau's heel. He was named Jacob. Isaac was sixty years old when she bore them.

27. The boys grew. Esau was a skillful hunter, a man of the field. Jacob was a quiet man, living in tents.

28. Now Isaac loved Esau, because he ate his venison. Rebekah loved Jacob.

**Narrator**

The twins are born, and their personalities are different from the start.

Esau is outdoors, hunting, strong, rough, a man of the field.

Jacob is quieter, closer to the tents, closer to home.

But verse 28 is where the family problem gets loud: Isaac loved Esau because he ate his venison. Rebekah loved Jacob.

That is favoritism.

And favoritism is not small. It teaches children to compete for love. It turns a home into teams. It makes people feel chosen or overlooked.

This family is carrying God's promise, but it is also carrying a wound.

## Genesis 25:29-34

**Scripture**

29. Jacob boiled stew. Esau came in from the field, and he was famished.

30. Esau said to Jacob, "Please feed me with some of that red stew, for I am famished." Therefore his name was called Edom.

31. Jacob said, "First, sell me your birthright."

32. Esau said, "Behold, I am about to die. What good is the birthright to me?"

33. Jacob said, "Swear to me first."

He swore to him. He sold his birthright to Jacob.

34. Jacob gave Esau bread and lentil stew. He ate and drank, rose up, and went his way. So Esau despised his birthright.

**Narrator**

The birthright was not just a family trophy. It was inheritance, responsibility, leadership, and in this family, connection to the promise.

Esau is hungry, and he treats the birthright like it is worth less than one meal.

Jacob is not innocent either. He sees his brother's weakness and uses the moment.

So both brothers are being exposed.

Esau lives for the immediate. Jacob schemes for advantage.

That is a real-life warning. Some people throw away long-term purpose for short-term appetite. Other people chase the right thing in the wrong way.

Now Genesis 26 turns to Isaac.

## Genesis 26:1-5

**Scripture**

1. There was a famine in the land, in addition to the first famine that was in the days of Abraham. Isaac went to Abimelech king of the Philistines, to Gerar.

2. Yahweh appeared to him, and said, "Don't go down into Egypt. Live in the land I will tell you about.

3. Live in this land, and I will be with you, and will bless you. For I will give to you, and to your offspring, all these lands, and I will establish the oath which I swore to Abraham your father.

4. I will multiply your offspring as the stars of the sky, and will give all these lands to your offspring. In your offspring all the nations of the earth will be blessed,

5. because Abraham obeyed my voice, and kept my requirements, my commandments, my statutes, and my laws."

**Narrator**

Isaac faces famine, just like Abraham did.

And this is a big test. When life gets hard, will Isaac run, or will he trust God in the land of promise?

God tells him not to go down into Egypt. Stay in the land. I will be with you. I will bless you.

That phrase, "I will be with you," is huge.

God is not only giving Isaac land and descendants. He is giving Isaac His presence.

Sometimes obedience is not dramatic. Sometimes obedience is staying where God told you to stay when fear is telling you to escape.

## Genesis 26:6-11

**Scripture**

6. Isaac lived in Gerar.

7. The men of the place asked him about his wife. He said, "She is my sister," for he was afraid to say, "My wife", lest, he thought, "the men of the place might kill me for Rebekah, because she is beautiful to look at."

8. When he had been there a long time, Abimelech king of the Philistines looked out at a window, and saw, and, behold, Isaac was caressing Rebekah, his wife.

9. Abimelech called Isaac, and said, "Behold, surely she is your wife. Why did you say, 'She is my sister'?"

Isaac said to him, "Because I said, 'Lest I die because of her.'"

10. Abimelech said, "What is this you have done to us? One of the people might easily have lain with your wife, and you would have brought guilt on us!"

11. Abimelech commanded all the people, saying, "He who touches this man or his wife will surely be put to death."

**Narrator**

This scene should sound familiar.

Abraham also said Sarah was his sister out of fear. Now Isaac repeats the same kind of lie with Rebekah.

This is what people mean when they say patterns can pass through a family.

Isaac received God's promise, but he also learned fear.

And Abimelech, a foreign king, has to confront him.

The Bible is honest here. Being chosen by God does not mean Isaac is automatically mature in every area. God is faithful, but Isaac still has to grow.

## Genesis 26:12-16

**Scripture**

12. Isaac sowed in that land, and reaped in the same year one hundred times what he planted. Yahweh blessed him.

13. The man grew great, and grew more and more until he became very great.

14. He had possessions of flocks, possessions of herds, and a great household. The Philistines envied him.

15. Now all the wells which his father's servants had dug in the days of Abraham his father, the Philistines had stopped, and filled with earth.

16. Abimelech said to Isaac, "Go away from us, for you are much mightier than we."

**Narrator**

God blesses Isaac even in a famine.

But blessing does not mean everyone celebrates you.

The Philistines envy him. They stop up the wells Abraham's servants had dug. In that region, wells were survival. Water meant life, animals, crops, and stability.

So this is not petty. This is pressure.

Isaac is blessed, but he is also pushed out.

Sometimes God's blessing on your life does not remove conflict. It gives you the strength to keep moving through it.

## Genesis 26:17-22

**Scripture**

17. Isaac departed from there, encamped in the valley of Gerar, and lived there.

18. Isaac dug again the wells of water, which they had dug in the days of Abraham his father, for the Philistines had stopped them after the death of Abraham. He called their names after the names by which his father had called them.

19. Isaac's servants dug in the valley, and found there a well of flowing water.

20. The herdsmen of Gerar argued with Isaac's herdsmen, saying, "The water is ours." So he called the name of the well Esek, because they contended with him.

21. They dug another well, and they argued over that, also. So he called its name Sitnah.

22. He left that place, and dug another well. They didn't argue over that one. So he called it Rehoboth. He said, "For now Yahweh has made room for us, and we will be fruitful in the land."

**Narrator**

This section is one of the most practical parts of Isaac's life.

He keeps digging.

First there is Esek, which means contention. Then Sitnah, which points to hostility. Then Rehoboth, broad places, room.

Isaac does not win every argument. He does not fight over every well. He keeps moving, keeps digging, keeps trusting, until God makes room.

That is a life nugget.

Not every fight is your assignment. Sometimes maturity is knowing when to move, keep working, and let God make room without you forcing it.

## Genesis 26:23-25

**Scripture**

23. He went up from there to Beersheba.

24. Yahweh appeared to him the same night, and said, "I am the God of Abraham your father. Don't be afraid, for I am with you, and will bless you, and multiply your offspring for my servant Abraham's sake."

25. He built an altar there, and called on Yahweh's name, and pitched his tent there. There Isaac's servants dug a well.

**Narrator**

God speaks directly into Isaac's fear: Don't be afraid, for I am with you.

That tells us Isaac's outside life was not the whole story. He had success, wells, flocks, and servants, but he still needed reassurance.

And Isaac responds with worship.

He builds an altar. He pitches his tent. His servants dig a well.

That is a beautiful order: worship, home, water.

Isaac is learning to build his life around God's presence, not just around survival.

## Genesis 26:26-33

**Scripture**

26. Then Abimelech went to him from Gerar with Ahuzzath his friend, and Phicol the captain of his army.

27. Isaac said to them, "Why have you come to me, since you hate me, and have sent me away from you?"

28. They said, "We saw plainly that Yahweh was with you. We said, 'Let there now be an oath between us, even between us and you, and let's make a covenant with you,

29. that you will do us no harm, as we have not touched you, and as we have done to you nothing but good, and have sent you away in peace.' You are now the blessed of Yahweh."

30. He made them a feast, and they ate and drank.

31. They rose up some time in the morning, and swore an oath to one another. Isaac sent them away, and they departed from him in peace.

32. The same day, Isaac's servants came, and told him concerning the well which they had dug, and said to him, "We have found water."

33. He called it "Shibah". Therefore the name of the city is "Beersheba" to this day.

**Narrator**

The people who pushed Isaac away now come back because they can see God is with him.

That is important. Isaac did not have to announce it. His life showed it.

They make peace, share a meal, and swear an oath.

Then, on the same day, Isaac's servants find water.

The story is showing us peace and provision arriving together.

Isaac has been digging through conflict, and now the Lord gives him room, relationship peace, and water.

## Genesis 26:34-35

**Scripture**

34. When Esau was forty years old, he took as wife Judith, the daughter of Beeri the Hittite, and Basemath, the daughter of Elon the Hittite.

35. They grieved Isaac's and Rebekah's spirits.

**Narrator**

These two verses set up Genesis 27.

Esau marries Hittite women, and it grieves Isaac and Rebekah.

Why does that matter?

Marriage in this story is not only romance. It affects family direction, worship, covenant identity, and the future of the promise.

Genesis is showing us that Esau is still not valuing the covenant the way he should.

Now the family tension reaches a breaking point.

## Genesis 27:1-4

**Scripture**

1. When Isaac was old, and his eyes were dim, so that he could not see, he called Esau his elder son, and said to him, "My son?"

He said to him, "Here I am."

2. He said, "See now, I am old. I don't know the day of my death.

3. Now therefore, please take your weapons, your quiver and your bow, and go out to the field, and get me venison.

4. Make me savory food, such as I love, and bring it to me, that I may eat, and that my soul may bless you before I die."

**Narrator**

Genesis 27 opens with Isaac old and nearly blind.

He wants to bless Esau.

But remember what God told Rebekah before the boys were born: the elder will serve the younger.

So Isaac's desire is clashing with God's word. He loves Esau, and he wants Esau to have the blessing.

Food is also part of the scene. Isaac wants the meal he loves before giving the blessing.

The family is about to make a spiritual decision in a very emotional, very messy way.

## Genesis 27:5-10

**Scripture**

5. Rebekah heard when Isaac spoke to Esau his son. Esau went to the field to hunt for venison, and to bring it.

6. Rebekah spoke to Jacob her son, saying, "Behold, I heard your father speak to Esau your brother, saying,

7. 'Bring me venison, and make me savory food, that I may eat, and bless you before Yahweh before my death.'

8. Now therefore, my son, obey my voice according to that which I command you.

9. Go now to the flock and get me two good young goats from there. I will make them savory food for your father, such as he loves.

10. You shall bring it to your father, that he may eat, so that he may bless you before his death."

**Narrator**

Rebekah hears the plan and moves fast.

She knows what God said about Jacob. But instead of trusting God to handle it, she tries to secure the promise through deception.

That is one of the main tensions in this chapter.

Rebekah may be aiming at the right outcome, but she chooses the wrong method.

This is real life. Sometimes people try to help God by manipulating people. But when we use deception to get something God promised, we still create damage.

## Genesis 27:11-17

**Scripture**

11. Jacob said to Rebekah his mother, "Behold, Esau my brother is a hairy man, and I am a smooth man.

12. What if my father touches me? I will seem to him as a deceiver, and I would bring a curse on myself, and not a blessing."

13. His mother said to him, "Let your curse be on me, my son. Only obey my voice, and go get them for me."

14. He went, and got them, and brought them to his mother. His mother made savory food, such as his father loved.

15. Rebekah took the good clothes of Esau, her elder son, which were with her in the house, and put them on Jacob, her younger son.

16. She put the skins of the young goats on his hands, and on the smooth of his neck.

17. She gave the savory food and the bread, which she had prepared, into the hand of her son Jacob.

**Narrator**

Jacob is worried, but notice what he is worried about.

He is not saying, "This is wrong." He is saying, "What if I get caught?"

That is a big difference.

Rebekah builds the disguise: Esau's clothes, goat skins, food Isaac loves.

This is not a small misunderstanding. This is a full plan.

And the sad thing is, the family knows each other's weaknesses. Isaac loves the taste of Esau's food. Esau is away hunting. Jacob can be coached. Rebekah knows how to work the moment.

Sin often uses family knowledge as a weapon.

## Genesis 27:18-25

**Scripture**

18. He came to his father, and said, "My father?"

He said, "Here I am. Who are you, my son?"

19. Jacob said to his father, "I am Esau your firstborn. I have done what you asked me to do. Please arise, sit and eat of my venison, that your soul may bless me."

20. Isaac said to his son, "How is it that you have found it so quickly, my son?"

He said, "Because Yahweh your God gave me success."

21. Isaac said to Jacob, "Please come near, that I may feel you, my son, whether you are really my son Esau or not."

22. Jacob went near to Isaac his father. He felt him, and said, "The voice is Jacob's voice, but the hands are the hands of Esau."

23. He didn't recognize him, because his hands were hairy, like his brother Esau's hands. So he blessed him.

24. He said, "Are you really my son Esau?"

He said, "I am."

25. He said, "Bring it near to me, and I will eat of my son's venison, that my soul may bless you."

He brought it near to him, and he ate. He brought him wine, and he drank.

**Narrator**

This section is painful because Jacob keeps lying.

He lies about his identity. He lies about the food. He even brings God's name into the lie when he says Yahweh gave him success.

Isaac is suspicious. The voice sounds like Jacob, but the hands feel like Esau.

This is the danger of deception: it starts needing more deception to survive.

One lie becomes another lie, then another, until the whole room is covered in it.

Jacob gets close to the blessing, but he is walking there in disguise.

## Genesis 27:26-29

**Scripture**

26. His father Isaac said to him, "Come near now, and kiss me, my son."

27. He came near, and kissed him. He smelled the smell of his clothing, and blessed him, and said,

"Behold, the smell of my son

is as the smell of a field which Yahweh has blessed.

28. God give you of the dew of the sky,

of the fatness of the earth,

and plenty of grain and new wine.

29. Let peoples serve you,

and nations bow down to you.

Be lord over your brothers.

Let your mother's sons bow down to you.

Cursed be everyone who curses you.

Blessed be everyone who blesses you."

**Narrator**

The blessing lands on Jacob.

It includes land, fruitfulness, grain, wine, authority, nations, and the language of curse and blessing.

That last line echoes God's promise to Abraham: blessed are those who bless you, cursed are those who curse you.

So this is not just a dad saying nice words. This is covenant language.

But the way it happens is deeply broken.

God's purpose is moving forward, but the family is not okay.

That is one of the hardest truths in Genesis: God can keep His promise through human mess, but the mess still hurts.

## Genesis 27:30-33

**Scripture**

30. As soon as Isaac had finished blessing Jacob, and Jacob had just gone out from the presence of Isaac his father, Esau his brother came in from his hunting.

31. He also made savory food, and brought it to his father. He said to his father, "Let my father arise, and eat of his son's venison, that your soul may bless me."

32. Isaac his father said to him, "Who are you?"

He said, "I am your son, your firstborn, Esau."

33. Isaac trembled violently, and said, "Who, then, is he who has taken venison, and brought it to me, and I have eaten of all before you came, and have blessed him? Yes, he will be blessed."

**Narrator**

Esau arrives right after Jacob leaves.

The timing is brutal.

Isaac realizes what happened, and he trembles violently. That is not a small reaction. He understands that he has blessed Jacob, and he cannot undo it.

And then Isaac says, "Yes, he will be blessed."

That line is important. Isaac seems to recognize that the blessing has landed where God had said it would land, even though the path was ugly.

The promise stands. But the family is breaking.

## Genesis 27:34-40

**Scripture**

34. When Esau heard the words of his father, he cried with an exceedingly great and bitter cry, and said to his father, "Bless me, even me also, my father."

35. He said, "Your brother came with deceit, and has taken away your blessing."

36. He said, "Isn't he rightly named Jacob? For he has supplanted me these two times. He took away my birthright. See, now he has taken away my blessing." He said, "Haven't you reserved a blessing for me?"

37. Isaac answered Esau, "Behold, I have made him your lord, and all his brothers I have given to him for servants. I have sustained him with grain and new wine. What then will I do for you, my son?"

38. Esau said to his father, "Do you have just one blessing, my father? Bless me, even me also, my father." Esau lifted up his voice, and wept.

39. Isaac his father answered him,

"Behold, your dwelling will be of the fatness of the earth,

and of the dew of the sky from above.

40. You will live by your sword, and you will serve your brother.

It will happen, when you will break loose,

that you will shake his yoke from off your neck."

**Narrator**

Esau's cry is one of the saddest moments so far.

And we need to hold two things together.

Esau despised his birthright earlier. He made real choices.

But Jacob also deceived him. Rebekah helped. Isaac played favorites. This pain is shared across the whole family.

Esau receives a word too, but it is not the covenant blessing Jacob received.

The consequences are now out in the open.

This is why Genesis does not treat deception like a clever shortcut. It may get Jacob the blessing, but it also tears the family apart.

## Genesis 27:41-46

**Scripture**

41. Esau hated Jacob because of the blessing with which his father blessed him. Esau said in his heart, "The days of mourning for my father are at hand. Then I will kill my brother Jacob."

42. The words of Esau, her elder son, were told to Rebekah. She sent and called Jacob, her younger son, and said to him, "Behold, your brother Esau comforts himself about you by planning to kill you.

43. Now therefore, my son, obey my voice. Arise, flee to Laban, my brother, in Haran.

44. Stay with him a few days, until your brother's fury turns away--

45. until your brother's anger turns away from you, and he forgets what you have done to him. Then I will send, and get you from there. Why should I be bereaved of you both in one day?"

46. Rebekah said to Isaac, "I am weary of my life because of the daughters of Heth. If Jacob takes a wife of the daughters of Heth, such as these, of the daughters of the land, what good will my life do me?"

**Narrator**

The chapter ends with hatred, fear, and separation.

Esau wants to kill Jacob. Rebekah sends Jacob away. She thinks it will be for a few days, but the story will stretch much longer than that.

This is the cost of the deception.

Jacob receives the blessing, but he loses home.

Rebekah secures the outcome she wanted, but she loses the nearness of her son.

Esau loses the blessing and turns bitter.

Isaac loses control of the family moment.

Everybody is touched by the damage.

That is the real-life lesson: sin rarely stays private. It spreads through relationships.

## Closing

So that is Day 10.

Genesis 25 begins by closing Abraham's life and moving the promise to Isaac.

Genesis 26 shows Isaac trying to live under that promise in the middle of famine, fear, conflict, and pressure.

Genesis 27 shows the family fracture around the blessing.

And honestly, this day is messy.

But it is also deeply helpful, because it teaches us how the Bible wants us to read real people.

Isaac is blessed by God, but he is still afraid.

Rebekah knows what God said, but she still manipulates.

Jacob is chosen, but he still deceives.

Esau is wrong to despise the birthright, but his pain is still real.

The Bible does not flatten people into cartoons. It tells the truth.

And through all of this, God's promise keeps moving.

That does not excuse the deception. It does not make the family wounds small. It means God's faithfulness is bigger than human failure.

If you carry one thing from Day 10, carry this:

God can keep His promise through imperfect people, but shortcuts still have consequences.

So do not confuse God's faithfulness with permission to manipulate.

Do not trade long-term purpose for short-term appetite.

Do not let favoritism, fear, or family patterns quietly shape the future.

And when you find yourself in conflict, remember Isaac at the wells.

Keep digging.

Keep trusting.

Not every well will be peaceful. Not every room will welcome you. But God knows how to make room for you without you becoming someone you are not.

Tomorrow, Day 11, we follow Jacob as he leaves home. He is carrying a blessing, but he is also carrying consequences. And on the road, in the middle of fear and uncertainty, God will meet him.

## Reusable Day Script Framework For Future Days

Use this framework for every Bible in One Year narrator script.

### 1. Header

- Title: `Bible In One Year - Day X Exact WEB Narrator Script`
- Day title from the master plan
- Reading range
- Bible text source
- Goal
- Estimated word count
- Estimated spoken time
- Estimated audio runtime with pauses/music

### 2. Opening

Length target: 2-3 minutes.

Opening should:

- start with the cliffhanger or biggest unresolved moment from the previous day
- connect the previous day to today's reading with a dramatic story handoff
- delay the formal welcome until the story tension is already clear
- include a clear line like "Welcome to Day X of Bible in One Year"
- name today's chapters and the main characters/conflict
- give 2-3 listening themes
- tell the listener what to watch for
- move directly into the first Scripture section by saying the chapter and verse range

### 3. Scripture Sections

Use natural sections, usually 3-7 verses at a time.

For each section:

```text
## Book Chapter:Start-End

**Scripture**

[Exact WEB text, verse-numbered.]

**Narrator**

[Short, clear explanation.]
```

Narrator explanations should:

- explain what just happened
- define important words or customs
- connect the section to the larger story
- give one practical nugget
- stay conversational and modern
- avoid preacher tone
- avoid baby talk
- help a 6th grader understand without talking down

### 4. Chapter Transitions

Between chapters, add one short bridge:

- "Now Genesis 26 turns to Isaac."
- "Now the family tension reaches a breaking point."
- "The promise continues, but the consequences are real."

### 5. Closing

Length target: 2-4 minutes.

Closing should:

- recap each chapter briefly
- name the main lesson
- explain why it matters in real life
- preview tomorrow's day
- end with one strong takeaway sentence

### 6. Runtime Targets

For 2 chapters: 24-32 minutes.

For 3 chapters: 30-40 minutes.

For 4 long chapters: 35-50 minutes.

If the reading itself is long, do not over-teach. Let Scripture carry more of the runtime and keep narrator sections tighter.

### 7. Voice Rules

- Warm.
- Clear.
- Modern.
- Helpful.
- Story-aware.
- Audio Bible drama energy without naming the technique.
- No shouting.
- No churchy filler.
- No over-explaining every verse.
- Give real context where it helps.
- Respect the listener's intelligence.
- Make the Bible understandable while still letting it feel holy.
