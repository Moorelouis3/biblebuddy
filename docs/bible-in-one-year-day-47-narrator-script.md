# Bible in One Year - Day 47 Narrator Script

**Reading:** Numbers 34-36; Deuteronomy 1

## Opening

Welcome to Day 47 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are reading Numbers chapters 34 through 36, and Deuteronomy chapter 1.

The theme is land boundaries and Moses looks back.

Numbers ends with the land being ordered.

Then Deuteronomy begins with Moses teaching the new generation to remember the journey.

## Numbers 34 verses 1 through 29

**Scripture**

The LORD gives Moses the borders of the land of Canaan.

The south, west, north, and east borders are described.

Eleazar, Joshua, and leaders from the tribes are appointed to divide the inheritance.

**Narrator**

The promise becomes specific here.

The land is not a vague dream.

God names boundaries.

Borders matter because inheritance matters.

Israel is not grabbing whatever land they want.

They are receiving what the LORD gives.

Leaders are appointed so the land can be divided with order.

God's promise includes real places, real tribes, and real responsibility.

## Numbers 35 verses 1 through 8

**Scripture**

The LORD commands Israel to give cities to the Levites.

The Levites receive cities to dwell in and suburbs for their cattle.

These cities are taken from the inheritance of the other tribes.

**Narrator**

The Levites do not receive one large tribal territory.

They receive cities scattered among the tribes.

That matters.

Levitical presence is spread throughout Israel.

The people who serve near holy things are not locked away from the nation.

They live among the people.

God places spiritual service inside the life of the land.

The Levite cities also show that worship and teaching are meant to be spread through Israel.

The Levites are not given one isolated province far away from everyone else.

They are placed among the tribes.

That means the people who serve the tabernacle are close to ordinary life in the land.

This matters because the promised land is not only about farms, borders, and houses.

It is about living as the LORD's people in every region.

The suburbs around the cities give space for animals and daily provision.

So even the servants of the sanctuary have practical needs.

God's plan makes room for worship, service, cattle, homes, and community life together.

## Numbers 35 verses 9 through 34

**Scripture**

The LORD tells Moses to appoint cities of refuge.

Someone who kills a person unintentionally may flee there until judgment.

The chapter distinguishes murder from manslaughter.

It requires witnesses.

It warns that blood defiles the land, and the land cannot be cleansed of blood except by the blood of the one who shed it.

**Narrator**

Cities of refuge are one of the clearest examples of justice and mercy standing together.

They prevent revenge from replacing judgment.

They also show that bloodguilt is serious.

The law does not treat all killing the same.

It asks what happened.

Was it murder?

Was it accidental?

Were there witnesses?

The city of refuge gives protection before judgment is finished.

That matters because grief can quickly turn into revenge.

The avenger of blood was usually a close family member of the person who died.

Without a refuge city, anger could move faster than truth.

So God builds a place where the case can be heard carefully.

The case must still be judged.

Witnesses matter.

Intent matters.

The difference between murder and manslaughter matters.

The LORD commands Israel to slow down and judge rightly.

The phrase blood defileth the land is also serious.

Violence is not only a private issue between people.

In the land where the LORD dwells, innocent blood pollutes the community.

God cares about life.

He cares about the land.

He cares about protecting the innocent and judging the guilty.

That means the promised land cannot become a place where violence is ignored.

Mercy does not erase justice.

Justice does not erase mercy.

Both belong under the rule of the LORD.

## Numbers 36 verses 1 through 13

**Scripture**

The leaders of Manasseh raise a concern about the inheritance of Zelophehad's daughters.

If they marry into another tribe, the inheritance may move from one tribe to another.

The LORD commands that they marry within their father's tribe.

The daughters do as the LORD commanded.

**Narrator**

Numbers ends with inheritance protected.

Earlier, Zelophehad's daughters received a right answer.

Now the tribe brings another real concern.

God's answer protects both the daughters and the tribal inheritance.

This is ordered justice.

God cares about the individual family.

God also cares about the larger covenant structure of the tribes.

Numbers ends with Israel ready to inherit the land carefully.

The final inheritance question about Zelophehad's daughters shows how carefully Numbers ends.

The daughters were protected earlier.

Now the tribe's inheritance is protected too.

This is not God reversing His care for the daughters.

It is God preserving both the family and the tribal inheritance.

The command to marry within their father's tribe keeps the land from permanently moving out of the tribe.

That matters because inheritance in Israel is tied to God's promise.

Land is not just private wealth.

It is covenant gift.

The daughters obey, and Numbers ends with careful obedience around inheritance.

That ending is quieter than the Red Sea or Sinai, but it matters deeply.

It shows justice for the daughters and justice for the tribe.

## Deuteronomy 1 verses 1 through 46

**Scripture**

Moses begins speaking to Israel on this side Jordan.

He remembers how the LORD told them to go from Horeb toward the land.

He remembers appointing leaders.

He remembers the command to go up and possess the land.

He remembers the spies, the people's fear, the LORD's anger, and their failed attempt to go up afterward.

**Narrator**

Deuteronomy begins as Moses' sermon to the new generation.

He retells the story because memory matters.

The people standing before him were shaped by what happened before.

Moses remembers leadership, command, fear, rebellion, judgment, and defeat.

He is not trying to shame them for no reason.

He is teaching them.

If they are going to enter the land faithfully, they must understand how unbelief wounded the last generation.

Deuteronomy begins with honest memory.

Deuteronomy 1 changes the tone because Moses begins preaching to the new generation.

He is not merely giving travel information.

He is interpreting the journey.

He reminds them that the LORD told Israel to leave Horeb and go toward the land.

He reminds them that leadership had to be shared because the people were many.

He reminds them of the spies and the fear that spread through the camp.

He reminds them that unbelief cost the first generation dearly.

This is honest memory.

Moses does not edit out the painful parts.

He tells the truth because the new generation must not repeat the same sins.

The phrase go in and possess the land is important because the land was God's gift, but Israel still had to respond with faith and obedience.

The earlier generation heard the command but let fear rule their hearts.

When they later tried to go up without the LORD's command, they were defeated.

That teaches the difference between faith and presumption.

Faith obeys when God speaks.

Presumption tries to act religious after refusing God's word.

Deuteronomy begins by teaching the new generation to remember the story rightly.

They are not entering the land because their parents were strong.

They are entering because the LORD is faithful and because they must learn from the past.

Before we close Day 47, hold the whole day together.

Numbers 34 gives boundaries.

Numbers 35 gives Levite cities and refuge cities.

Numbers 36 protects inheritance.

Deuteronomy 1 begins Moses' great review.

That means Day 47 moves from land order to memory.

God gives the land with borders.

God places servants among the tribes.

God protects justice through refuge.

God preserves inheritance.

Then Moses teaches the people to remember how unbelief wounded the previous generation.

This is a doorway day.

Numbers closes.

Deuteronomy opens.

The people stand between remembered failure and promised inheritance.

For a beginner, the lesson is simple.

God's promises are not vague.

They touch real places, real families, real leaders, real cities, real justice, and real history.

The LORD is preparing Israel not only to enter the land, but to live there wisely.

That is why memory matters.

People who forget the wilderness can repeat the wilderness.

Moses retells the story so the new generation can enter with humility, courage, and trust.

Day 47 teaches that inheritance must be received with responsibility.

The land is gift.

The land is ordered.

The land must be just.

The land must be remembered as part of God's faithful story.

There is one more thread tying Day 47 together: the land belongs to the LORD before it belongs to Israel.

That is why boundaries matter.

God is not giving Israel a vague promise and then letting them define it however they want.

He names the borders.

He appoints leaders.

He orders the inheritance.

The land is gift, but it is not chaos.

It comes with structure.

It comes with responsibility.

It comes with justice.

The Levite cities teach that worship and instruction must be woven throughout the nation.

The refuge cities teach that life and blood must be treated with holy seriousness.

The inheritance law teaches that family and tribe both matter in God's order.

Then Deuteronomy begins by showing that the land must also be entered with memory.

Moses is not merely reviewing facts.

He is forming a generation.

He wants them to feel the weight of what happened at Kadesh.

The older generation stood near the promise and let fear speak louder than God.

The new generation must not do the same.

That is why Deuteronomy starts with looking back.

Looking back is not meant to trap Israel in shame.

It is meant to teach wisdom.

The people need to remember the command to go up.

They need to remember the fear that spread.

They need to remember the defeat that came from trying to act after refusing God.

They need to remember that the LORD was faithful even when the people were not.

This prepares the reader for the whole book of Deuteronomy.

Moses will keep connecting memory to obedience.

Remember what God did.

Remember what unbelief cost.

Remember what the LORD commanded.

Remember who brought you out.

Remember that the land is gift.

Day 47 is therefore a transition from place to preaching.

Numbers finishes arranging the land.

Deuteronomy begins arranging the heart.

For Bible Buddy readers, that is the key.

God does not only care where Israel lives.

He cares how Israel remembers, trusts, judges, worships, and obeys once they live there.

The land without memory would become dangerous.

The land without justice would become polluted.

The land without refuge would become violent.

The land without Levite cities would lose spiritual witness among the tribes.

The land without protected inheritance would lose the order God gave.

So Day 47 teaches that the promise must be received as a holy responsibility.

The LORD gives.

The LORD orders.

The LORD warns.

The LORD teaches.

The people must enter with reverence.

This day also helps us understand why Deuteronomy feels different from Numbers.

Numbers often tells the journey as it happens.

Deuteronomy preaches the meaning of the journey.

Moses stands like a father teaching children before they step into a future he will not personally enter.

That gives the book emotional weight.

He knows his time is short.

He knows the land is near.

He knows the people need more than geography.

They need wisdom.

They need covenant memory.

They need hearts ready to obey.

So the first chapter of Deuteronomy is not just a history lesson.

It is a warning and an invitation.

Do not repeat unbelief.

Do not confuse presumption with faith.

Do not forget the LORD's command.

Trust the God who brought you this far.

That is how Day 47 prepares the next stage of Bible in One Year.

Before we close, let us slow down one more time over the main movements of this day.

Numbers 34 begins with borders.

That may sound dry at first.

But borders tell Israel that the promise is not imaginary.

God is not only giving them hope in a general way.

He is giving them an actual land with real edges, real tribes, real families, and real responsibilities.

The words south, west, north, and east remind us that the promise has shape.

Israel will be able to stand inside it.

They will be able to walk through it.

They will be able to plant, build, worship, and raise children there.

That matters because biblical faith is not only about feelings.

God makes promises that enter real life.

Then Numbers 34 names leaders to divide the land.

This also matters.

The inheritance is not meant to be grabbed by the strongest tribe or claimed by the loudest person.

God appoints a process.

He gives names.

He gives order.

The land must be received with justice.

For a beginner, this helps explain why so much of the Old Testament cares about land.

Land is not just dirt.

It is the place where God's covenant people will live before Him.

It is where worship, justice, family, farming, rest, and holiness will be practiced.

Numbers 35 then moves from borders to cities.

The Levites do not receive a normal tribal territory like the other tribes.

Instead, they receive cities scattered among Israel.

That detail is easy to skip.

But it tells us something important about worship.

The worship life of Israel is not locked away in one corner.

Levite cities spread spiritual responsibility throughout the land.

The people who serve around the sanctuary are also present among the tribes.

That helps Israel remember the LORD in daily life.

The pasturelands around those cities matter too.

The Levites are spiritual servants, but they still need homes, animals, work, and provision.

God does not treat spiritual service as if it removes ordinary needs.

He orders a community where worship and practical life belong together.

Then the cities of refuge show one of the deepest lessons in the day.

God knows that death creates pain.

God knows that pain can turn into revenge.

So He provides a place where a person accused of accidental killing can run before the case is judged.

This does not mean guilt is ignored.

It means the truth must be heard before blood is shed.

That is very important.

The city of refuge protects the accused from immediate revenge.

The trial protects the community from pretending murder is accidental.

The witnesses protect judgment from rumor.

The distinction between murder and manslaughter protects justice from being careless.

This is not a soft law.

It is a careful law.

It honors the value of human life.

It honors the pain of the victim's family.

It honors the need for truth.

It honors the holiness of the land.

When the passage says blood defiles the land, it is teaching that violence has spiritual weight.

Innocent blood is not just a private tragedy.

It stains the community.

The land where God dwells must not become comfortable with bloodshed.

That is why this section is so serious.

It teaches that God's people must not be ruled by revenge.

They must be ruled by righteousness.

Then Numbers 36 returns to the daughters of Zelophehad.

This may feel like a small family question after the large laws about cities and blood.

But it is not small.

It shows that God's justice reaches into inheritance, family lines, and the future of each tribe.

The daughters had received an inheritance because their father had no sons.

Now the leaders ask what happens if they marry outside their tribe.

Would the inheritance move away from the tribe God assigned it to?

The answer protects both things.

The daughters keep their inheritance.

The tribal inheritance stays within the tribe.

That shows careful justice.

God does not solve one problem by creating another.

He preserves family, tribe, promise, and order together.

For modern readers, this may feel distant.

But for Israel, inheritance was tied to identity and covenant.

The land was not a random possession.

It was part of God's promise to His people.

So Numbers closes by showing that the promise is being guarded down to family-level details.

Nothing is too small for God's covenant order.

Then Deuteronomy opens with Moses speaking.

This is a major shift.

Numbers has carried us through travel, rebellion, judgment, census, preparation, and land instructions.

Deuteronomy begins with memory.

Moses looks back because the people must not enter the land with a confused story.

They need to know how they got there.

They need to remember the unbelief at Kadesh-barnea.

They need to remember the command to move forward.

They need to remember that fear kept a generation out of the land.

They need to remember that presumption after disobedience did not become faith.

That is one of the most helpful lessons in the chapter.

Faith is not doing whatever we want while using God's name.

Faith listens to God.

Israel refused to go when God commanded them to go.

Then they tried to go after God warned them not to.

Both choices were unbelief.

One looked like fear.

The other looked like courage.

But neither was obedience.

That is why Moses retells the story.

The new generation must learn that obedience is not only about action.

It is about listening to the LORD's word at the right time.

Day 47 therefore holds together land, worship, justice, inheritance, memory, and obedience.

The land is real.

The worship is distributed.

The justice is careful.

The inheritance is protected.

The memory is necessary.

The obedience must be humble.

That is why this day matters so much.

It prepares Israel to hear Deuteronomy as more than review.

It is Moses teaching the people how to enter the promise with a heart trained by the past.

Before we close, listen again to the movement of the whole day.

Numbers 34 says the land has boundaries.

Numbers 35 says the land must include worship, justice, and mercy.

Numbers 36 says the land must protect inheritance from one generation to the next.

Then Deuteronomy 1 says the people must remember how they got to this moment.

That order matters.

The LORD does not give Israel a promise without shape.

He gives them a land that can be named.

He gives them leaders who can divide it.

He gives them Levite cities so service to God is present among the tribes.

He gives them refuge cities so justice does not become revenge.

He gives them inheritance laws so one family's portion is not swallowed by confusion.

And then He gives them Moses' sermon so the new generation does not forget the spiritual story behind the map.

This is why the end of Numbers and the beginning of Deuteronomy belong together.

Numbers says, here is the ordered life God is preparing for you.

Deuteronomy says, remember the LORD before you step into it.

The land can be measured.

The borders can be listed.

The cities can be counted.

The tribes can be assigned.

But if Israel forgets the LORD, the outward structure will not save them.

Moses knows that.

He knows that a people can stand right next to the promise and still miss it through unbelief.

He has seen that happen already.

At Kadesh-barnea, Israel heard God's command and looked at the giants instead of the LORD.

Their fear turned the promise into a threat.

Then, after judgment was announced, they tried to fix unbelief with delayed action.

They went up when God had told them not to go.

That is why Deuteronomy 1 is not just history.

It is pastoral warning.

Moses is saying, do not repeat the old pattern.

Do not call fear wisdom.

Do not call presumption courage.

Do not treat God's word as something you can obey later when it feels easier.

The new generation needs that lesson because they are about to face real cities, real enemies, real decisions, and real pressure.

They will need more than excitement.

They will need trust.

They will need memory.

They will need obedience that listens before it moves.

The refuge cities also belong in that lesson.

They teach Israel that life is precious and justice must be careful.

God's law makes room for the difference between murder and accidental death.

That is not weakness.

That is holy wisdom.

The promised land is not supposed to become a place where anger rules.

It is supposed to become a place where God teaches His people how to judge rightly.

The daughters of Zelophehad also belong in that lesson.

Their story shows that God's order is not careless with people.

Their inheritance matters.

Their tribe matters.

Their future matters.

The LORD preserves the promise at the level of households and names.

So when we read these chapters, we should not rush past the details.

The borders, cities, refuge laws, marriages, and memories all teach the same larger truth.

God is forming a people who must live by His word in the land He gives.

Day 47 therefore stands at a doorway.

Behind Israel is wilderness failure and wilderness mercy.

Ahead of Israel is the land of promise.

Moses stands between those two realities and teaches the people to remember.

That is the sound of Deuteronomy beginning.

It is not only Moses repeating old events.

It is Moses placing the past in front of the people so they can walk forward with wisdom.

## Closing

That is Day 47.

Numbers closes with land, refuge, justice, and inheritance.

Deuteronomy opens with Moses looking back.

God's people are near the promise, but they must enter with memory, obedience, and trust.
