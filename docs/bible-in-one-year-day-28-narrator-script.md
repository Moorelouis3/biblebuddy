# Bible In One Year - Day 28 Narrator Script

## Day 28 - The Tabernacle and Priesthood Begin

**Reading:** Exodus 25 through 28  
**Bible text:** King James Version, Exodus 25-28  
**Audio style:** Narrator teaching with Scripture reading  

## Opening

Welcome to Day 28 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are reading Exodus 25 through 28.

At first, these chapters may feel like a long list of materials, measurements, curtains, furniture, and clothing.

Gold.

Silver.

Bronze.

Linen.

Wood.

Oil.

Stones.

Curtains.

Rings.

Poles.

Garments.

But if we slow down, these chapters become beautiful.

Because Exodus 25 through 28 answers one of the biggest questions in the Bible:

How can a holy God dwell among His people?

Israel has been rescued from Egypt.

They have passed through the sea.

They have eaten manna.

They have stood at Sinai.

They have received covenant law.

The covenant has been sealed with blood.

And now God says, Let them make Me a sanctuary, that I may dwell among them.

That is the heart of today's reading.

God wants to dwell among His redeemed people.

But His presence is not casual.

His holiness is not small.

So He gives a pattern.

The ark.

The mercy seat.

The table.

The lampstand.

The curtains.

The veil.

The altar.

The courtyard.

The oil.

The priestly garments.

Every detail is teaching something.

Presence.

Mercy.

Sacrifice.

Light.

Provision.

Holiness.

Priesthood.

Approach.

So today, do not think of these chapters as just construction instructions.

Think of them as God teaching Israel how nearness with Him is possible.

## Exodus 25:1-9

**Scripture**

And the LORD spake unto Moses, saying,

Speak unto the children of Israel, that they bring me an offering: of every man that giveth it willingly with his heart ye shall take my offering.

And this is the offering which ye shall take of them; gold, and silver, and brass,

And blue, and purple, and scarlet, and fine linen, and goats' hair,

Oil for the light, spices for anointing oil, and for sweet incense,

Onyx stones, and stones to be set in the ephod, and in the breastplate.

And let them make me a sanctuary; that I may dwell among them.

**Narrator**

The tabernacle instructions begin with a willing offering.

That is important.

Israel has just come out of Egypt.

In Egypt, Pharaoh built through forced labor.

He demanded bricks.

He crushed bodies.

He used people to build his power.

But the LORD does not build His dwelling like Pharaoh.

God says to receive from every person who gives willingly from the heart.

The tabernacle begins with worshipful giving, not oppression.

And the materials are costly.

Gold, silver, bronze, fine linen, beautiful colors, skins, wood, oil, spices, and precious stones.

Many of these materials likely came from what Israel received from Egypt during the Exodus.

That means the wealth of Egypt is being redirected into worship.

What once belonged to a kingdom of bondage is now being used for the dwelling place of God.

Then God gives the purpose:

Let them make Me a sanctuary, that I may dwell among them.

That line is the center of the whole section.

God is not only giving Israel laws from a distance.

He is coming to dwell in the middle of the camp.

The holy God is making a way to live among His redeemed people.

## Exodus 25:10-22

**Scripture**

And they shall make an ark of shittim wood.

And thou shalt overlay it with pure gold, within and without shalt thou overlay it.

And thou shalt put into the ark the testimony which I shall give thee.

And thou shalt make a mercy seat of pure gold.

And thou shalt make two cherubims of gold.

And the cherubims shall stretch forth their wings on high, covering the mercy seat with their wings.

And there I will meet with thee, and I will commune with thee from above the mercy seat.

**Narrator**

The first piece of furniture described is the ark of the covenant.

That is not an accident.

God starts at the center.

The ark is a gold-covered chest placed in the Most Holy Place.

Inside it, Israel will keep the testimony, the covenant words God gives them.

So at the center of Israel's worship is God's word.

But on top of the ark is the mercy seat.

That phrase matters deeply.

The mercy seat is the place where God says He will meet with Moses.

It is the place connected with covering, mercy, and later atonement.

Cherubim overshadow it with their wings.

That should make us think back to Eden.

After sin entered the world, cherubim guarded the way to the tree of life.

Now cherubim appear in the tabernacle, near the place where God will meet with His people.

That is a beautiful Bible connection.

The tabernacle is showing that access to God's presence is possible, but only through the way God provides.

At the center is not human achievement.

At the center is God's holy presence meeting His people in mercy.

## Exodus 25:23-40

**Scripture**

Thou shalt also make a table of shittim wood.

And thou shalt overlay it with pure gold.

And thou shalt set upon the table shewbread before me alway.

And thou shalt make a candlestick of pure gold.

Of beaten work shall the candlestick be made.

Six branches shall come out of the sides of it.

And thou shalt make the seven lamps thereof: and they shall light the lamps thereof, that they may give light over against it.

**Narrator**

Next come the table and the lampstand.

The table holds the bread of the Presence, sometimes called showbread.

Bread is ordinary.

Bread is daily.

Bread is necessary.

But inside the holy place, bread becomes a sign of covenant fellowship and provision before God.

Israel lives before the face of the LORD.

Their life, food, and worship all depend on Him.

Then comes the golden lampstand.

It is made of pure gold, with branches, bowls, almond blossoms, and lamps.

This is not plain utility.

It is beauty.

Inside a covered tent, light must be provided.

The lampstand gives light in the holy place before the LORD.

And the design echoes a tree, with branches and blossoms.

That again can make us think of Eden.

The tabernacle is not Eden fully restored, but it carries Eden-like hints:

God's presence.

Cherubim.

Gold.

Beauty.

Light.

Life.

The holy place is not empty religious space.

It is filled with symbols of provision, beauty, light, and ordered worship.

## Exodus 26:1-14

**Scripture**

Moreover thou shalt make the tabernacle with ten curtains of fine twined linen, and blue, and purple, and scarlet.

With cherubims of cunning work shalt thou make them.

And thou shalt make curtains of goats' hair to be a covering upon the tabernacle.

And thou shalt make a covering for the tent of rams' skins dyed red, and a covering above of badgers' skins.

**Narrator**

Exodus 26 begins describing the tent itself.

Curtains of fine linen.

Blue, purple, and scarlet.

Cherubim worked into the fabric.

Layer after layer of covering.

From the outside, the tabernacle may not have looked flashy in the way people expect.

But inside, it was full of beauty.

That matters.

The tabernacle is both practical and glorious.

It is a tent that can travel through the wilderness.

But it is also sacred space.

The outer layers protect it.

The inner layers display beauty and holiness.

This teaches us that God's presence is graciously near, but still set apart.

Israel can camp around the tabernacle.

They can see it in the middle of the camp.

But it is not common space.

It is holy space.

And again, the cherubim remind us that nearness to God is not casual.

God is making a way to dwell among His people, but the way is ordered by His own holiness.

## Exodus 26:15-37

**Scripture**

And thou shalt make boards for the tabernacle of shittim wood standing up.

And thou shalt make bars of shittim wood.

And thou shalt rear up the tabernacle according to the fashion thereof which was shewed thee in the mount.

And thou shalt make a vail of blue, and purple, and scarlet, and fine twined linen.

And the vail shall divide unto you between the holy place and the most holy.

And thou shalt put the mercy seat upon the ark of the testimony in the most holy place.

**Narrator**

Now we hear about frames, sockets, bars, curtains, and the veil.

The tabernacle has structure.

It is not random.

It is not built according to Israel's imagination.

God gives the pattern.

That phrase matters:

according to the fashion shown in the mountain.

Moses is not inventing worship.

He is receiving it.

The veil separates the holy place from the Most Holy Place.

That veil is one of the most important symbols in the tabernacle.

It teaches nearness and separation at the same time.

God is dwelling among Israel.

That is nearness.

But the Most Holy Place is not open to everyone.

That is separation.

The ark and mercy seat are behind the veil.

The holy God is present, but His holiness cannot be handled casually.

Later in the Bible, when Jesus dies, the temple veil is torn from top to bottom.

That moment carries enormous meaning because of what begins here.

The veil shows the barrier.

The gospel will show how God opens the way.

## Exodus 27:1-8

**Scripture**

And thou shalt make an altar of shittim wood.

And thou shalt overlay it with brass.

And thou shalt make the horns of it upon the four corners thereof.

And thou shalt make his pans to receive his ashes, and his shovels, and his basons, and his fleshhooks, and his firepans.

And they shall make it hollow with boards: as it was shewed thee in the mount, so shall they make it.

**Narrator**

The bronze altar stands in the courtyard.

Before anyone reaches the holy place, there is an altar.

That location matters.

Sacrifice comes before approach.

The altar is where offerings will be made.

Blood will be shed.

Sin will be addressed through the system God provides.

The altar teaches that God's presence is not entered by casual confidence.

Approach requires atonement.

That may feel heavy, but it is also merciful.

God does not tell Israel to figure out their own way into His presence.

He provides a way.

He gives the altar.

He gives the instructions.

He gives the priests.

He gives the sacrifices.

The bronze altar tells the truth about sin, but it also tells the truth about grace.

God is making a way for sinful people to come near.

## Exodus 27:9-21

**Scripture**

And thou shalt make the court of the tabernacle.

And for the gate of the court shall be an hanging of twenty cubits, of blue, and purple, and scarlet, and fine twined linen.

And thou shalt command the children of Israel, that they bring thee pure oil olive beaten for the light, to cause the lamp to burn always.

In the tabernacle of the congregation without the vail, which is before the testimony, Aaron and his sons shall order it from evening to morning before the LORD.

**Narrator**

The courtyard creates a defined holy space around the tabernacle.

There is a boundary.

But there is also a gate.

That is important.

God's holiness creates boundaries, but His mercy provides a way in.

Israel cannot approach however they want.

But they are not left without access.

The entrance is real.

The altar is real.

The priesthood will be real.

Then the chapter ends with oil for the lamp.

The light is to burn continually.

Aaron and his sons must tend it from evening to morning.

This prepares us for the next chapter, where the priesthood is described in detail.

God's dwelling is not a neglected place.

It is served.

Tended.

Kept.

Ordered.

The continual light says that worship before the LORD is not careless or occasional.

It is faithful, repeated, and sustained.

## Exodus 28:1-14

**Scripture**

And take thou unto thee Aaron thy brother, and his sons with him, from among the children of Israel, that he may minister unto me in the priest's office.

And thou shalt make holy garments for Aaron thy brother for glory and for beauty.

And they shall make the ephod of gold, of blue, and of purple, of scarlet, and fine twined linen.

And thou shalt take two onyx stones, and grave on them the names of the children of Israel.

And Aaron shall bear their names before the LORD upon his two shoulders for a memorial.

**Narrator**

Exodus 28 turns from the place of worship to the people who serve there.

Aaron and his sons are set apart as priests.

That means they will represent the people before God and serve in the tabernacle.

Their garments are made for glory and beauty.

That phrase matters.

Priestly ministry is not ordinary clothing for ordinary work.

The garments communicate holiness, dignity, and weight.

The priest does not walk into God's service casually.

Then we hear about the ephod and the stones on the shoulders.

The names of Israel's tribes are engraved on stones.

Aaron carries them before the LORD on his shoulders.

That image is powerful.

The high priest does not come before God as a private individual.

He carries the people.

The tribes are represented before the LORD.

The priest bears their names.

Shoulders often picture strength and carrying.

So the image is simple but beautiful:

God's people are carried into holy service by the one appointed to represent them.

## Exodus 28:15-30

**Scripture**

And thou shalt make the breastplate of judgment with cunning work.

And thou shalt set in it settings of stones, even four rows of stones.

And the stones shall be with the names of the children of Israel, twelve, according to their names.

And Aaron shall bear the names of the children of Israel in the breastplate of judgment upon his heart, when he goeth in unto the holy place.

And thou shalt put in the breastplate of judgment the Urim and the Thummim.

**Narrator**

The breastpiece contains twelve stones.

Each stone is connected to one of Israel's tribes.

The tribes are represented individually, beautifully, and personally before God.

This is not a blurry crowd.

Each tribe has a name.

Each tribe is remembered.

Each tribe is carried.

And now the location changes from the shoulders to the heart.

Aaron carries their names over his heart when he goes into the holy place.

That is deeply pastoral.

The people are not forgotten in worship.

They are carried near God's presence.

Not only on the priest's shoulders, but over his heart.

Strength and affection.

Representation and care.

The Urim and Thummim are also placed in the breastpiece.

These are connected with priestly guidance and judgment before the LORD.

We are not told every detail about how they worked, but their placement shows that decisions and guidance for the people belong before God.

Israel's life is not meant to be led by guesswork or human pride.

The priest carries the people before the LORD for judgment, guidance, and remembrance.

## Exodus 28:31-43

**Scripture**

And thou shalt make the robe of the ephod all of blue.

And beneath upon the hem of it thou shalt make pomegranates of blue, and of purple, and of scarlet.

And his sound shall be heard when he goeth in unto the holy place before the LORD.

And thou shalt make a plate of pure gold, and grave upon it, like the engravings of a signet, HOLINESS TO THE LORD.

And it shall be upon Aaron's forehead.

And they shall make them linen breeches to cover their nakedness.

And it shall be a statute for ever unto him and his seed after him.

**Narrator**

The final part of Exodus 28 describes the rest of the priestly garments.

The robe.

The bells.

The pomegranates.

The tunic.

The turban.

The sash.

The linen garments.

Every piece teaches reverence.

Priestly service is holy work.

The bells on the robe mean the priest's movement is heard when he enters the holy place.

The gold plate on Aaron's forehead says:

Holiness to the LORD.

That phrase is one of the clearest summaries of priestly ministry.

The priest is marked as set apart to God.

His service, his body, his garments, his forehead, his work, all belong to the LORD.

The garments also cover nakedness.

That detail reaches back toward Genesis.

After sin entered the world, nakedness became connected with shame and exposure.

Here, priestly approach requires covering.

Sinful people cannot approach God's presence casually or exposed.

God provides garments for holy service.

Exodus 28 ends with seriousness:

The priests must serve according to God's instruction so they do not bear guilt and die.

That sounds heavy because God's holiness is real.

But it is also grace that God gives the instructions at all.

He is making a way for His people to be represented before Him.

## Closing

Day 28 may look like measurements, materials, furniture, and clothing at first glance.

But underneath every detail is a beautiful truth:

God wants to dwell among His people.

The tabernacle is not random religious decoration.

It is a holy dwelling place.

It teaches Israel how the God of Sinai can live in the middle of the camp.

The ark teaches covenant.

The mercy seat teaches meeting in mercy.

The bread teaches provision and fellowship.

The lampstand teaches light.

The curtains and veil teach beauty, holiness, nearness, and separation.

The altar teaches sacrifice before approach.

The courtyard teaches boundaries and access.

The oil teaches continual service.

The priestly garments teach representation, holiness, and care.

And all of it points forward.

The tabernacle prepares the Bible's imagination for the temple.

The priesthood prepares us to understand mediation.

The altar prepares us to understand sacrifice.

The veil prepares us to understand the way opened through Jesus.

And the desire of God to dwell among His people prepares us for the bigger story:

The Word became flesh and dwelt among us.

So do not skip the details too quickly.

The details are teaching us that God's presence is both gracious and holy.

He comes near.

But He teaches us how to approach.

He dwells among His people.

But He does so through mercy, sacrifice, priesthood, and His own provided way.

That is the gift of Day 28.

The holy God wants to be with His people.

And He provides the way.
