# Day 73 Narrator Script

## Opening

Welcome to Day 73 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are reading First Samuel 19 through 22.

The title for today is David Flees From Saul.

This day shows David moving from honored servant to hunted fugitive.

Saul's jealousy becomes open violence.

Jonathan protects David.

Michal helps David escape.

Samuel shelters David.

David and Jonathan weep together.

David runs to Nob.

David runs to Gath.

David hides in the cave of Adullam.

And then Saul's rage falls on the priests of the LORD.

These chapters are painful because David is not suffering for doing wrong.

He is suffering because Saul's heart is being ruled by fear, jealousy, and suspicion.

Yet the LORD keeps preserving David.

The anointed king is not on the throne yet.

He is in danger.

But he is not abandoned.

## First Samuel 19

First Samuel 19 begins with Saul speaking to Jonathan and to all his servants that they should kill David.

This is no longer hidden suspicion.

Saul's hostility is now an open command.

Jonathan, Saul's son, delights much in David.

So Jonathan tells David to hide.

Then Jonathan speaks good of David to Saul.

He says, Let not the king sin against his servant, against David, because he hath not sinned against thee.

Jonathan reminds Saul that David's works have been very good.

David put his life in his hand and slew the Philistine.

The LORD wrought great salvation for all Israel.

Saul saw it and rejoiced.

Why then would Saul sin against innocent blood by slaying David without cause?

Jonathan's speech is wise and courageous.

He honors his father as king, but he does not flatter his father's sin.

He names David's innocence.

He names the LORD's work.

He names murder as sin.

Saul listens for a time and swears that David shall not be slain.

Jonathan brings David back, and David is in Saul's presence as in times past.

But the peace does not last.

War comes again.

David fights the Philistines and slays them with a great slaughter.

Then the troubling spirit comes upon Saul as he sits in his house with his javelin in his hand.

David plays with his hand.

Saul seeks to smite David even to the wall with the javelin.

David slips away and escapes that night.

Saul sends messengers to David's house to watch him and slay him in the morning.

Michal, David's wife, warns him that if he does not save his life that night, tomorrow he will be slain.

She lets David down through a window.

David escapes and flees.

Michal takes an image, lays it in the bed, puts goat hair at the head, and covers it with a cloth.

When Saul's messengers come, she says David is sick.

Saul orders them to bring David up in the bed so he may slay him.

The deception is discovered.

Michal tells Saul that David threatened her, which is not what the narrator told us happened.

The chapter then moves to Samuel.

David flees to Samuel at Ramah and tells him all that Saul has done.

David and Samuel go to Naioth.

Saul hears where David is and sends messengers.

But when the messengers see the company of prophets prophesying and Samuel standing over them, the Spirit of God comes upon Saul's messengers, and they prophesy.

Saul sends others.

They also prophesy.

He sends a third group.

They also prophesy.

Finally Saul goes himself.

The Spirit of God comes upon him also.

He goes on and prophesies until he comes to Naioth.

He strips off his clothes and prophesies before Samuel, lying down naked all that day and night.

The saying returns: Is Saul also among the prophets?

The scene is strange, but the point is clear.

Saul cannot simply reach out and destroy David.

Even Saul's messengers are stopped.

Even Saul himself is overcome.

The LORD restrains the violent king and preserves the hunted servant.

## First Samuel 20

First Samuel 20 centers on David and Jonathan.

David flees from Naioth and comes to Jonathan.

He asks, What have I done?

What is mine iniquity?

What is my sin before thy father, that he seeketh my life?

David is not claiming to be sinless before God.

He is asking what crime he has committed against Saul.

Jonathan first thinks Saul would tell him if he planned to kill David.

But David explains that Saul knows Jonathan delights in David and may hide the matter from him.

David says there is but a step between him and death.

Jonathan asks what David wants him to do.

David proposes a test during the new moon feast.

David will be absent.

If Saul asks, Jonathan should say David earnestly asked leave to go to Bethlehem for a yearly sacrifice.

If Saul says, It is well, then David is safe.

If Saul is very wroth, then evil is determined by him.

Jonathan and David make covenant language together.

Jonathan asks David to show kindness to his house forever.

This matters because Jonathan knows the LORD will be with David.

Jonathan is Saul's son, but he is not fighting David for the throne.

He binds himself to David in loyal love.

Jonathan then sets up a sign involving arrows.

He will shoot three arrows as though at a mark.

If he tells the lad the arrows are on this side, David can come.

If he says the arrows are beyond thee, David must go, for the LORD has sent him away.

At the feast, David's place is empty.

Saul says nothing the first day.

On the second day, Saul asks why David has not come.

Jonathan gives the explanation.

Saul's anger is kindled against Jonathan.

He calls Jonathan the son of a perverse rebellious woman.

He says as long as David lives, Jonathan will not be established, nor his kingdom.

Saul commands Jonathan to send and fetch David, because he shall surely die.

Jonathan asks why David should be slain.

What has he done?

Saul casts a javelin at Jonathan.

Now Jonathan knows his father has determined to kill David.

Jonathan rises from the table in fierce anger and eats no meat the second day of the month, because he is grieved for David and because his father has done him shame.

In the morning, Jonathan goes out to the field.

He shoots the arrows.

He cries to the lad, Is not the arrow beyond thee?

That is the signal.

After the lad is sent away, David arises from a place toward the south, falls on his face to the ground, and bows three times.

David and Jonathan kiss one another and weep with one another, until David exceeds.

Jonathan says, Go in peace, because they have sworn both of them in the name of the LORD.

The chapter ends with David arising and departing, and Jonathan going into the city.

This is a heartbreaking separation.

Jonathan chooses faithfulness over family advantage.

David leaves behind the safety and friendship he had in Saul's house.

The road of exile begins.

## First Samuel 21

First Samuel 21 shows David on the run.

He comes to Nob to Ahimelech the priest.

Ahimelech is afraid and asks why David is alone.

David says the king has commanded him a business and that his men are appointed to a place.

David asks for bread.

Ahimelech says there is no common bread, but there is hallowed bread.

The priest gives David hallowed bread, because there is no bread there except the shewbread that was taken from before the LORD.

This moment will be remembered later in the Bible.

David is hungry.

He is in need.

The holy bread is given in a mercy-shaped situation.

But the chapter also notes a dangerous witness.

Doeg the Edomite, chiefest of Saul's herdmen, is detained before the LORD that day.

He sees enough to become a weapon later.

David then asks whether there is a spear or sword.

He says the king's business required haste.

Ahimelech says the sword of Goliath is there, wrapped in a cloth behind the ephod.

David says there is none like that; give it me.

The sword is heavy with memory.

David once defeated Goliath without a sword in his hand.

Now, in fear and flight, he carries Goliath's sword.

David then flees to Achish king of Gath.

Gath is Goliath's city.

The servants of Achish recognize David and say, Is not this David the king of the land?

Did they not sing, Saul hath slain his thousands, and David his ten thousands?

David lays up these words in his heart and is sore afraid of Achish.

So he changes his behavior before them and feigns himself mad.

He scrabbles on the doors of the gate and lets spittle fall down upon his beard.

Achish says he has enough mad men and does not need this fellow brought into his house.

David escapes, but the chapter feels low.

The anointed king is hungry, afraid, misleading a priest, carrying Goliath's sword, and pretending madness in enemy territory.

The Bible does not make David's suffering look clean and easy.

He is being preserved, but the path is hard.

## First Samuel 22

First Samuel 22 begins with David escaping to the cave Adullam.

When his brethren and all his father's house hear it, they go down to him.

Everyone in distress, everyone in debt, and everyone discontented gathers to him.

David becomes captain over them.

About four hundred men are with him.

This is not a polished royal court.

It is a gathering of troubled people around the rejected anointed one.

David then goes to Mizpeh of Moab and asks the king of Moab to let his father and mother stay there until David knows what God will do for him.

The prophet Gad tells David not to stay in the hold, but to depart into the land of Judah.

David obeys and comes into the forest of Hareth.

Then the scene returns to Saul.

Saul is sitting in Gibeah under a tree in Ramah, with his spear in his hand and his servants standing around him.

The spear keeps appearing near Saul.

It is like a symbol of his suspicious violence.

Saul accuses his servants.

He asks whether the son of Jesse will give them fields and vineyards or make them captains.

He complains that everyone has conspired against him and that no one is sorry for him.

Saul sounds isolated, suspicious, and self-pitying.

Doeg the Edomite speaks up.

He says he saw David coming to Nob, to Ahimelech.

He reports that Ahimelech inquired of the LORD for David, gave him victuals, and gave him Goliath's sword.

Saul sends for Ahimelech and all his father's house, the priests in Nob.

Ahimelech answers Saul with dignity.

He asks who is so faithful among Saul's servants as David, the king's son in law, who goes at Saul's bidding and is honorable in his house.

Ahimelech says he did not know anything about Saul's conflict with David.

Saul does not listen.

He says Ahimelech shall surely die, he and all his father's house.

Saul commands the footmen to turn and slay the priests of the LORD because their hand also is with David.

But the servants of the king will not put forth their hand to fall upon the priests of the LORD.

So Saul commands Doeg.

Doeg turns and falls upon the priests.

He kills eighty-five persons that wear a linen ephod.

Then Nob, the city of the priests, is struck with the edge of the sword: men, women, children, nursing infants, oxen, asses, and sheep.

This is one of Saul's darkest moments.

The king who failed to fully obey God's command against Amalek now destroys the priests of the LORD.

One son of Ahimelech, Abiathar, escapes and flees after David.

Abiathar tells David that Saul has slain the LORD's priests.

David says he knew that day, when Doeg was there, that he would surely tell Saul.

David says, I have occasioned the death of all the persons of thy father's house.

Then he tells Abiathar to abide with him.

Fear not.

He that seeketh my life seeketh thy life.

With me thou shalt be in safeguard.

David cannot undo what happened at Nob.

But he becomes a refuge for the survivor.

The hunted one shelters another hunted one.

## Full Lesson Review

Day 73 is about preservation under persecution.

Saul's sin deepens.

He tries to kill David.

He tries to use his household and servants against David.

He throws a javelin at Jonathan.

He massacres the priests at Nob.

The fall of Saul is no longer only about sacrifice at Gilgal or incomplete obedience with Amalek.

His heart is becoming violent toward the innocent.

David, meanwhile, is preserved through different people and strange circumstances.

Jonathan speaks for him.

Michal lets him down through a window.

Samuel shelters him.

The Spirit of God stops Saul's messengers.

Jonathan warns him with arrows.

Ahimelech gives bread.

Achish sends him away.

Troubled men gather around him.

Abiathar survives and comes to him.

The LORD's preservation does not mean David avoids fear.

It means Saul cannot overthrow what God has chosen.

The chapters also teach us the cost of faithful friendship.

Jonathan loses peace with his father because he loves righteousness and loves David.

He does not use David's danger to protect his own future.

He recognizes the LORD's hand.

And David learns the grief of leadership in exile.

People come to him wounded.

Others suffer because of Saul's rage.

David must learn to trust God while carrying sorrow.

## Guided Listening Review

Day 73 is heavy because innocence does not protect David from suffering.

David has served Saul.

David has fought Israel's enemies.

David has soothed Saul with music.

David has behaved wisely.

Yet Saul seeks his life.

That is one reason Jonathan's speech in chapter 19 matters so much.

Jonathan does not say David is useful only because he is popular.

He says David has not sinned against Saul.

He reminds Saul that the LORD wrought great salvation through David.

He calls the planned murder innocent blood.

Faithful friendship tells the truth even when the truth is costly.

Michal's rescue shows another layer of preservation.

David escapes through a window.

The scene feels fragile, hurried, and dangerous.

Then David runs to Samuel.

That move is important.

David goes to the prophet connected to his anointing.

He goes to the place where God's word has been present in his life.

Saul sends messengers, but the Spirit of God interrupts them.

Again and again the violence is restrained.

The point is not that David never feels afraid.

The point is that Saul cannot break through God's preserving hand.

Chapter 20 then slows everything down into covenant friendship.

David asks what he has done.

Jonathan has to face the truth about his father.

The arrows become a signal of heartbreak.

When Jonathan says the arrows are beyond the lad, David understands that he must go.

Their weeping is not weakness.

It is the grief of love, loyalty, and separation under evil pressure.

Jonathan is one of the most beautiful figures in these chapters because he does not treat David's calling as a threat to his own worth.

He strengthens David instead of competing with him.

Chapter 21 shows David in need.

He needs bread.

He needs a weapon.

He is afraid in Gath.

He does not look like the victorious young man standing over Goliath.

He looks hunted and vulnerable.

The Bible lets us see that even chosen servants can pass through confusing, low, and imperfect days.

Chapter 22 then shows two gatherings.

Troubled people gather to David.

Suspicious servants stand around Saul.

David becomes captain of the distressed, indebted, and discontented.

Saul sits with a spear and speaks out of paranoia.

Then Doeg's report leads to the slaughter of the priests.

The horror at Nob shows how far Saul has fallen.

The king who should protect the worship of the LORD destroys the priests of the LORD.

And yet Abiathar escapes to David.

David becomes a shelter for the survivor.

That is the shape of the day.

The hunted anointed one becomes a refuge for others who are hunted.

The wilderness is not wasted.

God is forming David's leadership there.

## Key Connections

There are three things to remember as David leaves Saul's house.

First, Saul's violence keeps spreading.

At first he fears David.

Then he throws spears.

Then he commands others to kill David.

Then he turns against Jonathan.

Then he destroys Nob.

Sin rarely stays small when it is protected instead of confessed.

Second, Jonathan's loyalty is covenant-shaped.

He does not love David only when it is easy.

He loves David when David's future appears to threaten Jonathan's own earthly future.

That makes Jonathan's friendship deeply different from Saul's jealousy.

Jonathan is not losing himself by honoring God's work in David.

He is showing faith.

Third, David's weakness does not cancel God's calling.

David is hungry at Nob.

David is afraid in Gath.

David hides in Adullam.

David carries grief after Nob.

Yet the LORD is still preserving him.

The road to the throne is not a straight road of visible success.

It is a road where God teaches David to lead wounded people while David himself is wounded.

One more pattern is important.

David keeps losing places that once felt safe.

Saul's court is no longer safe.

His own house is no longer safe.

The feast table is no longer safe.

Nob becomes dangerous because Doeg is watching.

Gath becomes dangerous because David is recognized.

Even the cave is filled with distressed people rather than comfort.

But the LORD's presence is not limited to safe places.

God preserves David in houses, fields, prophetic communities, roads, enemy towns, and caves.

That matters for understanding the wilderness years.

The wilderness is not proof that God has forgotten David.

It is the place where God's promise keeps holding David when every visible support is shaken.

The chapter also teaches that evil leadership hurts more than its direct target.

Saul wants David, but the priests of Nob die.

Saul's rage spills outward.

That is why David's future kingship must be different.

He must learn to protect life, receive the wounded, and fear the LORD more than his own insecurity.

## Closing

As you finish Day 73, remember that God's anointed king is preserved, but not by an easy path.

David runs.

David hides.

David weeps.

David is afraid.

David makes imperfect choices.

Yet the LORD keeps him.

Saul can throw spears.

Saul can send messengers.

Saul can rage.

Saul can destroy Nob.

But Saul cannot cancel the word of the LORD.

The next chapters will keep testing David.

Will he take revenge?

Will he grasp the throne?

Will he trust the LORD to judge between him and Saul?

For now, David is learning the wilderness road.
