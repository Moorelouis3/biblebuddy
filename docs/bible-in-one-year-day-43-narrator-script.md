# Bible in One Year - Day 43 Narrator Script

**Reading:** Numbers 18-21

## Opening

Welcome to Day 43 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are reading Numbers chapters 18 through 21.

The theme is provision, judgment, and the bronze serpent.

These chapters move through priestly responsibility, cleansing from death, water from the rock, Aaron's death, fiery serpents, and victories on the road.

Numbers keeps showing judgment and mercy side by side.

## Numbers 18 verses 1 through 32

**Scripture**

The LORD speaks to Aaron about the iniquity of the sanctuary and the priesthood.

The Levites are joined to Aaron to help with the service of the tabernacle.

The priests receive holy gifts from the offerings.

The Levites receive the tithe from Israel, and they give a tithe of the tithe to the LORD.

**Narrator**

Numbers 18 follows the rebellion of Korah.

That matters.

After people challenged the priesthood, God clarifies the priesthood.

Aaron and his sons have responsibility near the sanctuary.

The Levites help, but they must not cross into priestly service.

God also provides for them.

The priests and Levites do not receive land inheritance like the other tribes.

The LORD Himself is their portion, and the holy gifts support their service.

The phrase I have given thee the charge of mine offerings helps explain the whole chapter.

The priests are not simply receiving religious benefits.

They are carrying responsibility.

The sanctuary is holy, so the work near it must be guarded.

The Levites are joined to Aaron, but they are not allowed to take over Aaron's priestly role.

That distinction matters after Korah's rebellion.

God is making the boundary clear again.

The priesthood is not open to whoever wants status.

It is appointed by the LORD.

The phrase I am thy part and thine inheritance is also beautiful.

The priests do not receive land like the other tribes.

Their portion is the LORD Himself.

That does not mean they live without provision.

God gives them holy gifts from the offerings.

The Levites receive the tithe because they serve the tabernacle.

Then they give a tithe from the tithe.

That teaches that even those who receive support for ministry still honor the LORD with what they receive.

Numbers 18 is about responsibility, provision, and boundaries.

God provides for His servants, but He also defines their service.

## Numbers 19 verses 1 through 22

**Scripture**

The LORD gives the law of the red heifer.

A red heifer without spot is taken outside the camp and burned.

Its ashes are kept for the water of separation.

Anyone who touches a dead body is unclean and must be purified.

If he refuses purification, he defiles the tabernacle of the LORD.

**Narrator**

Numbers 19 deals with death.

That is important because the wilderness is now a place where a generation will die.

Death brings uncleanness.

God provides cleansing.

The red heifer is burned outside the camp.

Its ashes are used with water for purification.

This teaches that death's uncleanness cannot be ignored among a people where God dwells.

But it also teaches mercy.

God provides a way to be cleansed.

The red heifer law is connected to death.

That matters because the wilderness generation is under judgment.

Many people will die before the next generation enters the land.

Death is not treated as normal in the presence of a holy God.

Touching a dead body makes a person unclean.

Unclean does not always mean personally wicked.

It means the person is not ready to come near the holy place.

The red heifer is taken outside the camp.

It is burned, and its ashes are kept for the water of separation.

This water is used for purification.

The phrase water of separation means water connected to cleansing from death's defilement.

That helps beginners understand the chapter.

God is not ignoring death.

He is teaching Israel that death and holiness cannot be treated casually together.

But He also gives a way back.

The person who becomes unclean is not trapped forever.

God provides cleansing so life with the community and worship can continue.

The chapter is serious, but it is also merciful.

The LORD makes provision for people living in a death-marked wilderness.

## Numbers 20 verses 1 through 13

**Scripture**

The children of Israel come to Kadesh.

Miriam dies there.

The congregation has no water and gathers against Moses and Aaron.

The LORD tells Moses to speak to the rock.

Moses strikes the rock twice with his rod, and water comes out abundantly.

But the LORD says Moses and Aaron did not believe Him to sanctify Him before the people, so they will not bring the congregation into the land.

**Narrator**

This is one of the saddest moments in Moses' life.

The people complain again.

God tells Moses to speak to the rock.

Moses strikes it.

Water still comes out, because God is merciful to the thirsty people.

But Moses has dishonored the LORD in front of Israel.

Leadership matters.

God's holiness must be represented rightly.

Even faithful leaders are accountable before Him.

The phrase speak ye unto the rock is the command Moses receives.

That is why the moment is so serious.

God does not tell Moses to strike the rock this time.

He tells him to speak.

Moses gathers the people and says, Hear now, ye rebels.

Then he strikes the rock twice.

Water comes out abundantly because God still cares for the thirsty congregation.

But Moses has not represented God rightly.

The LORD says Moses and Aaron did not believe Him, to sanctify Him in the eyes of the children of Israel.

To sanctify the LORD means to treat Him as holy before the people.

Moses' anger makes the moment look like the water comes through his own frustration and power.

That matters because leaders teach people what God is like.

Moses has been faithful in many ways, but he is still accountable.

The consequence is painful.

He will not bring the congregation into the land.

This does not erase Moses' faithfulness.

It shows that God's holiness matters even in the life of a great leader.

The water is mercy for the people.

The judgment is discipline for Moses and Aaron.

Both truths stand together.

## Numbers 20 verses 14 through 29

**Scripture**

Moses sends messengers to the king of Edom, asking to pass through the land.

Edom refuses and comes out against Israel with much people and a strong hand.

Israel turns away.

Then the LORD tells Moses and Aaron to go up Mount Hor.

Aaron's garments are put on Eleazar his son, and Aaron dies there.

All the house of Israel mourns for Aaron thirty days.

**Narrator**

Edom descends from Esau, so this is a brother nation refusing passage.

Israel does not fight here.

They turn away.

Then the chapter shifts to Aaron's death.

The priestly garments pass from Aaron to Eleazar.

That detail matters.

Aaron dies, but the priesthood continues.

One generation is passing away, but God's provision for priestly service remains.

Edom's refusal also carries family history.

Edom comes from Esau.

Israel comes from Jacob.

So when Edom refuses passage, it is not just a random foreign nation saying no.

It is a brother people blocking the road.

Israel asks peacefully.

They promise not to pass through fields or vineyards.

They promise not to drink the water of the wells.

But Edom comes out with much people and a strong hand.

Israel turns away.

That detail matters because Israel does not fight every battle in front of them.

Sometimes obedience means turning away and taking the longer road.

Then Aaron dies on Mount Hor.

The transfer of garments is deeply important.

Priestly garments represent priestly office.

Aaron's death is real grief, but the priesthood does not die with him.

Eleazar receives the garments.

The office continues because God's provision continues.

The people mourn thirty days.

This is the end of a major life in Israel's story.

Aaron has failed, served, interceded, sinned, and stood in holy office.

Now the next generation of priestly leadership begins.

## Numbers 21 verses 1 through 9

**Scripture**

Israel fights the Canaanite king of Arad and the LORD gives victory.

Then the people become discouraged and speak against God and Moses.

The LORD sends fiery serpents, and many people die.

The people confess that they have sinned.

The LORD tells Moses to make a serpent of brass and set it on a pole.

Anyone who is bitten and looks at it lives.

**Narrator**

The bronze serpent is a mercy in the middle of judgment.

The bite is real.

The death is real.

The sin is confessed.

And God gives a remedy.

🐍 Serpents bring judgment.

🙏 The people confess sin.

👀 Looking receives life.

The healing is not complicated.

They must look at what God provides.

This scene will later become important in the New Testament when Jesus speaks about being lifted up.

The fiery serpents scene is simple and deep at the same time.

The people speak against God and against Moses.

They despise the way.

They despise the manna.

Once again, complaint becomes sin against the LORD.

The serpents bring judgment into the camp.

The people confess, We have sinned.

That confession matters.

They do not only say, "We are hurting."

They say, "We have sinned."

Moses intercedes again.

God tells him to make a serpent of brass and set it on a pole.

The remedy is strange but clear.

The bitten person must look at what God provides.

The power is not in human effort.

The power is not in the bronze itself as magic.

The healing comes through trusting God's appointed remedy.

That is why Jesus later connects this moment to Himself in John 3.

As Moses lifted up the serpent in the wilderness, the Son of Man must be lifted up.

The Old Testament scene teaches the pattern.

Judgment is real.

Sin is deadly.

God provides the way of life.

The bitten must look and live.

## Numbers 21 verses 10 through 35

**Scripture**

Israel journeys onward.

The people come to Beer, where the LORD gives water, and Israel sings about the well.

Israel asks Sihon king of the Amorites for passage, but he refuses and fights.

Israel defeats him.

Then Og king of Bashan comes out against them, and the LORD gives Israel victory.

**Narrator**

The chapter ends with forward movement.

There is water.

There is a song.

There is conflict.

There is victory.

Sihon and Og are powerful kings, but they cannot stop the LORD's purpose.

After so much failure in the wilderness, these victories remind us that God is still moving Israel toward the land.

The promise has not died in the desert.

The final section shows forward movement after so much death and failure.

Israel journeys.

God gives water at Beer.

The people sing, Spring up, O well.

That song matters because complaint has filled so many wilderness scenes.

Here, the people sing over God's provision.

Then Sihon king of the Amorites refuses peaceful passage.

Unlike Edom, Sihon attacks Israel.

The LORD gives Israel victory.

Og king of Bashan also comes out against them.

The LORD tells Moses not to fear him.

That phrase matters after the fear of giants in Numbers 13.

The people are learning that powerful enemies are not stronger than God's promise.

Sihon and Og become important names later in the Bible because their defeat proves that God can give victory east of the Jordan.

These victories remind Israel that the journey is not over.

The old generation is passing.

Leaders have failed.

People have complained.

Judgment has fallen.

But the LORD is still moving His people toward the land.

Day 43 holds judgment and mercy together.

The priesthood is guarded.

Death requires cleansing.

Moses is disciplined.

Aaron dies.

The people are bitten.

Yet God provides gifts, purification, water, a new priest, healing, songs, and victory.

The wilderness is severe, but it is not empty of grace.

God keeps making a way for His people to continue.

That is the heart of this lesson.

Sin has consequences.

God's holiness is serious.

But the LORD keeps providing what His people need to live, be cleansed, be healed, and keep moving.

Numbers 19 gives the law of the red heifer and cleansing from death.

God is teaching Israel that life near His presence requires cleansing.

Miriam dies.

The people lack water.

Water still comes, but Moses and Aaron are told they will not bring the congregation into the land.
