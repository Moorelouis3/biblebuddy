# Day 86 Narrator Script

## Opening

Welcome to Day 86 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are reading First Kings 16 through 19.

The title for today is Elijah Confronts Idolatry.

This day brings us into one of the most intense moments in the kingdom story.

Israel's throne is unstable.

Kings rise, sin, die, and get replaced.

Then Ahab comes to the throne and does more evil than the kings before him.

He marries Jezebel.

He serves Baal.

He builds an altar for Baal in Samaria.

Against that dark background, Elijah appears.

He announces that there will be no dew or rain except according to the word of the LORD.

The chapters move from drought, to a widow's house, to Mount Carmel, to fire from heaven, to rain returning, and finally to Elijah's fear and exhaustion in the wilderness.

Day 86 teaches that the LORD is not silent when idolatry becomes public and powerful.

It also teaches that even faithful servants can become tired, afraid, and in need of God's gentle care.

## First Kings 16

First Kings 16 continues the decline of the northern kingdom.

The word of the LORD comes against Baasha through Jehu the prophet.

Baasha had been lifted from the dust, but he walked in the way of Jeroboam and made Israel to sin.

That pattern matters.

Jeroboam's sin is still shaping the nation.

Israel keeps changing rulers, but the spiritual disease remains.

Elah reigns after Baasha, but Zimri kills him.

Zimri reigns only seven days.

When the people hear what he has done, they make Omri king.

Zimri burns the king's house over himself and dies.

Then Israel divides between Omri and Tibni until Omri prevails.

Omri builds Samaria and becomes a powerful king, but the Bible's main judgment is spiritual.

Omri wrought evil in the eyes of the LORD, and did worse than all that were before him.

Then Ahab, Omri's son, reigns.

The text says Ahab did evil in the sight of the LORD above all that were before him.

That is not a small statement.

The darkness deepens.

Ahab marries Jezebel, serves Baal, worships him, builds a house for Baal in Samaria, and makes a grove.

The chapter ends by remembering Jericho being rebuilt at the cost of Hiel's sons, according to the word of the LORD spoken by Joshua.

The reader should hear the warning.

God's word is not old noise.

It still stands.

## First Kings 17

First Kings 17 introduces Elijah the Tishbite.

He stands before Ahab and says there will be no dew or rain according to his word.

This directly confronts Baal worship because Baal was treated as a storm and fertility god.

The drought declares that the LORD, not Baal, rules rain, food, life, and the land.

Then the LORD hides Elijah by the brook Cherith.

The ravens bring him bread and flesh, and he drinks from the brook.

Elijah is bold before Ahab, but dependent before God.

When the brook dries up, the LORD sends him to Zarephath, to a widow.

This is surprising.

The prophet is sent outside Israel, into the region connected with Jezebel's world, and God provides through a poor widow with almost nothing left.

The widow has only a handful of meal and a little oil.

Elijah tells her to fear not and make him a little cake first.

The barrel of meal does not waste, and the cruse of oil does not fail.

The LORD sustains life in a place of famine.

Then the widow's son dies.

This brings the pain of the chapter to the surface.

The God who provides bread is also the God who must give life.

Elijah cries to the LORD, stretches himself upon the child, and the child's soul comes into him again.

The widow says, Now by this I know that thou art a man of God, and that the word of the LORD in thy mouth is truth.

That is the point.

In a time of false worship, the LORD proves His word true.

## First Kings 18

First Kings 18 moves from hidden provision to public confrontation.

After many days, the word of the LORD tells Elijah to show himself to Ahab, and God will send rain.

The famine is severe in Samaria.

Obadiah, who fears the LORD, has hidden one hundred prophets by fifty in caves and fed them with bread and water.

That detail matters.

Even in Ahab and Jezebel's kingdom, the LORD has servants who are quietly faithful.

Elijah meets Obadiah and tells him to announce his arrival to Ahab.

Obadiah is afraid, but Elijah assures him.

When Ahab sees Elijah, he calls him the troubler of Israel.

Elijah answers that Ahab has troubled Israel by forsaking the commandments of the LORD and following Baalim.

The question of the day is not political inconvenience.

The question is covenant rebellion.

Elijah calls Israel to Mount Carmel.

He asks, How long halt ye between two opinions?

If the LORD be God, follow him; but if Baal, then follow him.

The people answer not a word.

That silence is heavy.

Then the prophets of Baal call on Baal from morning until noon.

They leap upon the altar.

They cry aloud.

They cut themselves.

There is no voice.

There is no answer.

There is no regard.

Elijah repairs the altar of the LORD that was broken down.

He takes twelve stones, according to the number of the tribes of Jacob.

He drenches the sacrifice with water.

Then he prays that the people may know that the LORD is God and that He has turned their heart back again.

The fire of the LORD falls.

It consumes the burnt sacrifice, the wood, the stones, the dust, and the water in the trench.

The people fall on their faces and say, The LORD, he is the God; the LORD, he is the God.

After judgment on the false prophets, Elijah prays for rain.

At first there is nothing.

Then a little cloud rises out of the sea like a man's hand.

Soon the heaven is black with clouds and wind, and there is a great rain.

The drought ends by the word of the LORD.

## First Kings 19

First Kings 19 shows Elijah after Mount Carmel.

Jezebel threatens his life, and Elijah flees.

He goes into the wilderness, sits under a juniper tree, and asks that he might die.

This is not the voice of a lazy prophet.

This is the voice of an exhausted servant.

The LORD does not begin by scolding Elijah.

An angel touches him and tells him to arise and eat.

There is a cake and water.

Elijah eats, sleeps, and is touched again.

The journey is too great for him.

That line is tender.

God knows the limits of His servant.

Elijah goes to Horeb, the mount of God.

The LORD asks, What doest thou here, Elijah?

Elijah says he has been very jealous for the LORD, but Israel has forsaken the covenant, thrown down the altars, slain the prophets, and he alone is left.

Then the LORD passes by.

There is wind, earthquake, and fire, but the LORD is not in those.

After the fire comes a still small voice.

Elijah covers his face with his mantle.

The LORD gives him his next assignments: anoint Hazael, Jehu, and Elisha.

The LORD also tells him that seven thousand in Israel have not bowed to Baal.

Elijah is not alone.

The day ends with Elijah calling Elisha, who leaves his oxen and follows him.

## Full Lesson Review

Day 86 moves from royal collapse to prophetic confrontation.

First Kings 16 shows kings changing while sin remains.

First Kings 17 shows the LORD sustaining Elijah, a widow, and a child when the land is under drought.

First Kings 18 shows the LORD exposing Baal as powerless and turning Israel's attention back to the true God.

First Kings 19 shows the LORD caring for His exhausted prophet and continuing His work through future servants.

The day is full of fire, famine, prayer, fear, and mercy.

The central truth is clear: the LORD is God.

He rules rain.

He gives bread.

He raises the dead.

He answers by fire.

He speaks in a still small voice.

## Slow Walk Through the Day

Slow down with the contrast between Ahab and Elijah.

Ahab has a throne, a palace, an army, and public influence.

Elijah appears with only the word of the LORD.

That is the tension of the day.

The visible power belongs to the king.

The real authority belongs to God.

When Elijah announces no dew or rain, he is not making a weather prediction.

He is declaring that Baal cannot give what the people think Baal controls.

The drought is a confrontation with false worship.

Every dry field and empty table becomes a sermon against idolatry.

Then notice where God sends Elijah.

First, God sends him to a brook.

Then God sends him to a widow.

Neither place looks secure.

A brook can dry up.

A poor widow does not look like a storehouse.

But the LORD is teaching Elijah that provision comes from His command, not from the appearance of the source.

Ravens can feed a prophet.

A nearly empty barrel can keep a household alive.

The LORD is not limited by what looks small.

Mount Carmel then brings the private lesson into public view.

The prophets of Baal have numbers, noise, ritual, and desperation.

They cry, leap, and cut themselves.

But there is no answer.

Elijah repairs the altar of the LORD.

That detail matters because Israel's worship has been broken down.

He uses twelve stones, reminding the people of the whole covenant people, not only the divided northern kingdom.

When the fire falls, the issue is settled.

The LORD is not one god among many.

The LORD, He is the God.

Then First Kings 19 gives the listener mercy after the drama.

Elijah has seen fire fall, but he still becomes afraid.

He has prayed down rain, but he still becomes exhausted.

The Bible does not shame him for being human.

The LORD feeds him, lets him sleep, and speaks to him.

The still small voice teaches the listener that God's presence is not limited to the loudest moment.

The God of fire is also the God of quiet speech.

The God who confronts kings also restores tired servants.

## Prayerful Landing

Day 86 asks the listener to reject half-hearted loyalty.

How long halt ye between two opinions?

That question belongs to Carmel, but it also presses into every generation.

If the LORD is God, follow Him.

Do not treat Him as one option beside the idols of the age.

Do not measure truth by noise, numbers, or public power.

The prophets of Baal had all of that, and still no answer came.

The LORD answered by fire.

The LORD answered by rain.

The LORD answered by bread, oil, life, and a voice.

So Day 86 is not only a story about Elijah being brave.

It is a story about the LORD proving Himself faithful in drought, famine, confrontation, and exhaustion.

## Final Guided Recap

Before the closing, gather the four chapters one more time.

First Kings 16 shows the northern kingdom sinking deeper.

Baasha, Elah, Zimri, Omri, and Ahab remind the listener that a nation can keep changing leaders while staying trapped in the same rebellion.

The throne is active, but the heart of the kingdom is sick.

Ahab's marriage to Jezebel and worship of Baal make the crisis public and official.

First Kings 17 shows that the LORD can preserve life in hidden places.

Elijah is hidden by a brook.

Then he is sustained in a widow's house.

The widow's meal and oil teach daily dependence.

The raising of her son teaches that the LORD has authority over life itself.

First Kings 18 shows public decision.

Israel cannot keep limping between two opinions.

The broken altar must be repaired.

The false prophets must be exposed.

The LORD answers by fire so the people know His name.

Then rain returns because the same God who withheld rain is the God who gives it.

First Kings 19 shows that servants of God are not machines.

Elijah needs food, sleep, touch, correction, and a renewed assignment.

God tells him there are seven thousand who have not bowed to Baal.

The work is bigger than Elijah's fear.

The LORD still has people.

The LORD still has a plan.

The LORD still has another servant to call in Elisha.

## Closing

As you finish Day 86, remember Elijah on Carmel and Elijah under the tree.

The same LORD who answers with fire also feeds His tired servant.

God confronts idolatry with power, and He cares for His people with tenderness.
