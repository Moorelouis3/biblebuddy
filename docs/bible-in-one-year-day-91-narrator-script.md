# Day 91 Narrator Script

## Opening

Welcome to Day 91 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are reading Second Kings 14 through 17.

The title for today is Israel Falls to Assyria.

This day shows the northern kingdom moving toward exile.

Judah has kings with mixed obedience.

Israel has repeated conspiracy, violence, and idolatry.

Assyria begins to dominate the land.

The chapter sequence ends with the fall of Samaria and a long explanation of why Israel was carried away.

This is not random tragedy.

The text says Israel feared other gods, walked in the statutes of the heathen, rejected the LORD's statutes, followed vanity, and would not hear.

Day 91 is a warning day.

It teaches that covenant rebellion can become normal for so long that judgment finally arrives.

## Second Kings 14

Second Kings 14 begins with Amaziah of Judah.

He does right in the sight of the LORD, yet not like David.

The high places are not removed.

That mixed description matters.

A king can make real steps toward right and still leave unfinished obedience in place.

Amaziah defeats Edom in the valley of salt, but then pride rises.

He challenges Jehoash king of Israel.

Jehoash warns him with a parable about a thistle and a cedar, but Amaziah will not listen.

Judah is defeated.

Jerusalem's wall is broken, and treasures are taken.

The chapter then turns to Jeroboam the second of Israel.

He does evil, but the LORD sees Israel's bitter affliction and uses him to restore Israel's coast.

That mercy is striking.

God helps a suffering people even while their king is not faithful.

But mercy should have led them back to the LORD, and the following chapters show that Israel does not turn.

## Second Kings 15

Second Kings 15 feels like a collapsing throne room.

Azariah, also called Uzziah, reigns long in Judah, but he is struck with leprosy and lives in a several house.

In Israel, kings rise and fall quickly.

Zechariah reigns six months.

Shallum conspires against him and kills him.

Menahem kills Shallum.

Pul king of Assyria comes against the land, and Menahem pays him silver to confirm the kingdom in his hand.

Assyria is now pressing into Israel's story.

Pekahiah reigns and is killed by Pekah.

Pekah reigns, and Tiglath-pileser king of Assyria takes cities and carries people captive.

Hoshea then conspires against Pekah and kills him.

The repeated pattern is exhausting on purpose.

The northern kingdom is not stable.

Power is seized by blood.

Sin is repeated.

Assyria grows stronger.

Judah also comes under pressure when the LORD begins to send Rezin and Pekah against Judah.

The day is moving toward national crisis.

## Second Kings 16

Second Kings 16 shows Ahaz of Judah.

He does not do right like David.

He walks in the way of the kings of Israel and even makes his son pass through the fire.

That is a horrifying sign of how deeply pagan practices have entered Judah.

When Rezin and Pekah come against Jerusalem, Ahaz does not seek the LORD like a faithful son of David.

He sends to Tiglath-pileser king of Assyria and says, I am thy servant and thy son.

He takes silver and gold from the house of the LORD and from the king's house and sends it as a present.

Ahaz is buying help from Assyria with temple treasure.

Then he sees an altar in Damascus and sends the pattern of it to Urijah the priest.

The altar is built in Jerusalem.

Ahaz moves the bronze altar and rearranges worship around the Assyrian-style altar.

This is more than bad taste.

It is spiritual compromise.

The king is reshaping worship under foreign influence.

The chapter shows Judah leaning on Assyria and bending worship away from the LORD's order.

## Second Kings 17

Second Kings 17 is the central chapter of the day.

Hoshea becomes king of Israel.

He becomes servant to Assyria, then rebels by seeking help from Egypt.

The king of Assyria besieges Samaria for three years.

Samaria falls.

Israel is carried away into Assyria.

Then the chapter explains why.

Israel sinned against the LORD their God, who brought them up out of Egypt.

They feared other gods.

They walked in the statutes of the nations.

They built high places.

They set up images and groves.

They burned incense in all the high places.

They served idols.

The LORD testified against Israel and Judah by prophets and seers, saying, Turn ye from your evil ways.

But they would not hear.

They hardened their necks.

They rejected His statutes and covenant.

They followed vanity and became vain.

That phrase is devastating.

People become like what they worship.

The chapter also says Judah did not keep the commandments either.

The warning is not only for the north.

After Israel is exiled, foreign peoples are settled in Samaria.

They fear the LORD and serve their own gods.

The chapter ends with mixed worship continuing.

They do not truly fear the LORD according to the covenant.

## Full Lesson Review

Day 91 shows a kingdom reaching the consequence of long rebellion.

Amaziah's pride wounds Judah.

Israel's throne becomes violent and unstable.

Assyria enters as a dominating power.

Ahaz compromises worship and seeks security through Assyria.

Samaria falls, and Israel is carried away.

Second Kings 17 makes the meaning clear: exile comes because Israel refused the LORD's word again and again.

The LORD warned.

The people would not hear.

The fall of Israel is not the failure of God's power.

It is the result of covenant rebellion.

## Slow Walk

Listen carefully to the patience of God in this day.

The LORD does not send Israel away without warning.

He testifies by prophets and seers.

He names the evil.

He calls them to turn.

That means judgment comes after refused mercy.

Also notice the danger of mixed trust.

Ahaz uses the temple treasury to buy Assyrian help.

He changes worship after seeing Damascus.

He is not simply making political decisions.

His politics are reshaping worship.

That is why the chapter matters for beginners.

False trust rarely stays private.

It eventually changes what people honor, fear, and imitate.

Day 91 asks the listener to take God's warnings seriously before collapse becomes visible.

## Final Guided Recap

Before the closing, gather the movement again.

Second Kings 14 shows mixed kingship.

Amaziah does right in some ways, but the high places remain, and pride leads him into a foolish fight with Israel.

Jeroboam the second does evil, yet God restores Israel's border because He sees the affliction of His people.

That combination is important.

God's mercy is not proof that the people are spiritually healthy.

Mercy is an invitation to return.

Second Kings 15 shows the northern kingdom unraveling.

Kings are murdered.

Conspiracies multiply.

Assyria enters the land.

Captivity begins before the final fall.

The chapter feels repetitive because sin has become repetitive.

The names change, but the rebellion remains.

Second Kings 16 shows Ahaz importing foreign dependence into Judah's worship.

He calls himself the servant and son of Assyria's king.

He takes temple treasure for political rescue.

He copies an altar from Damascus.

This is what fear does when it is not governed by trust in the LORD.

It changes worship.

Second Kings 17 explains the fall of Israel.

The chapter does not leave the reader guessing.

Israel sinned against the LORD who brought them out of Egypt.

They feared other gods.

They rejected covenant.

They would not hear.

So the northern kingdom falls to Assyria.

The lesson is solemn: the God who saves His people from bondage also calls His people to covenant faithfulness.

## Closing

As you finish Day 91, remember the phrase, they would not hear.

The LORD had spoken.

The people had been warned.

The tragedy of Israel's fall is that they kept refusing the God who had rescued them from Egypt.
