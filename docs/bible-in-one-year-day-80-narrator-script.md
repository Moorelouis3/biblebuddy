# Day 80 Narrator Script

## Opening

Welcome to Day 80 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are reading Second Samuel 16 through 19.

The title for today is Absalom's Rebellion and David's Grief.

This day follows David outside Jerusalem while Absalom takes the city.

Ziba meets David with supplies and a questionable report about Mephibosheth.

Shimei curses David and throws stones.

David refuses to answer the curse with immediate revenge.

Hushai enters Absalom's court and frustrates Ahithophel's counsel.

Absalom is defeated.

Joab kills Absalom against David's command.

David grieves deeply over his son.

Then David returns toward Jerusalem, but the kingdom is still fragile.

This day is full of political danger, family sorrow, and the cost of rebellion.

## Second Samuel 16

Second Samuel 16 begins when David is a little past the top of the hill.

Ziba, the servant of Mephibosheth, meets him with asses saddled, bread, raisins, summer fruits, and wine.

David asks what these mean.

Ziba says the asses are for the king's household, the bread and fruit are for the young men, and the wine is for the faint in the wilderness.

David asks where Mephibosheth is.

Ziba claims Mephibosheth stayed in Jerusalem, hoping the house of Israel would restore the kingdom of Saul to him.

David gives Ziba all that belonged to Mephibosheth.

Later the story will complicate this report.

For now, David is receiving information under pressure, and pressure can make judgment difficult.

Then David comes to Bahurim.

Shimei, a man of Saul's house, comes out cursing.

He casts stones at David and his servants.

He says, Come out, thou bloody man, and thou man of Belial.

He claims the LORD has returned upon David all the blood of Saul's house and delivered the kingdom into Absalom's hand.

Abishai wants to go over and take off Shimei's head.

David refuses.

He says if the LORD has told Shimei to curse, who shall say, Wherefore hast thou done so?

David says his own son seeks his life, so how much more this Benjamite.

He tells them to let Shimei curse, because the LORD may look on David's affliction and requite him good for the cursing.

This is David low and humbled.

He does not claim every word Shimei says is true.

But he refuses to answer humiliation with quick vengeance.

Shimei goes along the hillside, cursing, throwing stones, and casting dust.

David and the people come weary and refresh themselves.

Meanwhile Absalom enters Jerusalem.

Hushai comes to him and says, God save the king.

Absalom questions why Hushai is not with David.

Hushai answers carefully that he will serve the one whom the LORD, the people, and all Israel choose.

Then Absalom asks Ahithophel what to do.

Ahithophel tells him to go in to David's concubines, whom David left to keep the house.

Absalom does this in the sight of all Israel.

The counsel of Ahithophel in those days is treated as if a man inquired at the oracle of God.

That shows how dangerous his counsel is.

The chapter ends with shame in David's house becoming public, just as Nathan had warned.

## Second Samuel 17

Second Samuel 17 opens with Ahithophel proposing a direct attack.

He asks to choose twelve thousand men, arise, and pursue David that night.

He says he will come upon David while he is weary and weak-handed.

He will make the people afraid, strike only the king, and bring all the people back to Absalom.

The saying pleases Absalom and the elders.

But Absalom also asks for Hushai's counsel.

Hushai says Ahithophel's counsel is not good at that time.

He reminds Absalom that David and his men are mighty and chafed in their minds, like a bear robbed of her whelps.

He says David is a man of war and will not lodge with the people.

If some of Absalom's men fall at first, fear will spread.

Hushai advises gathering all Israel from Dan to Beersheba and Absalom going in person.

His counsel sounds bigger and more impressive.

But the narrator tells us the LORD appointed to defeat the good counsel of Ahithophel, so that the LORD might bring evil upon Absalom.

That is the key.

Politics are moving, but God is ruling.

Hushai sends word through Zadok and Abiathar.

Jonathan and Ahimaaz wait by Enrogel.

A maidservant goes to tell them, but a lad sees them and tells Absalom.

They hide in a well at Bahurim.

A woman covers the well and spreads ground corn over it.

Absalom's servants search but do not find them.

The messengers reach David and tell him to arise and pass quickly over the water.

David and all the people pass over Jordan by morning.

Ahithophel sees that his counsel is not followed.

He saddles his ass, goes home, puts his household in order, and hangs himself.

Absalom passes over Jordan with the men of Israel.

He sets Amasa over the host instead of Joab.

David comes to Mahanaim.

Shobi, Machir, and Barzillai bring beds, basins, vessels, wheat, barley, flour, parched corn, beans, lentils, honey, butter, sheep, and cheese.

They say the people are hungry, weary, and thirsty in the wilderness.

The chapter begins with murderous counsel and ends with provision for weary people.

The LORD preserves David through counsel, messengers, hidden loyalty, and practical gifts.

## Second Samuel 18

Second Samuel 18 begins with David numbering the people and setting captains over thousands and hundreds.

David wants to go out with them, but the people say he should not.

They say David is worth ten thousand of them.

David stays by the gate.

He commands Joab, Abishai, and Ittai to deal gently for his sake with the young man Absalom.

All the people hear the command.

The battle happens in the wood of Ephraim.

The people of Israel are slain before David's servants.

The wood devours more people than the sword.

Absalom meets David's servants.

He rides upon a mule, and the mule goes under the thick boughs of a great oak.

Absalom's head catches hold of the oak.

He is taken up between heaven and earth, and the mule goes away.

A man sees it and tells Joab.

Joab asks why he did not kill Absalom.

The man says he would not put forth his hand against the king's son for a thousand shekels, because everyone heard David's command.

Joab takes three darts and thrusts them through Absalom's heart while he is yet alive in the oak.

Ten young men who bear Joab's armour surround Absalom and slay him.

They cast Absalom into a great pit in the wood and lay a very great heap of stones upon him.

All Israel flees.

The chapter notes that Absalom had reared a pillar for himself because he had no son to keep his name in remembrance.

His monument remains, but his rebellion ends in shame.

Ahimaaz wants to run with news.

Joab sends Cushi instead.

Eventually both run.

David sits between the two gates and waits.

The watchman sees a runner.

David hopes for good news.

Ahimaaz arrives and says all is well, but avoids telling clearly about Absalom.

Cushi arrives and says that the enemies of the king and all who rise against him to do hurt should be as that young man is.

David understands.

The king is much moved.

He goes up to the chamber over the gate and weeps.

He says, O my son Absalom, my son, my son Absalom.

Would God I had died for thee, O Absalom, my son, my son.

This is the cry of a father, not only a king.

The rebellion is defeated, but David is shattered.

## Second Samuel 19

Second Samuel 19 begins with Joab being told that the king weeps and mourns for Absalom.

The victory that day turns into mourning for all the people.

The people steal into the city like ashamed people fleeing battle.

Joab confronts David sharply.

He says David has shamed the faces of his servants who saved his life, his family, and his kingdom.

Joab says David seems to love his enemies and hate his friends.

He warns David to arise and speak comfortably to the servants, or not one will remain with him that night.

David arises and sits in the gate.

The people come before him.

Then the tribes of Israel argue about bringing the king back.

David sends to Zadok and Abiathar, asking why the elders of Judah should be last to bring the king back.

He also tells Amasa that he will be captain before him instead of Joab.

Judah's heart is bowed as one man, and they send word for David to return.

Shimei comes down with the men of Judah to meet David.

He confesses his wrong and asks David not to impute iniquity to him.

Abishai wants Shimei put to death.

David refuses, saying no man shall be put to death that day in Israel.

Mephibosheth also comes down.

He has not dressed his feet, trimmed his beard, or washed his clothes since David departed.

David asks why he did not go with him.

Mephibosheth says Ziba deceived him and slandered him.

David divides the land between Ziba and Mephibosheth.

Mephibosheth says Ziba may take all, since the king has come again in peace.

Barzillai comes down too.

He is eighty years old and had provided for David at Mahanaim.

David invites him to come to Jerusalem, but Barzillai says he is too old to enjoy court life.

He asks that Chimham go instead.

David blesses Barzillai and agrees.

The chapter ends with tension between Judah and Israel.

Israel asks why Judah stole the king away.

Judah answers harshly.

The words of the men of Judah are fiercer than the words of the men of Israel.

David is back, but unity is fragile.

## Full Lesson Review

Day 80 shows rebellion collapsing, but not without grief.

David is cursed by Shimei.

David is deceived or at least pressured by Ziba's report.

Absalom takes Jerusalem.

Ahithophel's counsel is defeated because the LORD appoints it.

David is preserved through messengers and loyal provision.

Absalom dies in the wood.

David grieves his son.

Then David returns, showing mercy to Shimei, hearing Mephibosheth, honoring Barzillai, and trying to reunite the kingdom.

But the cracks remain.

## Key Connections

First, David's humility under Shimei's curse shows a humbled king under God's discipline.

Second, Hushai's counsel shows that God can rule through ordinary strategy.

Third, Absalom's death shows the bitter end of stolen hearts and proud rebellion.

Fourth, David's grief shows that victory over rebellion can still feel like loss inside a family.

## Guided Listening Review

Day 80 keeps showing David as both king and father.

That tension is painful.

As king, David must survive Absalom's rebellion.

As father, David loves the son who is trying to take his throne.

Shimei's cursing in chapter 16 matters because David does not respond like a proud ruler trying to silence every insult.

Abishai wants quick violence.

David refuses.

David is not saying Shimei is righteous.

He is saying the moment belongs under God's hand.

The humbled king is willing to let the LORD judge the insult.

Then the story moves into counsel.

Ahithophel's plan is dangerous because it is strategically strong.

If Absalom follows it quickly, David may be destroyed while weary.

Hushai's counsel sounds grander and slower.

The narrator tells us why it works.

The LORD appoints the defeat of Ahithophel's good counsel.

God's providence is working through speeches, timing, messengers, and decisions.

The hidden messengers in the well are another reminder.

God's rescue may look like ordinary loyalty and quick thinking.

Then Absalom's death brings the rebellion to its bitter end.

His beauty and public image cannot save him.

He is caught in the oak, suspended between heaven and earth.

Joab kills him despite David's command.

The victory is real, but it does not feel clean.

David's cry over Absalom is one of the most human cries in the Old Testament.

He does not say, Good, the rebel is gone.

He says, My son, my son.

The grief does not undo Absalom's guilt, but it shows the cost of sin inside a family.

Chapter 19 then forces David back into public responsibility.

Joab's rebuke is harsh, but David must sit in the gate.

The people need the king.

David returns with mercy toward Shimei, complexity around Mephibosheth and Ziba, gratitude toward Barzillai, and tension between Judah and Israel.

The rebellion is over, but the kingdom is not magically healthy.

The return is fragile.

That is often how restoration feels in Scripture: real, but tender; hopeful, but not yet whole.

## Chapter Takeaways

From Second Samuel 16, remember David under humiliation.

Ziba's report requires discernment.

Shimei's curse requires patience.

Absalom's public sin shows that Nathan's warning is still unfolding.

From Second Samuel 17, remember that the LORD can defeat dangerous counsel without thunder from heaven.

He uses Hushai's words.

He uses hidden messengers.

He uses a woman covering a well.

He uses provision from loyal friends.

Providence often moves through ordinary faithfulness.

From Second Samuel 18, remember that Absalom's rebellion ends in the trees.

The son who stole hearts and built an image for himself cannot save himself.

Joab's action ends the threat, but it also violates David's command.

Victory and grief arrive together.

From Second Samuel 19, remember that returning is complicated.

David must speak to the people.

He must respond to Shimei.

He must listen to Mephibosheth.

He must honor Barzillai.

He must face tension between Judah and Israel.

The battle is over, but the work of wise rule continues.

This day teaches that sin fractures relationships in layers.

One victory does not instantly heal every wound.

Still, David returns.

Mercy is shown.

Provision is remembered.

And the kingdom keeps moving, even while it limps.

## Slow Recap

If you remember one movement from Day 80, remember this: David is preserved, but he is not untouched.

The LORD protects David from Absalom's rebellion.

Ahithophel's counsel is defeated.

The messengers get through.

The army wins.

Absalom dies.

David returns.

But preservation does not mean the day is painless.

David hears curses.

David receives uncertain reports.

David is publicly shamed by Absalom's actions.

David waits for news from the battle.

David loses his son.

David must be pulled back into public leadership while his heart is broken.

That combination helps us read David honestly.

He is not only a symbol of kingship.

He is a father grieving a rebel son.

He is a ruler responsible for a wounded people.

He is a sinner living through consequences.

He is also still the LORD's anointed king, preserved by God's purpose.

The return in chapter 19 is therefore not a neat ending.

Shimei needs mercy.

Mephibosheth and Ziba require discernment.

Barzillai deserves honor.

Judah and Israel are tense.

The kingdom is back under David, but the fractures are still visible.

The Bible is preparing us to long for a better Son of David, one whose victory heals more deeply than David's return could.

## Final Meditation

David's fragile return is one of the most honest parts of the chapter.

Many stories would end the moment the rebel dies.

The Bible keeps going.

David must face the people who supported him, the people who cursed him, the people who may have been misrepresented, and the tribes now arguing over who has the greater claim to bring him home.

Victory does not erase the work of wisdom.

A king has to do more than survive a battle.

He has to govern the aftermath.

He has to decide when to show mercy.

He has to listen when stories conflict.

He has to honor old faithfulness, like Barzillai's provision.

And he has to carry grief while still leading.

The LORD has preserved David, but the need for healing remains.

## Closing

As you finish Day 80, remember the sentence David cries over Absalom.

Would God I had died for thee.

The king survives, but the father grieves.

David returns to Jerusalem, but the kingdom is tender and tense.

The story keeps teaching us that sin fractures what only God can heal.
