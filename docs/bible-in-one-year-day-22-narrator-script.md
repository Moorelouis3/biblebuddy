# Bible In One Year - Day 22 Narrator Script

## Day 22 - God Hears Israel's Cry

**Reading:** Exodus 1 through 4
**Bible text:** King James Version, Exodus 1-4
**Audio style:** Cinematic Bible narration with Scripture reading and grounded explanation
**Target length:** 32-40 minutes

## Audio Direction For Future Days

Every Scripture section must be introduced out loud before the narrator reads the Bible text.

Example:

"Exodus chapter 1, verses 1 through 7."

Then the narrator reads the Bible text.

Do not skip the reference line. It helps the listener follow the journey while driving, walking, cleaning, or listening away from the screen.

## Opening

Yesterday, Genesis ended with Joseph in Egypt.

Not young Joseph in the pit.

Not Joseph standing before Pharaoh.

Not Joseph forgiving his brothers.

The last image was quieter than that.

Joseph was old.

Joseph died.

And his body was placed in a coffin in Egypt.

But before he died, Joseph made his family promise something.

He told them, God will surely visit you.

That sentence is the bridge from Genesis into Exodus.

Genesis ends with a coffin, but it does not end with despair.

It ends with a promise waiting to move.

So when Day 22 begins, we are not starting a random new book.

We are walking through the doorway Genesis left open.

The family of Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, and Joseph is still in Egypt.

But they are not just a small family anymore.

They have multiplied.

They have grown.

The promise God made to Abraham is becoming visible.

God said Abraham's descendants would become many.

God said they would be strangers in a land that was not theirs.

God said they would be afflicted.

And God said He would bring them out.

Now Exodus begins with that promise under pressure.

Because a new Pharaoh rises.

He does not know Joseph.

He does not honor the old story of rescue.

He does not look at Israel and see God's covenant.

He looks at Israel and sees a threat.

And fear turns into control.

Control turns into slavery.

Slavery turns into cruelty.

Cruelty turns into an order to kill Hebrew sons.

This is how Exodus opens.

Not with a hero walking on stage.

Not with Moses lifting a staff.

Not with the Red Sea splitting.

It opens with pressure.

With labor.

With bitter bondage.

With mothers trying to keep babies alive.

With a child placed into the river that Pharaoh meant for death.

And underneath all of it, the question is simple:

Does God hear His people when they cry?

Exodus answers yes.

But it answers slowly.

It answers through midwives.

Through a mother.

Through a sister watching from a distance.

Through Pharaoh's own daughter.

Through Moses running before he is ready.

Through years in Midian.

Through a burning bush in the wilderness.

Through the name of God.

And through a man who keeps telling God why he is not enough.

Day 22 is the beginning of deliverance.

But before deliverance becomes public, it begins hidden.

Let's begin.

## Exodus 1:1-7

Exodus chapter 1, verses 1 through 7.

**Scripture**

Now these are the names of the children of Israel, which came into Egypt; every man and his household came with Jacob.

Reuben, Simeon, Levi, and Judah,

Issachar, Zebulun, and Benjamin,

Dan, and Naphtali, Gad, and Asher.

And all the souls that came out of the loins of Jacob were seventy souls: for Joseph was in Egypt already.

And Joseph died, and all his brethren, and all that generation.

And the children of Israel were fruitful, and increased abundantly, and multiplied, and waxed exceeding mighty; and the land was filled with them.

**Narrator**

Exodus opens with names.

That can feel simple, but it is important.

The book begins by reminding us that the people in Egypt have a story.

They are not a crowd with no past.

They are the children of Israel.

They are the family of Jacob.

They are connected to Reuben, Simeon, Levi, Judah, and the rest of the sons we have been following through Genesis.

This is Abraham's family.

This is Isaac's family.

This is Jacob's family.

This is the family Joseph preserved during famine.

So Exodus is not beginning from zero.

It is continuing the promise.

Then we hear that Joseph died.

His brothers died.

That whole generation died.

Genesis is now in the past.

The people who personally remembered Joseph are gone.

But notice what does not die.

The promise does not die.

The covenant does not die.

God's faithfulness does not die.

The children of Israel are fruitful.

They increase abundantly.

They multiply.

They become exceeding mighty.

And the land is filled with them.

That language reaches all the way back to Genesis 1.

Be fruitful.

Multiply.

Fill the earth.

It also reaches back to God's promises to Abraham.

God told Abraham his descendants would be as many as the stars.

God told Jacob, I will make of thee a great nation there.

And now, in Egypt, that is happening.

Before Exodus shows us oppression, it shows us blessing.

That order matters.

Pharaoh's cruelty will not appear because Israel is weak.

It will appear because Israel is growing.

The enemy is not reacting to emptiness.

He is reacting to fruitfulness.

And that is a real-life lesson too.

Sometimes pressure increases around the very place where God is growing something.

Not every hard season means God has stopped working.

Sometimes the hardness is proof that something holy is pushing through the ground.

Israel is in Egypt.

Joseph is dead.

The old generation is gone.

But God's promise is alive.

## Exodus 1:8-14

Exodus chapter 1, verses 8 through 14.

**Scripture**

Now there arose up a new king over Egypt, which knew not Joseph.

And he said unto his people, Behold, the people of the children of Israel are more and mightier than we:

Come on, let us deal wisely with them; lest they multiply, and it come to pass, that, when there falleth out any war, they join also unto our enemies, and fight against us, and so get them up out of the land.

Therefore they did set over them taskmasters to afflict them with their burdens. And they built for Pharaoh treasure cities, Pithom and Raamses.

But the more they afflicted them, the more they multiplied and grew. And they were grieved because of the children of Israel.

And the Egyptians made the children of Israel to serve with rigour:

And they made their lives bitter with hard bondage, in morter, and in brick, and in all manner of service in the field: all their service, wherein they made them serve, was with rigour.

**Narrator**

Now the atmosphere changes.

A new king rises over Egypt.

And Exodus says he knew not Joseph.

That does not only mean he forgot a name.

It means Egypt no longer honors the story of Joseph.

Joseph saved Egypt during famine.

Joseph helped preserve the nation.

Joseph served Pharaoh with wisdom.

But now the memory of that grace is gone.

A new ruler looks at Joseph's people and sees danger.

This is what fear does.

Fear rewrites history.

Fear looks at neighbors and sees enemies.

Fear looks at growth and sees threat.

Fear looks at blessing and says, We need to control this.

Pharaoh says, The children of Israel are more and mightier than we.

Then he says, Let us deal wisely with them.

That phrase sounds calm.

It sounds strategic.

It sounds like leadership language.

But the wisdom of Pharaoh is not wisdom.

It is oppression dressed up as policy.

He turns fear into a plan.

He turns suspicion into a national program.

He sets taskmasters over the people.

He afflicts them with burdens.

He forces them to build treasure cities.

Pithom.

Raamses.

Brick.

Mortar.

Fields.

Rigour.

Exodus wants us to feel that slavery is not an idea on a page.

It is weight on the back.

It is torn hands.

It is exhaustion.

It is waking up already tired.

It is your body being used to build someone else's empire.

Their lives were made bitter.

That word matters.

Bitterness is what happens when suffering gets into the taste of daily life.

But then Exodus gives us the line Pharaoh cannot control.

The more they afflicted them, the more they multiplied and grew.

Pharaoh can increase pressure.

He cannot cancel promise.

He can make life bitter.

He cannot stop God from keeping His word.

This is one of the first great tensions of Exodus.

Egypt is strong.

Pharaoh is powerful.

The labor system is brutal.

But God is working underneath the surface.

Oppression is real.

But oppression is not sovereign.

## Exodus 1:15-22

Exodus chapter 1, verses 15 through 22.

**Scripture**

And the king of Egypt spake to the Hebrew midwives, of which the name of the one was Shiphrah, and the name of the other Puah:

And he said, When ye do the office of a midwife to the Hebrew women, and see them upon the stools; if it be a son, then ye shall kill him: but if it be a daughter, then she shall live.

But the midwives feared God, and did not as the king of Egypt commanded them, but saved the men children alive.

And the king of Egypt called for the midwives, and said unto them, Why have ye done this thing, and have saved the men children alive?

And the midwives said unto Pharaoh, Because the Hebrew women are not as the Egyptian women; for they are lively, and are delivered ere the midwives come in unto them.

Therefore God dealt well with the midwives: and the people multiplied, and waxed very mighty.

And it came to pass, because the midwives feared God, that he made them houses.

And Pharaoh charged all his people, saying, Every son that is born ye shall cast into the river, and every daughter ye shall save alive.

**Narrator**

Pharaoh's fear now becomes darker.

Forced labor was not enough.

He moves from oppression to attempted genocide.

He targets the sons.

He wants to cut off Israel's future.

If the baby is a daughter, she may live.

If the baby is a son, kill him.

That is the command.

And then Exodus does something powerful.

It names the midwives.

Shiphrah.

Puah.

Pharaoh is the king of Egypt, but in this section, the women are remembered by name.

The empire looks large.

The women look small.

But Scripture honors the ones who fear God.

The midwives refuse Pharaoh's command.

They save the boys alive.

The text says they feared God.

That phrase is not about being terrified of God in a shallow way.

It means God carries more weight than Pharaoh.

It means God's authority matters more than the king's threat.

It means when Pharaoh commands death and God values life, they choose God.

This is courage in a hidden place.

No army.

No stage.

No microphone.

No public applause.

Just women standing between a death command and newborn children.

Sometimes the first resistance to evil happens in ordinary rooms by people history would usually ignore.

And God sees it.

God deals well with the midwives.

The people multiply.

The promise keeps growing.

But Pharaoh hardens further.

He expands the command to all his people.

Every Hebrew son is to be thrown into the river.

The Nile becomes part of Pharaoh's death machine.

And that is exactly where Exodus 2 is going to take us.

Into the river.

Into the place meant for death.

And into the hidden beginning of deliverance.

## Exodus 2:1-10

Exodus chapter 2, verses 1 through 10.

**Scripture**

And there went a man of the house of Levi, and took to wife a daughter of Levi.

And the woman conceived, and bare a son: and when she saw him that he was a goodly child, she hid him three months.

And when she could not longer hide him, she took for him an ark of bulrushes, and daubed it with slime and with pitch, and put the child therein; and she laid it in the flags by the river's brink.

And his sister stood afar off, to wit what would be done to him.

And the daughter of Pharaoh came down to wash herself at the river; and her maidens walked along by the river's side; and when she saw the ark among the flags, she sent her maid to fetch it.

And when she had opened it, she saw the child: and, behold, the babe wept. And she had compassion on him, and said, This is one of the Hebrews' children.

Then said his sister to Pharaoh's daughter, Shall I go and call to thee a nurse of the Hebrew women, that she may nurse the child for thee?

And Pharaoh's daughter said to her, Go. And the maid went and called the child's mother.

And Pharaoh's daughter said unto her, Take this child away, and nurse it for me, and I will give thee thy wages. And the woman took the child, and nursed it.

And the child grew, and she brought him unto Pharaoh's daughter, and he became her son. And she called his name Moses: and she said, Because I drew him out of the water.

**Narrator**

Now the camera narrows.

Exodus moves from the nation to one household.

A man from Levi.

A woman from Levi.

A child born under Pharaoh's death sentence.

This baby is born into a world where being a Hebrew son means danger.

Before he can speak, the empire has already marked him for death.

His mother sees that he is a goodly child.

That phrase is tender.

It echoes the language of goodness that runs back into Genesis.

In a world where Pharaoh sees Hebrew sons as threats, this mother sees life.

She hides him for three months.

Try to feel that.

Every cry could expose him.

Every footstep outside could mean danger.

Every day is protection and fear mixed together.

Then she can hide him no longer.

So she makes an ark of bulrushes.

That word ark should make your ears wake up.

Genesis had an ark too.

Noah's ark carried life through waters of judgment.

Now this little ark carries one child through waters Pharaoh meant for death.

She places him by the river.

His sister stands at a distance and watches.

And then Pharaoh's daughter comes down to the water.

This is the scene turning in a way no human planner could control.

The daughter of the man ordering Hebrew sons to die finds a Hebrew son.

She opens the basket.

The baby cries.

And she has compassion.

Compassion enters the story from inside Pharaoh's own house.

Then the child's sister steps forward with quick wisdom.

Should I call a Hebrew woman to nurse the child for you?

Pharaoh's daughter says, Go.

And the child's own mother is brought back.

Now listen to the irony.

Pharaoh ordered Hebrew sons to die.

But Pharaoh's household protects this Hebrew son.

Pharaoh wanted the river to swallow him.

But the river becomes the place where he is drawn out.

Pharaoh wanted mothers robbed of sons.

But this mother is paid to nurse her own child.

God is already turning the story.

Quietly.

Beautifully.

Not yet with plagues.

Not yet with thunder.

Not yet with a staff raised over the sea.

But with a basket, a sister, a princess, a mother's arms, and a baby named Moses.

His name is connected to being drawn out of the water.

And later, Moses will lead Israel through water.

His personal rescue becomes a preview of the nation's rescue.

The deliverer is first delivered.

## Exodus 2:11-25

Exodus chapter 2, verses 11 through 25.

**Scripture**

And it came to pass in those days, when Moses was grown, that he went out unto his brethren, and looked on their burdens: and he spied an Egyptian smiting an Hebrew, one of his brethren.

And he looked this way and that way, and when he saw that there was no man, he slew the Egyptian, and hid him in the sand.

And when he went out the second day, behold, two men of the Hebrews strove together: and he said to him that did the wrong, Wherefore smitest thou thy fellow?

And he said, Who made thee a prince and a judge over us? intendest thou to kill me, as thou killedst the Egyptian? And Moses feared, and said, Surely this thing is known.

Now when Pharaoh heard this thing, he sought to slay Moses. But Moses fled from the face of Pharaoh, and dwelt in the land of Midian: and he sat down by a well.

Now the priest of Midian had seven daughters: and they came and drew water, and filled the troughs to water their father's flock.

And the shepherds came and drove them away: but Moses stood up and helped them, and watered their flock.

And when they came to Reuel their father, he said, How is it that ye are come so soon to day?

And they said, An Egyptian delivered us out of the hand of the shepherds, and also drew water enough for us, and watered the flock.

And he said unto his daughters, And where is he? why is it that ye have left the man? call him, that he may eat bread.

And Moses was content to dwell with the man: and he gave Moses Zipporah his daughter.

And she bare him a son, and he called his name Gershom: for he said, I have been a stranger in a strange land.

And it came to pass in process of time, that the king of Egypt died: and the children of Israel sighed by reason of the bondage, and they cried, and their cry came up unto God by reason of the bondage.

And God heard their groaning, and God remembered his covenant with Abraham, with Isaac, and with Jacob.

And God looked upon the children of Israel, and God had respect unto them.

**Narrator**

Now Moses is grown.

He has been raised in Pharaoh's world, but he goes out to his brethren.

That phrase matters.

He sees the Hebrews as his people.

He looks on their burdens.

He does not look away.

He sees an Egyptian beating a Hebrew.

The injustice is real.

The suffering is real.

But Moses responds before he has been sent by God.

He looks this way and that way.

He sees no one.

He kills the Egyptian and hides him in the sand.

This is not the Exodus yet.

This is not deliverance by God's hand.

This is Moses acting out of anger, urgency, and maybe a sincere desire for justice, but not yet under God's command.

The next day, he sees two Hebrew men fighting.

He tries to intervene.

And one of them says, Who made thee a prince and a judge over us?

That question cuts deep.

Moses fears the matter is known.

Pharaoh hears.

Moses runs.

The deliverer becomes a fugitive.

The man who was drawn out of water now disappears into the wilderness.

He goes to Midian.

And he sits down by a well.

That well scene should remind us of Genesis.

Isaac's wife Rebekah is connected to a well.

Jacob meets Rachel near a well.

Now Moses arrives at a well.

In the Bible, wells often become places where the next season begins.

Moses helps the daughters of Reuel when shepherds drive them away.

Again, we see something in him.

He reacts to oppression.

He protects the vulnerable.

But now he is far from Egypt.

He marries Zipporah.

He has a son.

He names him Gershom, because he says, I have been a stranger in a strange land.

That is Moses' life at this point.

Hebrew by birth.

Raised in Egypt.

Living in Midian.

Not fully at home anywhere.

Then the story lifts our eyes back to Egypt.

The king dies.

But the suffering does not die.

Israel sighs.

Israel cries.

Their cry comes up to God.

And then we get four verbs that change everything.

God heard.

God remembered.

God looked.

God had respect unto them.

That does not mean God forgot and suddenly remembered like a human.

In the Bible, when God remembers His covenant, it means He is about to act in faithfulness to what He promised.

He remembers Abraham.

He remembers Isaac.

He remembers Jacob.

He hears the groaning of their descendants.

Before Moses hears the call, God hears the cry.

Before Pharaoh sees plagues, God sees the people.

Before Israel sees freedom, God knows their suffering.

That is the heartbeat of Exodus.

God is not absent just because deliverance has not arrived yet.

## Exodus 3:1-12

Exodus chapter 3, verses 1 through 12.

**Scripture**

Now Moses kept the flock of Jethro his father in law, the priest of Midian: and he led the flock to the backside of the desert, and came to the mountain of God, even to Horeb.

And the angel of the LORD appeared unto him in a flame of fire out of the midst of a bush: and he looked, and, behold, the bush burned with fire, and the bush was not consumed.

And Moses said, I will now turn aside, and see this great sight, why the bush is not burnt.

And when the LORD saw that he turned aside to see, God called unto him out of the midst of the bush, and said, Moses, Moses. And he said, Here am I.

And he said, Draw not nigh hither: put off thy shoes from off thy feet, for the place whereon thou standest is holy ground.

Moreover he said, I am the God of thy father, the God of Abraham, the God of Isaac, and the God of Jacob. And Moses hid his face; for he was afraid to look upon God.

And the LORD said, I have surely seen the affliction of my people which are in Egypt, and have heard their cry by reason of their taskmasters; for I know their sorrows;

And I am come down to deliver them out of the hand of the Egyptians, and to bring them up out of that land unto a good land and a large, unto a land flowing with milk and honey; unto the place of the Canaanites, and the Hittites, and the Amorites, and the Perizzites, and the Hivites, and the Jebusites.

Now therefore, behold, the cry of the children of Israel is come unto me: and I have also seen the oppression wherewith the Egyptians oppress them.

Come now therefore, and I will send thee unto Pharaoh, that thou mayest bring forth my people the children of Israel out of Egypt.

And Moses said unto God, Who am I, that I should go unto Pharaoh, and that I should bring forth the children of Israel out of Egypt?

And he said, Certainly I will be with thee; and this shall be a token unto thee, that I have sent thee: When thou hast brought forth the people out of Egypt, ye shall serve God upon this mountain.

**Narrator**

Moses is not in a palace.

He is not standing in a court.

He is not leading an army.

He is keeping sheep.

Years have passed.

The man who once acted quickly in Egypt is now hidden in the wilderness.

And that is where God meets him.

This matters.

God often forms people in places that feel invisible.

Moses may think his public story is over.

But the wilderness is not wasted.

He leads the flock to Horeb, the mountain of God.

Then he sees a bush burning with fire, but not consumed.

The fire is real.

The bush is burning.

But it is not being destroyed.

That image is powerful.

God's presence is holy fire.

But His presence does not have to consume what He chooses to preserve.

Moses turns aside.

And when the Lord sees that Moses turns aside, God calls his name.

Moses.

Moses.

There is something intimate about that.

The cry of the nation has reached God.

But God calls one man by name.

Moses answers, Here am I.

Then God tells him to remove his shoes.

The ground is holy.

Not because the dirt itself is magical.

The ground is holy because God is there.

Then God identifies Himself.

I am the God of thy father.

The God of Abraham.

The God of Isaac.

The God of Jacob.

Exodus is anchored in Genesis.

The God speaking from the bush is the God of the promises.

The God who called Abraham is now calling Moses.

The God who promised land and descendants is now moving to rescue those descendants.

Then God says He has seen.

He has heard.

He knows.

And He has come down to deliver.

That phrase, I am come down, is massive.

The same God who came down to see Babel now comes down to rescue Israel.

At Babel, human pride tried to build upward and make a name.

Here, God comes down to deliver a people with no power to free themselves.

Then comes the shock.

God says, I will send thee unto Pharaoh.

Moses hears the mission and asks the question most of us would ask.

Who am I?

Who am I to go to Pharaoh?

Who am I to bring Israel out?

God's answer is not, Moses, you are impressive.

It is not, Moses, believe in yourself.

It is not, Moses, you have hidden leadership potential.

God says, Certainly I will be with thee.

That is the center.

The mission does not rest on Moses' self-image.

It rests on God's presence.

When God calls someone, the most important question is not, Who am I?

The deeper question is, Who is with me?

## Exodus 3:13-22

Exodus chapter 3, verses 13 through 22.

**Scripture**

And Moses said unto God, Behold, when I come unto the children of Israel, and shall say unto them, The God of your fathers hath sent me unto you; and they shall say to me, What is his name? what shall I say unto them?

And God said unto Moses, I AM THAT I AM: and he said, Thus shalt thou say unto the children of Israel, I AM hath sent me unto you.

And God said moreover unto Moses, Thus shalt thou say unto the children of Israel, The LORD God of your fathers, the God of Abraham, the God of Isaac, and the God of Jacob, hath sent me unto you: this is my name for ever, and this is my memorial unto all generations.

Go, and gather the elders of Israel together, and say unto them, The LORD God of your fathers, the God of Abraham, of Isaac, and of Jacob, appeared unto me, saying, I have surely visited you, and seen that which is done to you in Egypt:

And I have said, I will bring you up out of the affliction of Egypt unto the land of the Canaanites, and the Hittites, and the Amorites, and the Perizzites, and the Hivites, and the Jebusites, unto a land flowing with milk and honey.

And they shall hearken to thy voice: and thou shalt come, thou and the elders of Israel, unto the king of Egypt, and ye shall say unto him, The LORD God of the Hebrews hath met with us: and now let us go, we beseech thee, three days' journey into the wilderness, that we may sacrifice to the LORD our God.

And I am sure that the king of Egypt will not let you go, no, not by a mighty hand.

And I will stretch out my hand, and smite Egypt with all my wonders which I will do in the midst thereof: and after that he will let you go.

And I will give this people favour in the sight of the Egyptians: and it shall come to pass, that, when ye go, ye shall not go empty:

But every woman shall borrow of her neighbour, and of her that sojourneth in her house, jewels of silver, and jewels of gold, and raiment: and ye shall put them upon your sons, and upon your daughters; and ye shall spoil the Egyptians.

**Narrator**

Moses asks the question of the name.

When I go to Israel, what do I say?

If they ask, What is His name, what shall I tell them?

This is not small.

Moses is being sent to suffering people.

They have been crushed by labor.

They have watched sons die.

They have cried under bondage.

They do not need vague religion.

They need to know who has heard them.

God says, I AM THAT I AM.

I AM has sent me unto you.

This name is deep.

It speaks of God's self-existence.

God is not created.

God is not dependent.

God is not one more power inside the Egyptian system.

He is not waiting for Pharaoh's permission to be God.

He is.

He always is.

He will be faithful to who He is.

Then God ties that name to the covenant.

The LORD God of your fathers.

The God of Abraham.

The God of Isaac.

The God of Jacob.

So the name is not abstract.

It belongs to the God who made promises.

The God who walked with their fathers.

The God who said Joseph was right:

God will surely visit you.

Now God tells Moses what to say.

I have surely visited you.

That is the phrase Joseph died trusting.

Now it is becoming reality.

God tells Moses that Israel will listen.

But Pharaoh will not.

This is important because God does not send Moses with naive expectations.

He tells him the conflict will be hard.

The king of Egypt will resist.

Deliverance will require God's mighty hand.

God will stretch out His hand.

God will strike Egypt with wonders.

And after that, Pharaoh will let them go.

God also says Israel will not leave empty.

That matters because slavery takes.

Egypt has taken labor.

Taken years.

Taken children.

Taken dignity.

But God says the exploited people will walk out with provision.

The Exodus will not be a quiet escape by the powerless.

It will be God confronting empire, remembering covenant, and bringing His people out with a strong hand.

The God who says I AM is not only naming Himself.

He is announcing that Pharaoh is not ultimate.

Egypt is not ultimate.

Bondage is not ultimate.

I AM is.

## Exodus 4:1-17

Exodus chapter 4, verses 1 through 17.

**Scripture**

And the LORD said unto him, What is that in thine hand? And he said, A rod.

And he said, Cast it on the ground. And he cast it on the ground, and it became a serpent; and Moses fled from before it.

And the LORD said unto Moses, Put forth thine hand, and take it by the tail. And he put forth his hand, and caught it, and it became a rod in his hand:

And he said, O my Lord, send, I pray thee, by the hand of him whom thou wilt send.

And the anger of the LORD was kindled against Moses, and he said, Is not Aaron the Levite thy brother? I know that he can speak well. And also, behold, he cometh forth to meet thee: and when he seeth thee, he will be glad in his heart.

And thou shalt speak unto him, and put words in his mouth: and I will be with thy mouth, and with his mouth, and will teach you what ye shall do.

And he shall be thy spokesman unto the people: and he shall be, even he shall be to thee instead of a mouth, and thou shalt be to him instead of God.

And thou shalt take this rod in thine hand, wherewith thou shalt do signs.

**Narrator**

Moses keeps objecting.

First, Who am I?

Now, What if they do not believe me?

That is honest.

He is not walking back into Egypt with easy confidence.

He is going back to the people who once rejected his attempt to intervene.

He is going back to the place where Pharaoh wanted him dead.

So God gives signs.

The staff becomes a serpent.

Moses runs from it.

Then God tells him to take it by the tail, and it becomes a staff again.

That staff will matter later.

It will be lifted.

It will stretch over waters.

It will become a visible sign that Moses is not acting alone.

Then God gives another sign.

Moses' hand becomes leprous.

Then it is restored.

God is showing authority over the body, over disease, over what looks unclean and impossible.

Then the water of the Nile can become blood on dry ground.

That sign reaches straight into Egypt's power.

Remember, the Nile was the place Pharaoh used for death.

The Nile was Egypt's life source.

God is showing that He has authority over the river too.

Then Moses brings up his speech.

I am not eloquent.

I am slow of speech.

I am slow of tongue.

And God answers with one of the strongest questions in this section.

Who made man's mouth?

That question is not cruel.

It is grounding.

Moses is staring at his limitation.

God points him back to the Creator.

The One who made the mouth can be with the mouth.

God says, I will be with thy mouth, and teach thee what thou shalt say.

That is the promise.

Not, Moses, you will suddenly feel powerful.

Not, Moses, your insecurity will disappear overnight.

But, I will be with you.

Still, Moses says, Send someone else.

And the anger of the Lord is kindled.

That matters.

God is patient with fear, but fear cannot be allowed to become permanent refusal.

Even then, God provides Aaron.

Aaron can speak.

Aaron will meet Moses.

Aaron will help.

God does not cancel the mission because Moses feels weak.

And God does not pretend Moses has no weakness.

He provides help.

This is so real to life.

Sometimes God calls you into something bigger than your confidence.

And the answer is not always that you suddenly become fearless.

Sometimes the answer is presence.

Sometimes the answer is help.

Sometimes the answer is a staff in your hand and a brother on the road.

But the call still stands.

## Exodus 4:18-31

Exodus chapter 4, verses 18 through 31.

**Scripture**

And Moses went and returned to Jethro his father in law, and said unto him, Let me go, I pray thee, and return unto my brethren which are in Egypt, and see whether they be yet alive. And Jethro said to Moses, Go in peace.

And the LORD said unto Moses in Midian, Go, return into Egypt: for all the men are dead which sought thy life.

And Moses took his wife and his sons, and set them upon an ass, and he returned to the land of Egypt: and Moses took the rod of God in his hand.

And the LORD said unto Moses, When thou goest to return into Egypt, see that thou do all those wonders before Pharaoh, which I have put in thine hand: but I will harden his heart, that he shall not let the people go.

And thou shalt say unto Pharaoh, Thus saith the LORD, Israel is my son, even my firstborn:

And I say unto thee, Let my son go, that he may serve me: and if thou refuse to let him go, behold, I will slay thy son, even thy firstborn.

And it came to pass by the way in the inn, that the LORD met him, and sought to kill him.

Then Zipporah took a sharp stone, and cut off the foreskin of her son, and cast it at his feet, and said, Surely a bloody husband art thou to me.

So he let him go: then she said, A bloody husband thou art, because of the circumcision.

And the LORD said to Aaron, Go into the wilderness to meet Moses. And he went, and met him in the mount of God, and kissed him.

And Moses told Aaron all the words of the LORD who had sent him, and all the signs which he had commanded him.

And Moses and Aaron went and gathered together all the elders of the children of Israel:

And Aaron spake all the words which the LORD had spoken unto Moses, and did the signs in the sight of the people.

And the people believed: and when they heard that the LORD had visited the children of Israel, and that he had looked upon their affliction, then they bowed their heads and worshipped.

**Narrator**

Moses now moves.

After all the questions, all the hesitation, all the fear, he returns to Jethro and asks permission to go back to Egypt.

That is not a small step.

Egypt is where his old life broke open.

Egypt is where his first attempt failed.

Egypt is where Pharaoh's power waits.

But now Moses goes back with God's word.

He takes his wife.

He takes his sons.

And he takes the rod of God in his hand.

That staff is no longer just a shepherd's tool.

It is the rod of God.

Something ordinary has been caught up into God's mission.

Then God tells Moses the road ahead will be hard.

Pharaoh's heart will be hardened.

He will not release the people easily.

Again, God is not hiding the difficulty.

Deliverance will come, but it will come through confrontation.

Then God gives Moses the message:

Israel is my son, even my firstborn.

That line changes how we hear the whole story.

Israel is not merely a labor force.

Israel is not merely an ethnic group inside Egypt.

Israel is God's son.

Pharaoh has been attacking God's family.

So when God says, Let my son go, this is covenant language.

This is family language.

This is worship language.

Let my son go, that he may serve me.

Freedom in Exodus is not only escape from slavery.

It is freedom to belong to God.

Then we reach the strange circumcision scene.

It is brief and difficult.

Modern readers often feel confused by it.

But at the center is this:

Moses' own household must be under the covenant sign.

Circumcision was the sign God gave Abraham in Genesis 17.

Moses is being sent to represent the covenant God.

He cannot treat covenant obedience as optional in his own family.

The deliverer must not ignore the sign of belonging.

Then Aaron appears.

God keeps His word.

Aaron meets Moses at the mountain of God and kisses him.

Moses tells Aaron everything.

The brothers go together.

They gather the elders of Israel.

Aaron speaks the words.

The signs are shown.

And the people believe.

Then comes one of the most beautiful moments in the opening of Exodus.

When the people hear that the Lord has visited them and looked upon their affliction, they bow their heads and worship.

Think about that.

They are still in Egypt.

They are still enslaved.

Pharaoh has not let them go.

The plagues have not started.

The Red Sea has not opened.

But they worship because they know God has seen them.

Sometimes worship begins before circumstances change.

Not because the pain is gone.

But because the message has arrived:

God has heard.

God has seen.

God has visited His people.

## Closing

Day 22 opens a new book, but it continues the same promise.

Genesis ended with Joseph saying, God will surely visit you.

Exodus begins by showing that God does.

Israel multiplies in Egypt.

Pharaoh fears what he cannot control.

Fear becomes slavery.

Slavery becomes bitterness.

Bitterness becomes a death command.

But God is already moving.

Through Shiphrah and Puah.

Through a mother hiding her son.

Through a sister watching by the river.

Through Pharaoh's daughter showing compassion.

Through Moses being drawn out of water.

Through a failed first attempt.

Through exile in Midian.

Through years that looked like delay.

Through a bush burning but not consumed.

Through the name I AM.

Through signs.

Through Aaron.

Through a people who believe before they are free.

The big lesson is not that Moses is strong.

The big lesson is that God hears.

God remembers.

God sees.

God knows.

And God comes down.

That is where Exodus begins.

Deliverance does not begin when Pharaoh changes his mind.

It begins when God hears His people.

And if Day 22 teaches us anything, it is this:

You may feel hidden.

You may feel stuck.

You may feel like the story has been delayed so long that nothing is moving.

But Exodus says God can be at work before the rescue is visible.

He can preserve life in the place meant for death.

He can prepare a deliverer in the wilderness.

He can call someone who feels unqualified.

He can turn a staff into a sign.

He can make worship rise before freedom arrives.

And He can keep a promise long after the generation that first heard it has passed away.

Genesis ended with a coffin in Egypt.

Exodus begins with a cry from Egypt.

And the God of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob hears.

Tomorrow, the confrontation begins.

Moses and Aaron will stand before Pharaoh.

The first demand will be spoken.

Let my people go.

But Pharaoh will not bow quickly.

The struggle between empire and the living God is just beginning.
