# Bible In One Year - Day 22 Narrator Script

## Day 22 - God Hears Israel's Cry

**Reading:** Exodus 1 through 4  
**Bible text:** King James Version, Exodus 1-4  
**Audio style:** Narrator teaching with Scripture reading  

## Opening

Welcome to Day 22 of Bible in One Year.

Today we begin the book of Exodus.

Genesis ended with a coffin in Egypt.

Joseph died, but before he died, he said something full of hope:

God will surely visit you.

That sentence is the bridge into Exodus.

Because when Exodus opens, the family of Jacob is still in Egypt.

They are no longer just one household trying to survive famine.

They have multiplied.

They have grown.

They have become strong.

God's promise to Abraham is becoming visible.

But then the story darkens.

A new Pharaoh rises.

He does not know Joseph.

He does not honor what Joseph did for Egypt.

He looks at Israel's growth and sees a threat.

And that fear becomes control.

Control becomes slavery.

Slavery becomes cruelty.

Cruelty becomes a command to kill Hebrew sons.

So Exodus begins in tension.

God's promise is growing.

But Pharaoh is trying to crush it.

And the question hanging over the opening chapters is simple:

Does God hear His people when they are suffering?

Exodus answers yes.

But it does not answer with a quick rescue.

It answers through courageous midwives.

Through a hidden baby.

Through a basket in the river.

Through a failed first attempt by Moses.

Through years in the wilderness.

Through a burning bush.

Through the name of God.

And through a calling Moses feels too weak to carry.

Day 22 teaches us that deliverance often begins before anyone can see it clearly.

## Exodus 1:1-7

**Scripture**

Now these are the names of the children of Israel, which came into Egypt; every man and his household came with Jacob.

And Joseph died, and all his brethren, and all that generation.

And the children of Israel were fruitful, and increased abundantly, and multiplied, and waxed exceeding mighty; and the land was filled with them.

**Narrator**

Exodus begins by naming the sons of Israel who came into Egypt.

That matters because Exodus is not starting from scratch.

It is continuing Genesis.

The people in Exodus are the family God promised to Abraham.

They are the descendants of Isaac.

They are the household of Jacob.

They are the family Joseph preserved during famine.

Joseph dies.

His brothers die.

That whole generation passes away.

But the promise does not die.

The children of Israel are fruitful.

They increase abundantly.

They multiply.

The land is filled with them.

That language should sound familiar.

Back in Genesis, God told humanity to be fruitful and multiply.

God promised Abraham descendants.

God told Jacob He would make him a great nation in Egypt.

Now Exodus opens by showing that promise growing.

This is not just population growth.

This is covenant faithfulness.

God is keeping His word in a foreign land.

And that is important because before Exodus shows oppression, it shows blessing.

Pharaoh's cruelty will not come out of nowhere.

It will come as a reaction against what God is doing.

God is multiplying His people.

Pharaoh is about to try to control them.

## Exodus 1:8-14

**Scripture**

Now there arose up a new king over Egypt, which knew not Joseph.

And he said unto his people, Behold, the people of the children of Israel are more and mightier than we:

Come on, let us deal wisely with them; lest they multiply.

Therefore they did set over them taskmasters to afflict them with their burdens.

And they made their lives bitter with hard bondage, in morter, and in brick, and in all manner of service in the field.

**Narrator**

Then the atmosphere changes.

A new king rises over Egypt who does not know Joseph.

That phrase is doing more than telling us a ruler forgot a name.

It means Egypt has stopped honoring the story of how Joseph saved the nation.

The memory of grace has been replaced by fear.

Pharaoh looks at Israel's growth and says, They are more and mightier than we.

Notice what he sees.

He sees people.

He sees numbers.

He sees strength.

But he does not see God's promise.

To Pharaoh, blessing looks like threat.

So he says, Let us deal wisely with them.

That sounds reasonable at first.

It sounds like policy.

It sounds like strategy.

But the wisdom of Pharaoh is really control dressed up in public language.

Soon, taskmasters are set over Israel.

The people are afflicted with burdens.

Their lives become bitter with hard bondage.

Mortar.

Brick.

Field labor.

Harsh service.

Exodus wants us to feel that slavery is not an idea.

It enters the body.

It enters the back.

It enters the hands.

It enters the morning and the night.

It makes life bitter.

And this is how evil often grows.

Fear becomes suspicion.

Suspicion becomes policy.

Policy becomes oppression.

Oppression becomes cruelty.

Pharaoh thinks he is protecting Egypt.

But really, he is fighting against the blessing of God.

## Exodus 1:15-22

**Scripture**

And the king of Egypt spake to the Hebrew midwives, of which the name of the one was Shiphrah, and the name of the other Puah:

And he said, When ye do the office of a midwife to the Hebrew women, if it be a son, then ye shall kill him.

But the midwives feared God, and did not as the king of Egypt commanded them, but saved the men children alive.

Therefore God dealt well with the midwives.

And Pharaoh charged all his people, saying, Every son that is born ye shall cast into the river.

**Narrator**

Pharaoh's oppression now becomes darker.

Forced labor was not enough.

Now he targets the sons.

He commands the Hebrew midwives to kill baby boys at birth.

This is an attack on Israel's future.

If Pharaoh can destroy the sons, he can weaken the people.

He can control the next generation before it grows.

But Exodus does something beautiful here.

It names the midwives.

Shiphrah.

Puah.

Pharaoh is the king of Egypt, but in this scene, the women are the ones remembered by name.

Why?

Because they feared God.

That phrase matters.

Fearing God does not mean they were terrified of Him in a shallow way.

It means God carried more weight than Pharaoh.

God's command mattered more than Pharaoh's command.

God's authority stood above the king's authority.

So they refused to kill the children.

They preserved life.

And they did it in a world where the most powerful man in the land commanded death.

That is courage.

Not loud courage.

Not public stage courage.

Quiet, dangerous, faithful courage.

And God dealt well with them.

Then Pharaoh escalates again.

He commands all his people to throw every Hebrew son into the river.

The Nile becomes an instrument of death.

Exodus 1 ends in darkness.

But that darkness is exactly where Moses will be born.

## Exodus 2:1-10

**Scripture**

And the woman conceived, and bare a son: and when she saw him that he was a goodly child, she hid him three months.

And when she could not longer hide him, she took for him an ark of bulrushes, and daubed it with slime and with pitch, and put the child therein; and she laid it in the flags by the river's brink.

And the daughter of Pharaoh came down to wash herself at the river.

And when she had opened it, she saw the child: and, behold, the babe wept. And she had compassion on him.

And she called his name Moses: and she said, Because I drew him out of the water.

**Narrator**

Moses is born under a death sentence.

That is the world he enters.

His mother sees that he is a goodly child and hides him for three months.

The language reminds us of Genesis, where God saw creation as good.

Here, in the middle of Pharaoh's death command, a mother sees life worth protecting.

When she can hide him no longer, she makes a small ark of bulrushes.

That word matters.

The basket is like a tiny ark.

Noah was preserved through waters of judgment in Genesis.

Moses is preserved through waters of death in Exodus.

The river that Pharaoh wanted to use for killing becomes the place where God preserves the deliverer.

Pharaoh's daughter finds the basket.

She hears the baby crying.

And she has compassion.

That is shocking.

Compassion comes from inside Pharaoh's own household.

The house that ordered death now shelters the child who will one day confront Pharaoh.

And then the irony gets even stronger.

Moses' own mother is paid to nurse him.

Pharaoh's house unknowingly helps preserve the deliverer Pharaoh tried to destroy.

Moses is named because he was drawn out of the water.

That name becomes a preview.

One day, through Moses, Israel will also be brought through water.

God is already writing the pattern of deliverance into Moses' own life.

## Exodus 2:11-25

**Scripture**

And it came to pass in those days, when Moses was grown, that he went out unto his brethren, and looked on their burdens.

And he spied an Egyptian smiting an Hebrew, one of his brethren.

And he slew the Egyptian, and hid him in the sand.

Now when Pharaoh heard this thing, he sought to slay Moses. But Moses fled from the face of Pharaoh, and dwelt in the land of Midian.

And the children of Israel sighed by reason of the bondage, and they cried, and their cry came up unto God by reason of the bondage.

And God heard their groaning, and God remembered his covenant with Abraham, with Isaac, and with Jacob.

And God looked upon the children of Israel, and God had respect unto them.

**Narrator**

When Moses grows up, he goes out to his brothers.

That phrase matters.

Moses has been raised with access to Egyptian privilege, but he sees the Hebrews as his people.

He looks on their burdens.

He sees oppression.

He sees violence.

He sees an Egyptian beating a Hebrew.

And Moses acts.

But he acts impulsively.

He kills the Egyptian and hides him in the sand.

Moses cares about injustice, but he is not yet ready to deliver Israel.

His first attempt at rescue is not God's appointed rescue.

The next day, when he tries to intervene between two Hebrews, they reject him.

Who made thee a prince and a judge over us?

Moses realizes the matter is known.

Pharaoh hears.

Moses flees.

Now the man raised in Pharaoh's house becomes a stranger in Midian.

He marries Zipporah.

He names his son Gershom, saying he has been a stranger in a strange land.

This is a hidden season.

To us, it can look like the story has slowed down.

But God often forms people in places that look like delay.

Then Exodus turns our attention back to Israel.

They sigh.

They cry.

Their cry comes up to God.

And then come four beautiful movements.

God heard.

God remembered.

God looked.

God knew.

That is the turning point.

Before Moses stands before Pharaoh, God hears the groaning.

Before the plagues, God remembers the covenant.

Before the Red Sea, God looks upon His people.

The rescue begins in the heart and faithfulness of God.

## Exodus 3:1-12

**Scripture**

Now Moses kept the flock of Jethro his father in law, the priest of Midian.

And the angel of the LORD appeared unto him in a flame of fire out of the midst of a bush.

And he looked, and, behold, the bush burned with fire, and the bush was not consumed.

And God called unto him out of the midst of the bush, and said, Moses, Moses.

And he said, Here am I.

And he said, Draw not nigh hither: put off thy shoes from off thy feet, for the place whereon thou standest is holy ground.

And the LORD said, I have surely seen the affliction of my people which are in Egypt, and have heard their cry.

And I am come down to deliver them.

**Narrator**

Exodus 3 begins with Moses keeping sheep.

Not ruling.

Not speaking to crowds.

Not leading a nation.

Shepherding in the wilderness.

That is where God meets him.

The bush burns with fire, but it is not consumed.

Moses turns aside to see.

And God calls his name.

Moses, Moses.

The repeated name feels personal.

Like Abraham, Abraham.

Like Jacob, Jacob.

God is calling a man by name in a turning-point moment.

Then God tells him to remove his sandals.

The ground is holy.

Not because that dirt was naturally special.

But because God is present.

Ordinary wilderness ground becomes holy ground when God meets a person there.

Then God identifies Himself.

I am the God of thy father, the God of Abraham, the God of Isaac, and the God of Jacob.

Exodus deliverance is tied to Genesis promise.

God is not inventing a new concern.

He is keeping an old covenant.

Then God says He has seen Israel's affliction.

He has heard their cry.

He knows their sorrows.

And He has come down to deliver them.

That is one of the most important statements in Exodus.

God does not send Moses because Moses noticed a problem first.

God sends Moses because God has seen, heard, known, and come down.

Moses is being invited into God's rescue.

## Exodus 3:13-22

**Scripture**

And Moses said unto God, Behold, when I come unto the children of Israel, and shall say unto them, The God of your fathers hath sent me unto you; and they shall say to me, What is his name? what shall I say unto them?

And God said unto Moses, I AM THAT I AM.

And he said, Thus shalt thou say unto the children of Israel, I AM hath sent me unto you.

And God said moreover unto Moses, Thus shalt thou say unto the children of Israel, The LORD God of your fathers, the God of Abraham, the God of Isaac, and the God of Jacob, hath sent me unto you.

And I am sure that the king of Egypt will not let you go, no, not by a mighty hand.

And I will stretch out my hand, and smite Egypt with all my wonders.

**Narrator**

Moses asks a reasonable question.

If Israel asks God's name, what should he say?

These are suffering people.

They have been enslaved for years.

They need more than vague spiritual language.

They need to know who has sent Moses.

God says, I AM THAT I AM.

This name is deep.

It speaks of God's self-existence.

God is not becoming.

God is not dependent.

God is not propped up by Egypt, Pharaoh, Moses, or Israel.

He simply is.

He is the living God.

The faithful God.

The God who will be who He is.

And then God connects that name to covenant history.

The LORD God of your fathers.

The God of Abraham.

The God of Isaac.

The God of Jacob.

So the mystery of God's name is not detached from the story.

The I AM is the covenant God who made promises and remembers them.

Then God tells Moses the road will not be easy.

Pharaoh will not let the people go quickly.

God is honest about resistance.

That is important.

Resistance does not mean Moses misheard God.

Resistance does not mean God is surprised.

God already knows Pharaoh will resist.

But God also says He will stretch out His hand.

Egypt will not win because Pharaoh is strong.

Israel will be delivered because the LORD is stronger.

## Exodus 4:1-17

**Scripture**

And Moses answered and said, But, behold, they will not believe me, nor hearken unto my voice.

And the LORD said unto him, What is that in thine hand?

And he said, A rod.

And he said, Cast it on the ground.

And he cast it on the ground, and it became a serpent.

And Moses said unto the LORD, O my Lord, I am not eloquent.

And the LORD said unto him, Who hath made man's mouth?

Now therefore go, and I will be with thy mouth, and teach thee what thou shalt say.

And he said, O my Lord, send, I pray thee, by the hand of him whom thou wilt send.

And the anger of the LORD was kindled against Moses.

**Narrator**

Moses keeps objecting.

What if they do not believe me?

What if they do not listen?

I am not eloquent.

I am slow of speech.

Send someone else.

If we are honest, many of us understand Moses here.

The calling is large.

The enemy is powerful.

The people may reject him.

And Moses knows his own weakness.

God gives signs.

The staff becomes a serpent.

His hand becomes leprous and is restored.

Water from the Nile will become blood.

These signs are not random tricks.

They show authority over nature, disease, and Egypt's river.

Then Moses brings up his speech.

And God asks, Who made man's mouth?

That question is not harsh in a careless way.

It is a re-centering question.

Moses is staring at his limitation.

God points him back to the Creator.

The One who made the mouth can be with the mouth.

God says, I will be with thy mouth, and teach thee what thou shalt say.

That is the promise Moses needs.

Not self-confidence.

God-with-him confidence.

Still, Moses asks God to send someone else.

And the Lord is angry.

But even in anger, God provides Aaron.

Aaron will speak with Moses.

Moses will not go alone.

God does not let Moses' fear cancel the mission.

But He does provide help for the weakness Moses keeps naming.

## Exodus 4:18-31

**Scripture**

And Moses went and returned to Jethro his father in law, and said unto him, Let me go, I pray thee, and return unto my brethren which are in Egypt.

And the LORD said unto Moses in Midian, Go, return into Egypt.

And the LORD said unto Aaron, Go into the wilderness to meet Moses.

And Moses and Aaron went and gathered together all the elders of the children of Israel.

And Aaron spake all the words which the LORD had spoken unto Moses, and did the signs in the sight of the people.

And the people believed: and when they heard that the LORD had visited the children of Israel, and that he had looked upon their affliction, then they bowed their heads and worshipped.

**Narrator**

Moses returns to Jethro and asks permission to go back to Egypt.

That is a huge step.

Egypt is the place he fled.

Egypt is the place of Pharaoh.

Egypt is the place of old fear.

But now he returns with God's word.

God tells Moses that Pharaoh will resist.

Again, the mission begins with honesty.

Deliverance is certain, but it will not be simple.

There is also a difficult scene on the road involving circumcision.

It is brief, strange to modern readers, and easy to skip.

But it reminds us of something important.

Moses' household must not ignore the covenant sign.

The deliverer of Israel cannot treat covenant obedience as optional.

Then Aaron meets Moses at the mountain of God.

God provides the brother He promised.

Moses is not alone.

Together they gather the elders of Israel.

Aaron speaks the words.

The signs are shown.

And the people believe.

Then comes a beautiful ending to these opening chapters.

When Israel hears that the LORD has visited them and seen their affliction, they bow and worship.

Notice, they are still in Egypt.

They are still enslaved.

Pharaoh has not released them.

The plagues have not started.

The sea has not opened.

But they worship because they know God has heard.

Sometimes worship begins before circumstances change because faith has heard that God has seen.

## Closing

Day 22 opens Exodus with pressure, darkness, and hope.

Israel multiplies because God keeps His promise.

Pharaoh oppresses because fear wants control.

The midwives preserve life because they fear God more than the king.

Moses is born under a death sentence, but God preserves him through the water.

Moses runs to Midian, but God uses the wilderness.

Israel groans, and God hears.

God remembers.

God sees.

God knows.

Then God calls Moses from the burning bush and reveals His name:

I AM.

That is the foundation for everything that follows.

The Exodus will not happen because Moses is naturally confident.

It will not happen because Pharaoh is reasonable.

It will not happen because Israel is strong enough to free itself.

Deliverance begins with the God who hears.

The God who remembers.

The God who comes down.

The God who is.

And by the end of Exodus 4, the people bow their heads and worship.

They are not free yet.

But they know God has visited them.

That is where deliverance begins.

Not with Pharaoh changing his mind.

With God hearing His people.
