# Day 74 Narrator Script

## Opening

Welcome to Day 74 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are reading First Samuel 23 through 26.

The title for today is David Spares Saul.

This day follows David while Saul keeps hunting him.

David rescues Keilah.

David seeks the LORD for direction.

Jonathan strengthens David's hand in God.

Saul surrounds David, but a Philistine invasion interrupts the pursuit.

Then David has two opportunities to harm Saul.

In the cave, David cuts off the skirt of Saul's robe but will not strike him.

Later, David enters Saul's camp and takes Saul's spear and cruse of water but will not kill him.

Between those two mercy scenes, Abigail prevents David from shedding blood in anger against Nabal.

So the day is about restraint.

David is learning how to wait for the LORD.

He will not seize the kingdom by murdering the LORD's anointed.

He must also learn not to avenge himself against fools who insult him.

This is a deeply practical day.

It asks what faith looks like when revenge seems available.

## First Samuel 23

First Samuel 23 begins with David hearing that the Philistines fight against Keilah and rob the threshingfloors.

David inquires of the LORD, saying, Shall I go and smite these Philistines?

The LORD says, Go, and smite the Philistines, and save Keilah.

David's men are afraid.

They say they are already afraid in Judah; how much more if they go to Keilah against the armies of the Philistines?

David inquires of the LORD again.

The LORD answers again: Arise, go down to Keilah, for I will deliver the Philistines into thine hand.

David goes.

He fights the Philistines.

He brings away their cattle.

He saves the inhabitants of Keilah.

This matters because David is acting like a true shepherd of Israel even while Saul is hunting him.

Saul is king, but David rescues the threatened city.

Saul hears that David has come to Keilah.

Instead of rejoicing that Israel has been rescued, Saul sees an opportunity.

He says God has delivered David into his hand because David is shut in by entering a town with gates and bars.

Saul summons all the people to war to besiege David and his men.

David knows Saul secretly practices mischief against him.

Abiathar brings the ephod.

David asks the LORD whether Saul will come down and whether the men of Keilah will deliver him into Saul's hand.

The LORD says Saul will come down.

David asks whether the men of Keilah will deliver him and his men into Saul's hand.

The LORD says they will deliver him.

So David and his men, about six hundred, leave Keilah and go wherever they can go.

Saul stops the expedition when he hears David escaped.

This scene teaches us that David's rescue of Keilah does not guarantee Keilah will protect David.

David does good, but he still must flee.

Faithfulness does not always produce loyalty from people.

But David has something Saul lacks: he keeps asking the LORD.

David remains in the wilderness in strongholds.

Saul seeks him every day, but God delivers him not into Saul's hand.

That sentence is the anchor of the chapter.

Saul seeks him every day.

God does not deliver him.

Jonathan comes to David in the wood and strengthens his hand in God.

Jonathan says, Fear not, for the hand of Saul my father shall not find thee.

Thou shalt be king over Israel, and I shall be next unto thee.

Jonathan knows David's future and encourages him in God's promise.

The two make a covenant before the LORD.

Then Jonathan goes home, and David remains in the wood.

The Ziphites then betray David's location to Saul.

Saul blesses them in the name of the LORD, even while pursuing innocent blood.

Saul hunts David in the wilderness of Maon.

Saul and his men compass David and his men round about to take them.

Then a messenger comes to Saul, saying the Philistines have invaded the land.

Saul returns from pursuing David.

They call the place Sela-hammahlekoth, the rock of divisions.

The LORD again preserves David.

Not always with comfort.

Not always with obvious ease.

But Saul cannot close his hand around him.

## First Samuel 24

First Samuel 24 gives David a direct opportunity to kill Saul.

Saul returns from following the Philistines and hears David is in the wilderness of Engedi.

Saul takes three thousand chosen men and goes to seek David and his men upon the rocks of the wild goats.

He comes to sheepcotes by the way, where there is a cave.

Saul goes in to cover his feet.

David and his men are hiding in the sides of the cave.

David's men say the LORD has delivered Saul into David's hand.

They see this as the moment.

The enemy is vulnerable.

The path to the throne looks open.

David arises and cuts off the skirt of Saul's robe privily.

But afterward David's heart smites him because he cut off Saul's skirt.

Even that symbolic act troubles him.

David says the LORD forbid that he should do this thing unto his master, the LORD's anointed, to stretch forth his hand against him.

David restrains his servants with these words and does not allow them to rise against Saul.

Saul leaves the cave.

David also arises and goes out after him.

He cries, My lord the king.

When Saul looks behind him, David stoops with his face to the earth and bows himself.

David asks why Saul listens to men who say David seeks his hurt.

He says Saul's own eyes have seen how the LORD delivered Saul into his hand in the cave, and some urged David to kill him.

But David spared him.

David holds up the skirt of Saul's robe.

He says there is neither evil nor transgression in his hand, and he has not sinned against Saul.

Yet Saul hunts his soul to take it.

Then David says, The LORD judge between me and thee, and the LORD avenge me of thee: but mine hand shall not be upon thee.

That is the heart of the chapter.

David entrusts judgment to the LORD.

He does not deny that wrong has been done.

He does not pretend Saul is innocent.

But he refuses to take vengeance into his own hand.

Saul lifts up his voice and weeps.

He says David is more righteous than he.

David has rewarded him good, while Saul has rewarded David evil.

Saul recognizes that David will surely be king and that the kingdom of Israel will be established in his hand.

Saul asks David to swear that he will not cut off Saul's seed after him or destroy his name out of his father's house.

David swears to Saul.

Saul goes home.

David and his men go up unto the hold.

Even after Saul's tears, David does not simply move back into Saul's house.

Mercy is real, but wisdom remains.

David spares Saul, and David keeps distance.

## First Samuel 25

First Samuel 25 begins with the death of Samuel.

All Israel gathers, laments him, and buries him in his house at Ramah.

David then goes down to the wilderness of Paran.

The chapter introduces Nabal, a very great man with three thousand sheep and one thousand goats.

His wife Abigail is of good understanding and beautiful countenance, but Nabal is churlish and evil in his doings.

David hears that Nabal is shearing sheep.

He sends ten young men with a peaceful request.

They are to greet Nabal in David's name and remind him that David's men protected Nabal's shepherds.

David asks for whatever Nabal finds in his hand to give.

Nabal answers harshly.

Who is David?

Who is the son of Jesse?

There are many servants nowadays who break away from their masters.

Shall Nabal take his bread, water, and flesh prepared for his shearers and give it to men he does not know?

Nabal's insult is not only refusal.

He treats David like a runaway servant.

David's young men return and tell him.

David tells his men to gird on their swords.

About four hundred go up after David.

Two hundred remain by the stuff.

David is angry and ready to shed blood.

Here the chapter tests David differently.

In chapter 24, David refused to kill Saul.

In chapter 25, David is tempted to avenge himself against a foolish insult.

One of Nabal's young men tells Abigail what happened.

He says David's men were very good to them and were a wall unto them by night and day.

Abigail acts quickly.

She takes bread, wine, sheep, parched corn, raisins, and figs, and sends them ahead.

She meets David secretly.

When Abigail sees David, she hastes, lights off the ass, falls before David on her face, and bows herself to the ground.

She asks David to let the iniquity be on her.

She calls Nabal a man of Belial and says folly is with him.

Then she speaks wisdom.

She reminds David that the LORD has withholden him from coming to shed blood and from avenging himself with his own hand.

She asks David to receive the blessing she has brought.

She speaks of the LORD making David a sure house because David fights the battles of the LORD.

She says evil has not been found in David all his days.

She speaks of David's soul being bound in the bundle of life with the LORD his God.

She says when the LORD has appointed David ruler over Israel, this will not be grief to him, nor offense of heart, that he has shed blood causelessly or avenged himself.

Abigail sees David's future and speaks to his conscience.

David blesses the LORD, who sent her to meet him.

He blesses her advice.

He blesses her for keeping him from coming to shed blood and avenging himself with his own hand.

David receives from her hand and sends her home in peace.

When Abigail returns, Nabal is holding a feast like the feast of a king.

His heart is merry, and he is very drunken.

She tells him nothing until morning.

In the morning, when the wine is gone out of Nabal, she tells him.

His heart dies within him, and he becomes as a stone.

About ten days later, the LORD smites Nabal, and he dies.

David hears and blesses the LORD, who pleaded the cause of his reproach from the hand of Nabal and kept His servant from evil.

David then sends and communes with Abigail to take her as wife.

She becomes his wife.

This chapter is a gift because it shows David being corrected before he sins.

Abigail's wisdom protects David from bloodguilt.

The LORD handles Nabal.

David is spared from taking vengeance.

## First Samuel 26

First Samuel 26 gives another scene where David spares Saul.

The Ziphites again come to Saul and tell him David hides in the hill of Hachilah.

Saul arises with three thousand chosen men to seek David.

David sends spies and knows Saul has come.

David sees where Saul lies, with Abner and the people around him.

Saul is lying in the trench, and his spear is stuck in the ground by his bolster.

David asks who will go down with him to Saul.

Abishai says he will go.

So David and Abishai come by night.

Saul is sleeping.

The spear is stuck in the ground.

The people are asleep around him.

Abishai says God has delivered David's enemy into his hand that day.

He asks to smite Saul with the spear to the earth at once.

David refuses.

He says, Destroy him not: for who can stretch forth his hand against the LORD's anointed, and be guiltless?

David says the LORD shall smite Saul, or his day shall come to die, or he shall descend into battle and perish.

But the LORD forbid that David should stretch forth his hand against the LORD's anointed.

David takes the spear and the cruse of water from Saul's bolster.

No one sees.

No one knows.

No one wakes.

A deep sleep from the LORD has fallen upon them.

David passes over to the other side and stands on the top of a hill afar off.

He cries to the people and to Abner.

He asks why Abner has not kept the king.

He says someone came in to destroy the king.

He calls for the king's spear and cruse of water.

Saul knows David's voice.

David asks why Saul pursues him.

What has he done?

What evil is in his hand?

If the LORD stirred Saul up, let Him accept an offering.

If men have stirred Saul up, let them be cursed before the LORD, because they have driven David out from abiding in the inheritance of the LORD.

David says, Let not my blood fall to the earth before the face of the LORD.

Saul answers, I have sinned.

Return, my son David, for I will no more do thee harm, because my soul was precious in thine eyes this day.

David answers by sending the spear back.

He says the LORD render to every man his righteousness and his faithfulness.

The LORD delivered Saul into his hand, but David would not stretch forth his hand against the LORD's anointed.

Saul blesses David and says David shall both do great things and still prevail.

David goes on his way, and Saul returns to his place.

Again, David shows mercy.

Again, Saul speaks regret.

Again, David does not put himself back under Saul's control.

He trusts the LORD to judge and keeps walking.

## Full Lesson Review

Day 74 teaches restraint under pressure.

David does not only need courage to fight enemies.

He needs patience not to become like Saul.

He saves Keilah, but the city would hand him over.

He is betrayed by the Ziphites.

He is surrounded by Saul.

He is insulted by Nabal.

He has Saul vulnerable twice.

Again and again, David must decide whether to take matters into his own hands.

The cave scene and the camp scene both show David refusing to murder Saul.

The Abigail scene shows David being stopped from murdering Nabal's household in anger.

That is important.

David is not perfect.

He needs correction.

He needs wise counsel.

He needs the LORD to keep him from bloodguilt.

But David is learning to leave vengeance with God.

The LORD can judge Saul.

The LORD can deal with Nabal.

The LORD can preserve David.

The LORD can keep His promise without David forcing it through sin.

## Guided Listening Review

Day 74 is one of the clearest days in Scripture for learning the difference between opportunity and permission.

David has opportunities.

He can stay in Keilah and hope gratitude protects him.

He can kill Saul in the cave.

He can destroy Nabal's household.

He can kill Saul again in the camp.

But not every open door is a holy door.

That is why David's repeated inquiry of the LORD in chapter 23 is so important.

David asks before he rescues Keilah.

Then he asks again when his men are afraid.

Later he asks whether Saul will come down and whether Keilah will surrender him.

David is not making decisions by instinct alone.

He is seeking the LORD in danger.

The chapter also teaches that doing good does not mean people will always do good back.

David saves Keilah, yet Keilah would deliver him to Saul.

That could make a person bitter.

But David keeps moving under God's guidance.

Jonathan's visit in the wood is another mercy.

He strengthens David's hand in God.

That phrase is beautiful because Jonathan does not merely flatter David or tell him everything will be easy.

He points David back to God's promise.

Then chapter 24 places Saul inside David's reach.

David's men interpret the moment as God's gift.

David sees a boundary they are missing.

Saul is still the LORD's anointed.

David will not stretch out his hand against him.

Even cutting the robe troubles David's conscience.

That sensitivity matters.

David's heart is not hard toward small compromises.

He feels the weight of what he has done.

Chapter 25 then prevents us from pretending David's restraint is automatic.

Nabal insults him, and David is ready to answer with blood.

Abigail becomes God's interruption.

She acts quickly.

She speaks humbly.

She names the future God has for David.

She warns him that causeless bloodshed will become grief to him later.

David receives correction.

That is one mark of wisdom.

A fool like Nabal cannot be corrected.

A hunted man like David, in a heated moment, still listens when God sends wise counsel.

Chapter 26 repeats the mercy lesson so the reader cannot miss it.

This time David is not in a cave by accident.

He enters Saul's camp.

The spear is beside Saul.

Abishai is ready.

The whole camp sleeps because the LORD has sent deep sleep.

Still David refuses murder.

He trusts the LORD to decide Saul's end.

This is not passivity.

It is faith refusing to become revenge.

David will not use sin to hurry God's promise.

That is the mature center of the day.

## Key Connections

Three details help the day hold together.

First, David's restraint is not weakness.

The same David who fights Philistines and rescues Keilah refuses to murder Saul.

He is not afraid to act.

He is afraid to sin against the LORD.

That is a different kind of courage.

Second, Abigail's wisdom is part of God's protection.

God protects David from Saul, but He also protects David from David's own anger.

That is a mercy we should not miss.

Sometimes the LORD preserves us by stopping someone else.

Sometimes He preserves us by stopping us.

Third, Saul's regret is not the same as repentance.

He weeps in chapter 24.

He admits sin in chapter 26.

But he returns again to the same pursuit.

Words of sorrow are serious only when they turn into a changed path.

David therefore shows mercy without handing himself back into Saul's control.

He honors Saul as the LORD's anointed, but he does not pretend Saul is safe.

That combination of mercy and wisdom is part of David's formation.

One more connection helps the whole day.

The robe and the spear both belong to Saul's royal identity.

The robe is cut in the cave.

The spear is taken in the camp.

David holds proof that Saul's life was in his hand.

But David uses the proof to appeal, not to assassinate.

That is a very different use of power.

David does not deny the wrong done to him.

He speaks plainly.

He asks why Saul pursues him.

He asks the LORD to judge.

But he refuses to turn evidence into revenge.

That is why Abigail's words fit so well.

She wants David to have no grief later because he shed blood without cause.

The future king must arrive at the throne with clean hands, not with a trail of private vengeance.

## Closing

As you finish Day 74, think about the spear, the robe, and the food Abigail brings.

The robe proves David could have harmed Saul but did not.

The spear proves David could have killed Saul but refused.

Abigail's gift proves God can interrupt anger before it becomes bloodshed.

The lesson is not weakness.

David is not passive because he is afraid.

He is restrained because he fears the LORD.

He will fight when God sends him.

He will rescue when help is needed.

But he will not seize God's promise by wickedness.

That is the shape of faith in these chapters.
