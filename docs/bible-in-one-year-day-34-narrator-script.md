# Bible In One Year - Day 34 Narrator Script

## Day 34 - Worship, Holiness, and Clean Living

**Reading:** Leviticus 9 through 12
**Bible text:** King James Version, Leviticus 9-12
**Audio style:** One narrator, cinematic Bible teaching with Scripture reading
**Estimated spoken time:** about 18-22 minutes at a calm narrator pace

## Opening

Welcome to Day 34 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are reading Leviticus 9 through 12.

Yesterday Aaron and his sons were washed, clothed, anointed, and set apart for priestly service.

Now that priestly service begins in public.

This day moves in a strong order.

First, worship is accepted.

Then worship is judged.

Then holiness reaches into ordinary life.

So Day 34 is not only about sacrifice.

It is about what it means to live near a holy God.

Leviticus 9 is full of hope.

Aaron offers what the LORD commanded.

The glory of the LORD appears.

Fire comes out from before the LORD and consumes the offering.

The people shout and fall on their faces.

But Leviticus 10 turns suddenly.

Nadab and Abihu offer strange fire before the LORD.

Again fire comes out from before the LORD.

But this time it judges instead of accepts.

Then Leviticus 11 and 12 begin teaching Israel about clean and unclean life.

Food.

Bodies.

Contact with death.

Birth.

Washing.

Waiting.

Purification.

The movement matters.

The God who receives worship at the altar also shapes how His people live at the table, in the home, and in the camp.

And that is one reason Leviticus matters so much.

It shows that holiness is not trapped inside a ceremony.

Holiness begins at the altar, but it does not stay there.

It reaches schedules.

It reaches habits.

It reaches the body.

It reaches what is touched, eaten, carried, and brought near.

## Leviticus 9:1-24

Leviticus 9 verses 1 through 24.

**Scripture**

And it came to pass on the eighth day, that Moses called Aaron and his sons, and the elders of Israel;

And he said unto Aaron, Take thee a young calf for a sin offering, and a ram for a burnt offering, without blemish, and offer them before the LORD.

And unto the children of Israel thou shalt speak, saying, Take ye a kid of the goats for a sin offering; and a calf and a lamb, both of the first year, without blemish, for a burnt offering;

Also a bullock and a ram for peace offerings, to sacrifice before the LORD; and a meat offering mingled with oil: for to day the LORD will appear unto you.

And Moses said, This is the thing which the LORD commanded that ye should do: and the glory of the LORD shall appear unto you.

Aaron therefore went unto the altar, and slew the calf of the sin offering, which was for himself.

And he brought the people's offering, and took the goat, which was the sin offering for the people, and slew it, and offered it for sin, as the first.

And he brought the burnt offering, and offered it according to the manner.

And he brought the meat offering, and took an handful thereof, and burnt it upon the altar.

He slew also the bullock and the ram for a sacrifice of peace offerings, which was for the people.

And Aaron lifted up his hand toward the people, and blessed them.

And Moses and Aaron went into the tabernacle of the congregation, and came out, and blessed the people: and the glory of the LORD appeared unto all the people.

And there came a fire out from before the LORD, and consumed upon the altar the burnt offering and the fat: which when all the people saw, they shouted, and fell on their faces.

**Narrator**

Leviticus 9 begins on the eighth day.

That means the seven days of ordination are complete.

Aaron is no longer only being prepared.

Now he is stepping into priestly ministry before the people.

But the chapter does not begin by celebrating Aaron's status.

It begins with sacrifice.

Aaron offers for himself first.

That matters.

The priest who serves the people also needs atonement.

He is not naturally clean because he stands near the altar.

He must come the same basic way everyone else must come:

through the mercy God provides.

Then he offers for the people.

The chapter names several kinds of offerings because worship in Leviticus is not one flat act.

There is sin to be dealt with.

There is surrender to be pictured.

There is fellowship and peace to be expressed.

There is obedience in bringing what God has required.

That helps us read the altar more clearly.

The altar is not a place for one vague religious feeling.

It is a place where different needs are being answered under God's instruction.

Israel is learning that sin is real, forgiveness is costly, surrender matters, and peace with God is not treated lightly.

Moses says that if they do what the LORD commanded, the glory of the LORD will appear.

That line explains everything.

The point is not religious creativity.

The point is obedient worship.

God's presence is not produced by human excitement.

It is encountered in the path God has given.

Then the great moment comes.

Moses and Aaron bless the people.

The glory of the LORD appears.

And fire comes out from before the LORD and consumes the offering on the altar.

This is visible acceptance.

God is showing that the sacrifice offered His way is pleasing to Him.

The people shout and fall on their faces.

Those reactions belong together.

There is joy.

There is awe.

There is celebration.

And there is fear of the LORD.

That is a healthy Bible pattern.

When people really see God's holiness, they do not become casual.

They become glad and low before Him at the same time.

Leviticus 9 shows the beauty of right worship.

God is near.

And His nearness is life-giving when His people approach Him the way He has commanded.

That is the kind of joy Israel was made for:

forgiven,

ordered,

blessed,

and standing before the LORD without being consumed.

## Leviticus 10:1-7

Leviticus 10 verses 1 through 7.

**Scripture**

And Nadab and Abihu, the sons of Aaron, took either of them his censer, and put fire therein, and put incense thereon, and offered strange fire before the LORD, which he commanded them not.

And there went out fire from the LORD, and devoured them, and they died before the LORD.

Then Moses said unto Aaron, This is it that the LORD spake, saying, I will be sanctified in them that come nigh me, and before all the people I will be glorified.

And Aaron held his peace.

And Moses called Mishael and Elzaphan, the sons of Uzziel the uncle of Aaron, and said unto them, Come near, carry your brethren from before the sanctuary out of the camp.

And Moses said unto Aaron, and unto Eleazar and unto Ithamar, his sons, Uncover not your heads, neither rend your clothes; lest ye die.

**Narrator**

This is one of the most sobering moments in Leviticus.

And the placement is deliberate.

Leviticus 9 ended with fire from the LORD accepting worship.

Leviticus 10 begins with fire from the LORD judging false worship.

Nadab and Abihu are not strangers.

They are Aaron's sons.

They are men with priestly privilege.

They have been near holy things.

And yet they offer strange fire before the LORD.

The text explains the problem in one clear line:

which he commanded them not.

That is the heart of it.

They do not approach God on God's terms.

They bring what He did not authorize.

Maybe to other eyes it looked close enough.

But worship cannot be improved by human invention.

That warning echoes through the whole Bible.

The closer a person gets to holy service, the more dangerous it becomes to confuse personal preference with obedience.

Sincerity by itself is not enough if the worship itself rejects God's word.

The same fire that showed acceptance in the last chapter now shows judgment in this one.

Moses says, I will be sanctified in them that come nigh me.

That means the people closest to God's service must treat Him as holy.

The nearer a person comes to holy things, the more serious careless disobedience becomes.

Aaron's silence is one of the heaviest lines in the chapter.

Aaron held his peace.

He has just watched two sons die.

Leviticus does not make the scene easy.

It makes it serious.

God's presence is beautiful.

But it is never casual.

And this chapter also keeps us from separating love and holiness.

The LORD is not harsh because He is unstable.

He is holy because He is God.

That means His nearness is the greatest gift in the camp, but it must never be handled with presumption.

## Leviticus 10:8-20

Leviticus 10 verses 8 through 20.

**Scripture**

And the LORD spake unto Aaron, saying,

Do not drink wine nor strong drink, thou, nor thy sons with thee, when ye go into the tabernacle of the congregation, lest ye die.

And that ye may put difference between holy and unholy, and between unclean and clean;

And that ye may teach the children of Israel all the statutes which the LORD hath spoken unto them by the hand of Moses.

And Moses diligently sought the goat of the sin offering, and, behold, it was burnt.

And Aaron said unto Moses, Behold, this day have they offered their sin offering and their burnt offering before the LORD; and such things have befallen me.

And when Moses heard that, he was content.

**Narrator**

After the death of Nadab and Abihu, the LORD speaks directly to Aaron.

The priests must serve with clear judgment.

They must not enter holy service under the influence of wine or strong drink.

The principle is plain:

those who minister before the LORD must be sober, alert, and discerning.

Priestly work is not something to do in a fog.

It requires attention, judgment, and self-control.

Why?

Because priests must put difference between holy and unholy, and between unclean and clean.

That line reaches forward into the next chapters.

The priest is not only someone who handles offerings.

He is a guardian of distinction.

He helps Israel recognize what belongs near God's presence and what does not.

And he teaches the people the statutes of the LORD.

That means Leviticus is not only training priests.

Through the priests, it is training the whole nation.

Israel must become a people who can recognize difference instead of flattening everything into the same category.

That is why Leviticus keeps naming categories.

Holy and common.

Clean and unclean.

Allowed and forbidden.

The chapter closes with Moses and Aaron working through how the sin offering should be handled after such a painful day.

Moses is concerned because holy instructions matter.

Aaron answers from inside grief.

And when Moses hears him, he is content.

Even sorrow does not cancel holiness.

And holiness is not cold.

It is careful obedience before the living God.

Pain does not give God's people permission to forget God.

But God also knows how to deal with His people in their weakness, confusion, and grief.

## Leviticus 11:1-23

Leviticus 11 verses 1 through 23.

**Scripture**

And the LORD spake unto Moses and to Aaron, saying unto them,

Speak unto the children of Israel, saying, These are the beasts which ye shall eat among all the beasts that are on the earth.

Whatsoever parteth the hoof, and is clovenfooted, and cheweth the cud, among the beasts, that shall ye eat.

Nevertheless these shall ye not eat of them that chew the cud, or of them that divide the hoof:

the camel, because he cheweth the cud, but divideth not the hoof; he is unclean unto you.

And the swine, though he divide the hoof, and be clovenfooted, yet he cheweth not the cud; he is unclean to you.

These shall ye eat of all that are in the waters: whatsoever hath fins and scales in the waters, in the seas, and in the rivers, them shall ye eat.

And all that have not fins and scales in the seas, and in the rivers, shall be an abomination unto you.

And these are they which ye shall have in abomination among the fowls.

All fowls that creep, going upon all four, shall be an abomination unto you.

**Narrator**

Leviticus 11 begins the clean and unclean food laws.

To modern readers, these laws can feel strange.

Why all these categories?

Why does it matter what Israel eats?

At the deepest level, the chapter trains Israel to live by holy distinction.

The point is not merely nutrition.

The point is formation.

Every meal becomes an act of memory and obedience.

Israel cannot eat without remembering that they belong to the LORD.

Food becomes part of discipleship.

The plate in front of them is tied to covenant identity.

That means holiness is not only something that happens at the altar.

It reaches the table.

It reaches the home.

It reaches ordinary decisions.

And notice this:

God does not leave His people to guess.

He names categories.

He gives boundaries.

He teaches discernment.

That is an act of care.

The people who live near Him are not meant to drift through life without attention.

They are meant to be shaped by His word.

That is part of what makes Leviticus so valuable, even when it feels foreign at first.

It shows that God cares how His people think, choose, separate, and obey in ordinary moments.

## Leviticus 11:24-47

Leviticus 11 verses 24 through 47.

**Scripture**

And for these ye shall be unclean: whosoever toucheth the carcase of them shall be unclean until the even.

And whosoever beareth ought of the carcase of them shall wash his clothes, and be unclean until the even.

And upon whatsoever any of them, when they are dead, doth fall, it shall be unclean.

And every earthen vessel, whereinto any of them falleth, whatsoever is in it shall be unclean; and ye shall break it.

Nevertheless a fountain or pit, wherein there is plenty of water, shall be clean.

For I am the LORD your God: ye shall therefore sanctify yourselves, and ye shall be holy; for I am holy.

For I am the LORD that bringeth you up out of the land of Egypt, to be your God: ye shall therefore be holy, for I am holy.

This is the law of the beasts, and of the fowl, and of every living creature that moveth in the waters:

To make a difference between the unclean and the clean.

**Narrator**

The second half of Leviticus 11 moves from eating to contact.

Dead bodies of unclean creatures can make people and objects unclean.

Clothing, containers, ovens, and food can all be affected.

This is why the word unclean has to be handled carefully.

Unclean does not always mean wicked.

Often it means ritually unfit for holy approach because of contact with death, decay, or impurity.

Leviticus is teaching Israel that death does not belong at the center of life with God.

The LORD is the living God.

So contact with death creates a condition that must be answered before normal holy approach is restored.

That is why the laws keep slowing Israel down.

Uncleanness is not something to shrug off.

The camp must learn to notice it, answer it, and respect the boundary between life and death.

The chapter also gives practical responses:

wash,

wait until evening,

break a clay vessel if it has absorbed contamination,

and handle shared water carefully.

Again, this is not random detail.

It is holiness with texture.

The law reaches everyday objects and daily routines.

Then the chapter gives its own reason.

Be holy, for I am holy.

And then:

I am the LORD that bringeth you up out of the land of Egypt, to be your God.

That is crucial.

Israel is not being told to obey in order to earn redemption.

They are being taught holiness because they have been redeemed.

The God who brought them out now teaches them how to belong to Him.

That pattern still matters whenever we read the Bible well.

God rescues first, and then He teaches His rescued people how to live in a way that fits His character.

So these chapters are not small side rules after the real story.

They are part of the real story.

Redemption is producing a distinct people.

## Leviticus 12:1-8

Leviticus 12 verses 1 through 8.

**Scripture**

And the LORD spake unto Moses, saying,

Speak unto the children of Israel, saying, If a woman have conceived seed, and born a man child: then she shall be unclean seven days;

and in the eighth day the flesh of his foreskin shall be circumcised.

And she shall then continue in the blood of her purifying three and thirty days.

But if she bear a maid child, then she shall be unclean two weeks.

And when the days of her purifying are fulfilled, for a son, or for a daughter, she shall bring a lamb of the first year for a burnt offering, and a young pigeon, or a turtledove, for a sin offering.

Who shall offer it before the LORD, and make an atonement for her.

And if she be not able to bring a lamb, then she shall bring two turtles, or two young pigeons.

**Narrator**

Leviticus 12 gives instructions connected to childbirth.

This chapter is not saying childbirth is sinful.

It is dealing with ritual impurity connected to blood, birth, and returning to sanctuary worship.

Blood in Leviticus is serious because it is bound up with life.

The child is circumcised on the eighth day, tying birth to covenant identity.

So this chapter is not treating birth as unimportant.

It is treating birth as weighty.

Life, blood, covenant, and approach to God are all close together in Leviticus.

When the days of purification are complete, offerings are brought.

And then we see one of the gentle mercies repeated in the law:

if she cannot bring a lamb, she may bring two birds.

That means the poor are not shut out from restoration.

God's law makes room for the one with less.

The poor do not get a lesser mercy.

They get a fitting offering within the same covenant grace.

That detail matters later in the Bible.

In Luke 2, Mary brings the poor person's offering after Jesus is born.

So Leviticus is not dead background.

It helps us understand the world Jesus entered.

## Closing

Day 34 gives us a full picture of life near God.

Leviticus 9 shows accepted worship.

The priest offers what God commanded, the glory of the LORD appears, and fire falls.

Leviticus 10 shows judged worship.

Holy service cannot be treated casually.

Then Leviticus 11 and 12 take holiness into daily life:

meals,

bodies,

birth,

objects,

washing,

waiting,

and discernment.

This is one of the big lessons of Leviticus:

God is not only forming worship moments.

He is forming a holy people.

He is teaching Israel that rescue from Egypt was the beginning, not the end.

The redeemed must now learn how to live as the LORD's own people.

The same God who redeems also teaches distinction.

The same God who receives worship also defines worship.

And the same God who dwells among His people also teaches them how to remain near Him without treating His presence lightly.

Tomorrow Leviticus keeps moving through uncleanness, cleansing, and restoration,

until it reaches one of the most important holy days in Israel's worship life:

the Day of Atonement.
