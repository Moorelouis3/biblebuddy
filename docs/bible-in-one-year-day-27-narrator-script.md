# Bible In One Year - Day 27 Narrator Script

## Day 27 - Covenant Law and Covenant Blood

**Reading:** Exodus 21 through 24  
**Bible text:** King James Version, Exodus 21-24  
**Audio style:** Narrator teaching with Scripture reading  

## Opening

Welcome to Day 27 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are reading Exodus 21 through 24.

Yesterday, Israel stood at Mount Sinai.

They heard thunder.

They saw lightning.

They heard the trumpet.

They saw smoke on the mountain.

And God spoke the Ten Commandments.

Now, in today's reading, the law begins moving into everyday life.

That may not sound exciting at first.

Exodus 21 through 24 includes laws about servants, injuries, theft, animals, court cases, rest, feasts, strangers, enemies, and the promised land.

Some of it can feel far away from modern life because it speaks into an ancient world.

But if we slow down, we begin to see the heart of it.

God is forming a redeemed people.

Israel has just come out of Egypt.

They know what oppression feels like.

They know what it is like when powerful people crush the weak.

They know what it is like when work becomes abuse.

They know what it is like when cries are ignored.

So now God begins teaching them:

You must not become another Egypt.

You must not use power the way Pharaoh used power.

You must not ignore the vulnerable.

You must not twist justice.

You must not forget mercy.

You belong to Me now, and that changes ordinary life.

Then Exodus 24 seals the covenant with blood.

Moses reads the words.

The people agree.

Blood is sprinkled on the altar and on the people.

And then Israel's leaders see a vision of God and eat before Him.

So today's reading is not just law.

It is covenant life.

It is justice.

It is worship.

It is relationship.

It is the rescued people learning what life with a holy God looks like.

## Exodus 21:1-11

**Scripture**

Now these are the judgments which thou shalt set before them.

If thou buy an Hebrew servant, six years he shall serve: and in the seventh he shall go out free for nothing.

If he came in by himself, he shall go out by himself.

If he were married, then his wife shall go out with him.

And if a man sell his daughter to be a maidservant, she shall not go out as the menservants do.

If she please not her master, who hath betrothed her to himself, then shall he let her be redeemed.

**Narrator**

Exodus 21 begins with laws about Hebrew servants.

For modern readers, that can feel uncomfortable immediately.

And it should make us slow down.

Israel has just come out of slavery in Egypt.

So why begin here?

Because God is immediately regulating power.

He is speaking into an ancient world where debt-servitude existed.

People could become servants because of poverty, debt, or survival needs.

But Israel is not allowed to treat vulnerable people as disposable property.

There are limits.

There is release.

There are protections.

A Hebrew servant serves six years, and in the seventh goes free.

That is not Egypt.

Pharaoh wanted permanent control.

God places boundaries around human power.

Then the passage gives special attention to a vulnerable woman in a household.

Again, this can feel strange to us because the ancient world was very different from ours.

But the point inside the text is protection.

She cannot simply be used and thrown away.

She must be treated with responsibility.

The bigger lesson is this:

God's redeemed people must not copy the oppression they escaped.

The people who were once slaves must now learn how to treat the weak with justice.

## Exodus 21:12-36

**Scripture**

He that smiteth a man, so that he die, shall be surely put to death.

And if a man lie not in wait, but God deliver him into his hand; then I will appoint thee a place whither he shall flee.

And if men strive together, and one smite another with a stone, or with his fist, and he die not, but keepeth his bed:

If he rise again, and walk abroad upon his staff, then shall he that smote him be quit: only he shall pay for the loss of his time.

And if an ox gore a man or a woman, that they die: then the ox shall be surely stoned.

**Narrator**

This section moves into violence, injury, responsibility, and negligence.

It may feel very detailed.

But the main idea is simple:

Human life matters.

Harm matters.

Responsibility matters.

Israel is not allowed to shrug at violence.

They are not allowed to pretend accidents never have consequences.

They are not allowed to let revenge become chaos either.

The law distinguishes between intentional murder and accidental death.

It distinguishes between direct violence and negligence.

It even deals with an animal that becomes dangerous.

That may sound odd at first, but it teaches something important.

If you know something under your care is dangerous, you are responsible for how it affects other people.

Biblical justice is not careless.

It asks:

What happened?

Was it intentional?

Was there warning?

Was someone negligent?

What repair is required?

This is very different from revenge.

Revenge often acts out of anger.

God's law seeks truth, proportion, accountability, and the protection of life.

Israel is learning that covenant life must take harm seriously.

## Exodus 22:1-15

**Scripture**

If a man shall steal an ox, or a sheep, and kill it, or sell it; he shall restore five oxen for an ox, and four sheep for a sheep.

If fire break out, and catch in thorns, so that the stacks of corn, or the standing corn, or the field, be consumed therewith; he that kindled the fire shall surely make restitution.

If a man shall deliver unto his neighbour money or stuff to keep, and it be stolen out of the man's house; if the thief be found, let him pay double.

**Narrator**

Exodus 22 begins with theft and restitution.

Restitution means making a wrong right as much as possible.

That is an important Bible idea.

When someone steals, the answer is not only, punish the thief.

The loss must be repaired.

The person who was harmed must be considered.

If an animal is stolen, there is repayment.

If a fire damages a field, the one responsible must make restitution.

If something is entrusted to a neighbor and lost or stolen, the case must be handled carefully.

These laws teach that holiness reaches ordinary life.

Your neighbor's animal matters.

Your neighbor's field matters.

Your neighbor's tools matter.

Your neighbor's trust matters.

Sometimes people think spiritual life is only prayer, worship, and big religious moments.

But Exodus shows that life with God also includes how you handle borrowed property.

How you repair damage.

How you treat what belongs to someone else.

How you tell the truth when responsibility is unclear.

God is building a community where wrongs are not ignored.

They are addressed.

And whenever possible, they are repaired.

## Exodus 22:16-31

**Scripture**

Thou shalt neither vex a stranger, nor oppress him: for ye were strangers in the land of Egypt.

Ye shall not afflict any widow, or fatherless child.

If thou afflict them in any wise, and they cry at all unto me, I will surely hear their cry.

If thou lend money to any of my people that is poor by thee, thou shalt not be to him as an usurer.

Ye shall be holy men unto me.

**Narrator**

This section brings together holiness and compassion.

Israel must reject spiritual corruption.

They must reject idolatry and practices that would pull them away from the LORD.

But then God speaks directly about vulnerable people.

The stranger.

The widow.

The fatherless child.

The poor.

And He says, Do not oppress them.

Why?

Because Israel knows what oppression feels like.

They were strangers in Egypt.

They were the weak ones.

They were the people crying under heavy burdens.

And God heard them.

Now the God who heard Israel's cry says, I will hear their cry too.

That is powerful.

Israel must not think election means they can ignore suffering.

Being God's people should make them more attentive to the vulnerable, not less.

The rescued people must become compassionate people.

And then God says, Ye shall be holy men unto me.

Holiness is not only avoiding idols.

Holiness also shows up in how you treat people with less power than you.

## Exodus 23:1-9

**Scripture**

Thou shalt not raise a false report: put not thine hand with the wicked to be an unrighteous witness.

Thou shalt not follow a multitude to do evil.

Neither shalt thou countenance a poor man in his cause.

Thou shalt not wrest the judgment of thy poor in his cause.

And thou shalt take no gift: for the gift blindeth the wise, and perverteth the words of the righteous.

Also thou shalt not oppress a stranger: for ye know the heart of a stranger.

**Narrator**

Exodus 23 begins with truth.

Do not spread false reports.

Do not join a wicked crowd.

Do not twist justice.

Do not accept bribes.

Do not oppress the stranger.

This is courtroom language, but it also reaches the whole community.

God's people must be people of truth.

One phrase is especially important:

Thou shalt not follow a multitude to do evil.

In plain English, do not do wrong just because the crowd is doing it.

Crowds can pressure people.

Crowds can make evil feel normal.

Crowds can make lies feel safe.

But God's law says truth is not decided by popularity.

The section also gives a balanced view of justice.

Do not favor the powerful through bribes.

But also do not twist justice just because someone is poor.

That may surprise us.

But biblical justice is not about choosing a side because we like that side.

It is about truth before God.

And then the law says if your enemy's animal is lost or burdened, help it.

That small detail is beautiful.

God's law trains people not to let hostility erase basic mercy.

## Exodus 23:10-19

**Scripture**

And six years thou shalt sow thy land, and shalt gather in the fruits thereof:

But the seventh year thou shalt let it rest and lie still; that the poor of thy people may eat.

Six days thou shalt do thy work, and on the seventh day thou shalt rest: that thine ox and thine ass may rest, and the son of thy handmaid, and the stranger, may be refreshed.

Three times thou shalt keep a feast unto me in the year.

Thou shalt keep the feast of unleavened bread.

And the feast of harvest.

And the feast of ingathering.

**Narrator**

God's law does not only shape courtrooms.

It shapes time.

The land is to rest in the seventh year.

The people are to rest on the seventh day.

Workers rest.

Animals rest.

The stranger rests.

Even the land rests.

This is more than personal self-care.

It is justice.

It is trust.

It is mercy built into time.

Israel had come out of Egypt, where Pharaoh demanded more bricks, more labor, more production.

But the LORD builds rest into the life of His people.

Then God gives feasts.

Unleavened bread.

Harvest.

Ingathering.

Israel's year will preach to them.

Their calendar will remind them of redemption, provision, and gratitude.

Their time will tell the story of God.

That is an important idea.

God does not want Israel to remember Him only when they feel spiritual.

He builds memory into work, rest, food, harvest, and worship.

Life with God has rhythm.

## Exodus 23:20-33

**Scripture**

Behold, I send an Angel before thee, to keep thee in the way, and to bring thee into the place which I have prepared.

Beware of him, and obey his voice, provoke him not; for he will not pardon your transgressions: for my name is in him.

But if thou shalt indeed obey his voice, and do all that I speak; then I will be an enemy unto thine enemies.

Thou shalt make no covenant with them, nor with their gods.

By little and little I will drive them out from before thee.

**Narrator**

After the laws, God looks ahead to the journey.

Israel is not staying at Sinai forever.

They are moving toward the promised land.

And God says He will send His Angel before them.

This is a mysterious and weighty passage.

The Angel will guard them.

The Angel will bring them to the place God prepared.

And God says, My name is in him.

That means this is not an ordinary messenger.

God's own authority and presence are connected to this Angel.

Israel must listen carefully.

The same God who brought them out will lead them forward.

But there is also a warning.

Do not worship the gods of the land.

Do not make covenant with them.

Do not let the promised land become a place where Israel adopts the worship of the nations.

Then God says the land will not be taken all at once.

By little and little.

That phrase matters.

Promise often unfolds through process.

God could give instantly, but He says this will happen gradually.

Israel must learn patience, obedience, and faith over time.

The promised future is real, but it will not be rushed on Israel's terms.

## Exodus 24:1-8

**Scripture**

And Moses came and told the people all the words of the LORD, and all the judgments.

And all the people answered with one voice, and said, All the words which the LORD hath said will we do.

And Moses wrote all the words of the LORD.

And he builded an altar under the hill, and twelve pillars, according to the twelve tribes of Israel.

And Moses took the blood, and sprinkled it on the people, and said, Behold the blood of the covenant, which the LORD hath made with you concerning all these words.

**Narrator**

Exodus 24 brings the covenant ceremony.

Moses tells the people the words of the LORD.

The people answer:

All the words which the LORD hath said will we do.

Then Moses writes the words.

That matters because covenant is not vague feeling.

It has words.

It has commitments.

It has testimony that can be read and remembered.

Moses builds an altar and twelve pillars.

The altar represents worship before God.

The twelve pillars represent the twelve tribes of Israel.

Then sacrifices are offered.

And Moses takes blood.

Some blood is placed on the altar.

Some blood is sprinkled on the people.

Blood may feel strange to modern readers.

But in the Bible, blood represents life.

This covenant is serious.

Life is involved.

Sacrifice is involved.

God and the people are being bound in covenant.

Moses calls it the blood of the covenant.

Later, when Jesus shares the cup with His disciples, He will use covenant blood language too.

That connection matters.

The Bible keeps teaching that life with God is not casual.

Covenant is holy, costly, and gracious.

## Exodus 24:9-18

**Scripture**

Then went up Moses, and Aaron, Nadab, and Abihu, and seventy of the elders of Israel:

And they saw the God of Israel.

And there was under his feet as it were a paved work of a sapphire stone, and as it were the body of heaven in his clearness.

And upon the nobles of the children of Israel he laid not his hand: also they saw God, and did eat and drink.

And Moses went up into the mount, and a cloud covered the mount.

And Moses was in the mount forty days and forty nights.

**Narrator**

This is one of the most stunning scenes in Exodus.

After the covenant is sealed, Moses, Aaron, Nadab, Abihu, and seventy elders go up.

And the text says they saw the God of Israel.

We should understand this carefully.

The Bible later makes clear that no one can see the fullness of God's glory and live.

So this is a limited, protected vision.

But it is still breathtaking.

Under His feet is something like sapphire pavement.

Clear like the heavens.

The imagery is royal, holy, beautiful, and overwhelming.

And then the text says God did not lay His hand on them.

They saw God, and they ate and drank.

That is covenant fellowship.

The leaders of Israel do not merely receive rules from a distance.

They share a meal in God's presence.

The God of thunder and fire also invites covenant communion.

That is a powerful balance.

Holy fear and fellowship.

Reverence and nearness.

Law and meal.

Then Moses goes higher into the cloud.

The glory of the LORD rests on Sinai.

To Israel, the glory looks like devouring fire.

And Moses enters the cloud for forty days and forty nights.

This prepares us for what comes next:

the tabernacle instructions.

God is going to teach Israel how He will dwell among them.

## Closing

Day 27 teaches that God's covenant reaches ordinary life.

It reaches servants.

It reaches injuries.

It reaches theft.

It reaches borrowed property.

It reaches strangers, widows, orphans, and the poor.

It reaches courtrooms.

It reaches enemies.

It reaches Sabbath, land, feasts, and worship.

God is teaching Israel that redemption must shape community.

They are not allowed to become another Egypt.

They must not use power to crush the weak.

They must not twist justice.

They must not ignore the cry of the vulnerable.

They must not worship the gods of the land.

They belong to the LORD.

And belonging to the LORD changes everything.

Then the covenant is sealed with blood.

The words are read.

The people respond.

The altar is built.

The blood is sprinkled.

And the leaders eat before God.

This is not cold religion.

This is covenant relationship.

Holy.

Serious.

Gracious.

And deeply personal.

For us, this day reminds us that faith is not only what we say on the mountain.

It is how we live after we come down.

It is how we treat people.

How we handle responsibility.

How we tell the truth.

How we use power.

How we remember the vulnerable.

How we worship.

How we rest.

And how we understand life with God.

The rescued people are becoming covenant people.

And the same God who brought them out of Egypt is now teaching them how to live near Him.
