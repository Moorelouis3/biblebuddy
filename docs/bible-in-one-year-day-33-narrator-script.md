# Bible In One Year - Day 33 Narrator Script

## Day 33 - Guilt, Consecration, and Priests

**Reading:** Leviticus 5 through 8
**Bible text:** King James Version, Leviticus 5-8
**Audio style:** One narrator, cinematic Bible teaching with Scripture reading

## Opening

Welcome to Day 33 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are reading Leviticus 5 through 8.

Leviticus is still answering the question Exodus left us with.

God's glory is now dwelling among His people.

So how can sinful people live near a holy God?

Yesterday we saw burnt offerings, grain offerings, peace offerings, and sin offerings.

Today Leviticus gets even more practical.

It talks about guilt.

Confession.

Restitution.

Priestly responsibility.

And the consecration of Aaron and his sons.

These chapters teach that guilt is not only a feeling inside the heart.

Sometimes guilt means something has gone wrong before God.

Sometimes it means damage has been done to another person.

And when damage has been done, repentance is not vague.

It moves toward repair.

Leviticus 5 through 8 also shows that worship near God's presence must be tended carefully.

The altar fire must keep burning.

The offerings must be handled God's way.

And the priests must be washed, clothed, anointed, and marked with blood before they serve.

This is not random ritual.

This is God teaching His people how to come near.

## Leviticus 5:1-13

Leviticus 5 verses 1 through 13.

**Scripture**

And if a soul sin, and hear the voice of swearing, and is a witness, whether he hath seen or known of it.

If he do not utter it, then he shall bear his iniquity.

Or if a soul touch any unclean thing, whether it be a carcase of an unclean beast, or a carcase of unclean cattle, or the carcase of unclean creeping things, and if it be hidden from him; he also shall be unclean, and guilty.

Or if a soul swear, pronouncing with his lips to do evil, or to do good, whatsoever it be that a man shall pronounce with an oath, and it be hid from him; when he knoweth of it, then he shall be guilty in one of these.

And it shall be, when he shall be guilty in one of these things, that he shall confess that he hath sinned in that thing.

And he shall bring his trespass offering unto the LORD for his sin which he hath sinned.

**Narrator**

Leviticus 5 begins with everyday guilt.

Not dramatic rebellion.

Not a golden calf.

Not Pharaoh hardening his heart.

Ordinary sins from ordinary life.

A person may know the truth and refuse to speak.

A person may become unclean and not realize it at first.

A person may make a careless oath with the mouth.

The chapter is showing us that guilt is not only about huge public sins.

Small words matter.

Hidden uncleanness matters.

Silence can matter.

Careless promises can matter.

And then the text says something very important.

He shall confess that he hath sinned in that thing.

Confession means the person stops hiding.

He names the sin before God.

The offering is not magic.

It is not pretending sin did not happen.

The worshiper comes honestly, and God provides a way for forgiveness.

And notice the mercy.

If someone cannot afford a lamb, he may bring birds.

If birds are still too much, he may bring fine flour.

Forgiveness is not only for people with money.

God makes a way for the poor to come near too.

## Leviticus 5:14-19

Leviticus 5 verses 14 through 19.

**Scripture**

And the LORD spake unto Moses, saying,

If a soul commit a trespass, and sin through ignorance, in the holy things of the LORD; then he shall bring for his trespass unto the LORD a ram without blemish out of the flocks.

And he shall make amends for the harm that he hath done in the holy thing, and shall add the fifth part thereto.

And the priest shall make an atonement for him with the ram of the trespass offering, and it shall be forgiven him.

**Narrator**

Now Leviticus introduces the trespass offering, also called the guilt offering.

This offering teaches that guilt can create debt.

If someone sins in the holy things of the LORD, the answer is not simply, I feel bad.

The text says he shall make amends.

That means repair has to happen.

Then he adds the fifth part.

That is extra restitution.

The point is simple and serious.

When sin causes damage, repentance moves toward making it right.

God provides atonement.

The priest makes atonement.

The person is forgiven.

But forgiveness does not teach Israel to ignore harm.

God cares about mercy.

And God cares about repair.

## Leviticus 6:1-7

Leviticus 6 verses 1 through 7.

**Scripture**

And the LORD spake unto Moses, saying,

If a soul sin, and commit a trespass against the LORD, and lie unto his neighbour in that which was delivered him to keep, or in fellowship, or in a thing taken away by violence, or hath deceived his neighbour.

Or have found that which was lost, and lieth concerning it, and sweareth falsely.

Then it shall be, because he hath sinned, and is guilty, that he shall restore that which he took violently away, or the thing which he hath deceitfully gotten.

And he shall restore it in the principal, and shall add the fifth part more thereto.

And the priest shall make an atonement for him before the LORD: and it shall be forgiven him.

**Narrator**

This section is very important because it joins two things together.

Sin against a neighbor is also trespass against the LORD.

The sins listed here are practical.

Lying.

Stealing.

Oppressing.

Keeping lost property.

Swearing falsely.

These are not only social problems.

They are spiritual problems too.

The guilty person must restore what was taken.

Then he adds the fifth part more.

Again, repentance is not only words.

It is repair.

God is teaching Israel that worship and justice belong together.

You cannot bring an offering to God while pretending the person you harmed does not matter.

## Leviticus 6:8-13

Leviticus 6 verses 8 through 13.

**Scripture**

And the LORD spake unto Moses, saying,

Command Aaron and his sons, saying, This is the law of the burnt offering.

The burnt offering shall be upon the hearth upon the altar all night unto the morning, and the fire of the altar shall be burning in it.

And the priest shall put on his linen garment, and his linen breeches shall he put upon his flesh.

And the fire upon the altar shall be burning in it; it shall not be put out.

The fire shall ever be burning upon the altar; it shall never go out.

**Narrator**

Now the focus turns to the priests.

The altar fire must not go out.

That repeated command matters.

The fire is not treated like a casual campfire.

It belongs to holy service.

The priest even changes garments to handle the ashes.

That detail teaches reverence.

Even what is left after the offering is handled with care.

The altar fire points to continual worship and continual priestly responsibility.

Israel's life with God is not occasional.

The fire keeps burning.

The service continues.

The priests tend what belongs to the LORD.

## Leviticus 6:14-30

Leviticus 6 verses 14 through 30.

**Scripture**

And this is the law of the meat offering.

The sons of Aaron shall offer it before the LORD, before the altar.

And the remainder thereof shall Aaron and his sons eat: with unleavened bread shall it be eaten in the holy place.

It shall not be baken with leaven. I have given it unto them for their portion of my offerings made by fire.

It is most holy, as is the sin offering, and as the trespass offering.

Whatsoever shall touch the flesh thereof shall be holy.

**Narrator**

This section shows the priestly side of the offerings.

Part of the grain offering is burned to the LORD.

The rest belongs to Aaron and his sons.

But even what the priests eat is not casual food.

It is eaten in the holy place.

The text says it is most holy.

That phrase is important.

Holy means set apart.

Most holy means it is treated with special seriousness because it belongs so closely to God's service.

The priests are being taught that nearness to holy things requires careful obedience.

Nothing around the altar is ordinary.

## Leviticus 7:1-10

Leviticus 7 verses 1 through 10.

**Scripture**

Likewise this is the law of the trespass offering: it is most holy.

In the place where they kill the burnt offering shall they kill the trespass offering.

And the blood thereof shall he sprinkle round about upon the altar.

And the priest shall burn them upon the altar for an offering made by fire unto the LORD.

Every male among the priests shall eat thereof: it shall be eaten in the holy place: it is most holy.

**Narrator**

The trespass offering is also called most holy.

That keeps guilt from feeling small.

Guilt is not just a private emotion.

It is something that must be brought before the holy God.

The blood is applied to the altar.

The fat is burned to the LORD.

And the priests receive their appointed portion.

This shows order.

God defines how guilt is handled.

God defines how worship is offered.

God defines how the priests are provided for.

The whole system teaches that forgiveness is mercy, but it is never careless.

## Leviticus 7:11-21

Leviticus 7 verses 11 through 21.

**Scripture**

And this is the law of the sacrifice of peace offerings, which he shall offer unto the LORD.

If he offer it for a thanksgiving, then he shall offer with the sacrifice of thanksgiving unleavened cakes mingled with oil.

But the remainder of the flesh of the sacrifice on the third day shall be burnt with fire.

And the flesh that toucheth any unclean thing shall not be eaten; it shall be burnt with fire.

**Narrator**

The peace offering is different from the offerings that focus mainly on guilt.

It is about fellowship.

Thanksgiving.

Vows.

Freewill worship.

The worshiper is not only coming because something is wrong.

He may also come because he is grateful.

But joyful worship still has boundaries.

The meat must be eaten at the right time.

Uncleanness must not be brought into the meal.

That teaches a balanced picture.

Life near God is joyful.

But it is not careless.

Fellowship with God is a gift.

And holy gifts must be handled God's way.

## Leviticus 7:22-38

Leviticus 7 verses 22 through 38.

**Scripture**

And the LORD spake unto Moses, saying,

Speak unto the children of Israel, saying, Ye shall eat no manner of fat, of ox, or of sheep, or of goat.

Moreover ye shall eat no manner of blood, whether it be of fowl or of beast.

For whosoever eateth the fat of the beast, of which men offer an offering made by fire unto the LORD, even the soul that eateth it shall be cut off from his people.

This is the portion of the anointing of Aaron, and of the anointing of his sons, out of the offerings of the LORD made by fire.

**Narrator**

Israel is commanded not to eat the fat or the blood.

The fat represents the rich portions offered to the LORD.

The blood represents life.

So Israel is learning to treat life as belonging to God.

The best is not swallowed casually.

Life is not treated as common.

The chapter also names the priestly portions.

God provides for the priests through the offerings He assigns.

The whole chapter teaches order.

Burnt offering.

Grain offering.

Sin offering.

Trespass offering.

Peace offering.

Consecration.

Each offering has meaning because God is the one who defines worship.

## Leviticus 8:1-13

Leviticus 8 verses 1 through 13.

**Scripture**

And the LORD spake unto Moses, saying,

Take Aaron and his sons with him, and the garments, and the anointing oil, and a bullock for the sin offering.

And gather thou all the congregation together unto the door of the tabernacle of the congregation.

And Moses brought Aaron and his sons, and washed them with water.

And he put upon him the coat, and girded him with the girdle, and clothed him with the robe.

And Moses took the anointing oil, and anointed the tabernacle and all that was therein.

**Narrator**

Leviticus 8 moves from offerings to priests.

Aaron and his sons are not self-appointed.

They are brought forward by God's command.

The whole congregation gathers.

That matters because priesthood affects the whole people.

Moses washes them with water.

He clothes Aaron in priestly garments.

He anoints the tabernacle, the altar, and Aaron.

These actions teach that priestly service requires cleansing and consecration.

The garments are not costumes.

They mark Aaron as a representative set apart for holy work.

Before priests serve near God's presence, God prepares them His way.

## Leviticus 8:14-36

Leviticus 8 verses 14 through 36.

**Scripture**

And he brought the bullock for the sin offering.

And Moses slew it, and took the blood, and put it upon the horns of the altar round about with his finger.

And he brought the other ram, the ram of consecration.

And Moses took of the blood of it, and put it upon the tip of Aaron's right ear, and upon the thumb of his right hand, and upon the great toe of his right foot.

And ye shall not go out of the door of the tabernacle of the congregation in seven days.

Therefore shall ye abide at the door of the tabernacle of the congregation day and night seven days, and keep the charge of the LORD, that ye die not.

**Narrator**

The ordination continues with blood.

Blood is placed on Aaron's right ear.

His right thumb.

And his right big toe.

That picture is strange at first, but it is powerful.

The ear is marked for hearing God.

The hand is marked for serving God.

The foot is marked for walking in God's ways.

Priestly service claims the whole person.

Aaron and his sons are sprinkled with blood and oil.

They remain at the entrance of the tent for seven days.

And the warning is serious.

They must obey the command of the LORD so they do not die.

Leviticus is teaching that serving near God's presence is a mercy.

But it is also holy.

## Closing

Day 33 teaches that guilt must come into the light.

Sin must be confessed.

Damage must be repaired.

Worship must be tended.

And priests must be consecrated before they serve.

The God who names guilt also provides atonement.

The God who commands restitution also provides forgiveness.

And the God who dwells among His people also teaches them how to live near Him.

Tomorrow, the priesthood begins publicly.

The glory of the LORD appears.

And then Leviticus gives one of its sharpest warnings about treating holy things casually.
