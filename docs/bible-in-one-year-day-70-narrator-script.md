# Day 70 Narrator Script

## Opening

Welcome to Day 70 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are reading First Samuel 7 through 10.

The title for today is Israel Asks for a King.

This day moves from repentance and help from the LORD to Israel's demand for a king like the nations.

That movement is important.

First Samuel 7 shows Israel returning to the LORD.

The LORD delivers them from the Philistines.

Samuel raises Ebenezer and says, Hitherto hath the LORD helped us.

But then First Samuel 8 shows the people asking for a king.

Their request grows partly out of real leadership failure.

Samuel's sons do not walk in his ways.

But the request also exposes something deeper.

They want to be like all the nations.

God says they have rejected Him from reigning over them.

Then Saul is introduced.

He looks impressive.

He is providentially led to Samuel.

He is anointed and publicly chosen.

There is hope in the chapter, but also tension.

The question is not only, will Israel have a king?

The deeper question is, will Israel trust the LORD as King?

## First Samuel 7

First Samuel 7 begins with the ark at Kirjathjearim.

Eleazar is sanctified to keep it.

Many years pass.

The house of Israel laments after the LORD.

Samuel speaks to all Israel.

If ye do return unto the LORD with all your hearts, then put away the strange gods and Ashtaroth from among you, and prepare your hearts unto the LORD, and serve him only.

That is real repentance language.

Returning to the LORD is not only feeling sad.

They must put away strange gods.

They must prepare their hearts.

They must serve the LORD only.

Israel gathers at Mizpeh.

They draw water and pour it out before the LORD.

They fast.

They confess, We have sinned against the LORD.

Samuel judges Israel at Mizpeh.

When the Philistines hear that Israel has gathered, they go up against them.

Israel is afraid.

They ask Samuel not to cease crying unto the LORD for them.

Samuel offers a sucking lamb for a burnt offering.

He cries unto the LORD.

The LORD hears him.

As Samuel is offering, the Philistines draw near.

But the LORD thunders with a great thunder on that day upon the Philistines.

They are discomfited before Israel.

Israel pursues them.

Then Samuel takes a stone and sets it between Mizpeh and Shen.

He calls the name of it Ebenezer.

Hitherto hath the LORD helped us.

That line matters.

Ebenezer is not a monument to Israel's strength.

It is a witness to the LORD's help.

The Philistines are subdued.

The cities are restored.

Samuel judges Israel all the days of his life.

He goes in circuit from Bethel to Gilgal to Mizpeh and returns to Ramah.

First Samuel 7 shows what faithful leadership looks like in this moment.

Samuel calls for repentance.

Samuel prays.

Samuel offers.

Samuel marks the LORD's help.

The people are delivered because the LORD helps them.

## First Samuel 8

First Samuel 8 begins with a sad turn.

Samuel is old.

He makes his sons judges over Israel.

But his sons do not walk in his ways.

They turn aside after lucre.

They take bribes.

They pervert judgment.

That is real leadership failure.

The elders of Israel gather and come to Samuel at Ramah.

They say Samuel is old and his sons do not walk in his ways.

Then they ask, Make us a king to judge us like all the nations.

The request displeases Samuel.

Samuel prays unto the LORD.

The LORD tells Samuel to hearken to the voice of the people.

But God also names the deeper issue.

They have not rejected thee, but they have rejected me, that I should not reign over them.

That is the heart of the chapter.

The people have a real problem with Samuel's sons.

But their desired solution reveals a deeper heart problem.

They want to be like the nations.

They are not asking first, how can we be faithful under the LORD?

They are asking for visible human rule like everyone else.

Samuel warns them about the manner of the king.

He will take sons.

He will take daughters.

He will take fields.

He will take vineyards.

He will take servants.

He will take flocks.

The repeated word is take.

The king they want will cost them.

They will cry out because of the king they have chosen.

But the people refuse to obey Samuel's voice.

They say, Nay; but we will have a king over us.

That we also may be like all the nations.

First Samuel 8 is not saying monarchy itself could never fit God's plan.

Deuteronomy had already given instructions for a king.

The problem here is the people's heart.

They want a king in a way that rejects the LORD's kingship and imitates the nations.

## First Samuel 9

First Samuel 9 introduces Saul.

He is from Benjamin.

He is a choice young man and goodly.

There is not among the children of Israel a goodlier person than he.

From his shoulders and upward he is higher than any of the people.

The description makes Saul look impressive.

But the story begins with lost donkeys.

Saul's father Kish loses his asses.

Saul and a servant go searching.

They pass through several regions and do not find them.

Saul wants to return because he worries his father will stop caring about the donkeys and worry about them.

The servant suggests going to the man of God in the city.

That man is Samuel.

What looks like a search for animals is actually God's providence leading Saul to the prophet.

Before Saul comes, the LORD has already told Samuel that a man from Benjamin will arrive.

Samuel is to anoint him to be captain over God's people Israel.

He will save them out of the hand of the Philistines.

When Samuel sees Saul, the LORD identifies him.

Samuel honors Saul at the feast.

Saul is surprised.

He says he is from Benjamin, the smallest of the tribes, and his family is least of all the families of Benjamin.

The chapter holds together appearance, providence, and humility.

Saul looks impressive, but he is not self-appointed.

He is led through ordinary circumstances to Samuel.

The donkeys are found.

The real issue is Saul's calling.

First Samuel 9 teaches that God's providence can move through ordinary frustrations.

Lost donkeys become the road to a throne.

But the chapter also prepares us to watch Saul carefully.

Looking kingly is not the same as being faithful.

## First Samuel 10

First Samuel 10 begins with private anointing.

Samuel takes a vial of oil and pours it upon Saul's head.

He kisses him.

He says the LORD has anointed Saul to be captain over His inheritance.

That phrase matters.

Israel is still the LORD's inheritance.

Saul's authority is under God.

Samuel gives Saul signs.

He will meet men who tell him the donkeys are found.

He will meet men carrying offerings.

He will meet a company of prophets.

The Spirit of the LORD will come upon him.

He will prophesy and be turned into another man.

The signs happen.

God gives Saul another heart.

The people ask whether Saul is also among the prophets.

Then Samuel calls the people together at Mizpeh.

He reminds them that the LORD brought Israel up out of Egypt and delivered them from all kingdoms and oppressors.

But they have rejected their God, who saved them out of all adversities and tribulations, and have asked for a king.

The public selection happens by lot.

Saul is chosen.

But Saul is hiding among the stuff.

They bring him out.

He stands taller than any of the people.

Samuel says, See ye him whom the LORD hath chosen.

The people shout, God save the king.

Samuel tells the people the manner of the kingdom and writes it in a book.

Saul goes home to Gibeah.

A band of men whose hearts God has touched go with him.

But the children of Belial despise him and bring no presents.

Saul holds his peace.

First Samuel 10 ends with hope and tension.

Saul is anointed.

The Spirit has come upon him.

God has given him another heart.

The people have shouted for the king.

But the request itself came from mixed motives.

Some support Saul.

Some despise him.

The story of kingship has begun, but the deeper test is still ahead.

Will the king obey the LORD?

Will the people trust the LORD?

## Full Lesson Review

Day 70 moves through four major moments.

Repentance.

Request.

Providence.

Anointing.

In First Samuel 7, Israel returns to the LORD.

They put away strange gods.

They confess sin.

Samuel prays.

The LORD thunders.

Ebenezer marks God's help.

That chapter shows the right direction.

Return to the LORD.

Serve Him only.

Remember that He has helped.

In First Samuel 8, Israel asks for a king.

The problem with Samuel's sons is real.

But the people's desire to be like the nations reveals unbelief.

They want visible security more than covenant trust.

They want a king who will judge them and fight their battles.

But the LORD has been their true deliverer.

In First Samuel 9, Saul appears through ordinary providence.

A search for lost donkeys becomes a meeting with Samuel.

God is guiding events before Saul understands them.

In First Samuel 10, Saul is anointed and publicly chosen.

The Spirit of the LORD comes upon him.

God gives him another heart.

The people shout.

But tension remains.

This is why Day 70 is so important.

It does not let us treat leadership as simple.

Bad leadership creates real pain.

The desire for better leadership can still come from a heart that does not trust God.

Saul can be truly chosen and still need to obey.

The people can receive a king and still need to remember that the LORD is King.

Ebenezer should stand over the whole day.

Hitherto hath the LORD helped us.

If Israel remembers that, kingship can be received under God's rule.

If Israel forgets that, kingship will become another place where the heart turns from the LORD.

The issue is trust.

Will Israel trust the LORD who thunders for them?

Will they trust the LORD who rules over them?

Will Saul trust the LORD who anoints him?

The chapters ahead will answer those questions.

## Closing

That is Day 70.

First Samuel 7 through 10 moves from repentance at Mizpeh to Israel's demand for a king, then to Saul's meeting with Samuel and Saul's anointing.

The LORD has helped Israel.

The people still ask to be like the nations.

Saul begins with real promise.

But the deeper question remains: will Israel and her king trust the LORD as King?

## Final Meditation

Day 70 should be held together by the stone Samuel names Ebenezer.

Hitherto hath the LORD helped us.

That sentence is simple, but it should shape how the reader hears everything that follows.

Israel has a history of help from the LORD.

He delivered them from Egypt.

He brought them through the wilderness.

He gave them the land.

He raised judges.

He answered repentance at Mizpeh.

He thundered against the Philistines.

The LORD has helped.

That is why First Samuel 8 is so serious.

The people are not wrong to notice corruption in Samuel's sons.

Bribery and perverted judgment are real evils.

God does not ask His people to pretend bad leadership is good.

But Israel's request moves beyond grief over injustice.

They ask for a king like all the nations.

They want visible sameness with the world around them.

They want a king to judge them and fight their battles.

But the LORD has been the One fighting for them.

The LORD has been the One judging and saving.

This is why God says they have rejected Him from reigning over them.

The danger is not leadership structure alone.

The danger is misplaced trust.

A king may be given.

But if the people's heart trusts the king instead of the LORD, the king becomes a sign of unbelief.

Samuel's warning about taking is also important.

The king will take sons.

Take daughters.

Take fields.

Take vineyards.

Take servants.

Take flocks.

The repetition teaches that the king they desire will not simply give them security.

He will demand from them.

Human power often promises protection but practices taking.

The LORD had delivered and helped.

The king will take.

The contrast should be felt.

Then Saul's story begins in an ordinary way.

Lost donkeys.

A worried father.

A servant with a suggestion.

A journey through towns.

A meeting with Samuel.

The chapter reminds us that God's providence often works through ordinary frustrations.

Saul does not set out looking for a throne.

He looks for animals.

But the LORD has already told Samuel about him.

That means the monarchy begins under God's sovereignty, even though the people's request was mixed.

This is one of the complicated beauties of Scripture.

God can judge a wrong-hearted request and still rule over what comes next.

God can give Saul truly, while still exposing Israel's misplaced desire.

God can work through human motives without approving every motive.

First Samuel 10 adds even more tension.

Saul is anointed.

The Spirit comes upon him.

God gives him another heart.

The signs confirm the word.

The people shout for the king.

All of this is real.

Saul is not a fake king in the beginning.

His calling is real.

His opportunity is real.

The help of God is real.

But the test will be obedience.

Will Saul live under the word of the LORD?

Will he remember that Israel is the LORD's inheritance?

Will he rule as one who has received authority, not seized it?

Will the people remember Ebenezer once they have a king to look at?

That is the question Day 70 leaves with us.

It is easy to remember the LORD's help when a stone is freshly raised.

It is harder to remember when human power becomes visible and impressive.

Saul is tall.

Saul looks kingly.

But Israel's future will not rest on Saul's height.

It will rest on the LORD's faithfulness and the king's obedience to Him.

For the listener, Day 70 teaches a personal lesson too.

Good gifts can become dangerous when they become replacements for trust in God.

Leadership is good when it serves under the LORD.

Security is good when it does not become an idol.

Wisdom is good when it begins with God's word.

But when the heart says, make us like the nations, it is drifting from covenant identity.

The LORD's people are not called to be impressive copies of the world.

They are called to belong to the LORD.

So remember Ebenezer.

The LORD has helped.

The LORD is King.

Any human leader must stand under Him.

## Chapter By Chapter Takeaways

First Samuel 7 teaches repentance and remembrance.

Israel must put away strange gods.

Israel must prepare the heart for the LORD.

Israel must serve Him only.

Samuel prays.

The LORD thunders.

Ebenezer is raised.

The stone teaches that the LORD's help should be remembered, not replaced by confidence in human power.

First Samuel 8 teaches that a real problem can still lead to a wrong-hearted request.

Samuel's sons are corrupt.

The elders are not inventing that problem.

But wanting a king like the nations exposes a deeper desire.

They are tempted to trade covenant trust for visible sameness.

The warning about what the king will take should make the reader cautious about human power.

First Samuel 9 teaches hidden providence.

Saul searches for donkeys.

God is arranging a meeting.

Saul sees an ordinary errand.

The LORD sees the beginning of kingship.

The chapter teaches that God's guidance can be present in ordinary roads, delays, conversations, and frustrations.

First Samuel 10 teaches that Saul's kingship begins with real calling and real tension.

Samuel anoints him.

The Spirit comes upon him.

God gives him another heart.

The people publicly receive him.

Yet the earlier warning remains.

The king must still live under the LORD.

Together, these chapters teach that leadership must never replace trust in God.

The LORD helped Israel before Saul.

The LORD must rule over Israel after Saul.

The king is not the deepest hope.

The LORD is.

But the people's solution reveals their heart.

They want a king like all the nations.

But none of that removes the need for obedience.

The monarchy begins under God's authority, and it must remain there.

The people may have a king.

But the LORD remains King.

If Israel forgets that, the gift will become a snare.

Hitherto hath the LORD helped us.

Make us a king to judge us like all the nations.

The first sentence remembers God's help.

The second sentence reaches for national sameness.

The tension between those sentences explains the day.

Israel has seen the LORD deliver.

Israel has also seen human leadership fail.

The right response would be to seek the LORD's faithful order while remembering His kingship.

But the people want the kind of visible security the nations have.

That desire will shape the chapters ahead.

He is impressive.

His rise is full of providence.

But the question is not whether Saul looks like a king.

The question is whether Saul will obey like a servant of the LORD.

That question matters for every leader in Scripture.

Godly leadership is never merely height, charm, military ability, or public approval.

Godly leadership is faithfulness under God's word.

First Samuel 10 gives Saul real gifts and a real beginning.

The Spirit comes upon him.

God gives him another heart.

The people shout.

But the reader should remember Samuel's warning.

The king will take.

The people may later cry out.

So Day 70 is hopeful, but not naive.

The LORD is still ruling.

Saul is truly chosen.

Israel receives what it asked for.

But the heart issue remains.

Will they remember Ebenezer?

Will they remember the LORD helped them?

Will they remember that no king can replace God?

That is the question the next days will keep testing.

The final lesson is that Israel's request for a king must be read with memory.

If the people remember Egypt, they know the LORD delivered them.

If they remember the wilderness, they know the LORD sustained them.

If they remember Judges, they know the LORD raised deliverers again and again.

If they remember Mizpeh, they know the LORD thundered against the Philistines.

If they remember Ebenezer, they know the LORD helped them this far.

Saul's anointing is mercy because the LORD still governs the story.

Every failure will expose what the heart trusts.

So Day 70 is not only history.

It is a lesson in misplaced confidence.

Good leadership matters.

Justice matters.

Public order matters.

But none of those can replace the LORD.

Israel may have a king, but Israel must not forget the King above the king.
