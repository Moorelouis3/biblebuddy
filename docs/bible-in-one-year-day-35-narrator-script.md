# Bible In One Year - Day 35 Narrator Script

## Day 35 - Cleansing and the Day of Atonement

**Reading:** Leviticus 13 through 16
**Bible text:** King James Version, Leviticus 13-16
**Audio style:** One narrator, cinematic Bible teaching with Scripture reading

## Opening

Welcome to Day 35 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are reading Leviticus 13 through 16.

This is one of the hardest readings in Leviticus for modern readers.

Skin disease.

Garments.

Houses.

Bodily discharges.

Isolation.

Washing.

Waiting.

Blood.

The mercy seat.

The scapegoat.

At first, these chapters can feel uncomfortable or far away from daily life.

But remember the story.

God's holy presence is dwelling in the middle of Israel's camp.

So Israel must learn the difference between clean and unclean.

Not because God loves shame.

Not because bodies are bad.

But because life near a holy God requires careful attention to death, disease, decay, blood, disorder, sin, and restoration.

Leviticus 13 shows examination.

Leviticus 14 shows cleansing and return.

Leviticus 15 shows private body matters brought under God's holy care.

And Leviticus 16 brings everything to a high point with the Day of Atonement.

One goat dies.

One goat carries sin away.

And God provides cleansing deep enough for His people to remain near Him.

## Leviticus 13:1-46

Leviticus 13 verses 1 through 46.

**Scripture**

And the LORD spake unto Moses and Aaron, saying,

When a man shall have in the skin of his flesh a rising, a scab, or bright spot, and it be in the skin of his flesh like the plague of leprosy; then he shall be brought unto Aaron the priest, or unto one of his sons the priests.

And the priest shall look on the plague in the skin of the flesh.

And if the plague be deeper than the skin of his flesh, it is a plague of leprosy.

And the priest shall look on him, and pronounce him unclean.

And if the bright spot be white in the skin of his flesh, and in sight be not deeper than the skin, then the priest shall shut up him that hath the plague seven days.

And the priest shall look on him again the seventh day.

And the leper in whom the plague is, his clothes shall be rent, and his head bare.

And he shall put a covering upon his upper lip, and shall cry, Unclean, unclean.

He shall dwell alone; without the camp shall his habitation be.

**Narrator**

Leviticus 13 gives priests instructions for examining serious skin conditions.

The repeated phrase is important.

The priest shall look.

He does not guess.

He does not panic.

He examines carefully.

Sometimes the person is declared unclean.

Sometimes the person is shut up for seven days and examined again.

Sometimes the condition is declared clean.

This is not the priest acting like a modern doctor.

He is guarding the boundary between clean and unclean in a camp where God's presence dwells.

Being declared unclean does not automatically mean the person committed a moral sin.

That matters.

Uncleanness often means the person is not fit to enter holy space until cleansing happens.

The end of the section is painful.

The unclean person lives outside the camp.

That separation is serious.

But Leviticus 14 will show that outside the camp is not necessarily forever.

God also provides a way back.

## Leviticus 13:47-59

Leviticus 13 verses 47 through 59.

**Scripture**

The garment also that the plague of leprosy is in, whether it be a woollen garment, or a linen garment.

Whether it be in the warp, or woof; of linen, or of woollen; whether in a skin, or in any thing made of skin.

And if the plague be greenish or reddish in the garment, or in the skin, it is a plague of leprosy, and shall be shewed unto the priest.

And the priest shall look upon the plague, and shut up it that hath the plague seven days.

And if the plague be spread in the garment, either in the warp, or in the woof, or in a skin, that plague is a fretting leprosy; it is unclean.

He shall therefore burn that garment.

**Narrator**

Now the chapter moves from bodies to garments.

Fabric.

Leather.

Clothing.

Ordinary things people live with.

If a spreading mark appears in a garment, the priest examines it.

The garment may be isolated.

It may be washed.

Damaged parts may be torn out.

If the problem keeps spreading, it is burned.

That may sound strange, but the lesson is clear.

Uncleanness can spread into ordinary life.

God is teaching Israel not to ignore what spreads corruption.

Sometimes restoration is possible.

Sometimes removal is necessary.

Holiness is not only about the altar.

It reaches the things people wear and use every day.

## Leviticus 14:1-32

Leviticus 14 verses 1 through 32.

**Scripture**

And the LORD spake unto Moses, saying,

This shall be the law of the leper in the day of his cleansing: He shall be brought unto the priest.

And the priest shall go forth out of the camp; and the priest shall look, and, behold, if the plague of leprosy be healed in the leper.

Then shall the priest command to take for him that is to be cleansed two birds alive and clean, and cedar wood, and scarlet, and hyssop.

As for the living bird, he shall take it, and the cedar wood, and the scarlet, and the hyssop, and shall dip them in the blood of the bird that was killed over the running water.

And he shall sprinkle upon him that is to be cleansed from the leprosy seven times, and shall pronounce him clean, and shall let the living bird loose into the open field.

**Narrator**

Leviticus 14 is good news after the heaviness of Leviticus 13.

If the person is healed, the priest goes outside the camp.

That detail is beautiful.

The priest goes to the person who has been living outside.

He does not wait only at the center.

He goes out to examine and begin restoration.

The cleansing ceremony uses two birds.

Cedar wood.

Scarlet.

Hyssop.

Running water.

One bird dies.

The living bird is released into the open field.

The picture is powerful.

Death and release.

Cleansing and freedom.

The person who was outside the camp can begin moving back toward community and worship.

God does not only identify uncleanness.

He provides restoration.

## Leviticus 14:33-57

Leviticus 14 verses 33 through 57.

**Scripture**

And the LORD spake unto Moses and unto Aaron, saying,

When ye be come into the land of Canaan, which I give to you for a possession, and I put the plague of leprosy in a house of the land of your possession.

And he that owneth the house shall come and tell the priest.

Then the priest shall command that they empty the house, before the priest go into it to see the plague.

And he shall look on the plague.

And if the plague come again, and break out in the house, after that he hath taken away the stones, then the priest shall come and look.

And he shall break down the house, the stones of it, and the timber thereof.

This is the law for all manner of plague of leprosy, and scall.

To teach when it is unclean, and when it is clean.

**Narrator**

Now Leviticus looks ahead to houses in the land of Canaan.

That means these laws are not only for the wilderness.

They anticipate life in the promised land.

A house can be inspected.

Closed.

Scraped.

Repaired.

Cleansed.

Or, if the problem keeps returning, torn down.

Again, the lesson is not panic.

The priest looks carefully.

Restoration is attempted.

But spreading corruption cannot be ignored forever.

This section teaches that holiness belongs in homes too.

Israel's houses matter because God's people are called to live holy lives in the land He gives them.

## Leviticus 15:1-33

Leviticus 15 verses 1 through 33.

**Scripture**

And the LORD spake unto Moses and to Aaron, saying,

Speak unto the children of Israel, and say unto them, When any man hath a running issue out of his flesh, because of his issue he is unclean.

Every bed, whereon he lieth that hath the issue, is unclean.

And whomsoever he toucheth that hath the issue, and hath not rinsed his hands in water, he shall wash his clothes, and bathe himself in water, and be unclean until the even.

And when he that hath an issue is cleansed of his issue; then he shall number to himself seven days for his cleansing, and wash his clothes.

And the priest shall make an atonement for him before the LORD for his issue.

Thus shall ye separate the children of Israel from their uncleanness; that they die not in their uncleanness, when they defile my tabernacle that is among them.

**Narrator**

Leviticus 15 deals with bodily discharges.

This can feel uncomfortable.

But the chapter teaches that private body matters are still lived before a holy God.

Some discharges are abnormal.

Some are part of ordinary married life or the monthly cycle.

The laws include washing.

Waiting.

Counting days.

And sometimes bringing offerings after cleansing.

Again, impurity is not always moral sin.

Often it marks contact with bodily fluids, mortality, weakness, and boundaries around holy space.

The final reason is clear.

God's tabernacle is among Israel.

Because God dwells in the camp, uncleanness cannot be treated as if it does not matter.

But God also gives a path back.

## Leviticus 16:1-10

Leviticus 16 verses 1 through 10.

**Scripture**

And the LORD spake unto Moses after the death of the two sons of Aaron, when they offered before the LORD, and died.

And the LORD said unto Moses, Speak unto Aaron thy brother, that he come not at all times into the holy place within the veil before the mercy seat, which is upon the ark; that he die not.

Thus shall Aaron come into the holy place: with a young bullock for a sin offering, and a ram for a burnt offering.

And he shall take of the congregation of the children of Israel two kids of the goats for a sin offering, and one ram for a burnt offering.

And Aaron shall cast lots upon the two goats; one lot for the LORD, and the other lot for the scapegoat.

**Narrator**

Leviticus 16 begins by remembering the death of Nadab and Abihu.

That is not random.

This chapter explains how Aaron may safely enter the Most Holy Place.

He cannot come whenever he wants.

He cannot come however he wants.

He must come God's way.

The mercy seat is behind the veil.

The ark is there.

The holy presence of God is not approached casually.

Aaron brings a bull for himself.

That means even the high priest needs atonement.

Then two goats are brought for the people.

One is for the LORD.

One is for the scapegoat.

The Day of Atonement is beginning.

## Leviticus 16:11-28

Leviticus 16 verses 11 through 28.

**Scripture**

And Aaron shall bring the bullock of the sin offering, which is for himself, and shall make an atonement for himself, and for his house.

And he shall take a censer full of burning coals of fire from off the altar before the LORD, and his hands full of sweet incense beaten small, and bring it within the veil.

And he shall take of the blood of the bullock, and sprinkle it with his finger upon the mercy seat eastward.

Then shall he kill the goat of the sin offering, that is for the people, and bring his blood within the veil.

And he shall make an atonement for the holy place, because of the uncleanness of the children of Israel.

And Aaron shall lay both his hands upon the head of the live goat, and confess over him all the iniquities of the children of Israel.

And the goat shall bear upon him all their iniquities unto a land not inhabited.

**Narrator**

This is the heart of the Day of Atonement.

Aaron first makes atonement for himself and his house.

Then he brings incense within the veil so the cloud covers the mercy seat.

Blood is sprinkled on and before the mercy seat.

The blood is not decorative.

It speaks of life before God.

Atonement is made for the holy place because of Israel's uncleanness and sins.

That is serious.

Even the sanctuary must be cleansed because sinful people live near it.

Then comes the live goat.

Aaron lays both hands on its head.

He confesses over it all the iniquities of the children of Israel.

The goat carries those iniquities away into a land not inhabited.

One goat dies.

One goat carries sin away.

Together they show atonement and removal.

Sin is covered.

Sin is carried away.

God provides cleansing deep enough for His people to remain near Him.

## Leviticus 16:29-34

Leviticus 16 verses 29 through 34.

**Scripture**

And this shall be a statute for ever unto you: that in the seventh month, on the tenth day of the month, ye shall afflict your souls, and do no work at all.

For on that day shall the priest make an atonement for you, to cleanse you, that ye may be clean from all your sins before the LORD.

It shall be a sabbath of rest unto you, and ye shall afflict your souls, by a statute for ever.

And this shall be an everlasting statute unto you, to make an atonement for the children of Israel for all their sins once a year.

**Narrator**

The Day of Atonement becomes a yearly statute for Israel.

The people afflict their souls.

That means they humble themselves before God.

They do no work.

Why?

Because this day is about receiving cleansing from the LORD.

They do not produce atonement by their own effort.

God provides it through the priestly work He commands.

The text says they may be clean from all their sins before the LORD.

That is the great hope of the chapter.

Not partial cleansing.

Not pretending.

Not hiding.

Clean before the LORD.

Once a year, Israel is reminded that life near God's presence requires deep cleansing only God can provide.

## Closing

Day 35 teaches that God does not ignore uncleanness, sin, disease, decay, or disorder.

But He also does not leave His people outside forever.

Leviticus 13 examines uncleanness.

Leviticus 14 shows restoration.

Leviticus 15 gives a path back from bodily impurity.

And Leviticus 16 brings the whole system to the Day of Atonement.

The priest enters with blood.

The mercy seat is sprinkled.

The people's sins are confessed.

The goat carries them away.

This is one of the clearest pictures in the Old Testament of sin being covered and removed.

And it prepares the Bible reader for something even greater.

One day, the story will point beyond goats and priests and repeated sacrifices.

It will point to the final cleansing God provides through Christ.
