# Day 76 Narrator Script

## Opening

Welcome to Day 76 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are reading First Samuel 31 and Second Samuel 1 through 3.

The title for today is Saul Falls and David's Kingdom Begins.

This day is a hinge in the kingdom story.

Saul dies on Mount Gilboa.

Jonathan dies with him.

Israel is defeated by the Philistines.

David hears the news, mourns, and refuses to rejoice over Saul's death.

Then David asks the LORD where he should go.

He is anointed king over Judah in Hebron.

But the kingdom is not immediately united.

Ishbosheth, Saul's son, is made king over Israel by Abner.

Civil war begins between the house of Saul and the house of David.

So this is not a clean triumph scene.

David does not dance over Saul's grave.

David does not seize the whole nation in one impatient move.

The throne begins with grief, waiting, divided loyalty, and the slow working out of God's promise.

## First Samuel 31

First Samuel 31 brings Saul's story to its tragic end.

The Philistines fight against Israel.

The men of Israel flee.

They fall down slain in Mount Gilboa.

The Philistines follow hard upon Saul and upon his sons.

Jonathan, Abinadab, and Malchishua are killed.

Then the battle goes sore against Saul.

The archers hit him, and he is sore wounded.

Saul tells his armourbearer to draw his sword and thrust him through, lest the uncircumcised come and abuse him.

His armourbearer will not do it because he is sore afraid.

So Saul takes a sword and falls upon it.

When the armourbearer sees Saul is dead, he falls likewise upon his sword and dies with him.

The chapter says Saul, his three sons, his armourbearer, and all his men die that same day together.

This is the end of the first king Israel demanded.

Saul began with height, public promise, and early victory.

But he would not listen to the word of the LORD.

He feared people.

He grasped for control.

He hunted David.

He killed the priests at Nob.

He sought forbidden counsel at Endor.

Now he dies in defeat.

The men of Israel on the other side of the valley and across Jordan see that Israel has fled and that Saul and his sons are dead.

They forsake the cities and flee.

The Philistines come and dwell in them.

The next day the Philistines come to strip the slain.

They find Saul and his sons fallen in Mount Gilboa.

They cut off Saul's head, strip off his armour, and send word into the land of the Philistines to publish the news in the house of their idols and among the people.

They put Saul's armour in the house of Ashtaroth and fasten his body to the wall of Bethshan.

This is public humiliation.

But the chapter does not end with Philistine shame.

When the inhabitants of Jabeshgilead hear what the Philistines have done, valiant men arise.

They travel all night.

They take the body of Saul and the bodies of his sons from the wall of Bethshan.

They come to Jabesh, burn the bodies, take the bones, bury them under a tree, and fast seven days.

That action matters.

Jabeshgilead remembers Saul's earlier rescue.

They honor the fallen king and his sons.

The chapter is full of judgment, but it still pauses for courage, gratitude, and burial dignity.

## Second Samuel 1

Second Samuel opens after Saul's death.

David has returned from the slaughter of the Amalekites and stayed two days in Ziklag.

On the third day a man comes from Saul's camp with his clothes rent and earth upon his head.

He falls to the earth and does obeisance.

David asks where he came from.

The man says he escaped out of the camp of Israel.

David asks how the matter went.

The man reports that the people fled, many fell, and Saul and Jonathan are dead.

David asks how he knows.

The young man says he happened by chance upon Mount Gilboa and saw Saul leaning upon his spear.

He claims Saul asked him to stand upon him and slay him.

He says he killed Saul because Saul could not live after he had fallen.

He brings Saul's crown and bracelet to David.

Whether the man is telling the truth or trying to profit from Saul's death, David's response is clear.

David takes hold of his clothes and rends them.

The men with him do the same.

They mourn, weep, and fast until evening for Saul, for Jonathan, for the people of the LORD, and for the house of Israel.

David does not celebrate.

He grieves.

Then David questions the young Amalekite.

How was he not afraid to stretch forth his hand to destroy the LORD's anointed?

David commands that he be put to death.

David had refused again and again to kill Saul.

Now he will not honor a man who claims to have killed Saul.

The chapter then gives David's lament.

The beauty of Israel is slain upon the high places.

How are the mighty fallen.

Tell it not in Gath.

Publish it not in the streets of Askelon.

David grieves that Philistine daughters would rejoice over Israel's fall.

He calls for the mountains of Gilboa to have no dew or rain.

He remembers Saul and Jonathan as lovely and pleasant in their lives, and in death not divided.

He calls the daughters of Israel to weep over Saul.

Then he turns to Jonathan.

I am distressed for thee, my brother Jonathan.

Very pleasant hast thou been unto me.

Thy love to me was wonderful.

How are the mighty fallen, and the weapons of war perished.

This lament shows David's heart.

He can name tragedy without becoming bitter.

He can honor Saul without pretending Saul was innocent.

He can grieve Jonathan with deep personal love.

The kingdom begins under the sound of mourning, not triumphal noise.

## Second Samuel 2

Second Samuel 2 begins with David asking the LORD, Shall I go up into any of the cities of Judah?

The LORD says, Go up.

David asks, Whither shall I go up?

The LORD answers, Unto Hebron.

David goes with his wives and his men.

The men of Judah come and anoint David king over the house of Judah.

This matters because David still seeks the LORD even after Saul is dead.

He does not assume the next step.

He asks.

He waits for direction.

He goes where God sends him.

David is told that the men of Jabeshgilead buried Saul.

David sends messengers and blesses them for showing kindness to their lord Saul.

He asks the LORD to show kindness and truth to them, and he promises to requite them for what they have done.

David is not threatened by people honoring Saul.

He blesses courage and loyalty where he sees it.

But the kingdom remains divided.

Abner, the captain of Saul's host, takes Ishbosheth, Saul's son, and makes him king over Israel.

Ishbosheth reigns over Gilead, the Ashurites, Jezreel, Ephraim, Benjamin, and all Israel.

The house of Judah follows David.

Then Abner and the servants of Ishbosheth meet Joab and the servants of David by the pool of Gibeon.

The meeting becomes violent.

Twelve men from Benjamin and twelve from David's servants arise and fight.

Each catches his fellow by the head and thrusts his sword in his fellow's side.

They fall down together.

The place is called Helkathhazzurim.

The battle is sore that day.

Abner is beaten before the servants of David.

Asahel, Joab's brother, pursues Abner.

Asahel is swift of foot and will not turn aside.

Abner warns him to turn aside, take the armour of a young man, and stop chasing him.

Asahel refuses.

Abner warns him again, asking how he could hold up his face to Joab if he kills Asahel.

Still Asahel refuses.

Abner strikes him with the hinder end of the spear, and Asahel dies.

Everyone who comes to the place stands still.

Joab and Abishai pursue Abner.

The children of Benjamin gather behind Abner.

Abner calls to Joab, asking whether the sword shall devour forever.

He asks if Joab knows it will be bitterness in the latter end.

Joab blows a trumpet.

The people stop pursuing Israel.

The chapter ends with losses counted.

David's servants have struck many men of Benjamin and Abner's men, but Asahel is dead.

They bury Asahel in Bethlehem.

The war has begun, and it already tastes bitter.

## Second Samuel 3

Second Samuel 3 says there is long war between the house of Saul and the house of David.

David waxes stronger and stronger.

The house of Saul waxes weaker and weaker.

The chapter lists sons born to David in Hebron.

Then conflict erupts between Ishbosheth and Abner.

Ishbosheth questions Abner about Rizpah, Saul's concubine.

Abner becomes very angry.

He asks whether he is a dog's head.

He says he has shown kindness to the house of Saul and has not delivered Ishbosheth into David's hand.

Now he declares he will transfer the kingdom from the house of Saul and set up David's throne over Israel and Judah.

Ishbosheth cannot answer Abner a word because he fears him.

Abner sends messengers to David and offers covenant.

David agrees, but he requires Michal, Saul's daughter, to be returned to him.

Ishbosheth sends and takes her from Phaltiel, her husband.

Phaltiel goes weeping behind her until Abner tells him to return.

The political movement toward David's kingdom carries personal sorrow inside it.

Abner speaks with the elders of Israel and with Benjamin.

He says they sought David in times past to be king over them.

Now they should do it, because the LORD has spoken of David, saying He would save His people by David's hand.

Abner comes to David in Hebron.

David makes a feast for him.

Abner says he will gather all Israel to David so they may make a league with him.

David sends Abner away in peace.

But Joab returns from a raid and hears that Abner came and was sent away.

Joab rebukes David.

Then Joab sends messengers after Abner without David knowing.

Abner returns to Hebron.

Joab takes him aside in the gate to speak quietly and kills him for the blood of Asahel.

David hears it and declares himself and his kingdom guiltless before the LORD from the blood of Abner.

David commands Joab and all the people to mourn.

David follows the bier.

He lifts up his voice and weeps at Abner's grave.

He laments, Died Abner as a fool dieth?

The people notice David's grief and understand that the killing was not of the king.

David says he is weak that day, though anointed king, and the sons of Zeruiah are too hard for him.

The LORD shall reward the doer of evil according to his wickedness.

That sentence shows David's burden.

He is anointed, but he is not yet surrounded by peace.

He has power, but not simple control.

He must lead through grief, vengeance, divided loyalties, and violent men.

## Full Lesson Review

Day 76 shows the kingdom changing hands, but not in a simple way.

Saul falls under judgment.

Jonathan dies in battle.

Israel is shamed before the Philistines.

Jabeshgilead honors Saul's body.

David mourns Saul and Jonathan.

David asks the LORD before moving.

David becomes king over Judah.

Abner establishes Ishbosheth over Israel.

Civil war begins.

Abner kills Asahel.

Joab murders Abner.

David mourns again.

The repeated grief is important.

The rise of David's kingdom is not built on David rejoicing over death.

It is built under the fear of the LORD.

David refuses to touch Saul.

David refuses to reward a man who claims to have killed Saul.

David blesses Jabeshgilead for honoring Saul.

David mourns Abner publicly and separates himself from Joab's vengeance.

This is the shape of a king learning to rule before God.

## Key Connections

First, Saul's end proves that rejecting God's word has real consequences.

His death is tragic, not satisfying.

Second, David's grief shows a heart different from Saul's jealousy.

David does not need to hate Saul in order to receive the throne God promised.

Third, Hebron shows the promise beginning, but not fully completed.

David is king over Judah, while Israel remains divided.

Fourth, Joab's murder of Abner shows that David's kingdom will face trouble from inside his own circle.

The battle is not only outside.

Sin can sit very near the throne.

## Guided Listening Review

Slow down over David's responses in this day.

When Saul dies, David mourns.

That matters because David had every human reason to feel relief.

Saul hunted him, threw spears at him, drove him into exile, and made his life unstable for years.

But David still calls Saul the LORD's anointed.

David's grief shows that Saul's hatred has not turned him into a vengeful man.

When the Amalekite brings Saul's crown and bracelet, he likely expects reward.

He thinks David will hear the news as political opportunity.

But David hears bloodguilt.

That teaches the reader how David understands the throne.

The kingdom is not a prize to grab by any available means.

It is a calling under God.

Then notice Hebron.

David asks the LORD where to go.

He does not say, Saul is dead, so I can finally do whatever I want.

He asks.

That small act is a major difference between David and Saul.

Saul often acted from pressure and then tried to explain himself.

David pauses for direction.

At Hebron, Judah anoints him, but only Judah.

The promise is real, but the fulfillment is partial.

God may open the next door without opening every door at once.

David must reign faithfully over what God gives before the whole nation is united.

The conflict between Abner and Joab also prepares us for the complicated world around David.

Abner is strong, political, and self-protective.

Joab is loyal to David in one sense, but violent and dangerous.

Ishbosheth is king in name, yet afraid of Abner.

David is anointed, yet he has to lead in a world of rival houses and wounded men.

Still, the LORD is moving the promise forward.

David grows stronger and stronger.

The house of Saul grows weaker and weaker.

But the text does not let us confuse God's purpose with human bloodlust.

David mourns Abner.

David refuses Joab's murder.

David publicly separates his kingdom from that blood.

David's rise is not supposed to look like Saul's paranoia or Joab's revenge.

It is supposed to be shaped by the fear of the LORD.

## Chapter Takeaways

From First Samuel 31, remember the tragedy of Saul.

His fall is not only a battlefield loss.

It is the end of a reign that kept resisting the LORD.

From Second Samuel 1, remember the shape of David's mourning.

David refuses to let ambition erase grief.

He teaches Judah to lament Saul and Jonathan.

From Second Samuel 2, remember that David still asks God for direction.

The death of Saul does not make David independent.

The promise of the throne does not make prayer unnecessary.

From Second Samuel 3, remember that political progress can still be morally dangerous.

Abner's transfer of loyalty may help David's kingdom, but Joab's murder brings bloodguilt near the throne.

David must publicly reject what Joab has done.

The reader should see a pattern forming.

David is not perfect, but he keeps showing concern for the LORD's judgment.

He asks before moving.

He mourns before celebrating.

He blesses courage.

He rejects murder.

He refuses to let the kingdom be established by revenge.

That is why this day matters for the larger Bible story.

The king Israel needs cannot simply be strong.

He must fear God.

He must value life.

He must know that the throne belongs under the LORD.

## Slow Recap

If you remember one movement from Day 76, remember this: David receives the beginning of the kingdom with tears in his eyes.

That is not how human ambition usually works.

Human ambition often says, My enemy is gone, now I can rise.

David's response says something different.

Saul is dead, Jonathan is dead, Israel is wounded, and the name of the LORD's people has been mocked among the Philistines.

That is not a moment for selfish celebration.

It is a moment for lament.

David's lament over Saul and Jonathan also teaches the reader how to speak about complicated people.

Saul sinned grievously.

Saul also had been the LORD's anointed king.

Jonathan had loved David faithfully.

Israel had lost mighty men.

David's song lets grief be complex without becoming dishonest.

Then the early chapters of Second Samuel show that the kingdom begins in pieces.

Judah receives David.

Israel follows Ishbosheth.

Abner negotiates.

Joab murders.

David mourns.

The promise is moving, but the road is stained by human conflict.

That helps us read the Bible with patience.

God's promises are sure, but the people through whom they move are often messy, fearful, proud, and wounded.

David's task is to keep fearing the LORD in the middle of that mess.

## Final Meditation

The promise of God does not remove the need for faithful steps.

David has already been anointed by Samuel.

He has already been preserved from Saul.

He has already seen the LORD keep him through caves, exile, enemies, and grief.

But when Saul dies, David still does not rush.

He does not treat providence as permission to become careless.

He asks the LORD.

He receives Judah's anointing.

He mourns what must be mourned.

He refuses bloodguilt when it appears useful.

He keeps walking with the LORD in a world that is not yet whole.

Waiting well does not end the moment one promise begins to open.

Sometimes a promise opens into more responsibility, more complexity, and more need for prayer.

That is the heart of Day 76.

## Closing

As you finish Day 76, remember that God's promise often arrives through grief, waiting, and complicated obedience.

David does not take the throne by revenge.

He asks the LORD.

He mourns the dead.

He honors what is honorable.

He rejects bloodguilt.

The kingdom has begun, but the road ahead will still require patience, wisdom, and the fear of the LORD.
