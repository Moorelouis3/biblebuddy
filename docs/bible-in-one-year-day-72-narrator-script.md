# Day 72 Narrator Script

## Opening

Welcome to Day 72 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are reading First Samuel 15 through 18.

The title for today is Saul Is Rejected and David Appears.

This is one of the major turning points in the Old Testament story.

Saul is rejected as king.

David is anointed in Bethlehem.

David enters Saul's service.

David faces Goliath in the name of the LORD.

Then David rises in the eyes of Israel while Saul grows jealous and afraid.

The day moves from one king's failure to another king's hidden beginning.

It also gives us one of the clearest lessons in Scripture: to obey is better than sacrifice.

That sentence is not only about Saul.

It is about every attempt to use religious language to cover a heart that will not listen to God.

At the same time, these chapters show us that God sees differently than people see.

People look on outward appearance.

The LORD looks on the heart.

## First Samuel 15

First Samuel 15 begins with Samuel reminding Saul that the LORD sent him to anoint Saul as king.

Therefore Saul must listen to the voice of the words of the LORD.

That opening matters.

Saul's authority came from God, so Saul is under God's word.

The command concerns Amalek.

The LORD remembers what Amalek did to Israel when Israel came up from Egypt.

This reaches back to Exodus, where Amalek attacked Israel in the wilderness.

Now Saul is commanded to smite Amalek.

The command is severe.

The chapter is serious.

Saul gathers the people.

He warns the Kenites to depart from among the Amalekites, because they showed kindness to Israel.

That detail shows that judgment is not careless.

The Kenites are distinguished from Amalek.

Saul smites the Amalekites.

But he takes Agag the king alive.

He and the people spare the best of the sheep, oxen, fatlings, lambs, and all that is good.

They will not utterly destroy them.

Everything vile and refuse they destroy utterly.

That is selective obedience.

Saul obeys the parts that fit his own judgment.

He destroys what seems worthless.

He keeps what seems valuable.

But partial obedience is not obedience.

The word of the LORD comes to Samuel.

The LORD says it repents Him that He set up Saul to be king, because Saul has turned back from following Him and has not performed His commandments.

Samuel is grieved.

He cries unto the LORD all night.

When Samuel goes to Saul, he is told that Saul came to Carmel and set him up a place.

Saul has made some kind of monument or public marker for himself.

That detail shows pride around his disobedience.

When Saul meets Samuel, he says, Blessed be thou of the LORD: I have performed the commandment of the LORD.

Samuel answers with one of the sharpest questions in the chapter: What meaneth then this bleating of the sheep in mine ears, and the lowing of the oxen which I hear?

Saul says the people spared the best for sacrifice unto the LORD.

He shifts blame.

He makes the disobedience sound religious.

Samuel tells Saul to stay and listen.

He reminds Saul that when Saul was little in his own sight, the LORD made him head of the tribes of Israel.

The LORD sent him on a journey and gave him a command.

Why then did Saul not obey the voice of the LORD?

Saul insists he has obeyed.

He says the people took of the spoil to sacrifice.

Then Samuel says the line that defines the chapter:

Hath the LORD as great delight in burnt offerings and sacrifices, as in obeying the voice of the LORD?

Behold, to obey is better than sacrifice, and to hearken than the fat of rams.

Rebellion is as the sin of witchcraft.

Stubbornness is as iniquity and idolatry.

Because Saul has rejected the word of the LORD, the LORD has rejected him from being king.

Saul says, I have sinned.

But even his confession is mixed.

He says he feared the people and obeyed their voice.

Then he asks Samuel to pardon him and turn again with him so he may worship.

Samuel refuses at first.

As Samuel turns away, Saul lays hold on the skirt of his mantle, and it rends.

Samuel says the LORD has rent the kingdom from Saul that day and given it to a neighbor better than he.

Saul then asks Samuel to honor him before the elders and before Israel.

That request shows Saul is still deeply concerned with public image.

Samuel returns, and Saul worships the LORD.

Then Samuel commands Agag to be brought.

Agag comes delicately, thinking the bitterness of death is past.

Samuel executes judgment.

The chapter ends with distance and grief.

Samuel comes no more to see Saul until the day of his death.

Nevertheless Samuel mourns for Saul.

The LORD rejects Saul from being king over Israel.

The rejection is not treated casually.

Samuel grieves.

The LORD's word is serious.

Saul's fall is tragic.

## First Samuel 16

First Samuel 16 begins with the LORD asking Samuel, How long wilt thou mourn for Saul, seeing I have rejected him from reigning over Israel?

Samuel's grief is real, but God's purpose is moving forward.

The LORD tells him to fill his horn with oil and go to Jesse the Bethlehemite, because God has provided Himself a king among Jesse's sons.

Samuel fears Saul will hear and kill him.

The LORD gives him a way to go with a sacrifice.

Samuel comes to Bethlehem, and the elders tremble at his coming.

They ask if he comes peaceably.

Samuel says he comes peaceably and calls Jesse and his sons to the sacrifice.

When Eliab comes, Samuel thinks surely the LORD's anointed is before Him.

Eliab must have looked impressive.

But the LORD says, Look not on his countenance, or on the height of his stature, because I have refused him.

The LORD seeth not as man seeth.

Man looketh on the outward appearance, but the LORD looketh on the heart.

That sentence corrects the way Israel looked at Saul.

Saul was tall and impressive.

But height is not holiness.

Appearance is not obedience.

Jesse makes seven of his sons pass before Samuel.

The LORD has not chosen them.

Samuel asks if all the children are there.

Jesse says there remains yet the youngest, and behold, he keeps the sheep.

David is not even in the room.

He is overlooked by the family gathering.

But he is not overlooked by God.

Samuel says they will not sit down until he comes.

David is brought in.

He is ruddy, with beautiful countenance and goodly to look to.

The LORD says, Arise, anoint him: for this is he.

Samuel takes the horn of oil and anoints him in the midst of his brethren.

The Spirit of the LORD comes upon David from that day forward.

Then Samuel rises and goes to Ramah.

At the same time, the Spirit of the LORD departs from Saul, and an evil spirit from the LORD troubles him.

Saul's servants suggest finding a man who can play the harp.

David is described as cunning in playing, mighty, valiant, prudent in matters, comely, and the LORD is with him.

Saul sends to Jesse and brings David.

David comes to Saul, stands before him, and Saul loves him greatly.

David becomes his armor bearer.

When the troubling spirit comes upon Saul, David takes a harp and plays.

Saul is refreshed and well, and the evil spirit departs from him.

This is a strange and powerful arrangement.

The rejected king is helped by the chosen king.

Saul does not yet understand who David is.

But the reader knows.

God's chosen king is already in Saul's house.

## First Samuel 17

First Samuel 17 brings us to David and Goliath.

The Philistines gather their armies.

Israel and Saul gather in the valley of Elah.

A champion comes out of the Philistine camp.

His name is Goliath of Gath.

The text describes his height, helmet, coat of mail, greaves, target, spear, and shield bearer.

Everything about Goliath is meant to look impossible.

He stands and cries to the armies of Israel.

He challenges them to choose a man to fight him.

If the Israelite wins, the Philistines will serve Israel.

If Goliath wins, Israel will serve the Philistines.

Saul and all Israel hear those words and are dismayed and greatly afraid.

David enters the story through an ordinary errand.

Jesse sends him with parched corn, loaves, and cheeses.

David rises early, leaves the sheep with a keeper, and goes as Jesse commanded.

He comes to the trench as the host is going forth to fight.

Then Goliath comes up and speaks the same words.

David hears them.

That moment changes everything.

Israel hears the giant and trembles.

David hears the giant and asks, Who is this uncircumcised Philistine, that he should defy the armies of the living God?

David sees what others are missing.

Goliath is not merely big.

Goliath is defying the living God.

Eliab, David's oldest brother, becomes angry and accuses David of pride and curiosity.

David asks, Is there not a cause?

His words are reported to Saul.

Saul sends for him.

David tells Saul, Let no man's heart fail because of him; thy servant will go and fight with this Philistine.

Saul says David cannot fight Goliath because David is a youth, and Goliath has been a man of war from his youth.

David answers from shepherd life.

He kept his father's sheep.

When a lion or a bear came and took a lamb, David went out after it, smote it, and delivered the lamb.

The LORD delivered him from the paw of the lion and the paw of the bear.

The LORD will deliver him from the hand of this Philistine.

David is not trusting in himself.

He is remembering the LORD's past help.

Saul puts his armor on David.

David tries to go, but he has not proved it.

He takes it off.

He chooses five smooth stones from the brook, puts them in a shepherd's bag, takes his sling, and draws near to the Philistine.

Goliath curses David by his gods.

David answers, Thou comest to me with a sword, and with a spear, and with a shield.

But I come to thee in the name of the LORD of hosts, the God of the armies of Israel, whom thou hast defied.

David says the LORD will deliver Goliath into his hand so all the earth may know there is a God in Israel.

He says all the assembly shall know that the LORD saveth not with sword and spear, for the battle is the LORD's.

Then David runs toward the army.

He takes a stone, slings it, and smites the Philistine in his forehead.

Goliath falls upon his face to the earth.

David has no sword in his hand, so he takes Goliath's sword and cuts off his head.

When the Philistines see their champion dead, they flee.

The men of Israel and Judah arise and pursue.

The battle is won because the LORD gives victory through the one everyone underestimated.

## First Samuel 18

First Samuel 18 shows David's rise and Saul's jealousy.

The soul of Jonathan is knit with the soul of David.

Jonathan loves him as his own soul.

Jonathan makes a covenant with David.

He strips himself of his robe, garments, sword, bow, and girdle, and gives them to David.

Jonathan is Saul's son.

Yet he recognizes something honorable in David.

David goes wherever Saul sends him and behaves himself wisely.

Saul sets him over the men of war, and he is accepted in the sight of all the people and Saul's servants.

Then the women come out singing and dancing.

They say, Saul hath slain his thousands, and David his ten thousands.

Saul is very wroth.

The saying displeases him.

He says they have ascribed to David ten thousands and to him thousands.

What can David have more but the kingdom?

From that day forward, Saul eyes David.

Jealousy enters Saul's heart and changes how he sees everything.

The next day, the troubling spirit comes upon Saul.

David plays with his hand as at other times.

Saul has a javelin in his hand.

He casts the javelin, saying he will smite David even to the wall.

David avoids it twice.

Saul is afraid of David because the LORD is with him and departed from Saul.

That is the key.

Saul's fear is not only political.

He senses that God's favor rests on David.

Saul removes David from him and makes him captain over a thousand.

David goes out and comes in before the people.

David behaves himself wisely in all his ways, and the LORD is with him.

All Israel and Judah love David.

Saul then uses marriage promises as a trap.

He offers Merab, then gives her to another.

He sees that Michal loves David and uses the situation to endanger him.

Saul hopes the hand of the Philistines will be against David.

But David succeeds.

David brings the required dowry.

Michal becomes his wife.

Saul sees and knows the LORD is with David.

He becomes David's enemy continually.

The chapter ends with David behaving more wisely than all the servants of Saul, so that his name is much set by.

David is rising.

Saul is shrinking inwardly.

The difference is not talent alone.

The LORD is with David.

## Full Lesson Review

Day 72 teaches obedience, heart, courage, and jealousy.

Saul fails because he rejects the word of the LORD.

He keeps what God commanded him to destroy.

He claims obedience while the sheep are still bleating.

He uses sacrifice language to cover rebellion.

Samuel's answer is the framework for the whole day: to obey is better than sacrifice.

Then David appears.

He is not chosen because he looks most impressive.

He is chosen because the LORD sees the heart.

David is anointed, but he does not immediately sit on a throne.

He goes back into hiddenness.

He serves.

He plays the harp.

He runs errands.

He faces a giant.

He trusts the LORD.

Then his success exposes Saul's fear.

Saul cannot rejoice in what God is doing through David, because Saul sees David as a threat to his own name.

The reader is meant to compare Saul and David.

Saul fears the people.

David fears the LORD.

Saul worries about honor before Israel.

David wants all the earth to know there is a God in Israel.

Saul uses religious words after disobedience.

David speaks God's name before battle.

Saul is rejected.

David is chosen.

But David's road will be long.

## Guided Listening Review

Day 72 needs to be heard as one connected movement.

First, Saul rejects the word of the LORD.

Then the LORD reveals the kind of king He is choosing.

Then David's faith exposes Saul's fear.

Then David's success exposes Saul's jealousy.

In chapter 15, the bleating sheep are important because they make hidden disobedience audible.

Saul can say, I have performed the commandment of the LORD.

But the animals answer him.

The sound in the camp tells the truth.

That is why Samuel's question is so piercing.

What means this bleating?

Saul wants to talk about sacrifice.

Samuel talks about obedience.

Saul wants to blame the people.

Samuel talks to the king who received the command.

Saul wants honor before the elders.

Samuel says the kingdom has been torn from him.

The lesson is not that sacrifices were bad.

God commanded sacrifices in His law.

The lesson is that sacrifice cannot be used as a costume for rebellion.

Then chapter 16 takes us to Bethlehem.

Samuel is still grieving Saul, but God sends him forward.

That matters because grief over failure is real, but God's purpose is not dead.

Eliab looks like the obvious choice.

God says no.

The other sons pass by.

God says no.

David is out with the sheep.

He is the youngest.

He has to be called in from the field.

That picture teaches the reader how God chooses.

The one overlooked by the room is seen by the LORD.

The anointing happens before David has a crown, a palace, or a public throne.

God's calling often begins before people understand it.

Then chapter 17 shows David's heart in public danger.

Goliath is enormous, but David hears more than size.

He hears defiance against the living God.

That is why his courage is different from mere bravery.

David does not say he is stronger than Goliath.

He says the battle is the LORD's.

He remembers the lion and the bear, because past deliverance teaches present trust.

The sling and stone matter because God saves without the normal symbols of power.

Finally, chapter 18 shows that David's rise becomes Saul's torment.

Jonathan can love David without jealousy.

Saul cannot.

Jonathan gives David robe and weapons.

Saul throws a javelin.

Jonathan sees God's hand and makes covenant.

Saul sees a rival and becomes afraid.

The reader should feel the difference between a surrendered heart and a threatened heart.

When the LORD is with David, Jonathan rejoices and Saul panics.

That is the searching question of the day.

Can we rejoice when God is with someone else?

Or do we turn blessing into threat because we are guarding our own name?

## Key Connections

There are three threads to keep close.

First, Saul's sin is not a small administrative mistake.

He rejects the word of the LORD.

That phrase explains why the consequence is so severe.

The issue is not that Saul misunderstood a minor detail.

The issue is that Saul treated God's command as something he could edit.

Second, David's anointing does not immediately remove difficulty.

He is chosen in chapter 16, but he is still sent, still serving, still carrying food, still facing danger, and still waiting.

The anointing gives certainty about God's choice, not instant comfort.

Third, David and Goliath is not mainly a story about personal confidence.

It is about the honor of the living God.

David's courage comes from seeing the battle rightly.

Goliath is large, but he is not ultimate.

Israel is afraid, but Israel is not alone.

The LORD saves not with sword and spear.

That truth exposes Saul, strengthens David, and prepares the reader for the long conflict between a fearful king and a faithful servant.

One more thing should be noticed.

David is not introduced as a flawless hero who never needs mercy.

He is introduced as the LORD's choice.

That is different.

The story will later show David's sins honestly.

But here the contrast is between a king who keeps resisting God's word and a servant whose faith is directed toward God's honor.

So when David runs toward Goliath, do not hear only a lesson about bravery.

Hear a lesson about worship.

David cannot bear to hear the living God defied.

His courage is fueled by the name of the LORD, not by self-confidence.

That is why the victory becomes testimony for all the earth.

## Closing

As you finish Day 72, hold onto the central truths.

Obedience matters more than religious appearance.

God sees the heart.

The battle belongs to the LORD.

Jealousy can turn blessing into threat when the heart is not surrendered.

David's story is only beginning.

Saul's rejection has been announced, but Saul still sits on the throne.

David has been anointed, but he will have to wait.

The chapters ahead will show whether David will grasp for the kingdom or trust the God who chose him.
