# Bible In One Year - Day 25 Narrator Script

## Day 25 - Through the Sea and Into the Wilderness

**Reading:** Exodus 13 through 16  
**Bible text:** King James Version, Exodus 13-16  
**Audio style:** Narrator teaching with Scripture reading  

## Opening

Welcome to Day 25 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are walking through Exodus 13 through 16.

Yesterday, Israel walked out of Egypt.

Passover happened.

The lamb was slain.

The blood was placed on the doorposts.

The firstborn of Egypt died.

Pharaoh finally told Israel to go.

And after generations of slavery, the people of Israel left Egypt as free people.

But today's reading teaches us something very important.

Freedom does not mean the journey is finished.

In many ways, freedom is where the journey begins.

Israel is out of Egypt, but Egypt is not fully out of Israel.

They have been rescued, but they still panic.

They have seen God's power, but they still complain.

They have walked out under God's mighty hand, but they still need to learn how to trust Him one day at a time.

That is why Exodus 13 through 16 is so helpful.

It shows us what happens after deliverance.

God teaches His people to remember.

He leads them by cloud and fire.

He brings them to the sea.

He opens the sea.

He defeats Pharaoh's army.

He gives them a song of worship.

He heals bitter water.

He gives bread from heaven.

And He teaches them rest.

So listen for one major theme today:

God does not only bring His people out.

He teaches His people how to live free.

## Exodus 13:1-10

**Scripture**

And the LORD spake unto Moses, saying,

Sanctify unto me all the firstborn, whatsoever openeth the womb among the children of Israel, both of man and of beast: it is mine.

And Moses said unto the people, Remember this day, in which ye came out from Egypt, out of the house of bondage; for by strength of hand the LORD brought you out from this place.

Seven days thou shalt eat unleavened bread, and in the seventh day shall be a feast to the LORD.

And thou shalt shew thy son in that day, saying, This is done because of that which the LORD did unto me when I came forth out of Egypt.

**Narrator**

Exodus 13 begins with memory.

That may feel surprising.

Israel has just left Egypt.

You might expect the next instruction to be about travel routes, military plans, or survival strategies.

But God begins by teaching them how to remember.

He says, Sanctify unto me all the firstborn.

To sanctify means to set apart as belonging to God.

The firstborn sons of Israel had just been spared on Passover night.

So now the firstborn are marked as belonging to the LORD.

This is not random.

God is teaching Israel that rescue creates belonging.

The saved life now belongs to the Savior.

And then Moses says, Remember this day.

That line matters because God knows how quickly people forget.

Israel could forget the bitterness of slavery.

They could forget the night of Passover.

They could forget the blood on the doorposts.

They could forget that freedom came by the strength of God's hand.

So God builds remembrance into their calendar.

Their worship will teach their children.

Their meals will tell the story.

Their yearly rhythms will keep saying:

We were slaves.

The LORD brought us out.

And we belong to Him.

## Exodus 13:11-16

**Scripture**

And it shall be when the LORD shall bring thee into the land of the Canaanites, as he sware unto thee and to thy fathers, and shall give it thee,

That thou shalt set apart unto the LORD all that openeth the matrix.

And it shall be when thy son asketh thee in time to come, saying, What is this? that thou shalt say unto him,

By strength of hand the LORD brought us out from Egypt, from the house of bondage.

**Narrator**

God now connects the firstborn command to the future.

Israel is not in the promised land yet.

They have not reached Canaan.

They have not crossed the wilderness.

They have not stood at Sinai.

But God speaks as if His promise is sure.

When the LORD brings you into the land, keep remembering.

That is the idea.

And notice the question:

When thy son asketh thee, What is this?

God expects children to ask questions about worship.

The rituals are not meant to be empty routines.

They are teaching tools.

When a child sees a firstborn animal set apart, or hears about redemption, the parent is supposed to explain the story.

Not with complicated religious language.

But plainly.

The LORD brought us out of Egypt.

We were in bondage.

He rescued us by strength of hand.

This is how faith gets carried forward.

One generation tells the next generation what God has done.

Redemption is not supposed to become a private memory.

It becomes a family story.

A worship story.

A community story.

A story children can ask about and understand.

## Exodus 13:17-22

**Scripture**

And it came to pass, when Pharaoh had let the people go, that God led them not through the way of the land of the Philistines, although that was near.

For God said, Lest peradventure the people repent when they see war, and they return to Egypt.

But God led the people about, through the way of the wilderness of the Red sea.

And Moses took the bones of Joseph with him.

And the LORD went before them by day in a pillar of a cloud, to lead them the way; and by night in a pillar of fire, to give them light.

**Narrator**

This section gives us one of the most comforting details in the Exodus story.

God does not lead Israel by the shortest road.

The shorter route would have taken them toward the land of the Philistines.

But that road also meant possible war.

And Israel was not ready.

They had been slaves for generations.

They were not trained soldiers.

They were newly freed, fragile, frightened, and still learning how to trust.

So God leads them the long way.

And the long way is mercy.

That is worth slowing down for.

Sometimes we assume the best road is the fastest road.

But God sees dangers we do not see.

He knows what His people are ready for.

He knows what would crush them.

He knows what battles need to wait.

The shortest route is not always the wisest route.

Then we get a beautiful detail:

Moses took the bones of Joseph.

Joseph had asked for this back in Genesis 50.

He believed God would surely visit His people and bring them out of Egypt.

Now, centuries later, Joseph's bones are leaving with Israel.

That means God's promise outlived Joseph.

It outlived slavery.

It outlived generations of waiting.

Even the bones are preaching:

God kept His word.

And then the LORD leads by cloud and fire.

Israel does not have a map.

They have God's presence.

Cloud by day.

Fire by night.

Guidance for newly freed people who do not yet know the way.

## Exodus 14:1-9

**Scripture**

And the LORD spake unto Moses, saying,

Speak unto the children of Israel, that they turn and encamp before Pi-hahiroth, between Migdol and the sea.

For Pharaoh will say of the children of Israel, They are entangled in the land, the wilderness hath shut them in.

And I will harden Pharaoh's heart, that he shall follow after them.

And the Egyptians pursued after them, all the horses and chariots of Pharaoh, and his horsemen, and his army.

**Narrator**

Now the story turns tense again.

God leads Israel to camp near the sea.

From a human perspective, it looks dangerous.

From Pharaoh's perspective, it looks foolish.

He says, They are entangled in the land.

In other words, They are trapped.

The sea is in front of them.

The wilderness is around them.

Egypt is behind them.

Pharaoh thinks Israel has walked into a dead end.

So he changes his mind.

He gathers his chariots, his horsemen, and his army.

The text mentions six hundred chosen chariots.

Chariots were elite military power in the ancient world.

This is not a small group chasing Israel.

Egypt is sending its strongest visible force after a newly freed slave people.

And that contrast is the point.

Israel has no army that can match Egypt.

They have no strategy that can beat Pharaoh's chariots.

They cannot outrun them.

They cannot outfight them.

But God is not surprised.

He already said He would be honored over Pharaoh.

The place that looks like a trap is about to become the place where God's glory is revealed.

## Exodus 14:10-14

**Scripture**

And when Pharaoh drew nigh, the children of Israel lifted up their eyes, and, behold, the Egyptians marched after them.

And they were sore afraid.

And the children of Israel cried out unto the LORD.

And they said unto Moses, Because there were no graves in Egypt, hast thou taken us away to die in the wilderness?

And Moses said unto the people, Fear ye not, stand still, and see the salvation of the LORD.

The LORD shall fight for you, and ye shall hold your peace.

**Narrator**

Israel sees the army coming, and fear takes over.

And honestly, the fear makes sense.

The army is real.

The chariots are real.

The sea is real.

The danger is real.

The Bible does not pretend danger is imaginary.

But fear becomes dangerous when it starts rewriting the story.

The people say, Were there no graves in Egypt?

That is bitter sarcasm.

Egypt was famous for tombs and death.

And now Israel is saying, Moses, did you bring us out here because Egypt did not have enough graves?

Fear has made slavery sound safer than freedom.

That happens more often than we want to admit.

When freedom becomes uncomfortable, bondage can start looking better in our memory.

But Moses answers with one of the strongest lines in Exodus:

Fear not.

Stand still.

See the salvation of the LORD.

The people cannot save themselves here.

They cannot open the sea.

They cannot defeat Egypt.

They cannot solve the impossible setup.

So God is about to show them salvation.

Before Israel learns how to fight battles, build a tabernacle, or obey laws at Sinai, they must first learn this:

The LORD saves helpless people.

## Exodus 14:15-22

**Scripture**

And the LORD said unto Moses, Wherefore criest thou unto me? speak unto the children of Israel, that they go forward.

But lift thou up thy rod, and stretch out thine hand over the sea, and divide it.

And the angel of God, which went before the camp of Israel, removed and went behind them.

And Moses stretched out his hand over the sea; and the LORD caused the sea to go back by a strong east wind all that night.

And the children of Israel went into the midst of the sea upon the dry ground.

**Narrator**

First Moses says, Stand still.

Now God says, Go forward.

That is not a contradiction.

It is timing.

Faith is not always the same posture.

Sometimes faith waits.

Sometimes faith walks.

The question is not whether waiting or walking is always better.

The question is:

What is God saying now?

Moses lifts the staff.

The cloud moves.

And that detail is beautiful.

The presence of God had been leading in front of Israel.

Now it moves behind them.

The LORD stands between Israel and Egypt.

The same presence that guides also guards.

Then a strong east wind blows through the night.

The sea divides.

And Israel walks through on dry ground.

The waters become a wall on their right and on their left.

This is more than an escape route.

It is like a new creation moment.

In Genesis, God separated waters and brought out dry land.

Here, God separates waters again and brings His people through on dry ground.

The sea that looked like death becomes the path to life.

## Exodus 14:23-31

**Scripture**

And the Egyptians pursued, and went in after them to the midst of the sea.

And the LORD troubled the host of the Egyptians.

And took off their chariot wheels.

And the Egyptians said, Let us flee from the face of Israel; for the LORD fighteth for them against the Egyptians.

And the waters returned, and covered the chariots, and the horsemen, and all the host of Pharaoh.

Thus the LORD saved Israel that day out of the hand of the Egyptians.

**Narrator**

Egypt follows Israel into the sea.

The army still thinks it can win.

But then the LORD troubles the Egyptians.

The chariot wheels come off.

The soldiers panic.

And finally they say the truth out loud:

The LORD fights for Israel.

That line matters.

Israel does not defeat Egypt by military skill.

The LORD Himself fights for His people.

The army that charged in with confidence now wants to flee.

But the waters return.

The chariots are covered.

The horsemen are covered.

Pharaoh's power collapses in the sea.

And the text says:

Thus the LORD saved Israel that day.

That is the meaning of the sea crossing.

It is salvation.

The threat behind them is broken.

The people stand on the other side.

And they see the great work of the LORD.

The result is reverent fear and faith.

They feared the LORD.

They believed the LORD.

And they believed His servant Moses.

## Exodus 15:1-10

**Scripture**

Then sang Moses and the children of Israel this song unto the LORD.

I will sing unto the LORD, for he hath triumphed gloriously: the horse and his rider hath he thrown into the sea.

The LORD is my strength and song, and he is become my salvation.

Pharaoh's chariots and his host hath he cast into the sea.

The depths have covered them: they sank into the bottom as a stone.

**Narrator**

After the sea, Israel sings.

That is important.

They do not only escape.

They worship.

The first major song in the Bible rises from deliverance.

I will sing unto the LORD.

Why?

Because He has triumphed gloriously.

The horse and rider have been thrown into the sea.

The thing that terrified Israel becomes the thing they sing about.

That is what worship can do.

Worship takes the fear that used to dominate the story and puts it under the truth of God's victory.

Then they say:

The LORD is my strength and song.

Not just, God gave me strength.

But, the LORD is my strength.

Not just, God gave me a song.

But, the LORD is my song.

The rescued people now have a new center.

Their praise is not built on their courage.

It is built on God's salvation.

## Exodus 15:11-18

**Scripture**

Who is like unto thee, O LORD, among the gods?

Who is like thee, glorious in holiness, fearful in praises, doing wonders?

Thou in thy mercy hast led forth the people which thou hast redeemed.

Thou shalt bring them in, and plant them in the mountain of thine inheritance.

The LORD shall reign for ever and ever.

**Narrator**

The song now moves from what God did to who God is.

Who is like unto thee, O LORD?

That question is worship.

Egypt had many gods.

Pharaoh claimed power.

The Nile had been treated like a source of life.

The sun was connected to Egyptian worship.

But none of Egypt's gods could save Egypt from the LORD.

Israel is learning that the LORD is not one power among many equal powers.

He is incomparable.

Glorious in holiness.

That means God's power is not dirty power.

It is not selfish power.

It is not cruel power.

It is holy power.

He judges evil and rescues His people in faithfulness.

Then the song looks forward:

Thou shalt bring them in.

Israel is not in the promised land yet.

They are barely out of Egypt.

But the song already trusts the destination.

God did not rescue them to abandon them in the wilderness.

He brought them out so He could bring them in.

## Exodus 15:19-21

**Scripture**

And Miriam the prophetess, the sister of Aaron, took a timbrel in her hand.

And all the women went out after her with timbrels and with dances.

And Miriam answered them, Sing ye to the LORD, for he hath triumphed gloriously; the horse and his rider hath he thrown into the sea.

**Narrator**

Miriam now appears as a worship leader.

She is called a prophetess.

That means she is not just a background character.

She has a recognized place among God's people.

And this fits the Exodus story.

Women have been important from the beginning.

The Hebrew midwives feared God.

Moses' mother hid him.

Moses' sister watched over him.

Pharaoh's daughter drew him from the water.

And now Miriam leads the women in praise.

They take timbrels.

They dance.

They answer the song.

The bodies that had known slavery now move in celebration.

The people who had groaned under bondage now sing about victory.

Deliverance changes the sound of the community.

## Exodus 15:22-27

**Scripture**

So Moses brought Israel from the Red sea, and they went out into the wilderness of Shur.

And they went three days in the wilderness, and found no water.

And when they came to Marah, they could not drink of the waters of Marah, for they were bitter.

And the people murmured against Moses, saying, What shall we drink?

And he cried unto the LORD; and the LORD shewed him a tree, which when he had cast into the waters, the waters were made sweet.

And he said, I am the LORD that healeth thee.

**Narrator**

This is such an honest part of the Bible.

Israel sings beside the sea.

Then three days later, they are thirsty.

That is real life.

Sometimes a spiritual high is followed by a very practical need.

They find water at Marah, but they cannot drink it because it is bitter.

Marah means bitter.

The name fits the moment.

And the people complain.

But Moses cries to the LORD.

God shows him a tree.

The tree is cast into the waters.

And the waters are made sweet.

The point is not that the tree is magic.

The point is that God can heal bitter places.

The wilderness is not outside His care.

Then God reveals Himself:

I am the LORD that healeth thee.

Israel needs to know God as more than the One who defeated Pharaoh.

They need to know Him as the One who can heal thirst, bitterness, weakness, and fear on the journey.

## Exodus 16:1-3

**Scripture**

And they took their journey from Elim, and all the congregation of the children of Israel came unto the wilderness of Sin.

And the whole congregation of the children of Israel murmured against Moses and Aaron in the wilderness.

And the children of Israel said unto them, Would to God we had died by the hand of the LORD in the land of Egypt, when we sat by the flesh pots, and when we did eat bread to the full.

**Narrator**

Now the crisis is hunger.

Israel enters the wilderness of Sin.

That is a place name, not a statement that the location itself is sinful.

But what happens there does reveal the heart.

The whole congregation murmurs.

The complaint spreads.

Hunger is real.

The need is real.

But fear begins editing their memory.

They remember Egypt as a place of full pots and plenty of bread.

But they forget the bricks.

They forget the taskmasters.

They forget the crying.

They forget the killing of sons.

This is what fear can do.

It can make bondage look better than it was.

Israel is physically out of Egypt, but their memory is still being shaped by Egypt.

God is going to feed them.

But He is also going to train them.

## Exodus 16:4-12

**Scripture**

Then said the LORD unto Moses, Behold, I will rain bread from heaven for you.

And the people shall go out and gather a certain rate every day, that I may prove them, whether they will walk in my law, or no.

At even, then ye shall know that the LORD hath brought you out from the land of Egypt.

And in the morning, then ye shall see the glory of the LORD.

**Narrator**

God answers hunger with bread from heaven.

That phrase is beautiful.

Israel cannot grow this bread.

They cannot buy it.

They cannot store it like Egypt's grain.

It comes from above.

It comes as gift.

But the gift comes with instructions.

They are to gather a certain amount every day.

Daily bread.

Daily limits.

Daily trust.

God says this will prove them.

That does not mean God lacks information.

It means the test will reveal and train their hearts.

Manna is food, but it is also formation.

God is teaching former slaves not to live by panic.

Not to hoard.

Not to control tomorrow.

But to receive what He gives today.

The wilderness becomes a classroom.

And breakfast becomes a lesson in faith.

## Exodus 16:13-21

**Scripture**

And it came to pass, that at even the quails came up, and covered the camp.

And in the morning the dew lay round about the host.

And when the dew that lay was gone up, behold, upon the face of the wilderness there lay a small round thing.

And when the children of Israel saw it, they said one to another, It is manna: for they wist not what it was.

And Moses said unto them, This is the bread which the LORD hath given you to eat.

**Narrator**

In the evening, quail cover the camp.

In the morning, something small appears with the dew.

The people look at it and ask, What is it?

That is where manna gets its name.

God's provision is so unfamiliar that they do not even know what to call it.

That is important.

Sometimes God's provision does not look like what we expected.

Israel knew Egyptian food.

They knew slave food.

They knew the systems they had lived under for generations.

But this is different.

This is wilderness bread.

Bread from heaven.

Moses says, This is the bread the LORD has given you to eat.

Then the people gather according to need.

Some gather more.

Some gather less.

But everyone has enough.

God's provision is not built on greed.

It is built on enough.

No one lacks.

No one has extra.

The miracle is not only that food appears.

The miracle is that God's way feeds the whole community.

## Exodus 16:22-30

**Scripture**

And it came to pass, that on the sixth day they gathered twice as much bread.

And he said unto them, This is that which the LORD hath said, To morrow is the rest of the holy sabbath unto the LORD.

Six days ye shall gather it; but on the seventh day, which is the sabbath, in it there shall be none.

So the people rested on the seventh day.

**Narrator**

Here we meet Sabbath before the Ten Commandments are given at Sinai.

That matters.

God is already teaching Israel that rest belongs to freedom.

They are no longer slaves under Pharaoh.

Their worth is not measured by endless production.

They do not have to live like life depends on never stopping.

On the sixth day, God gives twice as much.

Unlike the other days, it does not spoil overnight.

God provides enough so they can rest.

But some people still go out looking on the seventh day.

That little detail is deeply human.

Rest can feel wrong when slavery has trained you to keep producing.

Stopping can feel unsafe when fear has trained you to control everything.

But God treats rest as obedience.

Sabbath is not laziness.

It is trust.

It is a weekly way of saying:

God is provider.

I am not Pharaoh's slave anymore.

I can stop because God does not stop being faithful.

## Exodus 16:31-36

**Scripture**

And the house of Israel called the name thereof Manna.

And it was like coriander seed, white; and the taste of it was like wafers made with honey.

And Moses said, This is the thing which the LORD commandeth, Fill an omer of it to be kept for your generations.

And the children of Israel did eat manna forty years, until they came to a land inhabited.

**Narrator**

The manna is described as white like coriander seed and tasting like wafers made with honey.

That detail is easy to skip, but it is beautiful.

God's wilderness provision is not described as punishment food.

Even in the desert, there is sweetness.

Then God tells Israel to keep a portion for future generations.

A jar of manna becomes a memory.

Future Israelites who never stood in the wilderness still need to know how God fed His people.

This fits the whole day.

Remember the firstborn.

Remember the feast.

Remember the sea.

Remember the song.

Remember the bread.

God wants His people to carry the story.

And then the chapter says Israel ate manna forty years.

Not one morning.

Not one week.

Forty years.

That teaches steady faithfulness.

The God who opened the sea also fed His people morning after morning.

Sometimes God's mercy is dramatic, like the waters standing up.

And sometimes God's mercy is quiet, like bread waiting on the ground when the morning comes.

## Closing

Day 25 teaches us that deliverance is only the beginning of formation.

Israel is out of Egypt.

But now they must learn how to live with God.

They must learn to remember.

They must learn to follow.

They must learn not to panic at the sea.

They must learn to worship after rescue.

They must learn to bring bitter places to God.

They must learn to receive daily bread.

They must learn to rest.

And that is still deeply relevant.

Many people know what it is like to be rescued from something, but still carry old fears, old habits, old memories, and old survival patterns.

God does not shame Israel for needing formation.

He teaches them.

He leads them.

He protects them.

He feeds them.

He heals them.

He gives them rhythms.

He stays with them.

The same God who brings His people out also walks with them through the wilderness.

So the question today is not only:

Has God rescued me?

It is also:

Am I learning how to live free?

Am I remembering what He has done?

Am I following His presence, even when the road is longer?

Am I trusting Him at the sea?

Am I letting rescue become worship?

Am I receiving today's bread without trying to control tomorrow?

Am I resting like someone who no longer belongs to Pharaoh?

The LORD who saved Israel did not abandon them after Passover.

He led them through the sea and into the wilderness.

And there, one step at a time, He taught them the life of freedom.
