# Day 67 Narrator Script

## Opening

Welcome to Day 67 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are reading Judges 20 and 21, then Ruth 1 and 2.

The title for today is Civil War and Ruth's Loyal Love.

This day gives one of the strongest contrasts in this part of Scripture.

Judges ends with national collapse.

Ruth begins with quiet faithfulness inside ordinary family grief.

Judges shows Israel breaking apart.

Ruth shows loyal love holding fast.

Judges shows everyone doing what is right in his own eyes.

Ruth shows a Moabite widow clinging to Naomi and choosing Naomi's God.

That contrast is not accidental.

The Bible is showing that even in the days of the judges, when Israel is dark and disordered, the LORD is still preserving hope.

So listen for two movements today.

First, the grief of sin inside Israel.

Second, the quiet mercy of God in a small family story that will lead toward David.

## Judges 20

Judges 20 responds to the horror at Gibeah.

All the children of Israel gather together as one man, from Dan even to Beersheba.

The Levite tells the story.

The tribes ask what should be done.

The nation decides to act against Gibeah.

At first, this seems like Israel may finally respond to evil with justice.

But the situation becomes even more tragic.

Benjamin refuses to hand over the wicked men of Gibeah.

Instead of judging the evil within their tribe, they gather for battle against the other tribes of Israel.

So the sin of Gibeah becomes civil war.

Brother fights brother.

The covenant people tear into themselves.

Israel asks counsel of God.

Judah goes first.

But Israel is defeated.

They weep before the LORD.

They ask again.

They are defeated again.

The chapter slows us down because the issue is not simple.

Israel is responding to real evil, but the cost is devastating.

They fast.

They offer burnt offerings and peace offerings.

Phinehas is mentioned, connecting this moment to priestly mediation and covenant seriousness.

The LORD finally says to go up, and He will deliver Benjamin into their hand.

Israel sets an ambush.

Gibeah is struck.

Benjamin is crushed.

Thousands die.

Only a remnant of Benjamin escapes to the rock Rimmon.

Judges 20 teaches that sin inside God's people is not small.

It can spread from one city to one tribe to the whole nation.

It can force judgment that wounds everyone.

This is one of the reasons Judges is so painful.

The enemy is not only outside Israel.

The collapse is inside.

The people who were meant to live as a holy nation are now destroying one another.

## Judges 21

Judges 21 shows the bitter aftermath.

Israel has nearly destroyed Benjamin.

Then the people weep and ask why one tribe should be lacking in Israel.

Their grief is real.

But their solutions are still deeply troubled.

They had sworn not to give their daughters to Benjamin.

Now that vow creates a crisis.

They look for a city that did not come up to the assembly.

Jabeshgilead is struck.

Young women are taken for Benjamin.

Still there are not enough wives.

Then another plan is formed around the daughters of Shiloh.

The whole chapter feels broken.

Israel is trying to repair one disaster with more troubling acts.

The final verse gives the diagnosis again.

In those days there was no king in Israel: every man did that which was right in his own eyes.

That is the ending of Judges.

Not triumph.

Not peace.

Not clean resolution.

Moral confusion.

Rash vows.

Civil grief.

Human schemes.

The book ends by making the reader feel the need for faithful rule under God.

If you read Judges correctly, you should not come away saying, Israel only needs a stronger personality.

You should come away saying, Israel needs the LORD's rule in the heart of the people.

They need justice.

They need worship.

They need mercy.

They need covenant obedience.

They need a king only if that king leads under God's word.

## Ruth 1

Then Ruth begins.

The opening words are important.

Now it came to pass in the days when the judges ruled.

Ruth is set inside that same dark period.

But the story becomes quiet and personal.

There is famine in Bethlehemjudah.

Bethlehem means house of bread, yet there is no bread.

Elimelech takes Naomi and their two sons to Moab.

The sons marry Moabite women, Orpah and Ruth.

Then Elimelech dies.

The sons die.

Naomi is left without her husband and sons.

This is deep vulnerability.

Naomi hears that the LORD has visited His people in giving them bread.

So she begins to return to Judah.

On the road, she tells Orpah and Ruth to go back.

She prays that the LORD will deal kindly with them.

Naomi's grief is honest.

She feels empty.

She says the hand of the LORD has gone out against her.

Orpah eventually returns.

But Ruth clave unto her.

Ruth's words are some of the most famous in Scripture.

Whither thou goest, I will go.

Where thou lodgest, I will lodge.

Thy people shall be my people, and thy God my God.

Where thou diest, will I die, and there will I be buried.

Ruth is not only choosing Naomi emotionally.

She is joining herself to Naomi's people and to the LORD.

That is loyal love.

That is costly faith.

That is a Moabite widow stepping into the people of God.

When Naomi comes back to Bethlehem, the city is moved.

Naomi says, call me not Naomi, call me Mara.

Naomi means pleasant.

Mara means bitter.

She says she went out full, and the LORD brought her home again empty.

But the reader can see something Naomi cannot yet see.

Ruth is standing with her.

And they return at the beginning of barley harvest.

The chapter ends with quiet hope.

Naomi feels empty.

But Ruth is with her.

The harvest is beginning.

God is already working.

## Ruth 2

Ruth 2 shows that hope through ordinary provision.

Naomi has a kinsman of her husband's, a mighty man of wealth, named Boaz.

Ruth asks to go to the field and glean.

Gleaning was part of God's mercy in the law.

The poor, the stranger, the widow, and the fatherless could gather what remained after the harvesters.

It was not glamorous, but it was provision with dignity.

Ruth goes.

The text says her hap was to light on a part of the field belonging unto Boaz.

It looks like chance.

But in Ruth, ordinary events are full of hidden providence.

Boaz notices Ruth.

He asks who she is.

He has heard about her loyalty to Naomi.

He tells her not to glean in another field.

He commands the young men not to touch her.

He invites her to drink from the vessels.

Ruth falls on her face and asks why she has found grace in his eyes, seeing she is a stranger.

Boaz answers with blessing.

The LORD recompense thy work, and a full reward be given thee of the LORD God of Israel, under whose wings thou art come to trust.

That phrase is beautiful.

Ruth has come under the wings of the LORD.

Boaz becomes one of the ways the LORD shelters her.

He invites her to eat.

He tells his workers to let fall handfuls of purpose.

Ruth gathers an ephah of barley and brings food home to Naomi.

Naomi asks where Ruth gleaned.

When she hears the man's name is Boaz, hope awakens.

Blessed be he of the LORD, who hath not left off his kindness to the living and to the dead.

Naomi says Boaz is one of their next kinsmen.

The redemption theme has begun.

Ruth stays close by the maidens of Boaz through barley harvest and wheat harvest.

## Full Lesson Review

Day 67 is one of the Bible's great contrasts.

Judges 20 and 21 show Israel tearing itself apart.

Ruth 1 and 2 show loyal love and quiet provision.

Judges ends with no king and every man doing what is right in his own eyes.

Ruth begins in those same days and shows one woman doing what is faithful, costly, and loving.

Judges shows vows that trap people.

Ruth shows commitment that protects Naomi.

Judges shows women harmed by violent schemes.

Ruth shows Boaz using power to protect a vulnerable woman.

Judges shows Israel's tribes in grief.

Ruth shows a Moabite woman being brought near.

This does not mean Ruth ignores suffering.

Naomi's pain is real.

The famine is real.

The deaths are real.

The vulnerability of widowhood is real.

But Ruth shows that the LORD can work in quiet places.

He works through a road back to Bethlehem.

He works through a daughter-in-law's loyalty.

He works through the beginning of barley harvest.

He works through gleaning laws.

He works through Boaz's kindness.

He works through Naomi slowly beginning to see hope again.

This matters because the Bible is not only about public battles and national leaders.

It is also about ordinary faithfulness.

It is about meals, fields, family loyalty, grief, and kindness.

It is about a widow from Moab coming under the wings of the LORD.

And it is about God's larger plan moving through small acts of loyal love.

The line of David is quietly being prepared.

That means the hope Israel needs after Judges is already being planted in Ruth.

## Closing

That is Day 67.

Judges 20 and 21 show civil war, grief, and the bitter end of everyone doing what is right in his own eyes.

Ruth 1 and 2 show loyal love, ordinary provision, and the LORD's hidden kindness.

In the days of the judges, darkness is real.

But God is not absent.

He is quietly preparing hope through Ruth, Naomi, Boaz, and the line that will lead to David.

## Final Meditation

Day 67 teaches us how the Bible can put deep darkness and quiet hope beside each other without pretending either one is small.

Judges 20 and 21 are not quickly fixed by Ruth 1 and 2.

The civil war was real.

The near loss of Benjamin was real.

The grief of Israel was real.

The final diagnosis of Judges was real.

Every man did what was right in his own eyes.

Ruth does not erase that.

Instead, Ruth shows that the LORD can preserve faithfulness in the same historical period.

That matters.

Sometimes the Bible shows God's work through national events.

Sometimes it shows God's work through one widow's loyalty on a road.

Both matter to God.

Judges 20 shows a nation gathering because evil has become unbearable.

But the chapter also shows how sin within the covenant people brings a cost that falls across the whole nation.

Benjamin's refusal to surrender the guilty men makes the wound deeper.

Israel wins in the end, but the victory is soaked in grief.

That is not the kind of victory that makes people boast.

It is the kind that makes people weep.

Judges 21 then shows how hard it is for people to repair damage when they are still guided by confused wisdom.

They want Benjamin to survive.

That desire is understandable.

But their methods remain troubling.

Vows, violence, and schemes continue.

The book ends without clean human resolution.

The reader is left longing for something better.

Then Ruth opens in Bethlehem and Moab.

There is no army.

No national assembly.

No battlefield.

But the stakes are still high.

Naomi's family is in danger of disappearing.

Ruth and Orpah are widows.

Naomi feels bitter and empty.

Ruth's choice matters because loyal love is costly.

She leaves Moab's future.

She leaves what would be familiar.

She binds herself to Naomi.

She binds herself to Naomi's people.

Most importantly, she names Naomi's God as her God.

That is why Ruth's words should not be treated as sentimental poetry only.

They are covenant-shaped commitment.

They are faith spoken on a road of grief.

They are loyalty when loyalty is expensive.

Then Ruth 2 shows faithfulness in the field.

Ruth does not sit still and wait for rescue to appear.

She goes to glean.

She works.

She asks humbly.

Boaz uses his position to protect.

He does not use power to take from the vulnerable.

He uses power to shelter Ruth.

That contrast with Judges is important.

Judges has shown power harming the vulnerable.

Boaz shows power protecting the vulnerable.

Judges has shown men doing what is right in their own eyes.

Boaz acts in a way that reflects the LORD's kindness and law.

Judges ends with social collapse.

Ruth begins with covenant kindness in ordinary life.

Naomi's change is also important.

She begins by saying the LORD has brought her home empty.

But by the end of Ruth 2, she is blessing the LORD.

She sees kindness to the living and to the dead.

Hope has not fully arrived yet, but it has a name.

Boaz.

One of our next kinsmen.

This is how the LORD often works in Ruth.

Not with thunder.

Not with a battlefield.

Not with a prophet's speech.

With food.

With a field.

With a protector.

With a harvest.

With a loyal woman.

With a bitter woman beginning to hope.

Day 67 therefore trains the reader not to despise quiet faithfulness.

The LORD can be at work in a field while the nation is broken.

The LORD can be preserving a royal line through a foreign widow.

The LORD can be answering bitterness through barley.

The LORD can be preparing David through Boaz and Ruth.

So carry both halves of the day together.

Judges shows what happens when covenant life collapses.

Ruth shows that the LORD's kindness has not collapsed.

Judges makes us long for righteous rule.

Ruth quietly begins the family line through which David will come.

The darkness is real.

The hope is real too.

## How To Listen To This Day

Listen to Judges 20 with grief, not excitement.

The battle is not a clean adventure story.

It is a family war inside the people of God.

The chapter teaches that refusing to judge sin can bring a larger judgment later.

Listen to Judges 21 with honesty.

The people are trying to preserve Benjamin, but their methods still show the confusion of the age.

They are grieved, but grief alone does not make wisdom.

They need the LORD's order, not only human repair.

Then listen to Ruth 1 as a breath of quiet faithfulness.

Ruth's loyalty does not happen after everything becomes easy.

It happens on the road of loss.

She chooses Naomi while Naomi feels empty.

She chooses the LORD before she knows how the story will end.

Listen to Ruth 2 as providence in ordinary clothing.

Ruth goes to glean.

Boaz notices.

The law provides.

Naomi recognizes kindness.

No angel announces the plan.

No prophet explains the hidden pattern.

But the LORD is working.

That is one of Ruth's great gifts to the reader.

It teaches us to see mercy in ordinary faithfulness.

It teaches us that small acts of kindness may belong to a story larger than anyone can see.

It teaches us that God can begin answering national need through household loyalty.

The line to David begins quietly here.

That means Day 67 should leave the reader humbled and hopeful.

Humbled by the ruin of Judges.

Hopeful because the LORD is already planting mercy in Ruth.

## Chapter By Chapter Takeaways

Judges 20 teaches that justice delayed inside the covenant people can become justice paid for with terrible grief.

Gibeah's sin should have been judged.

Benjamin should not have protected wickedness.

Israel's outrage is understandable.

But the chapter still feels devastating because civil war tears the people apart.

The reader should feel that sin inside God's people is never private and never small.

It can require judgment that leaves everyone wounded.

Judges 21 teaches that human schemes cannot heal covenant collapse cleanly.

The people want Benjamin preserved.

That desire makes sense.

But their vows and solutions show continued confusion.

They are trying to repair brokenness while still acting from broken wisdom.

That is why the final line of Judges matters so much.

No king.

Everyone doing what is right in his own eyes.

Ruth 1 teaches loyal love in the middle of grief.

Naomi is not pretending.

She feels bitter.

She feels empty.

She speaks out of pain.

But Ruth clings.

Ruth chooses Naomi's people.

Ruth chooses Naomi's God.

The chapter shows faithfulness on a road, before any visible reward appears.

Ruth gleans.

Boaz protects.

The law provides.

But the reader can see God's mercy gathering grain by grain.

Together, these chapters teach that the LORD can place hope beside ruin.

Ruth begins by planting the answer.

The line to David is not introduced through a palace.

It begins with widows, a field, and a redeemer's kindness.

So Day 67 moves from a nation tearing itself apart to a field where mercy starts growing.
