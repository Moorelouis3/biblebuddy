# Bible in One Year - Day 65 Narrator Script

**Reading:** Judges 12-15

## Opening

Welcome to Day 65 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are reading Judges 12-15.

The title of today's lesson is Samson Begins His Troubled Calling.

As you listen, keep your Bible open if you can.

Each section begins with the Scripture range.

Then we slow down and explain what was just read in plain language.

The goal is not to make the Bible feel complicated.

The goal is to help you understand what the passage is saying, why it matters, and how it fits into the larger story of Scripture.

## Judges 12 - Conflict After Jephthah

**Scripture**

Read Judges 12.

**Narrator**

Judges 12 shows conflict inside Israel after a deliverance story. Ephraim confronts Jephthah, and the result is civil bloodshed. The word Shibboleth becomes a tragic test at the Jordan crossings. The chapter shows that Israel's danger is not only foreign oppression. The people are fractured within.

Now slow that down.

Conflict After Jephthah is not just a label for the scene. It is a doorway into the meaning of the passage.

This section is teaching us to notice civil conflict, Ephraim, Shibboleth, division, and fragile unity.

A beginner should not rush past those details.

They are the pieces that explain why the story matters.

Ask what the phrase is showing.

Is it showing God's mercy?

Is it showing Israel's weakness?

Is it showing a warning that will matter later?

Is it showing how the LORD keeps His promise even when people are unstable?

Those questions help the passage open up.

In Judges 12, the reader is not only collecting facts. The reader is learning how covenant life works after the land has been entered and after leadership begins to change.

The LORD is still the main character.

People may be brave, weak, confused, angry, faithful, or foolish, but the LORD is the One who sees the whole story.

He gives mercy.

He gives warning.

He exposes compromise.

He raises help when His people cry out.

That means this passage should be read with both honesty and hope.

Honesty, because the Bible does not hide sin, fear, pride, division, or foolish choices.

Hope, because the LORD is still working even when His people are not impressive.

This is why Joshua and Judges are so important in Bible in One Year.

Joshua shows the promise being received.

Judges shows what happens when received blessing is not guarded by remembered obedience.

So when you hear this section, listen for the pattern.

God gives.

People respond.

People drift.

God warns.

People suffer.

God shows mercy.

The pattern is painful, but it teaches us why the Bible keeps pointing forward to a deeper rescue than temporary deliverance.

The people need more than land.

They need faithful hearts before the LORD.

## Holding the Day Together

Day 65 teaches that Samson's story is not simple hero worship. God is at work, but Samson's appetites and anger show how troubled the period of the judges has become.

There is one more layer to notice.

These chapters are not isolated stories dropped into the Bible at random.

They continue the covenant story that began with God's promises, moved through Egypt, crossed the wilderness, and entered the land.

When Israel remembers the LORD, there is life, courage, order, and mercy.

When Israel forgets the LORD, confusion grows quickly.

That pattern is one of the main lessons of Joshua and Judges.

God is faithful.

His people are often unstable.

The story keeps showing the need for more than temporary rescue.

The people need hearts that remember, trust, and obey the LORD.

That is why these chapters matter for a beginner.

They teach us not to read the Bible as a list of heroes who always do the right thing.

They teach us to read the Bible as the honest story of God dealing with real people.

Some people act with courage.

Some people act with foolishness.

Some people start well and end badly.

Some people are weak, but the LORD still uses them.

Through all of it, the Bible keeps telling the truth.

Sin brings damage.

Forgetting God brings drift.

Divided worship brings confusion.

But the LORD remains merciful, patient, just, and faithful to His purposes.


## Final Teaching Pass

Before we close, let us walk through the day one more time in a very simple way.

Bible reading becomes clearer when we ask what the passage is doing.

Some passages give commands.

Some passages tell history.

Some passages warn.

Some passages show failure.

Some passages show mercy.

In this day, those things are woven together.

The story is not trying to make Israel look impressive.

It is telling the truth about a people who received promises, saw God's power, and still needed to remember Him.

That is why these chapters should be read slowly.

When the passage talks about land, it is not only talking about dirt on a map.

The land is inheritance.

It is the place God promised.

It is the place where Israel must learn to live as His people.

When the passage talks about leaders, it is not only giving names.

Leadership reveals the spiritual health of the people.

A faithful leader points the people back to the LORD.

A proud or careless leader pulls the people toward danger.

When the passage talks about enemies, it is not only giving military history.

Enemies expose fear, compromise, dependence, and the need for deliverance.

When the passage talks about worship, it is not only describing religious activity.

Worship reveals loyalty.

Who will Israel serve?

Whose word will they trust?

Whose ways will they follow when pressure comes?

Those are the questions under the surface.

A beginner may feel overwhelmed by names, places, tribes, battles, vows, judges, and family conflicts.

That is normal.

The way to follow the story is to keep returning to the main thread.

The LORD gives.

The LORD warns.

The LORD delivers.

The LORD judges evil.

The LORD remains faithful.

Israel receives blessing.

Israel struggles to remember.

Israel needs more than outward rescue.

Israel needs a heart that stays with God.

That is why Joshua and Judges are so important.

Joshua shows the goodness of promise fulfilled.

Judges shows the sorrow of promise received but not guarded.

The people can live in the land and still drift from the LORD.

They can have history and still forget.

They can have victories and still fall into pride.

They can have deliverers and still need deeper rescue.

That is not just ancient history.

It teaches us how the Bible understands the human heart.

People can be helped by God and still wander.

People can be warned by God and still ignore Him.

People can cry out in trouble and still return to old patterns later.

The Bible is honest about that.

It does not pretend that one emotional moment fixes everything.

It keeps showing that faithfulness must be remembered, taught, practiced, and guarded.

So as you listen to this day, do not only ask, what happened next?

Ask, what is this showing me about God?

Ask, what is this showing me about people?

Ask, what is this showing me about obedience, memory, worship, compromise, courage, and mercy?

Those questions make the reading come alive.

They help each section become more than information.

They turn the day into Bible study.

And they prepare us for the rest of Scripture.

Because the story will keep moving forward.

Israel will continue to need better leadership.

Israel will continue to need a faithful king.

Israel will continue to need hearts changed by God.

The failures in Judges are not the end of the Bible story.

They are part of the reason the Bible keeps pointing ahead.

God's people need rescue that reaches deeper than a battlefield.

They need rescue from sin, forgetfulness, idolatry, pride, fear, and divided hearts.

That deeper need will keep growing clearer as the Bible continues.

For now, let this day teach you to read with patience.

Notice repeated phrases.

Notice warnings.

Notice mercy.

Notice where people remember the LORD.

Notice where they forget Him.

And notice that God keeps working even in messy chapters.

Grace comes first.

When grace is separated from obedience, people become careless.

The Bible holds them together.

## Full Lesson Review

Day 65 continues Jephthah's aftermath and then begins Samson's troubled calling.

Judges 12 opens with conflict inside Israel.

The men of Ephraim gather against Jephthah and threaten to burn his house.

This is tragic because the enemy is no longer only outside Israel.

Tribal pride and anger turn Israelites against Israelites.

Jephthah answers that he called Ephraim, but they did not deliver him.

Then Gilead fights Ephraim.

At the passages of Jordan, the word Shibboleth becomes a test.

Those who cannot pronounce it are killed.

A word becomes the line between life and death.

The scene shows how fractured Israel has become.

After Jephthah, the chapter briefly records Ibzan, Elon, and Abdon.

Their accounts are short.

They judged Israel for a time, had families or influence, died, and the story moved on.

The repeated deaths remind us that each judge is temporary.

Judges 13 then opens the Samson story.

Israel again does evil in the sight of the LORD.

The LORD delivers them into the hand of the Philistines forty years.

Samson's birth is announced to Manoah's wife, who is barren.

The angel says she will bear a son.

He is to be a Nazarite unto God from the womb.

No razor shall come on his head.

He shall begin to deliver Israel out of the hand of the Philistines.

That word begin matters.

Samson will not finish the whole deliverance.

His calling is real, but the story remains troubled.

Manoah prays for instruction.

The angel returns.

Manoah and his wife learn that the child must be raised under a holy calling.

When the angel ascends in the flame of the altar, Manoah fears they will die.

His wife answers with wisdom.

If the LORD meant to kill them, He would not have received the offering or shown them these things.

Then Samson is born, blessed by the LORD, and the Spirit of the LORD begins to move him.

Judges 14 immediately shows tension.

Samson goes down to Timnath and sees a Philistine woman.

He tells his parents to get her for him to wife.

His parents ask whether there is no woman among his own people.

The text says the matter was of the LORD, because He sought an occasion against the Philistines.

This does not make Samson's impulses pure.

It shows that God is ruling even through a complicated and morally messy story.

Samson kills a young lion when the Spirit of the LORD comes mightily upon him.

Later he finds honey in the carcass.

That strange detail becomes his riddle at the feast.

The Philistine companions pressure his wife.

She presses Samson.

The riddle is solved through manipulation.

Samson responds with anger and violence.

The Spirit of the LORD comes upon him, and conflict with the Philistines deepens.

Judges 15 continues the cycle of personal revenge becoming national conflict.

Samson's wife has been given to another man.

Samson burns Philistine fields with foxes and firebrands.

The Philistines retaliate by burning her and her father.

Samson strikes them.

Then he dwells in the rock Etam.

Judah comes not to join him in deliverance, but to bind him and hand him over.

Their question is painful.

Knowest thou not that the Philistines are rulers over us.

They sound resigned to oppression.

Samson allows them to bind him, but when he comes to Lehi, the Spirit of the LORD comes mightily upon him.

The cords become as flax.

He takes a new jawbone of an ass and slays a thousand men.

Then the strong man becomes thirsty.

He calls on the LORD.

God opens a hollow place and gives water.

The chapter ends by saying Samson judged Israel in the days of the Philistines twenty years.

Day 65 therefore teaches a complicated mercy.

Samson is set apart from the womb, yet often driven by appetite and anger.

The Spirit empowers him, yet his relationships and choices are messy.

Israel needs deliverance, yet Judah seems more comfortable with Philistine rule than with Samson's disruption.

The LORD gives strength and water.

The LORD begins deliverance.

But the story keeps making us feel the need for a better savior than Samson.

That is the point.

Judges is not asking us to admire every deliverer without question.

It is showing God's mercy in a collapsing people and making us hunger for righteous rule.

One more detail makes Day 65 especially important.

Samson is called before he can choose anything.

The announcement comes before his birth.

The Nazarite sign is placed over his life from the womb.

His mother receives instructions before Samson ever speaks.

This means Samson's life begins with grace and calling.

But the chapters also show that calling does not make a person wise automatically.

Samson has real strength.

Samson has real purpose.

Samson is moved by the Spirit of the LORD.

Yet Samson also follows desire, plays with danger, speaks roughly, and is caught in cycles of revenge.

That tension is the reason his story is so memorable.

The reader is not supposed to flatten him into only a hero or only a failure.

He is a deliverer raised by God's mercy inside a broken generation.

His victories are real.

His flaws are real.

God's rule over the story is real.

Israel's need is also real.

Judah's fear in chapter 15 makes that need painfully clear.

They know the Philistines rule over them.

But instead of longing for freedom, they bind Samson to keep the peace.

Oppression has started to feel normal.

That is one of the saddest parts of the day.

The people who need deliverance are afraid of the disturbance deliverance brings.

So Day 65 is not only about Samson's strength.

It is about Israel's condition.

It is about a people so used to bondage that the deliverer becomes a problem to manage.

It is about God's mercy breaking into a situation where even His people have stopped expecting freedom.

The jawbone victory and the water from the hollow place both point back to dependence.

Samson can strike a thousand men, but he cannot keep himself alive without water from God.

The strong man still has to pray.

The LORD still has to provide.

That keeps the lesson from becoming a celebration of human power.

Strength is not salvation by itself.

Calling is not wisdom by itself.

Victory is not spiritual health by itself.

The LORD is the true rescuer, even when He uses a deeply complicated servant.

## Closing

That is Day 65.

Samson Begins His Troubled Calling helps us keep following the Bible's honest story of promise, failure, mercy, warning, and deliverance.

Carry this with you today: the LORD remains faithful, even when His people desperately need to be called back to Him.
