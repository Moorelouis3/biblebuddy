# Day 81 Narrator Script

## Opening

Welcome to Day 81 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are reading Second Samuel 20 through 23.

The title for today is David's Later Reign and Mighty Men.

This day shows the later shape of David's kingdom.

There is still rebellion.

There is still bloodshed.

There is still famine connected to unresolved guilt from Saul's house.

There are still battles with Philistine giants.

But there is also one of David's great songs of praise.

David looks back over the LORD's deliverance and says, The LORD is my rock, and my fortress, and my deliverer.

The day ends by naming David's mighty men.

These chapters remind us that David's reign is not only one man's story.

It includes wise women, fierce soldiers, painful consequences, public justice, worship, memory, and the LORD's covenant faithfulness.

## Second Samuel 20

Second Samuel 20 begins with Sheba, a man of Belial, blowing a trumpet and saying Israel has no part in David.

The wound between Israel and Judah has not healed.

Absalom's rebellion is over, but unrest remains.

The men of Israel follow Sheba, while the men of Judah stay with David.

David returns to Jerusalem and deals with the ten concubines Absalom had publicly shamed.

Then David tells Amasa to gather Judah within three days.

Amasa delays, so David sends Abishai after Sheba.

Joab goes too.

At Gibeon, Joab meets Amasa.

Joab takes Amasa by the beard as if to kiss him, but strikes him in the belly with the sword.

Amasa dies in the highway.

Again Joab's violence appears near David's throne.

The pursuit continues to Abel.

Joab's men batter the wall of the city.

A wise woman cries from the city and asks Joab why he will swallow up a city and a mother in Israel.

Joab says he only wants Sheba.

The woman speaks with wisdom to the people.

They cut off Sheba's head and throw it to Joab.

The city is spared.

The chapter ends with an administration list: Joab over the host, Benaiah over the Cherethites and Pelethites, Adoram over tribute, Jehoshaphat recorder, Sheva scribe, Zadok and Abiathar priests, and Ira a chief ruler about David.

The kingdom continues, but the reader should feel the tension.

Order exists, yet violence remains close.

## Second Samuel 21

Second Samuel 21 opens with famine for three years.

David inquires of the LORD.

The LORD says the famine is because of Saul and his bloody house, because he slew the Gibeonites.

The Gibeonites were not Israelites, but Israel had sworn to spare them in Joshua's day.

Saul had violated covenant mercy.

David asks what should be done.

The Gibeonites ask for seven men of Saul's sons to be delivered to them.

David spares Mephibosheth, Jonathan's son, because of the LORD's oath between David and Jonathan.

But seven descendants of Saul are given.

Rizpah, the mother of two of them, spreads sackcloth on a rock and guards the bodies from birds and beasts.

Her grief is long and visible.

When David hears what Rizpah has done, he gathers the bones of Saul and Jonathan from Jabeshgilead, gathers the bones of those who were hanged, and buries them in the sepulchre of Kish.

After that, God is entreated for the land.

The chapter then returns to battles with Philistine giants.

David grows faint in battle.

Abishai rescues him.

David's men swear he will no longer go out with them to battle, so he does not quench the light of Israel.

Other giants fall by the hands of David's servants.

The chapter shows David aging, the land needing justice, and faithful servants carrying the fight.

## Second Samuel 22

Second Samuel 22 is David's song to the LORD.

David speaks the words of this song in the day the LORD delivers him out of the hand of all his enemies and out of the hand of Saul.

He says the LORD is his rock, fortress, deliverer, God, strength, buckler, horn of salvation, high tower, refuge, and savior.

David remembers distress.

The waves of death compassed him.

The floods of ungodly men made him afraid.

The sorrows of hell compassed him.

The snares of death prevented him.

In distress, David called upon the LORD.

The LORD heard his voice.

The song describes the LORD coming in storm, smoke, fire, darkness, thunder, and arrows.

The LORD draws David out of many waters and delivers him from the strong enemy.

David says the LORD was his stay.

This song is not David boasting that life was easy.

It is David testifying that God rescued him when he could not rescue himself.

David also speaks of walking before the LORD.

He says God's way is perfect, the word of the LORD is tried, and God is a buckler to all who trust in Him.

The chapter ends with praise among the nations and mercy promised to David and to his seed forevermore.

That final line matters because it reaches back to the Davidic covenant.

David's rescue is personal, but it is also covenant-shaped.

## Second Samuel 23

Second Samuel 23 gives David's last words and the list of mighty men.

David is called the son of Jesse, the man raised up on high, the anointed of the God of Jacob, and the sweet psalmist of Israel.

He says the Spirit of the LORD spoke by him.

He speaks of a ruler over men who must be just, ruling in the fear of God.

That is the kingly ideal.

David knows the LORD has made with him an everlasting covenant, ordered in all things and sure.

Then the chapter names mighty men.

Josheb-basshebeth, Eleazar, and Shammah are highlighted.

Eleazar stands when Israel retreats and fights until his hand cleaves to the sword.

Shammah stands in a field of lentils and defends it.

Three mighty men break through the Philistine host to draw water from Bethlehem's well because David longed for it.

David will not drink it.

He pours it out to the LORD, saying it is the blood of men who went in jeopardy of their lives.

The chapter names Abishai, Benaiah, and many others.

The list teaches that David's kingdom was carried by costly loyalty.

It also names Uriah the Hittite at the end.

That name quietly reminds the reader of David's sin.

The Bible remembers both mighty loyalty and terrible failure.

## Full Lesson Review

Day 81 shows the later reign of David with honesty.

There is rebellion from Sheba.

There is Joab's murder of Amasa.

There is famine because Saul broke covenant with the Gibeonites.

There is Rizpah's grief.

There are battles where David needs help.

There is a great song of deliverance.

There are mighty men whose courage matters.

The day is not polished propaganda.

It is a layered kingdom story.

David is preserved by the LORD, supported by servants, corrected by consequences, and still resting on covenant mercy.

## Key Connections

First, Sheba's rebellion shows that political fractures remain after Absalom.

Second, Rizpah's vigil shows grief and unresolved injustice becoming visible.

Third, David's song teaches the reader how to remember deliverance.

Fourth, the mighty men list shows that faithful service is costly and remembered.

## Slow Walk Through the Day

Slow down with Second Samuel 20 first.

Sheba's words are not random political noise.

He says, We have no part in David.

That phrase attacks covenant unity.

David has returned after Absalom, but the hearts of the people are still bruised, proud, and divided.

One angry voice can gather a movement when a nation has not healed.

The Bible does not pretend that rebellion ends cleanly just because the main battle is over.

Sometimes the sword stops, but the fracture remains.

That is why this chapter feels unsettled.

David is king again, but the kingdom is still fragile.

Amasa's death also needs attention.

Joab greets him like a brother and kills him like an enemy.

The kiss and the sword stand side by side.

This is one of the dark patterns around Joab.

He is useful in battle, but he is dangerous near power.

He protects David's throne in one sense, yet he also stains the story with blood.

The reader should not confuse effectiveness with righteousness.

Joab can win fights, organize armies, and remove threats, but that does not make his violence clean.

Then the wise woman of Abel enters the story.

She asks Joab why he will destroy a mother in Israel.

She knows the city has value before God.

She knows wisdom can preserve life.

She does not have a throne, sword, or army, but she has understanding.

Her words stop a siege.

In a chapter full of male ambition and bloodshed, the city is spared through a woman's wisdom.

That matters.

The Bible keeps showing that God's story often turns through people who do not look powerful at first glance.

Now slow down with Second Samuel 21.

The famine is not treated as a weather problem only.

David inquires of the LORD.

The answer reaches backward into Saul's violence against the Gibeonites.

Israel had sworn to spare them.

Saul broke faith.

The point is not that every famine always has the same cause.

The point here is that covenant-breaking can poison the land, even when time has passed.

The LORD remembers vows.

The LORD remembers blood.

The LORD cares about people who were vulnerable under Israel's power.

Rizpah's vigil is one of the most painful scenes in David's later reign.

She cannot reverse the deaths.

She cannot command a court.

She cannot rebuild Saul's house.

But she can stay.

She spreads sackcloth on a rock and guards the bodies from birds and beasts.

Her grief becomes public testimony.

Her sorrow forces David to act with burial honor.

This is important for the listener.

The Bible does not rush past mourning.

It does not treat grief as useless.

Rizpah's long watch becomes part of how the land moves toward peace.

Then David's age appears in battle.

David, the giant killer, grows faint.

Abishai rescues him.

The men say David must no longer go out to battle with them, lest he quench the light of Israel.

The king who once stood over Goliath now needs others to stand for him.

That does not make David worthless.

It shows a new season.

The kingdom is not preserved by David's strength alone.

God has raised servants around him.

The mighty men matter because David is not the whole story.

Now slow down with Second Samuel 22.

David's song is the heart of the day.

It is placed after years of danger, grief, battles, and rescue.

David does not say, I was never afraid.

He says the waves of death compassed him.

He does not say, My enemies were weak.

He says the strong enemy hated him.

He does not say, I saved myself.

He says the LORD drew him out of many waters.

That is the testimony.

The LORD is rock when David is unstable.

The LORD is fortress when David is exposed.

The LORD is deliverer when David is trapped.

The LORD is high tower when David is hunted.

This is why the song piles up images.

One word is not enough for David's memory of God.

He needs rock, fortress, deliverer, shield, horn, tower, refuge, and savior.

Each word shows another side of rescue.

The storm language also matters.

The earth shakes.

Smoke comes out of God's nostrils.

Fire comes out of His mouth.

Darkness is under His feet.

Thunder fills the heavens.

David is saying that when the LORD rises to save, creation itself is pictured as trembling before Him.

The God who seemed silent was not absent.

The God who seemed hidden was not powerless.

The LORD heard David's cry.

That is the center.

Now slow down with Second Samuel 23.

David's last words describe the ruler God desires.

He that ruleth over men must be just, ruling in the fear of God.

This is not merely advice for ancient kings.

It is a standard for power.

Authority is supposed to live under the fear of God.

When rulers forget God, people suffer.

When rulers fear God, justice has a place to stand.

David also says the LORD made with him an everlasting covenant, ordered in all things and sure.

That phrase carries the whole David story.

David's house survives because God made a covenant.

David's hope is not that he never failed.

David's hope is that the LORD is faithful.

Then the mighty men are named.

This list can feel like names only, but it is more than that.

It is a memorial of costly loyalty.

Eleazar's hand cleaves to the sword.

Shammah stands in a field when others flee.

Three men risk their lives for water from Bethlehem.

David pours that water out to the LORD because he will not treat their sacrifice like a casual drink.

Loyalty is precious.

Sacrifice should not be consumed thoughtlessly.

The final name, Uriah the Hittite, lands heavily.

The list of honor ends with the man David wronged.

The Bible does not erase him.

David's kingdom has glory, courage, songs, and covenant.

It also has sin that must be remembered honestly.

## Guided Listening Reflection

Listen for three movements in this day.

First, listen for the damage left by sin.

Sheba's rebellion, Joab's murder, Saul's broken covenant, and David's past failure with Uriah all show that sin leaves marks.

The Bible is honest about those marks.

It does not decorate David's reign to make him look untouched.

Second, listen for the mercy that still holds the story.

The city of Abel is spared.

The land is entreated.

David is rescued.

The covenant is remembered.

The mighty men are honored.

The LORD keeps working in a story that has real wounds.

Third, listen for the way God uses many kinds of faithfulness.

The wise woman speaks.

Rizpah watches.

Abishai rescues.

The mighty men fight.

David sings.

Each act is different, but each one matters.

Faithfulness does not always look like the same task.

Sometimes it is courage in battle.

Sometimes it is wisdom in speech.

Sometimes it is grief that refuses to disappear.

Sometimes it is praise after survival.

Day 81 invites the listener to hold all of that together.

The kingdom is complicated.

The people are flawed.

The consequences are real.

The LORD is still rock, fortress, and deliverer.

## Final Guided Recap

Before the closing, gather the whole day one more time.

Second Samuel 20 teaches that a kingdom can be restored officially while still needing healing deeply.

David is back in Jerusalem, but Sheba can still stir Israel with one sentence.

That shows how fragile unity is when pride and suspicion remain under the surface.

The chapter also warns that brutal people can stand very close to public order.

Joab helps remove Sheba's threat, but he murders Amasa along the way.

The Bible lets the listener feel the discomfort.

The right outcome does not justify every method used to reach it.

Then the wise woman of Abel shows another way.

She does not save the city by force.

She saves it through careful speech and courageous wisdom.

Second Samuel 21 teaches that the LORD cares about old wrongs.

The Gibeonites were not forgotten by God.

Saul's house had violated covenant, and the land could not simply pretend it never happened.

Rizpah's grief teaches the listener to honor sorrow instead of rushing past it.

Her sackcloth on the rock becomes a witness that the dead matter, the grieving matter, and justice matters.

The battles with giants then remind us that David is aging.

He is still David, but he is not self-sufficient.

Others must fight around him.

Second Samuel 22 teaches the language of praise after rescue.

David remembers fear, death, enemies, and distress.

But he interprets his life through the LORD's deliverance.

The listener should hear David's song as testimony, not as decoration.

Every image of God in the song comes from lived need.

Rock is what David needed when the ground shook.

Fortress is what David needed when enemies surrounded him.

Deliverer is what David needed when he could not get himself out.

Second Samuel 23 teaches that covenant and service belong together.

David rests on the everlasting covenant.

The mighty men show costly loyalty.

Uriah's name keeps the story honest.

So Day 81 ends with worship that does not deny pain, courage that does not erase sin, and covenant mercy that remains stronger than David's failures.

## Prayerful Landing

Let this day settle with one honest thought.

The LORD does not need David's story to look clean in order to prove His faithfulness.

He is faithful in a story with rebellion, murder, famine, grief, age, battle, praise, and remembered sin.

That is part of the comfort of Scripture.

God's covenant mercy is not fragile.

It can meet real history.

It can preserve a wounded kingdom.

It can receive a song from a man who has known both rescue and failure.

So listen to David's final witness with humility.

The LORD is not only the God of beginnings.

He is the God who remains faithful through later chapters, painful consequences, and weary seasons.

## Closing

As you finish Day 81, remember David's song.

The LORD is the rock.

The LORD is the fortress.

The LORD is the deliverer.

David's story is full of danger, grief, sin, courage, and mercy.

But through it all, the LORD remains faithful to His covenant.
