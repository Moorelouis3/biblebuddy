# Day 84 Narrator Script

## Opening

Welcome to Day 84 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are reading First Kings 8 through 11.

The title for today is Temple Glory and Solomon's Fall.

This day begins with glory.

The ark comes into the temple.

The cloud fills the house of the LORD.

Solomon blesses the people and prays a long dedication prayer.

The LORD appears to Solomon again.

The queen of Sheba comes to hear his wisdom.

Solomon's wealth and fame grow.

But the day ends in tragedy.

Solomon loves many strange women.

His heart is turned after other gods.

The kingdom will be torn.

This day teaches that a glorious beginning does not remove the need for a faithful heart.

## First Kings 8

First Kings 8 opens with Solomon assembling the elders of Israel.

The ark of the covenant is brought up from the city of David.

The priests bring the ark into the most holy place, under the wings of the cherubims.

Inside the ark are the two tables of stone from Horeb.

When the priests come out, the cloud fills the house of the LORD.

The priests cannot stand to minister because of the cloud.

The glory of the LORD fills the house.

Solomon blesses the LORD, who spoke with His mouth to David and fulfilled it with His hand.

He says the LORD kept His promise.

Then Solomon stands before the altar, spreads his hands toward heaven, and prays.

He confesses there is no God like the LORD in heaven above or earth beneath.

He asks God to keep covenant and mercy.

He knows the temple cannot contain God.

The heaven and heaven of heavens cannot contain Him.

Yet Solomon asks the LORD to hear prayers made toward this place.

His prayer covers many situations: sin between neighbors, defeat before enemies, drought, famine, plague, strangers who come from far countries, battle, and exile.

Again and again, Solomon asks God to hear in heaven, forgive, act, and teach His people the good way.

The prayer understands human sin clearly.

There is no man that sinneth not.

The chapter ends with sacrifices and feasting.

The dedication is full of covenant memory, glory, prayer, forgiveness, and joy.

## First Kings 9

First Kings 9 says the LORD appears to Solomon a second time.

God says He has heard Solomon's prayer and supplication.

He has hallowed the house.

His name, eyes, and heart will be there perpetually.

But God also gives a warning.

If Solomon walks before Him with integrity and uprightness, God will establish the throne of his kingdom.

But if Solomon or his children turn from following the LORD, serve other gods, and worship them, Israel will be cut off from the land, and the house will become a proverb and byword.

People will ask why the LORD did this, and the answer will be that Israel forsook the LORD.

The warning matters because temple glory is not a substitute for covenant faithfulness.

The chapter then describes Solomon's building projects, labor force, offerings, and ships.

The kingdom is impressive.

But the warning hangs over the chapter.

The presence of the temple does not make disobedience safe.

## First Kings 10

First Kings 10 describes the queen of Sheba coming to test Solomon with hard questions.

She hears of Solomon's fame concerning the name of the LORD.

She comes with a very great train, spices, gold, and precious stones.

Solomon answers all her questions.

Nothing is hidden from him.

When she sees his wisdom, house, food, servants, ministers, apparel, cupbearers, and ascent to the house of the LORD, there is no more spirit in her.

She says the half was not told her.

She blesses the LORD who delighted in Solomon and set him on the throne of Israel.

Solomon's wisdom becomes witness to the nations.

The chapter then describes gold, shields, ivory throne, vessels, ships, silver, horses, chariots, and wealth.

Solomon exceeds all the kings of the earth for riches and wisdom.

All the earth seeks to Solomon to hear his wisdom.

This is dazzling.

But the abundance also raises tension.

The law had warned Israel's king not to multiply horses, wives, silver, and gold.

First Kings 10 shows Solomon's greatness, but it also prepares us for chapter 11.

## First Kings 11

First Kings 11 begins with a devastating turn.

King Solomon loved many strange women.

They were from nations concerning which the LORD said Israel should not go in to them, because they would turn away their heart after their gods.

Solomon clave unto these in love.

He had many wives and concubines.

When Solomon was old, his wives turned away his heart after other gods.

His heart was not perfect with the LORD his God, as was the heart of David his father.

Solomon goes after Ashtoreth and Milcom.

He builds high places for Chemosh and Molech.

The LORD is angry because Solomon's heart is turned from the LORD God of Israel, who had appeared to him twice.

God says the kingdom will be rent from him and given to his servant.

For David's sake, it will not happen in Solomon's days, and one tribe will remain for David's sake and Jerusalem's sake.

Adversaries rise: Hadad, Rezon, and Jeroboam.

Ahijah the prophet meets Jeroboam and tears a new garment into twelve pieces.

He gives Jeroboam ten pieces, signifying that the kingdom will be torn.

Yet God also gives Jeroboam a conditional promise if he will walk in God's ways.

Solomon seeks to kill Jeroboam, and Jeroboam flees to Egypt.

The chapter ends with Solomon's death and Rehoboam reigning in his stead.

The fall is tragic because Solomon had wisdom, glory, temple, warning, and divine appearance.

But his heart turned.

## Full Lesson Review

Day 84 begins with temple glory and ends with kingdom fracture.

The ark enters.

The cloud fills the house.

Solomon prays for forgiveness and mercy.

The LORD warns Solomon to walk faithfully.

The queen of Sheba sees wisdom and blesses the LORD.

Solomon's wealth grows.

Then Solomon's loves turn his heart away.

The kingdom will be torn.

The lesson is clear: glory around a person cannot replace loyalty within the heart.

## Key Connections

First, the temple dedication centers on God's covenant faithfulness.

Second, Solomon's prayer knows that God's people will need forgiveness.

Third, the LORD's warning makes obedience essential.

Fourth, Solomon's fall shows the danger of divided love.

## Slow Walk Through the Day

Begin with the ark entering the temple.

This is not just furniture being moved.

The ark is the ark of the covenant of the LORD.

It carries the memory of God's covenant presence with Israel.

When the priests bring it into the most holy place, under the wings of the cherubims, the temple reaches its central purpose.

The house is not merely complete because the structure is finished.

The house is complete because the covenant symbol is brought into its place before the LORD.

Inside the ark are the two tables of stone from Horeb.

That detail matters.

At the center of the temple is covenant testimony.

Israel's worship is not built on vague spiritual feeling.

It is built around the God who spoke, commanded, redeemed, and made covenant.

Then the cloud fills the house.

The priests cannot stand to minister.

The glory of the LORD fills the house of the LORD.

This moment should slow the listener down.

Human ministry stops because divine glory fills the space.

The temple is beautiful, but the glory is not manufactured by gold or cedar.

The glory is the LORD's presence.

Solomon's blessing remembers that God spoke with His mouth and fulfilled with His hand.

That is one of the great covenant truths of the chapter.

God says what He will do.

God does what He has said.

The temple is visible proof that the LORD keeps promise.

Now listen to Solomon's prayer.

Solomon knows the temple cannot contain God.

The heaven and heaven of heavens cannot contain Him.

That means Solomon is not confused.

He is not saying God is trapped inside a building.

He is asking the infinite God to hear prayers directed toward the place where He has put His name.

The prayer keeps repeating the need for God to hear in heaven and forgive.

Solomon names ordinary and severe situations.

Sin between neighbors.

Defeat before enemies.

Drought.

Famine.

Pestilence.

Strangers coming from far countries.

Battle.

Captivity.

Exile.

The prayer is realistic about human failure.

There is no man that sinneth not.

That line is crucial.

The dedication of the temple is not built on the fantasy that Israel will never sin again.

It is built on the plea that when they sin, repent, pray, and turn, the LORD will hear and forgive.

The temple is a place of glory, but it is also a place for sinners to seek mercy.

Now move to First Kings 9.

The LORD appears to Solomon again.

He says He has heard the prayer.

He has hallowed the house.

His name, eyes, and heart will be there perpetually.

Those words are tender and weighty.

But the warning follows immediately.

If Solomon walks before the LORD, the throne is established.

If Solomon or his children turn aside, serve other gods, and worship them, Israel will be cut off.

The house itself will become a proverb and byword.

This is one of the most important lessons in the whole Solomon story.

A holy building does not make disobedience harmless.

The temple is not a shield for idolatry.

God's presence is covenant blessing, not permission to rebel.

The rest of the chapter shows building, labor, offerings, and ships.

The kingdom looks strong.

But the warning still stands over everything.

Now listen to First Kings 10.

The queen of Sheba comes because Solomon's fame has spread concerning the name of the LORD.

That phrase matters.

Solomon's wisdom is not supposed to terminate on Solomon.

It is supposed to point to the LORD who gave it.

The queen sees his wisdom, house, servants, order, food, apparel, cupbearers, and ascent to the house of the LORD.

There is no more spirit in her.

She says the half was not told her.

She blesses the LORD.

This is the high point of Solomon's witness to the nations.

A foreign queen sees wisdom and blesses Israel's God.

Yet the same chapter piles up wealth, gold, horses, chariots, and splendor.

The listener should remember Deuteronomy's warnings about kings.

The king must not multiply horses.

The king must not multiply wives.

The king must not greatly multiply silver and gold.

First Kings 10 is dazzling, but it is also dangerous.

The story is glowing at the edges before the fire of chapter 11 breaks out.

Now slow down with First Kings 11.

King Solomon loved many strange women.

That opening line is devastating because the issue reaches the heart.

The LORD had warned Israel that these nations would turn their heart after other gods.

Solomon clave unto these in love.

The word clave shows attachment.

Solomon's problem is not merely administrative.

It is affectional.

His loves reorder his worship.

When Solomon is old, his wives turn away his heart after other gods.

The wise king becomes the divided king.

The temple builder builds high places for false gods.

The man who prayed for Israel to be forgiven now leads Israel into deep offense.

The LORD is angry because Solomon's heart is turned from the LORD God of Israel, who appeared to him twice.

That makes the fall heavier.

Solomon did not lack light.

He had wisdom.

He had warning.

He had temple glory.

He had answered prayer.

He had divine appearances.

Still, his heart turned.

The kingdom will be torn.

Ahijah's torn garment gives a picture to the judgment.

The outer kingdom fracture begins with an inner heart fracture.

Solomon's divided love becomes a divided kingdom.

## Guided Listening Reflection

Day 84 should be heard as one long contrast.

Cloud and idols.

Prayer and disobedience.

Wisdom and folly.

Glory and fracture.

The beginning shows what God gives.

The ending shows what divided love destroys.

Do not rush past the fact that Solomon's fall happens after great blessing.

Spiritual privilege does not make the heart automatically faithful.

The listener should also notice how Solomon's prayer almost prepares us to understand the future.

He prays for forgiveness when God's people sin, lose battles, experience drought, suffer famine, or go into exile.

Later Israel will need exactly that mercy.

The dedication prayer becomes a map for repentance.

The temple is dedicated in glory, but the people will survive only by God's covenant mercy.

This day also teaches that wisdom must be guarded.

Solomon had wisdom, but he did not guard his heart.

He could answer hard questions from the queen of Sheba, but he did not keep his loves under the LORD's command.

That is a warning for every listener.

Knowing truth is not the same as loving God with an undivided heart.

By the end of the day, the torn garment is already hanging over the kingdom.

The LORD preserves one tribe for David's sake and Jerusalem's sake, but the fracture is coming.

The covenant promise remains, yet Solomon's sin brings real consequences.

Hold both truths together.

God is faithful.

Sin is destructive.

The temple is glorious.

The heart must still belong to the LORD.

## Final Guided Recap

Before the closing, walk through the contrast again.

First Kings 8 begins with covenant glory.

The ark is placed in the most holy place.

The cloud fills the house.

The priests cannot stand to minister.

Solomon blesses the LORD because God has fulfilled what He spoke.

Then Solomon prays with both reverence and realism.

He knows God cannot be contained by the house.

He also knows the people will need mercy again and again.

That prayer is one of the great moments in Kings because it joins glory with forgiveness.

The temple is not a monument to human success.

It is a place where God's sinful people will seek the God who hears from heaven.

First Kings 9 gives the divine answer and warning.

The LORD hears.

The LORD hallows the house.

The LORD puts His name there.

But He also warns that turning after other gods will bring judgment.

The listener should hear this warning as mercy.

God tells Solomon the truth before Solomon's fall.

He does not hide the danger.

First Kings 10 shows Solomon at the height of visible glory.

The queen of Sheba sees wisdom that takes her breath away.

She blesses the LORD.

The nations are being drawn by the gift God gave Solomon.

Yet the wealth, horses, and splendor begin to sound like the warning given for kings.

The chapter shines, but it also trembles.

First Kings 11 shows the heart turning.

Solomon's many loves lead him after other gods.

The temple builder becomes a high-place builder.

The wise king becomes divided.

The LORD announces that the kingdom will be torn.

The torn garment in Ahijah's hands makes the judgment visible.

So Day 84 teaches that the heart must be guarded after blessing, not only before blessing.

Glory does not make obedience optional.

Wisdom does not protect a person who refuses to keep loving the LORD first.

The story begins with a cloud filling the house and ends with a kingdom about to tear.

That is why the day should be heard with awe and warning together.

## Prayerful Landing

Let the listener end this day with a guarded heart.

Solomon's story warns us that a person can stand near holy things and still drift toward false worship.

He can pray true words and later love what turns him away.

He can build for the LORD and later build for idols.

This is why the heart must not be left unattended.

The question is not only what Solomon knew.

He knew much.

The question is what Solomon loved.

His loves pulled his worship apart.

Day 84 is therefore not just ancient history.

It is a warning about divided devotion.

The LORD deserves the whole heart.

Temple glory, wisdom, wealth, influence, and public honor cannot make a divided heart safe.

The good news inside the warning is that the LORD speaks before judgment.

He warns Solomon.

He names the danger.

He keeps covenant for David's sake.

Even here, God's faithfulness is still visible.

But the warning remains sharp: keep your heart with the LORD.

## Closing

As you finish Day 84, remember the cloud and the torn garment.

The cloud shows the glory of God's presence.

The torn garment shows the consequence of a divided heart.

Solomon's story warns us to keep loving the LORD with an undivided heart.
