# Bible in One Year - Day 57 Narrator Script

**Reading:** Joshua 4-7

## Opening

Welcome to Day 57 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are reading Joshua chapters 4 through 7.

The theme is memorial stones, Jericho, and Achan.

Israel has crossed the Jordan.

Now the LORD teaches them to remember, renews covenant signs at Gilgal, gives Jericho into Joshua's hand, rescues Rahab, and exposes hidden sin in the camp.

This day moves from miracle to memory.

It moves from worship to victory.

It moves from victory to warning.

That order matters.

God's people must remember His works.

They must obey His word.

They must not treat success as permission to become careless with sin.

## Joshua 4 verses 1 through 24

**Scripture**

After all the people pass over Jordan, the LORD commands Joshua to choose twelve men, one from each tribe.

They take twelve stones from the place where the priests stood in the Jordan.

The stones are set up as a memorial.

Future children will ask what the stones mean.

Israel must tell them that the LORD dried up the Jordan, as He dried up the Red Sea.

**Narrator**

Joshua 4 turns miracle into memory.

The Jordan crossing is not supposed to be forgotten.

The LORD commands twelve stones to be taken from the river.

One man from each tribe carries a stone.

That means the memorial belongs to all Israel.

The crossing was not a private experience for a few leaders.

It was a covenant moment for the whole people.

The stones are set up so children will ask questions.

What mean ye by these stones?

That question matters.

God expects the next generation to learn the story.

The stones are not decoration.

They are teaching tools.

When children ask, parents must answer.

They must say that the waters of Jordan were cut off before the ark of the covenant of the LORD.

They must remember that Israel crossed on dry ground.

Joshua connects the Jordan to the Red Sea.

The LORD dried up the Red Sea when Israel came out of Egypt.

Now He dries up the Jordan as Israel enters the land.

That means the new generation is being taught that the same God is still leading.

The miracle also has a public purpose.

All the people of the earth may know the hand of the LORD, that it is mighty.

Israel must fear the LORD their God forever.

So the stones teach both witness and worship.

They tell the nations something about God's power.

They tell Israel something about faithful fear.

For Bible Buddy readers, this chapter is a clear reminder that faith needs memory.

God's people can forget even great miracles.

So the LORD builds reminders into the life of His people.

Memory is not nostalgia.

Memory trains trust.

The stones say, the LORD opened the way here.

The stones say, tell your children.

The stones say, fear the LORD.

## Joshua 5 verses 1 through 15

**Scripture**

The kings west of Jordan hear that the LORD dried up the Jordan, and their hearts melt.

The LORD commands Joshua to circumcise the children of Israel.

Israel keeps the Passover at Gilgal.

The manna ceases after they eat the old corn of the land.

Joshua sees a man with a drawn sword.

The man identifies himself as captain of the host of the LORD.

Joshua falls on his face and worships.

**Narrator**

Joshua 5 is surprising because it slows down before Jericho.

The reader might expect immediate battle.

Instead, the LORD brings Israel back to covenant signs.

The men born in the wilderness had not been circumcised.

So before the walls fall, Israel must be marked again as the covenant people.

This teaches that identity comes before battle.

Israel is not merely an army.

They are the people of the LORD.

Gilgal becomes a place of renewal.

The reproach of Egypt is rolled away.

Then Israel keeps the Passover.

That is important.

They are standing inside the promised land, but they must remember the night of rescue from Egypt.

Passover tells them who saved them.

The land does not erase the exodus.

The blessing does not replace the memory of deliverance.

Then the manna ceases.

For forty years, manna had fed Israel in the wilderness.

Now they eat the fruit of the land.

This is a transition from wilderness provision to land provision.

The LORD provided in the wilderness.

The LORD provides in the land.

His care changes form, but His faithfulness continues.

Then Joshua meets the captain of the LORD's host.

Joshua asks, Art thou for us, or for our adversaries?

The answer is not what we might expect.

Nay; but as captain of the host of the LORD am I now come.

Joshua must learn that the real question is not whether God joins Joshua's side as a tool.

The real question is whether Joshua bows before the LORD's command.

Joshua falls on his face.

He asks, What saith my lord unto his servant?

Then he is told to remove his shoe because the place is holy.

That sounds like Moses at the burning bush.

Joshua is being reminded that holy ground comes from God's presence.

Before Jericho, Joshua is not given a pep talk about his greatness.

He is brought low in worship.

That is how faithful leadership stands before battle.

## Joshua 6 verses 1 through 27

**Scripture**

Jericho is shut up because of Israel.

The LORD tells Joshua that He has given Jericho into his hand.

Israel must march around the city with priests, trumpets, and the ark.

On the seventh day they march seven times.

The priests blow the trumpets, the people shout, and the wall falls down flat.

Rahab and her family are spared.

The city is devoted to destruction, but silver, gold, brass, and iron go into the treasury of the LORD.

**Narrator**

Joshua 6 is the fall of Jericho.

The chapter begins by saying Jericho was straitly shut up.

No one went out.

No one came in.

From a human point of view, Jericho is closed and fortified.

But the LORD says, See, I have given into thine hand Jericho.

The victory is announced before the battle begins.

The battle plan is unusual.

Marching.

Priests.

Trumpets.

The ark.

Silence.

Shouting.

This is not a normal siege strategy.

The point is obedience.

Israel must do what the LORD says even when the command does not look impressive.

The ark is central again.

God's presence is at the center of the march.

The walls fall after Israel follows God's word.

That teaches that the victory belongs to the LORD.

Rahab is spared.

This matters.

Joshua 2 promised mercy.

Joshua 6 keeps that promise.

Rahab and her family are brought out.

Judgment falls on Jericho, but mercy is real for the household marked by the sign.

The chapter also warns Israel about devoted things.

The city is accursed, or devoted to destruction.

Israel must not take what belongs under God's command.

Silver, gold, brass, and iron go into the treasury of the LORD.

The victory is not a chance for private greed.

The chapter ends with Joshua's fame spreading.

But the reader should not miss the real point.

Jericho falls because the LORD gives the city.

Israel's part is obedience.

Rahab's rescue shows mercy.

The devoted things show holy seriousness.

## Joshua 7 verses 1 through 26

**Scripture**

The children of Israel commit a trespass in the accursed thing because Achan takes what was devoted.

Israel sends men against Ai and is defeated.

Joshua falls before the ark.

The LORD tells him to get up because Israel has sinned.

Achan is exposed by tribe, family, household, and man.

He confesses that he took a Babylonish garment, silver, and gold.

Judgment falls, and the place is called the valley of Achor.

**Narrator**

Joshua 7 is a hard chapter.

It comes right after Jericho.

That placement matters.

Victory does not make Israel immune to sin.

The chapter says the children of Israel committed a trespass, even though Achan is the one named.

That teaches that hidden sin can damage the covenant community.

Achan takes from the devoted things.

He sees.

He covets.

He takes.

He hides.

That pattern is spiritually dangerous.

It echoes the old pattern of sin: desire, taking, hiding, and shame.

Then Israel attacks Ai with confidence.

Ai is smaller than Jericho.

The spies say only a few thousand men are needed.

But Israel is defeated.

Their hearts melt and become as water.

The people who just saw Jericho fall now feel fear because the LORD is not with their disobedience.

Joshua mourns before the ark.

He is distressed and confused.

But the LORD tells him, Get thee up.

Wherefore liest thou thus upon thy face?

Israel hath sinned.

The answer is not military strategy.

The answer is covenant sin.

The hidden thing must be brought into the light.

Achan is exposed step by step.

Tribe.

Family.

Household.

Man.

He confesses that he took a garment, silver, and gold and hid them in the earth.

The hidden treasure becomes public evidence.

The judgment is severe because the sin was severe.

Israel cannot begin life in the land by treating God's command lightly.

For modern readers, this chapter can feel heavy.

It should.

Joshua 7 teaches holy seriousness.

God's people cannot use yesterday's victory as cover for today's disobedience.

The LORD who gave Jericho also exposes Achan.

This chapter keeps victory from becoming pride.

Day 57 therefore holds memory, worship, victory, mercy, and warning together.

The stones say remember.

Gilgal says renew covenant.

Jericho says obey the LORD.

Rahab says mercy is real.

Achan says hidden sin is deadly.

## Closing

That is Day 57.

Joshua 4 through 7 teaches memory, covenant renewal, obedience, mercy, and the seriousness of sin.

The LORD opens the Jordan and commands memorial stones.

He brings Israel back to covenant signs before battle.

He gives Jericho into their hand and keeps mercy for Rahab.

He also exposes Achan's hidden sin.

Victory is not permission to become careless.

God's people must remember, worship, obey, and walk honestly before the LORD.

## Full Lesson Review

Day 57 begins after the Jordan crossing, but the first command is not to rush forward.

The LORD commands remembrance.

Joshua 4 tells Israel to take twelve stones out of Jordan, from the place where the priests' feet stood firm.

Those stones are not random rocks.

They are witnesses.

They carry a story.

When children later ask, What mean ye by these stones, Israel must answer.

They must tell the next generation that the waters of Jordan were cut off before the ark of the covenant of the LORD.

That is why this chapter is so important.

The miracle is not meant to fade into a private memory.

It is meant to become family teaching.

It is meant to become worship.

It is meant to become testimony.

The people who walked through Jordan must become people who explain what the LORD has done.

This is one reason the memorial stones matter for BibleBuddy notes too.

A reader should not only know that stones were stacked.

A reader should know why the stones were stacked.

They were for memory.

They were for children.

They were for public witness.

They were for the fear of the LORD in all the earth.

Then Joshua 5 shows Israel stopping at Gilgal.

Again, the movement is slower than we might expect.

The people have crossed the Jordan.

Jericho is near.

The armies of Canaan are afraid.

But before battle, the LORD deals with covenant identity.

The new generation must be circumcised.

The reproach of Egypt is rolled away.

They keep the passover.

They eat the old corn of the land.

The manna ceases.

This chapter teaches that victory does not come before covenant.

Israel is not first a military force.

Israel is the LORD's covenant people.

They must bear the sign of the covenant.

They must remember deliverance from Egypt.

They must learn that the wilderness provision has brought them into land provision.

The manna stopping is not abandonment.

It is transition.

The LORD fed them in the wilderness.

Now the LORD feeds them in the land.

That same chapter brings Joshua near Jericho.

Joshua sees a man over against him with his sword drawn in his hand.

Joshua asks, Art thou for us, or for our adversaries.

The answer is not what Joshua might expect.

Nay; but as captain of the host of the LORD am I now come.

That moment humbles Joshua.

The conquest is not about God joining Joshua's agenda.

Joshua must bow before the LORD's commander.

He falls on his face.

He worships.

He is told to loose his shoe from off his foot, for the place where he stands is holy.

Jericho will not fall because Joshua is clever.

Jericho will fall because the battle belongs to the LORD.

Joshua 6 proves that.

The strategy is strange.

The people march.

The priests blow trumpets.

The ark goes with them.

They circle the city.

They are silent until the appointed shout.

This is obedience before it is explanation.

Jericho is shut up.

No one goes out.

No one comes in.

But the LORD says, See, I have given into thine hand Jericho.

The gift is spoken before the walls fall.

Faith receives God's word before sight receives proof.

For six days they march once.

On the seventh day they march seven times.

Then the people shout.

The wall falls down flat.

The city is taken.

Yet the story also remembers Rahab.

Joshua commands that Rahab and her household be brought out.

The scarlet cord was not forgotten.

Mercy spoken in chapter 2 is mercy kept in chapter 6.

That matters.

The LORD judges Jericho, but He does not forget the house marked for mercy.

Rahab lives.

Her family lives.

She dwells in Israel.

Then Joshua 7 changes the tone sharply.

The chapter begins with the children of Israel committing a trespass in the accursed thing.

Achan takes what was devoted.

The hidden sin of one man brings trouble on the camp.

Israel goes up against Ai with confidence, but they are defeated.

The hearts of the people melt.

Joshua falls before the ark.

He grieves.

He asks why the LORD brought them over Jordan.

The LORD answers with clarity.

Israel hath sinned.

This is the needed word.

The problem is not that the LORD is weak.

The problem is not that Ai is too strong.

The problem is covenant disobedience inside Israel.

The accursed thing has been taken.

The people cannot stand before their enemies while this sin remains hidden.

The chapter then exposes Achan.

He saw a goodly Babylonish garment.

He saw silver.

He saw gold.

He coveted.

He took.

He hid them in the earth in the midst of his tent.

That order is painfully familiar.

He saw.

He wanted.

He took.

He hid.

Sin often moves that way.

But hidden sin is not hidden from God.

The LORD brings it into the light.

The valley of Achor becomes a place of trouble.

Day 57 must hold both Jericho and Achan.

If we only hear Jericho, we may think victory is automatic.

If we only hear Achan, we may forget mercy.

The chapter sequence teaches both.

The LORD gives victory.

The LORD keeps mercy.

The LORD requires holiness.

The LORD exposes hidden sin.

The stones call Israel to remember.

Gilgal calls Israel to covenant renewal.

The captain calls Joshua to worship.

Jericho calls Israel to obey.

Rahab calls Israel to marvel at mercy.

Achan calls Israel to fear the LORD and walk honestly.

That is why this day has such weight.

It is not just action.

It is formation.

Israel is being formed as a people who remember, worship, obey, receive mercy, and take sin seriously.

For a listener today, the lesson is still direct.

Do not forget what God has done.

Do not treat your children as if they can understand faith without testimony.

Do not rush into battles while neglecting covenant faithfulness.

Do not assume God is merely on your side.

Bow before the LORD and ask what He commands.

Do not confuse visible success with permission to hide sin.

Do not despise mercy when God gives it to someone like Rahab.

And do not imagine that secret disobedience is harmless.

The LORD is faithful.

The LORD is holy.

The LORD saves.

The LORD judges.

Day 57 makes us sit with all of that.

## Final Meditation

Let Day 57 land as a lesson about what happens after God brings His people through.

Crossing Jordan was not the end of formation.

It was the beginning of a new kind of responsibility.

The stones teach that memory must be protected.

God's people forget easily.

Children are born who did not see the river stop.

Families settle into ordinary life and may stop asking how they arrived there.

So the LORD commands a visible witness.

The stones stand so that a question will be asked.

The question opens a door for testimony.

The testimony turns memory into worship.

That is the first shape of Day 57.

Remember what the LORD has done, and teach it clearly.

Then Gilgal teaches that covenant identity matters before public victory.

Israel does not rush straight to Jericho as if the battle is only tactical.

They bear the covenant sign.

They keep passover.

They eat from the produce of the land.

They learn that the LORD who fed them with manna has now brought them into a new provision.

The people must know who they are before they march around the wall.

They belong to the LORD.

Jericho then teaches obedience that may look strange to human wisdom.

Marching quietly around a fortified city is not a normal siege plan.

But the plan comes from the LORD.

The question is not whether Israel can explain every part of it.

The question is whether Israel will obey.

Rahab's rescue teaches that mercy is not forgotten in judgment.

The spies had promised.

The scarlet cord had marked the house.

When the wall falls, the promise is kept.

That is important because God's judgment never makes Him unreliable.

He remembers the one who trusted His name.

Finally Achan teaches that victory does not make sin small.

Hidden disobedience inside the camp wounds the whole people.

The garment, silver, and gold buried in the tent are not private harmless things.

They are covenant trouble.

Day 57 therefore refuses shallow celebration.

It gives us stones and circumcision, passover and Jericho, Rahab and Achan.

It says remember.

It says worship.

It says obey.

It says trust mercy.

It says fear hidden sin.

This is not a scattered day.

It is a deeply ordered day.

The LORD is forming a people who must carry memory, covenant, obedience, mercy, and holiness together.

One more way to hear Day 57 is to notice the questions it creates.

When children ask about the stones, will the older generation answer with clear testimony?

When the people stand near Jericho, will they remember they belong to the LORD before they think about battle?

When the captain of the LORD's host appears, will Joshua try to command the moment, or will he worship?

When the walls fall, will Israel remember mercy for Rahab?

When Achan hides the devoted things, will Israel learn that God sees what no one else sees?

Those questions keep the day from becoming flat.

The chapters are not merely reporting events.

They are shaping the conscience of God's people.

They teach that memory needs words.

Covenant needs obedience.

Worship needs humility.

Mercy needs to be honored.

Sin needs to be brought into the light.

The same LORD is present through all of it.

He is present at Jordan.

He is present at Gilgal.

He is present near Jericho.

He is present when Rahab is rescued.

He is present when Achan is exposed.

That means Day 57 is not five disconnected stories.

It is one holy lesson about life before the LORD after He has brought His people into promise.
