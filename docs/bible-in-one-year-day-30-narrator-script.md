# Bible In One Year - Day 30 Narrator Script

## Day 30 - God's Presence and Renewed Obedience

**Reading:** Exodus 33 through 36  
**Bible text:** King James Version, Exodus 33-36  
**Audio style:** Narrator teaching with Scripture reading  

## Opening

Welcome to Day 30 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are reading Exodus 33 through 36.

This day comes after one of Israel's lowest moments.

The golden calf.

While Moses was on the mountain, the people made an idol.

They took gold, shaped it into a calf, and called it worship.

The people who had been rescued from Egypt almost immediately copied the kind of false worship they had just been delivered from.

So now Exodus 33 opens with a serious question hanging over the story.

Will God's presence still go with Israel?

The promised land is still ahead.

The journey is not over.

The covenant has been broken.

Moses has interceded.

Judgment has fallen.

And now the issue is not only, Will Israel reach the land?

The deeper issue is, Will God go with them?

That is what makes today's reading so powerful.

Moses understands something many people miss:

The blessing is not enough without the presence of God.

Land is not enough.

Success is not enough.

Provision is not enough.

Even answered promises are not enough if God Himself is absent.

So today we will see Moses plead for God's presence.

We will see God reveal His name.

We will see the covenant renewed.

We will see Moses' face shine.

And then we will see something beautiful:

The same people who gave gold for an idol now bring willing gifts for God's dwelling.

Exodus 33 through 36 is a story of mercy after failure.

It shows us that the story moves forward not because Israel is impressive, but because the LORD is merciful, gracious, patient, faithful, and willing to dwell with His people.

## Exodus 33:1-6

**Scripture**

And the LORD said unto Moses, Depart, and go up hence, thou and the people which thou hast brought up out of the land of Egypt.

Unto the land which I sware unto Abraham, to Isaac, and to Jacob.

And I will send an angel before thee.

For I will not go up in the midst of thee; for thou art a stiffnecked people: lest I consume thee in the way.

And when the people heard these evil tidings, they mourned.

And no man did put on him his ornaments.

**Narrator**

Exodus 33 begins in the aftermath of the golden calf.

God tells Moses the journey toward the promised land will continue.

The land promised to Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob is still real.

But then comes the devastating warning:

I will not go up in the midst of thee.

That sentence is heavy.

God is saying the people can still move toward the land, but His presence will not go with them in the same way because they are stiff-necked.

Stiff-necked means stubborn.

It pictures an animal that refuses to turn.

Israel has been resistant, hard, and unwilling to bend under God's word.

The people hear this and mourn.

They remove their ornaments.

That detail matters because ornaments had just been connected to the golden calf.

Their beauty, wealth, and decoration are now set aside in grief.

The seriousness of their sin is finally landing.

And the lesson is clear:

God's gifts are never meant to replace God Himself.

The land without God's presence would be empty blessing.

It would be promise without nearness.

And Moses knows that is not enough.

## Exodus 33:7-11

**Scripture**

And Moses took the tabernacle, and pitched it without the camp, afar off from the camp, and called it the Tabernacle of the congregation.

And it came to pass, that every one which sought the LORD went out unto the tabernacle of the congregation, which was without the camp.

And it came to pass, as Moses entered into the tabernacle, the cloudy pillar descended, and stood at the door of the tabernacle.

And the LORD spake unto Moses face to face, as a man speaketh unto his friend.

**Narrator**

Moses takes a tent and pitches it outside the camp.

This is called the tent of meeting.

The location matters.

Outside the camp.

Away from the center.

Israel's sin has disrupted nearness.

The tabernacle instructions were about God dwelling among His people, but the golden calf has created a crisis.

So now those who seek the LORD go out to the tent.

When Moses enters, the pillar of cloud descends.

The people stand at their tent doors and worship.

And the text says the LORD speaks to Moses face to face, as a man speaks to his friend.

That does not mean Moses sees the full essence of God.

Later in this same chapter, Moses will be told he cannot see God's face in that ultimate sense and live.

So the phrase is describing direct covenant access.

Moses has an unusual closeness with God.

He speaks with the LORD in a personal, direct way.

That matters because Moses is standing in the gap for a guilty people.

Israel's hope is not their own record.

Their hope is the mercy of God and the intercession of Moses.

## Exodus 33:12-23

**Scripture**

And Moses said unto the LORD, See, thou sayest unto me, Bring up this people.

Now therefore, I pray thee, if I have found grace in thy sight, shew me now thy way.

And he said, My presence shall go with thee, and I will give thee rest.

And he said unto him, If thy presence go not with me, carry us not up hence.

For wherein shall it be known here that I and thy people have found grace in thy sight? is it not in that thou goest with us?

And he said, I beseech thee, shew me thy glory.

**Narrator**

This is one of the greatest intercession scenes in the Old Testament.

Moses does not ask only for success.

He does not ask only for land.

He does not ask only for protection.

He asks for God's presence.

He says, If Your presence does not go with us, do not bring us up from here.

That is stunning.

Moses would rather stay in the wilderness with God than enter the promised land without Him.

He understands that the presence of the LORD is what makes Israel distinct.

Not their size.

Not their power.

Not their moral record.

Not their wisdom.

God's presence.

That is the difference.

Then God promises:

My presence shall go with thee, and I will give thee rest.

That is mercy.

After the golden calf, God does not abandon the covenant story.

He answers Moses with presence.

Then Moses asks for more:

Show me Thy glory.

And God's answer is fascinating.

He says He will make His goodness pass before Moses and proclaim His name.

That means God's glory is deeply connected to His character.

Moses asks to see glory, and God answers by revealing goodness, mercy, and His name.

God also protects Moses.

Moses cannot see the fullness of God's face and live.

So God places him in the cleft of the rock and covers him as His glory passes by.

God reveals Himself, but He also protects Moses from more than Moses can bear.

## Exodus 34:1-9

**Scripture**

And the LORD said unto Moses, Hew thee two tables of stone like unto the first.

And the LORD descended in the cloud, and stood with him there, and proclaimed the name of the LORD.

And the LORD passed by before him, and proclaimed,

The LORD, The LORD God, merciful and gracious, longsuffering, and abundant in goodness and truth,

Keeping mercy for thousands, forgiving iniquity and transgression and sin, and that will by no means clear the guilty.

And Moses made haste, and bowed his head toward the earth, and worshipped.

**Narrator**

Exodus 34 is one of the most important chapters in the Bible for understanding who God is.

God gives Moses new tablets.

The first tablets had been broken after the golden calf.

New tablets do not erase the sin, but they show covenant mercy.

God is renewing what Israel broke.

Then the LORD descends in the cloud and proclaims His name.

Listen to how God describes Himself:

Merciful.

Gracious.

Longsuffering.

Abundant in goodness and truth.

Keeping mercy.

Forgiving iniquity, transgression, and sin.

And also just.

He will by no means clear the guilty.

That balance is important.

God is not soft on sin.

But He is rich in mercy.

He is not careless with evil.

But He is ready to forgive.

This is not Moses making up a comforting idea about God.

This is God revealing God.

And Moses immediately bows and worships.

Then he pleads again:

Go among us.

Pardon our sin.

Take us for Your inheritance.

Moses knows Israel's only hope is the character God just revealed.

## Exodus 34:10-28

**Scripture**

And he said, Behold, I make a covenant.

Observe thou that which I command thee this day.

Take heed to thyself, lest thou make a covenant with the inhabitants of the land whither thou goest.

But ye shall destroy their altars, break their images, and cut down their groves.

For thou shalt worship no other god: for the LORD, whose name is Jealous, is a jealous God.

And he was there with the LORD forty days and forty nights; he did neither eat bread, nor drink water.

And he wrote upon the tables the words of the covenant, the ten commandments.

**Narrator**

God renews the covenant and repeats warnings Israel desperately needs.

Do not make covenant with the inhabitants of the land.

Do not adopt their worship.

Break down their altars.

Do not worship another god.

This matters especially after the golden calf.

Israel has already shown how quickly worship can be corrupted.

They do not need vague spirituality.

They need covenant loyalty.

God says His name is Jealous.

That can sound strange to modern ears because we often think of jealousy as petty insecurity.

But God's jealousy is covenant love that refuses to share His people with idols.

It is not insecurity.

It is holy love.

Like a faithful spouse who will not treat betrayal as harmless, God will not treat idolatry as no big deal.

He rescued Israel for Himself.

The commands about feasts, firstborn, Sabbath, and worship anchor Israel again in covenant rhythms.

Their calendar, work, offerings, and worship must all remind them who they belong to.

Moses stays with the LORD forty days and forty nights.

The covenant words are written again.

The story is moving forward, but only by mercy.

## Exodus 34:29-35

**Scripture**

And it came to pass, when Moses came down from mount Sinai with the two tables of testimony in Moses' hand, that Moses wist not that the skin of his face shone while he talked with him.

And when Aaron and all the children of Israel saw Moses, behold, the skin of his face shone; and they were afraid to come nigh him.

And Moses called unto them; and Aaron and all the rulers of the congregation returned unto him.

And till Moses had done speaking with them, he put a vail on his face.

**Narrator**

When Moses comes down from the mountain, his face shines.

He does not even realize it at first.

He has been speaking with the LORD, and the glory has left a visible mark.

The people are afraid to come near him.

That fear makes sense.

The glory reflected on Moses reminds them that he has been in the presence of the holy God.

Moses calls them near and speaks God's commands to them.

Then he covers his face with a veil.

This scene will become important later in Scripture.

Paul will reflect on Moses' veil in 2 Corinthians when talking about old covenant glory and the greater glory revealed in Christ.

But even here in Exodus, the point is powerful:

Time with God changes a person.

Moses is not glowing because of self-improvement.

He is reflecting what he has encountered.

The glory is not from Moses.

It is from the LORD.

## Exodus 35:1-19

**Scripture**

And Moses gathered all the congregation of the children of Israel together, and said unto them,

These are the words which the LORD hath commanded, that ye should do them.

Six days shall work be done, but on the seventh day there shall be to you an holy day, a sabbath of rest to the LORD.

Take ye from among you an offering unto the LORD: whosoever is of a willing heart, let him bring it, an offering of the LORD.

And every wise hearted among you shall come, and make all that the LORD hath commanded.

**Narrator**

Now the story turns toward the tabernacle work.

But before Moses calls for materials and craftsmen, he repeats the Sabbath command.

That order matters.

Even holy work must be done God's way.

Building the tabernacle matters.

The work is sacred.

The materials are important.

The craftsmanship matters.

But Israel must not become so busy building for God that they ignore God's command to rest.

That is a lesson people still need.

Good work can become disordered if it stops listening to God.

Then Moses calls for a willing offering.

Gold, silver, bronze, fabric, skins, wood, oil, spices, stones, and skilled labor.

This should sound familiar from the tabernacle instructions earlier.

But now the difference is response.

Will the people bring what is needed?

And the phrase is important:

Whosoever is of a willing heart.

This is not Pharaoh-style forced labor.

This is redeemed generosity.

The people are invited to give for God's dwelling.

## Exodus 35:20-29

**Scripture**

And they came, every one whose heart stirred him up, and every one whom his spirit made willing.

And they brought the LORD's offering to the work of the tabernacle of the congregation.

And they came, both men and women, as many as were willing hearted.

And all the women that were wise hearted did spin with their hands.

The children of Israel brought a willing offering unto the LORD, every man and woman, whose heart made them willing.

**Narrator**

This is one of the most beautiful reversals after the golden calf.

In Exodus 32, the people gave gold for an idol.

Now, in Exodus 35, the people bring gifts for God's sanctuary.

The repeated language is beautiful:

Willing heart.

Stirred spirit.

Willing hearted.

Men and women bring what they have.

Jewelry.

Yarn.

Linen.

Skins.

Silver.

Bronze.

Wood.

Stones.

Spices.

Oil.

Skilled women spin fabric.

Craftsmen prepare for the work.

The whole community begins turning toward obedience.

This does not mean the golden calf never happened.

It does mean mercy is producing renewed obedience.

That is important.

God's grace does not make sin meaningless.

But it can turn the same hands that built false worship toward true worship.

The people who failed are not beyond being stirred again.

The LORD's mercy is rebuilding what idolatry damaged.

## Exodus 35:30-36:7

**Scripture**

And Moses said unto the children of Israel, See, the LORD hath called by name Bezaleel.

And he hath filled him with the spirit of God, in wisdom, in understanding, and in knowledge, and in all manner of workmanship.

And he hath put in his heart that he may teach, both he, and Aholiab.

And they received of Moses all the offering, which the children of Israel had brought for the work.

And they brought yet unto him free offerings every morning.

And they spake unto Moses, saying, The people bring much more than enough for the service of the work.

**Narrator**

Moses names Bezalel and Oholiab as leaders for the work.

They are filled with the Spirit of God for craftsmanship.

That is worth noticing.

Sometimes people think the Spirit's work is only about speaking, preaching, or visibly religious moments.

But here, the Spirit gives wisdom, understanding, knowledge, design, craftsmanship, and the ability to teach others.

Artistic skill matters.

Practical skill matters.

Teaching skill matters.

Beauty in worship matters.

God fills people for the work He calls them to do.

Then the people keep bringing offerings.

Morning after morning.

So much comes in that the craftsmen tell Moses:

The people bring much more than enough.

That phrase is beautiful.

More than enough.

The community that had just broken covenant is now overflowing in willing obedience.

Moses has to command the people to stop bringing gifts.

That is almost funny in the best way.

The problem is not shortage.

The problem is abundance.

Mercy has stirred generosity.

## Exodus 36:8-38

**Scripture**

And every wise hearted man among them that wrought the work of the tabernacle made ten curtains of fine twined linen, and blue, and purple, and scarlet.

And he made curtains of goats' hair for the tent over the tabernacle.

And he made boards for the tabernacle of shittim wood.

And he made a vail of blue, and purple, and scarlet, and fine twined linen.

And he made an hanging for the tabernacle door of blue, and purple, and scarlet, and fine twined linen.

**Narrator**

Exodus 36 describes the tabernacle beginning to take shape.

Curtains.

Cherubim.

Coverings.

Boards.

Bars.

Veil.

Entrance.

Some of this may sound repetitive because earlier chapters gave the instructions.

But the repetition has a purpose.

Earlier, God commanded.

Now Israel obeys.

That matters deeply after the golden calf.

At the calf, Israel invented worship.

They shaped gold according to their own desire.

They called it by God's name.

They acted as if worship could be made up by human imagination.

But now, the tabernacle is being built according to God's word.

This is obedience replacing invention.

The wise-hearted workers make what God commanded.

The materials are shaped into holy space.

The veil is made.

The entrance is made.

The dwelling place of God is becoming visible among the people.

The story is not fully repaired yet.

The tabernacle is not finished until later.

But the direction has changed.

Mercy has led to renewed obedience.

The people are no longer giving gold to an idol.

They are building according to the pattern God gave.

## Closing

Day 30 shows us that the promised land is not enough without the presence of God.

That is the heart of this reading.

Moses understands that success without God would be empty.

Blessing without God would be hollow.

Arriving without God would not be true victory.

So he pleads:

If Your presence does not go with us, do not bring us up from here.

And God answers with mercy.

He reveals His name.

Merciful.

Gracious.

Longsuffering.

Abundant in goodness and truth.

Forgiving.

And just.

That revelation becomes one of the great foundations for knowing God's character in the rest of Scripture.

Then the covenant is renewed.

Moses' face shines.

The Sabbath is repeated.

The people bring willing offerings.

Bezalel and Oholiab are filled with wisdom for the work.

And the tabernacle begins to take shape.

This is what mercy can do.

Mercy does not pretend the golden calf never happened.

Mercy does not say sin does not matter.

But mercy makes a way forward.

Mercy restores worship.

Mercy stirs willing hearts.

Mercy turns gifts once used wrongly into gifts offered rightly.

For us, Day 30 asks a searching question:

Do we want God's gifts more than God Himself?

Would we be satisfied with the land if His presence did not go with us?

Would success be enough?

Would comfort be enough?

Would answered prayers be enough?

Moses teaches us to want God Himself.

And Exodus 34 teaches us why we can come back after failure.

Because the LORD is merciful and gracious.

Slow to anger.

Abundant in goodness and truth.

The story moves forward because of who God is.

And that is good news for every person who knows what it is like to fail, grieve, repent, and need mercy to begin again.
