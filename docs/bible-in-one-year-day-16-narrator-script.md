# Bible In One Year - Day 16 Narrator Script

## Day 16 - Faithful In The Hidden Place

**Reading:** Genesis 39 and 40  
**Bible text:** King James Version, Genesis 39-40  
**Audio style:** Narrator teaching with Scripture reading  

## Opening

Welcome to Day 16 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are reading Genesis 39 and 40.

Day 15 began Joseph's testing at home. Joseph was hated by his brothers, thrown into a pit, sold toward Egypt, and separated from his father.

Day 16 shows us what happens after Joseph arrives in Egypt.

This is not the part of the story where everything suddenly gets easy.

Joseph is taken into Potiphar's house. He serves faithfully. He is trusted with responsibility. Then he is tempted, falsely accused, and sent to prison.

And then, even in prison, Joseph keeps serving.

The key phrase in this reading is simple:

The Lord was with Joseph.

That does not mean Joseph's life is easy.

It means God's presence is not limited to comfortable places.

## Genesis 39:1-6

**Scripture**

1. And Joseph was brought down to Egypt; and Potiphar, an officer of Pharaoh, captain of the guard, an Egyptian, bought him of the hands of the Ishmeelites, which had brought him down thither.

2. And the LORD was with Joseph, and he was a prosperous man; and he was in the house of his master the Egyptian.

3. And his master saw that the LORD was with him, and that the LORD made all that he did to prosper in his hand.

4. And Joseph found grace in his sight, and he served him: and he made him overseer over his house, and all that he had he put into his hand.

5. And it came to pass from the time that he had made him overseer in his house, and over all that he had, that the LORD blessed the Egyptian's house for Joseph's sake; and the blessing of the LORD was upon all that he had in the house, and in the field.

6. And he left all that he had in Joseph's hand; and he knew not ought he had, save the bread which he did eat. And Joseph was a goodly person, and well favoured.

**Narrator**

Genesis 39 begins with Joseph in Egypt.

That matters because Joseph did not choose this road.

He did not move to Egypt for opportunity. He was sold there.

He has lost his robe, his family, his freedom, and any clear view of how his dreams could ever come true.

But verse 2 says something powerful:

The Lord was with Joseph.

That is the heartbeat of Genesis 39.

Joseph's circumstances look like abandonment, but the text says the opposite.

God is with him.

Potiphar is not a random man either.

He is an officer of Pharaoh and captain of the guard.

So Joseph is a slave, but he has been placed inside a household connected to Egyptian power.

Joseph cannot see it yet, but God is quietly positioning him near the world he will one day help lead.

And notice what Potiphar sees.

He sees that the Lord is with Joseph.

Potiphar may not understand Israel's God, but he can see the result of God's favor.

Joseph's work prospers.

His responsibility grows.

The house is blessed because Joseph is there.

That is an important Bible pattern.

Joseph becomes faithful over Potiphar's house before he becomes faithful over Egypt.

He learns stewardship in a hidden place before he is ever given public authority.

## Genesis 39:7-10

**Scripture**

7. And it came to pass after these things, that his master's wife cast her eyes upon Joseph; and she said, Lie with me.

8. But he refused, and said unto his master's wife, Behold, my master wotteth not what is with me in the house, and he hath committed all that he hath to my hand;

9. There is none greater in this house than I; neither hath he kept back any thing from me but thee, because thou art his wife: how then can I do this great wickedness, and sin against God?

10. And it came to pass, as she spake to Joseph day by day, that he hearkened not unto her, to lie by her, or to be with her.

**Narrator**

Now the test changes.

Joseph has already been tested by betrayal.

Now he is tested by temptation.

Potiphar's wife casts her eyes upon Joseph.

That phrase is important because Genesis often uses seeing language when desire starts moving in a dangerous direction.

Eve saw the fruit.

Judah saw Tamar.

Now Potiphar's wife sees Joseph.

And her command is direct.

Lie with me.

This is not subtle.

But it is also not simple, because she has power in the house.

Joseph is enslaved. She is the master's wife.

Joseph is vulnerable, even though he has responsibility.

His answer is one of the clearest moral moments in Genesis.

He says Potiphar has trusted him with everything.

To do this would betray that trust.

But Joseph does not stop there.

He says, How then can I do this great wickedness, and sin against God?

That is the deepest reason.

Joseph is far from home.

His father will not know.

His brothers will not know.

Nobody from Canaan is watching him.

But Joseph knows God is watching.

His holiness is not based on who can see him.

His holiness is based on the God he belongs to.

And verse 10 says she spoke to him day by day.

That matters.

This temptation is not one moment.

It is repeated pressure.

Many people do not fall because temptation spoke once.

They fall because it kept speaking.

Joseph's wisdom is not only that he refuses to lie with her.

He refuses to be with her.

He does not treat temptation like a conversation he can safely manage forever.

## Genesis 39:11-18

**Scripture**

11. And it came to pass about this time, that Joseph went into the house to do his business; and there was none of the men of the house there within.

12. And she caught him by his garment, saying, Lie with me: and he left his garment in her hand, and fled, and got him out.

13. And it came to pass, when she saw that he had left his garment in her hand, and was fled forth,

14. That she called unto the men of her house, and spake unto them, saying, See, he hath brought in an Hebrew unto us to mock us; he came in unto me to lie with me, and I cried with a loud voice:

15. And it came to pass, when he heard that I lifted up my voice and cried, that he left his garment with me, and fled, and got him out.

16. And she laid up his garment by her, until his lord came home.

17. And she spake unto him according to these words, saying, The Hebrew servant, which thou hast brought unto us, came in unto me to mock me:

18. And it came to pass, as I lifted up my voice and cried, that he left his garment with me, and fled out.

**Narrator**

This scene is painful because Joseph does the right thing and still gets punished.

He goes into the house to do his work.

The house is empty.

Potiphar's wife grabs his garment.

Joseph runs.

That is wisdom.

Sometimes the most spiritual thing you can do is leave the room.

But the garment becomes evidence against him.

And this is not the first time a garment has been used in Joseph's story.

In Genesis 37, Joseph's brothers took his robe, dipped it in blood, and used it to deceive Jacob.

In Genesis 39, Potiphar's wife takes Joseph's garment and uses it to deceive the household.

That is an aha moment.

Joseph keeps losing garments, and other people keep using those garments to tell false stories about him.

The robe was used to make Jacob think Joseph was dead.

This garment is used to make Potiphar think Joseph is guilty.

Potiphar's wife also calls Joseph a Hebrew.

She uses his outsider identity against him.

She makes him sound like a threat.

Then she shifts blame toward Potiphar by saying, The Hebrew servant, which thou hast brought unto us.

She is not only accusing Joseph.

She is pressuring Potiphar emotionally.

Joseph may be innocent, but he has no power to control the story being told about him.

## Genesis 39:19-23

**Scripture**

19. And it came to pass, when his master heard the words of his wife, which she spake unto him, saying, After this manner did thy servant to me; that his wrath was kindled.

20. And Joseph's master took him, and put him into the prison, a place where the king's prisoners were bound: and he was there in the prison.

21. But the LORD was with Joseph, and shewed him mercy, and gave him favour in the sight of the keeper of the prison.

22. And the keeper of the prison committed to Joseph's hand all the prisoners that were in the prison; and whatsoever they did there, he was the doer of it.

23. The keeper of the prison looked not to any thing that was under his hand; because the LORD was with him, and that which he did, the LORD made it to prosper.

**Narrator**

Joseph is thrown into prison.

And Genesis does not rush to explain everything.

It does not give Joseph a public defense.

It does not say Potiphar apologized.

It does not immediately vindicate Joseph.

It simply says he was there in the prison.

That is hard.

Joseph did the right thing, and the result got worse.

Genesis is honest about that.

Faithfulness does not always bring immediate rescue.

But verse 21 repeats the line we need:

The Lord was with Joseph.

God was with him in Potiphar's house.

God is with him in prison.

The location changes, but God's faithfulness does not.

And the pattern repeats.

In Potiphar's house, Joseph is trusted with responsibility.

In prison, Joseph is trusted with responsibility.

In Potiphar's house, the work prospers.

In prison, the work prospers.

This is not because prison is good.

Prison is unjust here.

The point is that injustice does not cancel God's presence.

Genesis 39 ends with Joseph still not free.

But it does not end hopeless.

The Lord is with him.

And that means the story is still moving.

## Genesis 40:1-8

**Scripture**

1. And it came to pass after these things, that the butler of the king of Egypt and his baker had offended their lord the king of Egypt.

2. And Pharaoh was wroth against two of his officers, against the chief of the butlers, and against the chief of the bakers.

3. And he put them in ward in the house of the captain of the guard, into the prison, the place where Joseph was bound.

4. And the captain of the guard charged Joseph with them, and he served them: and they continued a season in ward.

5. And they dreamed a dream both of them, each man his dream in one night, each man according to the interpretation of his dream, the butler and the baker of the king of Egypt, which were bound in the prison.

6. And Joseph came in unto them in the morning, and looked upon them, and, behold, they were sad.

7. And he asked Pharaoh's officers that were with him in the ward of his lord's house, saying, Wherefore look ye so sadly to day?

8. And they said unto him, We have dreamed a dream, and there is no interpreter of it. And Joseph said unto them, Do not interpretations belong to God? tell me them, I pray you.

**Narrator**

Genesis 40 begins after these things.

That means Joseph is still waiting.

He has been betrayed by his brothers.

He has been sold into Egypt.

He has been falsely accused.

He has been put into prison.

And now two of Pharaoh's officials arrive.

The butler, or cupbearer, handled Pharaoh's drink.

The baker handled Pharaoh's food.

These were trusted positions because access to a king's food and drink could involve loyalty, danger, and even the threat of poisoning.

So their arrival is not random.

Joseph is in prison, but God is placing him near Pharaoh's inner circle before Joseph ever enters Pharaoh's palace.

And then Joseph does something beautiful.

He notices their faces.

He sees that they are sad.

That detail matters because Joseph has plenty of pain of his own.

But prison has not made him blind to other people.

He still serves.

He still notices.

He still asks questions.

And when they say there is no interpreter, Joseph points interpretation back to God.

He does not say, I am amazing at dreams.

He says, Do not interpretations belong to God?

Joseph has a gift, but he knows the gift is not disconnected from God.

## Genesis 40:9-15

**Scripture**

9. And the chief butler told his dream to Joseph, and said to him, In my dream, behold, a vine was before me;

10. And in the vine were three branches: and it was as though it budded, and her blossoms shot forth; and the clusters thereof brought forth ripe grapes:

11. And Pharaoh's cup was in my hand: and I took the grapes, and pressed them into Pharaoh's cup, and I gave the cup into Pharaoh's hand.

12. And Joseph said unto him, This is the interpretation of it: The three branches are three days:

13. Yet within three days shall Pharaoh lift up thine head, and restore thee unto thy place: and thou shalt deliver Pharaoh's cup into his hand, after the former manner when thou wast his butler.

14. But think on me when it shall be well with thee, and shew kindness, I pray thee, unto me, and make mention of me unto Pharaoh, and bring me out of this house:

15. For indeed I was stolen away out of the land of the Hebrews: and here also have I done nothing that they should put me into the dungeon.

**Narrator**

The cupbearer's dream fits his work.

There is a vine.

There are grapes.

There is Pharaoh's cup.

And Joseph says the three branches mean three days.

In three days, Pharaoh will lift up the cupbearer's head and restore him.

That phrase, lift up your head, is important.

It can mean being raised into favor.

It can also mean being brought into judgment.

For the cupbearer, it means restoration.

Then Joseph asks to be remembered.

That request is very human.

Joseph trusts God, but he also wants justice.

He says, I was stolen away.

That is the first time Joseph names what happened to him that directly.

He was not simply relocated.

He was stolen.

He also says he has done nothing to deserve the dungeon.

Joseph is faithful, but he is not pretending the wrongs against him are small.

That is a helpful picture of faith.

Faith does not require you to call injustice okay.

Joseph can trust God with the dream and still ask the cupbearer to remember him.

## Genesis 40:16-19

**Scripture**

16. When the chief baker saw that the interpretation was good, he said unto Joseph, I also was in my dream, and, behold, I had three white baskets on my head:

17. And in the uppermost basket there was of all manner of bakemeats for Pharaoh; and the birds did eat them out of the basket upon my head.

18. And Joseph answered and said, This is the interpretation thereof: The three baskets are three days:

19. Yet within three days shall Pharaoh lift up thy head from off thee, and shall hang thee on a tree; and the birds shall eat thy flesh from off thee.

**Narrator**

The baker speaks after hearing the cupbearer's good news.

He hopes his dream will also mean restoration.

His dream also fits his work.

There are baskets.

There is baked food for Pharaoh.

But there is a troubling detail.

The birds are eating the food out of the basket.

Joseph gives the interpretation plainly.

The three baskets are three days.

But this time, lift up your head means judgment, not restoration.

The baker will be executed.

This is a hard moment because Joseph must tell the truth even when the truth is painful.

That shows integrity.

Joseph does not use God's gift only when the message is encouraging.

He also speaks truth when the message is heavy.

A gift from God has to be used faithfully, not just pleasantly.

## Genesis 40:20-23

**Scripture**

20. And it came to pass the third day, which was Pharaoh's birthday, that he made a feast unto all his servants: and he lifted up the head of the chief butler and of the chief baker among his servants.

21. And he restored the chief butler unto his butlership again; and he gave the cup into Pharaoh's hand:

22. But he hanged the chief baker: as Joseph had interpreted to them.

23. Yet did not the chief butler remember Joseph, but forgat him.

**Narrator**

On the third day, both interpretations come true.

The cupbearer is restored.

The baker is judged.

That proves Joseph's interpretation was from God.

And if we were writing the story, this is probably where we would free Joseph.

The cupbearer would remember him.

Pharaoh would hear about him.

Joseph would leave prison immediately.

But Genesis does not end the chapter that way.

It ends with one painful sentence:

The cupbearer did not remember Joseph, but forgot him.

That is heavy.

Joseph serves faithfully.

He notices another man's pain.

He gives a true interpretation.

He asks to be remembered.

And he is forgotten.

But here is the difference between what people do and what God does.

The cupbearer forgets Joseph.

God does not.

The delay is real, but delay is not abandonment.

Joseph is still in prison, but the connection has been made.

The door is not open yet, but God has already placed a future witness inside Pharaoh's court.

## Closing

Day 16 ends with Joseph still waiting.

That is important.

Genesis does not rush Joseph from the pit to the palace.

It takes us through the hidden places.

Potiphar's house.

Repeated temptation.

False accusation.

Prison.

Serving other people while still hurting.

Being forgotten after helping someone else.

And through all of it, the anchor remains:

The Lord was with Joseph.

That does not make the prison good.

It does not make the accusation fair.

It does not make the waiting easy.

But it means Joseph is not alone.

And it means the hidden season is not wasted.

If you carry one thing from Genesis 39 and 40, carry this:

God is not only with people when the doors open.

He is also with them while they wait behind the door.

Day 17 will show what happens when Pharaoh dreams and the forgotten Joseph is finally remembered.

But Day 16 teaches us to trust God before the breakthrough comes.

## Reusable Day Script Framework

- Open with where the story left off.
- Read the passage in natural sections.
- Teach the scene in simple language.
- Explain phrases that unlock the meaning.
- Connect the passage to real life without turning it into generic motivation.
- End with the main truth the listener should carry into the next day.
