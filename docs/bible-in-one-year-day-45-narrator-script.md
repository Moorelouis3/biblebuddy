# Bible in One Year - Day 45 Narrator Script

**Reading:** Numbers 26-29

## Opening

Welcome to Day 45 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are reading Numbers chapters 26 through 29.

The theme is a new generation counted.

The first wilderness generation has fallen.

But God's promise has not failed.

Now the LORD counts a new generation, protects inheritance, appoints Joshua, and orders Israel's worship before they enter the land.

## Numbers 26 verses 1 through 65

**Scripture**

After the plague, the LORD tells Moses and Eleazar to count the congregation.

The children of Israel are counted by tribes and families.

The count is connected to the inheritance of the land.

The chapter also counts the Levites.

It closes by saying that none of the first counted generation remains except Caleb and Joshua.

**Narrator**

This census is not just a list.

It is proof that God preserved a people.

The unbelieving generation died in the wilderness, just as the LORD warned.

But Israel did not disappear.

The promise continues through their children.

The count prepares for inheritance.

??? Tribes preserved

?? Land prepared

God is moving from wilderness judgment toward promised inheritance.

The phrase after the plague matters because the census comes after judgment.

Numbers has shown a generation falling in the wilderness.

Now the LORD counts the people again.

This is not only administration.

It is evidence that God has preserved Israel.

The first census counted the generation that came out of Egypt.

This census counts the generation that will stand near the land.

The names and families can feel slow, but they are telling the reader something important.

Israel did not vanish in the wilderness.

The promise survived judgment.

The tribes are still there.

The families are still there.

The inheritance is still ahead.

The land will be divided according to the count.

That means the census is connected to future possession.

God is not only counting bodies.

He is preparing inheritance.

The ending of the chapter is sobering.

None of the men from the first numbered generation remain except Caleb and Joshua.

That proves God's warning was true.

But the presence of a new generation proves God's promise is also true.

Judgment did not cancel covenant.

Death in the wilderness did not defeat the LORD's purpose.

For beginners, this chapter teaches that lists in the Bible can carry deep meaning.

Every name says, "God has kept a people."

Every tribe says, "The promise continues."

Every inheritance detail says, "The land is still before them."

## Numbers 27 verses 1 through 11

**Scripture**

The daughters of Zelophehad come before Moses, Eleazar, the princes, and the congregation.

Their father died in the wilderness and had no sons.

They ask why his name should be removed from his family.

Moses brings their cause before the LORD.

The LORD says they speak right and gives a law for inheritance.

**Narrator**

This moment matters because the daughters bring a real family problem into the presence of God.

Their concern is not selfish ambition.

They want their father's name and inheritance preserved.

Moses does not guess.

He brings the case before the LORD.

God's answer protects inheritance and gives Israel a just pattern for the future.

This shows that God's law is not blind to real people.

It speaks into actual family life.

The daughters of Zelophehad are important because they bring a question the existing pattern has not clearly answered.

Their father has died.

He has no sons.

They do not want his name to disappear from his family.

In the land inheritance system, family name and land are connected.

So their request is about more than property.

It is about belonging, memory, and justice within the covenant community.

Moses brings their cause before the LORD.

That phrase is beautiful.

Moses does not dismiss them.

He does not invent a careless answer.

He brings the case to God.

The LORD says, The daughters of Zelophehad speak right.

That is a powerful affirmation.

God hears the concern of these women and gives a just ruling that shapes Israel's inheritance law.

This shows that God's law is not flat or careless.

It can address real family situations with wisdom.

It also reminds the reader that women are not invisible in the story.

Their faithful appeal becomes part of Israel's legal memory.

The passage teaches that justice protects inheritance, names, and families.

It also teaches that difficult questions should be brought before the LORD rather than answered with pride or impatience.

## Numbers 27 verses 12 through 23

**Scripture**

The LORD tells Moses to go up and see the land.

Moses will not enter because he rebelled at the waters of Meribah.

Moses asks God to set a man over the congregation so they will not be like sheep without a shepherd.

The LORD appoints Joshua.

Moses lays his hands on him before Eleazar and the congregation.

**Narrator**

Moses cannot enter the land.

That is painful.

But Moses' heart is still pastoral.

He asks God to care for the people after him.

The phrase sheep without a shepherd shows their need for guidance, protection, and leadership.

Joshua is appointed publicly.

The mission does not die with Moses.

God provides the next leader for the next stage.

Moses seeing the land but not entering it is one of the most emotional moments in Numbers.

The LORD lets him see what he has been leading toward.

But Moses will not cross over because of Meribah.

That consequence still stands.

Even great leaders are accountable to the holiness of God.

What stands out is Moses' response.

He does not only think about himself.

He asks the LORD to appoint someone over the congregation.

The phrase sheep without a shepherd shows deep pastoral concern.

Sheep need guidance.

They need protection.

They need someone to lead them to pasture and keep them from scattering.

Moses knows Israel cannot enter the next stage leaderless.

The LORD appoints Joshua, a man in whom is the spirit.

Joshua has already appeared as Moses' servant and as one of the faithful spies.

Now he is publicly commissioned.

Moses lays his hands on him before Eleazar and the congregation.

This public act matters.

The people need to see that Joshua's leadership is not self-appointed.

The mission continues by God's provision.

Moses will die.

Joshua will lead.

The promise is bigger than one servant's lifetime.

## Numbers 28 verses 1 through 31

**Scripture**

The LORD gives commands about offerings.

There are daily offerings, Sabbath offerings, monthly offerings, Passover offerings, Unleavened Bread offerings, and offerings for the day of Firstfruits.

The offerings are called a sweet savor unto the LORD.

**Narrator**

After counting and leadership, God turns to worship.

That order matters.

The new generation does not only need land.

They need life with God.

Daily offerings teach regular devotion.

Sabbath offerings teach weekly rhythm.

Feast offerings teach holy memory.

?? Daily worship

?? Holy time

?? Harvest gratitude

God is preparing Israel to enter the land as worshipers, not just settlers.

Numbers 28 may feel like a sudden return to offerings, but its placement is important.

The new generation has been counted.

Inheritance questions have been addressed.

Joshua has been appointed.

Now God orders worship.

That teaches the new generation what life in the land is for.

They are not entering Canaan merely to own farms and build houses.

They are entering as the LORD's covenant people.

Daily offerings teach that worship must be steady.

Morning and evening offerings place the day before God.

Sabbath offerings teach weekly rhythm.

Monthly offerings mark time as belonging to the LORD.

Passover and Unleavened Bread keep redemption in front of the people.

Firstfruits teaches gratitude for harvest.

The phrase sweet savour unto the LORD means the offering is pleasing when brought according to God's command.

This does not mean God needs food.

It means worship offered rightly is received by Him.

The chapter teaches that Israel's calendar must be shaped by memory, gratitude, atonement, and devotion.

The land will be a gift, but worship must remain central.

## Numbers 29 verses 1 through 40

**Scripture**

The LORD gives offerings for the seventh month.

The chapter includes the day of blowing trumpets, the Day of Atonement, and the feast of Tabernacles.

Many offerings are repeated day by day.

Moses tells the children of Israel all that the LORD commanded.

**Narrator**

The seventh month is full of worship.

Trumpets gather attention.

The Day of Atonement calls for humility.

Tabernacles teaches joy and remembrance.

The repetition can feel long, but it is teaching steady worship.

Israel's year is not supposed to be shaped only by farming, travel, and family plans.

It is shaped by the LORD.

The God who gives the land also orders the calendar.

Numbers 29 continues the worship calendar, especially the seventh month.

The seventh month is full of holy moments.

The blowing of trumpets calls attention and gathers the people.

The Day of Atonement calls Israel to humility and cleansing.

The feast of Tabernacles reminds them of life with God and His provision.

The repeated offerings can feel overwhelming, but repetition is part of the point.

Worship is not a random burst of emotion once in a while.

It is ordered.

It is repeated.

It is remembered.

It shapes the year.

The feast of Tabernacles especially teaches joy and dependence.

Israel will one day live in the land, but they must not forget the wilderness.

They must remember that the LORD sustained them when they lived in temporary shelters.

This keeps prosperity from creating forgetfulness.

The calendar teaches the people to remember rescue, seek atonement, celebrate provision, and keep time under God's rule.
