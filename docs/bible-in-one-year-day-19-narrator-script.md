# Bible In One Year - Day 19 Narrator Script

## Day 19 - Joseph Reveals Himself

**Reading:** Genesis 45 and 46  
**Bible text:** King James Version, Genesis 45-46  
**Audio style:** Narrator teaching with Scripture reading  

## Opening

Welcome to Day 19 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are reading Genesis 45 and 46.

Day 18 ended with Judah standing before Joseph and offering himself in Benjamin's place.

That was the moment Joseph had been waiting for.

Not because Joseph wanted to play games.

Not because he enjoyed watching his brothers suffer.

But because reconciliation could not be built on pretending.

Joseph needed to know whether the brothers who abandoned him would abandon Rachel's other son too.

And Judah answered that question.

He did not run.

He did not blame Benjamin.

He did not protect himself first.

He offered himself.

So now the room is ready for truth.

Picture the scene.

The brothers are standing inside the house of the Egyptian ruler.

They think Benjamin's life is hanging in the balance.

They think this powerful man can decide their future with one word.

They still do not know that the man in front of them is Joseph.

The brother they sold.

The brother they covered with a lie.

The brother whose cries they ignored.

And now Joseph can no longer hold the truth inside.

Genesis 45 is the release.

Genesis 46 is the movement that follows.

Truth comes out.

Tears break open.

Forgiveness becomes practical.

Jacob hears the impossible news.

And the covenant family begins the journey down into Egypt.

## Genesis 45:1-3

**Scripture**

Then Joseph could not refrain himself before all them that stood by him; and he cried, Cause every man to go out from me.

And there stood no man with him, while Joseph made himself known unto his brethren.

And he wept aloud: and the Egyptians and the house of Pharaoh heard.

And Joseph said unto his brethren, I am Joseph; doth my father yet live?

And his brethren could not answer him; for they were troubled at his presence.

**Narrator**

Genesis 45 begins with emotion Joseph can no longer restrain.

That phrase matters.

Joseph has been holding himself together for a long time.

He recognized his brothers back in Genesis 42, but they did not recognize him.

He heard them speak about their guilt.

He turned away and wept privately.

He saw Benjamin and had to leave the room again because his heart was overwhelmed.

But now Judah has spoken.

The brother who once suggested selling Joseph has now offered himself for Benjamin.

The old wound has been tested, and something has changed.

So Joseph clears the room.

No Egyptian servants.

No officials.

No public audience.

Some moments are too painful and too holy to happen in front of everyone.

This is family truth.

The governor of Egypt is about to stop speaking from behind the mask of power.

He is about to speak as a brother.

Then Joseph weeps aloud.

Not quietly.

Not politely.

The Egyptians hear it.

Pharaoh's house hears it.

Imagine that sound.

Years of grief.

Years of silence.

Years of survival.

Years of wondering about his father.

Years of remembering the pit, the sale, the false accusation, the prison, the waiting, and the dreams.

All of it comes out in tears.

Then Joseph says the words that change everything:

I am Joseph.

No long introduction.

No title.

No speech about his power.

Just identity.

And the brothers cannot answer him.

Of course they cannot.

The man they fear is the brother they betrayed.

The ruler they bowed before is the dreamer they sold.

The one with power to punish is the one they wronged.

Grace can feel terrifying at first when guilt is still louder than trust.

## Genesis 45:4-8

**Scripture**

And Joseph said unto his brethren, Come near to me, I pray you.

And they came near.

And he said, I am Joseph your brother, whom ye sold into Egypt.

Now therefore be not grieved, nor angry with yourselves, that ye sold me hither: for God did send me before you to preserve life.

For these two years hath the famine been in the land: and yet there are five years, in the which there shall neither be earing nor harvest.

And God sent me before you to preserve you a posterity in the earth, and to save your lives by a great deliverance.

So now it was not you that sent me hither, but God.

**Narrator**

Joseph does something tender here.

He says, Come near to me.

Fear pulls people backward.

Shame keeps distance.

But Joseph invites them closer.

Then he says it again, but this time with the wound named clearly:

I am Joseph your brother, whom ye sold into Egypt.

That sentence is important.

Joseph does not pretend.

He does not say, Do not worry, it was nothing.

He does not erase their responsibility.

He does not call evil good.

He says, You sold me.

That is the truth.

Forgiveness in Genesis 45 is not denial.

It is not pretending the pit was harmless.

It is not acting like slavery, accusation, prison, and years away from home were small things.

But Joseph also says something else.

God sent me before you to preserve life.

This is one of the deepest providence statements in Genesis.

Joseph holds two truths together.

You sold me.

God sent me.

Human beings acted wickedly.

God was still working.

Those two things are not the same.

Joseph is not saying the brothers were innocent.

He is saying their evil did not become ultimate.

God's purpose was higher than their sin.

And notice what that purpose was.

To preserve life.

The famine is not over.

There are still five years left.

Joseph's position in Egypt is not only about his personal success.

It is about saving the family of promise.

Abraham's line.

Isaac's line.

Jacob's household.

The covenant family through whom God said all nations would be blessed.

If Joseph had not been in Egypt, the family might not have survived the famine.

So Joseph can look back and say, God was doing more than I knew.

That does not make the betrayal okay.

But it does mean betrayal did not get the final word.

## Genesis 45:9-15

**Scripture**

Haste ye, and go up to my father, and say unto him, Thus saith thy son Joseph, God hath made me lord of all Egypt: come down unto me, tarry not.

And thou shalt dwell in the land of Goshen, and thou shalt be near unto me.

And there will I nourish thee; for yet there are five years of famine.

And he fell upon his brother Benjamin's neck, and wept; and Benjamin wept upon his neck.

Moreover he kissed all his brethren, and wept upon them: and after that his brethren talked with him.

**Narrator**

Joseph's forgiveness becomes practical.

That is easy to miss.

He does not only say kind words.

He makes a plan.

He says, Go get my father.

Come down to Egypt.

Dwell in Goshen.

Be near me.

I will nourish you.

This is mercy with food, land, protection, and a future attached to it.

Goshen matters because it becomes a place where Jacob's family can live near Joseph but remain distinct.

They are shepherds.

They have flocks and herds.

They are not Egyptians.

And later in the story, that distinction will matter.

God is preserving the family inside Egypt without making them disappear into Egypt.

Then Joseph embraces Benjamin.

Benjamin is his full brother, Rachel's other son.

The brother who became the center of the final test.

And they weep together.

Then Joseph kisses all his brothers and weeps over them.

Do not rush past that.

The brothers who once stripped him are now embraced by him.

The men who sold him now receive his tears.

And only after that do they talk with him.

At first they could not answer.

Now they can speak.

Mercy has begun to make conversation possible again.

That is how restoration often works.

Truth comes first.

Then fear has to quiet down.

Then mercy has to be received.

Then speech can return.

## Genesis 45:16-28

**Scripture**

And the fame thereof was heard in Pharaoh's house, saying, Joseph's brethren are come.

And it pleased Pharaoh well, and his servants.

And Pharaoh said unto Joseph, Say unto thy brethren, This do ye; lade your beasts, and go, get you unto the land of Canaan.

And take your father and your households, and come unto me: and I will give you the good of the land of Egypt.

And Joseph gave them wagons, according to the commandment of Pharaoh, and gave them provision for the way.

And they went up out of Egypt, and came into the land of Canaan unto Jacob their father,

And told him, saying, Joseph is yet alive, and he is governor over all the land of Egypt.

And Jacob's heart fainted, for he believed them not.

And when he saw the wagons which Joseph had sent to carry him, the spirit of Jacob their father revived.

And Israel said, It is enough; Joseph my son is yet alive: I will go and see him before I die.

**Narrator**

Now Pharaoh enters the story with favor.

That is surprising.

The family did not come to Egypt with power.

They came because famine made them desperate.

They came as outsiders.

They came with guilt.

And yet because of Joseph, Pharaoh welcomes them.

He sends wagons.

He promises the good of the land.

He provides for the journey.

The same Egypt that received Joseph as a slave is now sending vehicles to carry Jacob with honor.

That reversal is beautiful.

Joseph also gives gifts.

Clothing.

Silver.

Animals.

Bread.

Food for the road.

Remember, Joseph was stripped of his robe by these brothers.

Now Joseph sends garments with them.

Grace does not only refuse revenge.

Grace gives.

But Joseph also gives one warning:

See that ye fall not out by the way.

In plain language, do not quarrel on the road.

That warning is wise.

The brothers are carrying shocking news.

They are carrying old guilt.

They have to tell Jacob the truth somehow.

This could easily become blame, defensiveness, or fighting.

Joseph knows mercy has entered the story, but the family still needs wisdom.

Then they reach Jacob.

They tell him Joseph is alive.

And Jacob does not believe them.

Can you blame him?

For years, Jacob has lived with Joseph as dead.

He has replayed the bloodied robe.

He has carried grief in his body and memory.

So when the brothers say Joseph is alive and ruler over Egypt, it sounds impossible.

But then Jacob sees the wagons.

Visible proof.

Provision he can look at.

Evidence that the news is real.

And his spirit revives.

That word matters.

Grief had pressed him down for years.

Now hope begins breathing again.

Jacob says, It is enough.

Joseph my son is yet alive.

I will go and see him before I die.

## Genesis 46:1-7

**Scripture**

And Israel took his journey with all that he had, and came to Beersheba, and offered sacrifices unto the God of his father Isaac.

And God spake unto Israel in the visions of the night, and said, Jacob, Jacob.

And he said, Here am I.

And he said, I am God, the God of thy father: fear not to go down into Egypt; for I will there make of thee a great nation.

I will go down with thee into Egypt; and I will also surely bring thee up again: and Joseph shall put his hand upon thine eyes.

And Jacob rose up from Beersheba: and the sons of Israel carried Jacob their father, and their little ones, and their wives, in the wagons which Pharaoh had sent to carry him.

**Narrator**

Genesis 46 is a transition chapter.

And transition chapters are easy to underestimate.

The reveal has happened.

Joseph is alive.

Provision is waiting.

But now Jacob has to move.

And that is not small.

Imagine being Jacob.

Canaan is the land tied to promise.

Abraham lived there.

Isaac lived there.

Jacob returned there after years away.

The land is part of covenant memory.

And now, late in life, Jacob is leaving it for Egypt.

Yes, Joseph is there.

Yes, food is there.

Yes, wagons have come.

But Egypt is still foreign.

Powerful.

Unfamiliar.

Spiritually complicated.

So Jacob stops at Beersheba.

That matters.

Beersheba is not just a travel stop.

It is a place connected to Abraham and Isaac.

It carries family memory.

Before Jacob steps fully into the unfamiliar future, he worships the God of his father Isaac.

That is wisdom.

Before geography changes, his heart reorients.

Then God speaks in the visions of the night.

Jacob, Jacob.

The repeated name is personal.

Tender.

Weighty.

God meets Jacob right at the border of fear and obedience.

And He says, Fear not to go down into Egypt.

That tells us Jacob was afraid.

Even good news can come with fear.

Even provision can require a step that feels unsettling.

But God does not shame Jacob for needing reassurance.

He gives him a promise:

I will go down with thee into Egypt.

That is the heart of the chapter.

God is not sending Jacob away from His presence.

God is going with him.

And God adds that He will bring him up again.

The family is going down, but Egypt is not the final word.

God already holds the return.

## Genesis 46:8-27

**Scripture**

And these are the names of the children of Israel, which came into Egypt, Jacob and his sons.

All the souls of the house of Jacob, which came into Egypt, were threescore and ten.

**Narrator**

The next part of Genesis 46 is a list of names.

And for modern readers, lists can feel easy to skip.

But slow down.

This genealogy matters.

Genesis is showing us who enters Egypt.

This is not just a crowd.

Not just a migration statistic.

Not just background information.

These are real people.

Sons.

Daughters.

Grandchildren.

Households.

Stories.

Some names carry honor.

Some carry pain.

Some belong to people we know more about.

Some are only named briefly.

But all of them matter because the covenant promise is moving through real people.

The family enters Egypt as a household.

Later, in Exodus, they will leave as a nation.

That is why this list matters.

Genesis is quietly showing the seed form of Israel before Israel becomes a nation in the fuller sense.

The number seventy gives a sense of completeness.

The whole covenant household is going down.

And remember what God just said:

I will there make of thee a great nation.

So this genealogy is not filler.

It is the beginning of that promise taking shape in a new place.

## Genesis 46:28-34

**Scripture**

And he sent Judah before him unto Joseph, to direct his face unto Goshen.

And Joseph made ready his chariot, and went up to meet Israel his father, to Goshen.

And presented himself unto him; and he fell on his neck, and wept on his neck a good while.

And Israel said unto Joseph, Now let me die, since I have seen thy face, because thou art yet alive.

And Joseph said unto his brethren, and unto his father's house, I will go up, and shew Pharaoh, and say unto him, My brethren, and my father's house, which were in the land of Canaan, are come unto me.

And the men are shepherds.

For every shepherd is an abomination unto the Egyptians.

**Narrator**

Judah is sent ahead.

That detail is worth noticing.

Judah, the brother who once helped sell Joseph, is now guiding the family toward Joseph.

Genesis has been changing him in front of us.

He was exposed by Tamar.

He became surety for Benjamin.

He offered himself in Benjamin's place.

And now he leads the way to Goshen.

Repentance does not only feel sorry.

It begins to become useful.

Then Joseph makes ready his chariot.

Picture that.

Joseph is governor over Egypt.

He has power, servants, authority, responsibility.

But when Jacob arrives, Joseph is simply a son going to meet his father.

He falls on Jacob's neck and weeps a good while.

Scripture lets the moment breathe.

Years of grief meet one embrace.

The son Jacob thought was dead is alive.

The father Joseph has longed for is in his arms.

Jacob says, Now let me die, since I have seen thy face, because thou art yet alive.

That does not mean Jacob wants death in a dark way.

It means the grief that haunted him has finally been answered.

He has seen Joseph's face.

The lie is broken.

Then Joseph prepares the family for life in Egypt.

He tells them to explain that they are shepherds.

And Genesis adds that shepherds are an abomination to the Egyptians.

That cultural detail matters.

It explains why Goshen will be useful.

Jacob's family can live in Egypt, receive provision from Egypt, and still remain distinct from Egypt.

God is preserving the covenant family without letting them disappear into the surrounding culture.

## Closing

Day 19 brings together truth, tears, forgiveness, provision, transition, and presence.

Joseph reveals himself.

The brothers face the living person they betrayed.

Joseph names the wrong honestly.

But he also sees God's preserving hand.

He says, You sold me.

And he says, God sent me.

That is not denial.

That is maturity.

That is providence.

That is a man who has suffered deeply but refuses to let human evil become larger than God's purpose.

Then Jacob hears the news.

At first he cannot believe it.

But when he sees the wagons, his spirit revives.

And when he begins the journey, God meets him at Beersheba.

Fear not to go down into Egypt.

I will go down with thee.

That promise matters far beyond Jacob.

Because God's people will spend generations in Egypt.

Egypt will become a place of preservation, then later a place of oppression.

And one day God will bring them up again through the Exodus.

But here, in Genesis 46, the first word over the journey is not fear.

It is presence.

God goes with His people.

That is the comfort of this day.

God can work through what others meant for harm.

God can revive hearts that grief has nearly shut down.

God can lead His people into unfamiliar places without abandoning His promise.

And the greatest gift on the road is not the wagons.

Not even Goshen.

The greatest gift is the God who says:

I will go down with thee.
