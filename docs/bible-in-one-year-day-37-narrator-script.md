# Bible in One Year - Day 37 Narrator Script

**Reading:** Leviticus 21-24

## Opening

Welcome to Day 37 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are reading Leviticus chapters 21 through 24.

The theme is priests, feasts, and sacred order.

Leviticus has been teaching us that God is holy, and that His people are called to live near Him with reverence.

Today that holiness touches priests, offerings, the yearly feasts, the lamp in the sanctuary, the bread before the LORD, the holy name of God, and justice inside the camp.

This reading may feel strange in places.

It talks about priestly restrictions, blemishes, holy food, feast days, oil, bread, blasphemy, and judgment.

But the thread is not random.

The thread is this:

If the LORD dwells among His people, then worship matters.

Time matters.

Speech matters.

Justice matters.

The God who lives among Israel is holy, so the people must learn to treat His presence, His name, and His ways as holy.

## Leviticus 21 verses 1 through 6

**Scripture**

Leviticus 21 begins with the LORD speaking to Moses about the priests, the sons of Aaron.

The priests are told not to defile themselves for the dead among the people, except for close family.

They must not copy pagan mourning practices like making baldness on the head, shaving the corner of the beard, or cutting the flesh.

The reason is that they offer the offerings of the LORD and the bread of their God.

They must be holy.

**Narrator**

This section begins with death and mourning.

That may feel like an odd place to start.

But in Leviticus, death is connected to uncleanness because death is part of the broken world.

The priests serve near the altar.

They handle holy things.

So their contact with death is more restricted than ordinary Israelite life.

That does not mean priests are not allowed to grieve.

God gives close family exceptions.

A priest may become defiled for his mother, father, son, daughter, brother, or unmarried sister.

That matters because holiness does not erase human sorrow.

But even sorrow must not become false worship.

The shaving and cutting practices named here were connected to pagan mourning customs.

Israel's priests must not grieve like the nations who do not know the LORD.

The phrase "holy unto their God" is the key.

Holy means set apart.

The priests belong to the LORD in a public way.

Their lives are meant to teach Israel that God's presence is not casual.

## Leviticus 21 verses 7 through 9

**Scripture**

The priests are also given marriage restrictions.

They must not take a wife who is profane or put away from her husband.

The people must sanctify the priest because he offers the bread of God.

The LORD says, "I the LORD, which sanctify you, am holy."

The section also warns that the conduct of a priest's daughter can profane her father.

**Narrator**

This section moves from mourning to household life.

For the priests, family life and altar service are connected.

The phrase "the bread of thy God" points to the offerings brought near to the LORD.

The priest is not simply doing a job.

He represents holy service before the people.

So his marriage and household cannot publicly contradict the holiness of that service.

This is not saying ordinary marriages do not matter.

It is saying priestly nearness brings added responsibility.

The line "I the LORD am holy" explains the whole section.

The priest is not holy because he is impressive.

He is holy because the LORD sanctifies him.

Sanctify means to set apart.

The priest belongs to God's service, so his life must point beyond himself.

For a beginner, the big idea is simple:

The closer someone serves near holy things, the more seriously that service must be treated.

## Leviticus 21 verses 10 through 15

**Scripture**

The high priest receives stricter commands.

The anointing oil has been poured upon his head, and he is consecrated to wear the holy garments.

He must not uncover his head or tear his clothes in mourning.

He must not defile himself even for his father or mother.

He must not go out of the sanctuary in a way that profanes it.

He must marry a virgin from his own people, so that his seed is not profaned.

**Narrator**

The high priest carries the highest priestly responsibility in Israel.

That is why the commands become stricter.

The phrase "the anointing oil" is important.

Oil marked him as consecrated.

He was set apart for a special work before the LORD.

The holy garments also mattered.

They were not costumes.

They showed that the high priest's work was sacred.

Because of that, even his mourning boundaries were different.

This can sound severe to modern ears.

But Leviticus is teaching through visible signs.

The high priest represents access to the holy place.

His role points to life with God, not death.

So the symbols around him must not be confused.

The phrase "do not profane the sanctuary" means he must not treat God's dwelling place as common.

Profane means to make something holy seem ordinary or cheap.

The high priest's life must protect the meaning of the sanctuary.

## Leviticus 21 verses 16 through 24

**Scripture**

The LORD tells Moses that a descendant of Aaron with a blemish may not approach to offer the bread of God.

The passage names blindness, lameness, deformity, broken body parts, skin disease, and other conditions.

The man may still eat the bread of his God, both most holy and holy.

But he must not go in unto the veil or come near the altar, so that he does not profane the sanctuary.

Moses speaks these commands to Aaron, to his sons, and to all the children of Israel.

**Narrator**

This section is easy to misunderstand.

It is not saying a person with a physical blemish has no value.

The text says he may still eat the holy food.

He still belongs to the priestly family.

He is not thrown away.

The restriction is about altar service.

In Leviticus, the altar and sanctuary are full of symbols.

The animals offered must be without blemish.

The priest who represents that offering must also visibly point to wholeness.

That symbol was not about personal worth.

It was about the picture being taught at the altar.

For Christians reading later in the Bible, this also helps us understand why Jesus is presented as the perfect High Priest and the spotless sacrifice.

Leviticus is training us to see that coming near to God requires holiness, wholeness, and God's appointed way.

## Leviticus 22 verses 1 through 9

**Scripture**

Leviticus 22 continues with the priests and the holy things of the children of Israel.

The priests must not profane the holy name of the LORD.

If a priest is unclean, he must not eat of the holy things until he is clean.

The chapter mentions leprosy, bodily issues, contact with the dead, creeping things, and other sources of uncleanness.

The priest must wash and wait until evening.

Then he may eat the holy things.

**Narrator**

Holy food is food connected to worship.

It is not ordinary leftovers.

It belongs to the service of the LORD.

That is why the priests must handle it carefully.

The phrase "separate themselves from the holy things" means the priest must step back when he is unclean.

This is not only about private health.

It is about reverence before God.

The holy things offered by Israel belong to the LORD first.

If a priest eats them while unclean, he treats holy gifts as common food.

The washing and waiting show that uncleanness is serious, but not hopeless.

There is a way back.

The priest can be cleansed.

He can return to eating the holy things.

Leviticus keeps holding together two truths:

God is holy.

God provides a way for His people to return.

## Leviticus 22 verses 10 through 16

**Scripture**

This section explains who may eat the holy food.

No stranger may eat it.

A hired servant may not eat it.

Someone bought by the priest may eat it.

Those born in the priest's house may eat it.

A priest's daughter who marries a stranger may not eat it, but if she returns to her father's house as a widow or divorced woman with no child, she may eat of her father's bread.

The holy things must not be profaned.

**Narrator**

This section is about boundaries around holy food.

The phrase "no stranger shall eat" means the holy portions are not open to everyone.

They belong to the priestly household.

That may sound narrow, but the point is order.

Holy gifts have a holy use.

They are not common snacks for anyone nearby.

The details about servants and daughters show that Leviticus is thinking carefully about household belonging.

Who belongs to the priest's house?

Who is outside that household?

Who has returned to it?

The law is drawing lines because holy food represents holy service.

If the lines are ignored, the people begin to lose the difference between holy and common.

And that difference is one of the main lessons of Leviticus.

## Leviticus 22 verses 17 through 25

**Scripture**

The LORD tells Moses to speak to Aaron, his sons, and all the children of Israel.

When anyone brings an offering to the LORD for a vow or freewill offering, it must be a male without blemish.

A blind, broken, maimed, diseased, or damaged animal must not be offered.

Israel must not bring corrupt offerings to the LORD.

**Narrator**

The phrase "without blemish" means whole, sound, and fit for the altar.

Israel must not bring God what is careless, corrupt, or damaged.

The offering teaches the worshiper something.

God is holy.

God is worthy.

God should not receive what people would be ashamed to give anywhere else.

This connects with the priestly restrictions in the previous chapter.

Both the priest and the offering are part of a holy picture.

The altar is teaching Israel that coming near to God is costly and reverent.

For a beginner, do not read this as God needing animals.

Read it as God teaching His people not to treat worship casually.

The gift says something about how the giver sees God.

## Leviticus 22 verses 26 through 33

**Scripture**

This section gives additional offering rules.

A newborn animal must remain with its mother seven days before it may be offered.

An animal and its young must not be killed on the same day.

A thanksgiving sacrifice must be eaten the same day.

The people must keep the commandments and not profane the holy name of the LORD.

The LORD says He brought Israel out of Egypt to be their God.

**Narrator**

The end of Leviticus 22 connects offerings to God's rescue.

The phrase "brought you out of the land of Egypt" matters.

Israel does not obey to become rescued.

Israel obeys because they have been rescued.

God brought them out to be their God.

That means worship is covenant response.

The rules about animals may seem small, but they teach mercy, order, and reverence.

The thanksgiving sacrifice being eaten the same day teaches that worship has God's timing.

The phrase "not profane my holy name" pulls the whole chapter together.

God's name represents who He is.

The people must not make His name look common by careless worship.

They are holy because the LORD sanctifies them.

## Leviticus 23 verses 1 through 3

**Scripture**

Leviticus 23 begins the feasts of the LORD.

The LORD tells Moses to speak to the children of Israel about holy convocations.

The weekly Sabbath comes first.

Six days work may be done, but the seventh day is the Sabbath of rest, a holy convocation.

It is the Sabbath of the LORD in all their dwellings.

**Narrator**

Before the yearly feasts are named, Sabbath comes first.

That matters.

God starts with the weekly rhythm of rest and worship.

The phrase "holy convocation" means a sacred gathering.

Time itself is being set apart for the LORD.

The Sabbath teaches Israel that life is not only production.

They are not slaves in Egypt anymore.

They belong to the LORD.

So every week, their calendar preaches a sermon:

Rest because God rules.

Gather because God is worthy.

Stop because life is more than labor.

## Leviticus 23 verses 4 through 14

**Scripture**

The chapter moves to Passover, Unleavened Bread, and Firstfruits.

Passover is in the first month, on the fourteenth day at evening.

The feast of Unleavened Bread follows for seven days.

Then, when Israel enters the land and reaps the harvest, they must bring a sheaf of the firstfruits to the priest.

They must not eat bread, parched corn, or green ears until the offering is brought.

**Narrator**

Passover remembers rescue.

Unleavened Bread keeps the memory of deliverance alive.

Firstfruits teaches trust at the beginning of harvest.

That order matters.

Israel's worship calendar begins with grace.

God rescued them from Egypt before they ever harvested a field in Canaan.

Then, when the harvest comes, the first portion belongs to the LORD.

Firstfruits says:

Before I enjoy the fullness, I honor the Giver.

Before I act like this crop belongs only to me, I remember the LORD.

This is worship woven into time, food, land, and memory.

## Leviticus 23 verses 15 through 22

**Scripture**

Israel must count seven Sabbaths from the day after the Firstfruits offering.

Then they bring a new meat offering to the LORD.

This feast is connected to harvest and rejoicing.

The section also commands Israel not to reap the corners of the field or gather every gleaning.

The leftovers are for the poor and the stranger.

The LORD says, "I am the LORD your God."

**Narrator**

This section shows that worship and mercy belong together.

The feast of Weeks celebrates harvest.

But right in the middle of harvest celebration, God speaks about the poor and the stranger.

That is not an interruption.

It is part of holiness.

The phrase "leave them unto the poor, and to the stranger" teaches that God's people must not turn blessing into selfishness.

They may harvest, but they must not scrape the field bare.

They must leave room for vulnerable people to gather food with dignity.

This matters later in the Bible when Ruth gleans in the field of Boaz.

Leviticus is not only ritual.

It is forming a merciful community.

## Leviticus 23 verses 23 through 32

**Scripture**

The LORD gives the memorial of blowing trumpets.

It is a holy convocation.

Then the tenth day of the seventh month is the Day of Atonement.

The people must afflict their souls and offer an offering made by fire.

They must do no work on that day.

It is a Sabbath of rest.

**Narrator**

Trumpets gather attention.

The sound calls the people to stop and remember before God.

Then comes the Day of Atonement.

This is one of the most serious days in Israel's year.

The phrase "afflict your souls" means the people humble themselves before the LORD.

They do not treat sin lightly.

They do not rush past uncleanness.

They pause as a whole nation before God's holiness and mercy.

The Day of Atonement has already been explained in Leviticus 16.

Here, it is placed into the calendar.

That means atonement is not only a ritual somewhere in the tabernacle.

It becomes part of Israel's yearly memory.

Every year, the people are reminded:

Sin must be dealt with.

Mercy must come from God.

## Leviticus 23 verses 33 through 44

**Scripture**

The LORD gives the feast of Tabernacles.

For seven days, Israel rejoices before the LORD.

They take branches from goodly trees, palm branches, thick trees, and willows of the brook.

They dwell in booths for seven days.

The reason is so their generations may know that the LORD made the children of Israel dwell in booths when He brought them out of Egypt.

**Narrator**

Tabernacles is a feast of joy and memory.

The booth is important.

It is a temporary shelter.

Israel lives in booths to remember that they were once wilderness travelers.

They did not always have houses, fields, walls, and settled life.

They depended on the LORD.

So the feast teaches humility inside celebration.

Rejoice, but remember.

Enjoy the land, but remember the wilderness.

Live in blessing, but remember who carried you.

This is a beautiful pattern.

God does not want His people to forget their story once life becomes more comfortable.

Memory protects gratitude.

Gratitude protects worship.

## Leviticus 24 verses 1 through 4

**Scripture**

The LORD commands the children of Israel to bring pure olive oil beaten for the light.

The lamp must burn continually.

Aaron must order it from evening to morning before the LORD continually.

It is a statute forever in their generations.

**Narrator**

Leviticus now moves from the calendar back inside the sanctuary.

The phrase "pure oil olive beaten for the light" tells us this lamp is not casual.

The oil is prepared for holy use.

The light burns before the LORD.

The phrase "from evening unto the morning" gives a picture of continual care.

The lamp is tended regularly.

It is not ignored.

For Israel, this light is a sanctuary sign.

The holy place is not dark and abandoned.

There is ordered light before the LORD.

Worship requires attention.

Someone must bring the oil.

Someone must tend the lamp.

Holy things are cared for.

## Leviticus 24 verses 5 through 9

**Scripture**

The LORD commands twelve cakes of bread to be made from fine flour.

They are set in two rows on the pure table before the LORD.

Pure frankincense is placed on each row.

Every Sabbath, the bread is set in order before the LORD continually.

It is for Aaron and his sons, and they eat it in the holy place.

**Narrator**

The bread before the LORD is sometimes called the bread of the Presence.

There are twelve loaves.

That points to the twelve tribes of Israel.

The bread is set in order before God.

This is not random decoration.

It is a sign that the life of Israel is continually before the LORD.

The table, the bread, the rows, the frankincense, and the Sabbath rhythm all teach ordered worship.

The priests eat the bread in a holy place because it is most holy.

Again, Leviticus keeps teaching the same lesson:

Holy gifts must be handled in a holy way.

## Leviticus 24 verses 10 through 16

**Scripture**

A man whose mother is an Israelite and whose father is an Egyptian goes out among the children of Israel.

He fights with an Israelite man in the camp.

He blasphemes the name of the LORD and curses.

The people bring him to Moses and put him in ward until the mind of the LORD is shown.

The LORD commands judgment for blaspheming His name.

**Narrator**

This section moves from sanctuary order to camp disorder.

A fight breaks out.

Then the holy name is blasphemed.

To blaspheme means to speak against God with contempt.

The name of the LORD is not just a label.

God's name reveals who He is.

To curse His name is to attack His honor.

The people do not invent a punishment on the spot.

They wait until the mind of the LORD is shown.

That detail matters.

Even in a serious case, Israel must not act by impulse.

The community needs God's judgment, not mob anger.

Holiness shapes speech, and holiness also shapes how judgment is handled.

## Leviticus 24 verses 17 through 23

**Scripture**

The chapter gives laws about taking life, killing animals, and causing injury.

It says breach for breach, eye for eye, tooth for tooth.

The same law applies to the stranger and to one born in the land.

Moses speaks to the children of Israel, and they carry out the LORD's command.

**Narrator**

The phrase "eye for eye, tooth for tooth" can sound harsh if we hear it wrongly.

In context, it limits punishment.

It means justice must be measured.

The punishment must fit the injury.

It must not become revenge without boundaries.

The same law for the stranger and the Israelite also matters.

Justice is not supposed to change based on who has more power.

The foreigner and the native-born stand under the same legal standard.

So Leviticus 24 ends by showing that God's holiness reaches public justice.

The same God who cares about the lamp in the sanctuary also cares about speech, violence, injury, and fairness in the camp.

Worship and daily life cannot be separated.

## Closing

Before we close, hold the pieces of the day together.

Leviticus 21 is not only about priests having special rules.

It is about the people who come closest to the altar learning that holy service cannot be handled casually.

Leviticus 22 is not only about which food may be eaten.

It is about holy gifts belonging first to the LORD, even when they also become provision for the priests.

Leviticus 23 is not only a list of holidays.

It is the shape of Israel's year.

Week after week, season after season, the people are taught to remember creation, rescue from Egypt, harvest provision, atonement, humility, joy, and life under God's care.

Leviticus 24 is not only about lamps, bread, and a hard legal case.

It shows the sanctuary and the camp belonging to the same God.

The light before the LORD matters.

The bread before the LORD matters.

The name of the LORD matters.

The way people treat one another matters.

That is why this reading belongs together.

Holiness is not locked inside the tabernacle.

Holiness moves outward into families, calendars, speech, work, food, grief, celebration, and justice.

And that is why these chapters still ask us a serious question:

Do we treat God as holy only when we are in a religious moment?

Or do we let His holiness reorder the ordinary parts of life too?

That is Day 37.

Leviticus 21 through 24 shows priests, offerings, feasts, lamps, bread, speech, and justice under the holiness of God.

The big lesson is simple:

When the LORD lives among His people, nothing is ordinary in the same way anymore.

Priests must treat holy service with reverence.

Offerings must honor the One receiving them.

Time must be ordered around worship, memory, humility, mercy, and joy.

The lamp must keep burning.

The bread must remain before the LORD.

The holy name must not be treated as common.

Justice must be measured and equal.

Leviticus is not trying to make life complicated for no reason.

It is teaching Israel how to live near a holy God.

And for us as readers, it teaches us to slow down.

God's presence is not background noise.

His name is not empty speech.

His worship is not casual.

His justice is not random.

He is the LORD who sanctifies His people.

He is holy.

And He teaches His people to live every part of life before Him.
