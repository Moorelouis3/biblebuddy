# Study Notes Content Guidelines

The Study Notes system uses four sections:

- What Is Happening Here?
- Key Phrases
- Key Truths
- Why This Matters

The goal is to teach, not merely summarize. Each note should help a reader understand something they did not understand before opening it.

## Finalized Reader Framework

For Bible Reader study notes, use this frame by default:

1. What Is Happening Here?
2. Key Phrases
3. Key Truths
4. Why This Matters

Use this writing rhythm for the intro and outro style sections:

```text
Short intro paragraph.
2-3 sentences that set up the scene or life connection.

🌿 Emoji teaching point.
Clear sentence that teaches one idea.

📖 Emoji teaching point.
Clear sentence that teaches one idea.

🧠 Emoji teaching point.
Clear sentence that teaches one idea.

Short outro paragraph.
2-3 sentences that land the point and help the reader carry it forward.
```

This rhythm is now the preferred Bible chapter Study Notes style:

- short teaching intro
- a few emoji teaching lines
- short teaching outro
- conversational but not shallow
- simple enough for everyday readers
- strong enough to teach something new

## Writing Standard

Write like a wise Bible teacher having coffee with a friend:

- Clear language
- Simple explanations
- Everyday words
- Interesting observations
- Warm, conversational teaching

Avoid academic commentary, sermon language, and generic motivational lines.

## What Is Happening Here?

Explain the scene for someone reading the Bible for the first time.

Answer:

- What is happening?
- Why is it happening?
- What is the main point?
- What should the reader notice?

Do not only restate the verse. Explain the movement, setting, and meaning.

Use the finalized intro/list/outro rhythm here. Start with 2-3 sentences that explain the scene, add several emoji teaching lines, then close with 2-3 sentences that state the main point.

## Key Phrases

Each phrase should feel like a small Bible study.

Include:

- Meaning
- Context
- Significance
- Interesting facts
- Biblical connections

Do not treat Key Phrases like a short summary list.

Before writing Key Phrases, ask: "What would a brand-new Bible reader ask about in these verses?"

Choose phrases that explain:

- places and locations
- people and family names
- objects and materials
- actions and commands
- directions and movement
- symbols and repeated images
- unusual KJV wording
- cultural or historical details
- theological ideas that may not be obvious

Never skip a phrase simply because it seems obvious. If the phrase needs definition, cultural background, geography, Bible connection, or plain-English explanation, include it.

The goal is more needed phrases with meaningful explanations, not fewer phrases with broad summaries.

The reader should feel, "I never knew that."

Genesis 1 is the benchmark for this. The notes slow down over phrases like "In the beginning," "without form, and void," and "the Spirit of God moved" because those are the exact places a new reader would ask questions. Every future chapter should follow that same instinct: identify the important study phrases, not merely the main summary points.

Before finalizing, ask:

```text
If a brand-new Christian read this passage, what questions would they ask?
```

If those questions are not answered in Key Phrases or the surrounding notes, the notes are incomplete.

## Key Truths

These are not observations or summaries. They are major lessons the passage reveals.

Focus on what the passage teaches about:

- God
- Humanity
- Sin
- Faith
- Creation
- God's character

## Why This Matters

Answer, "So what?"

Connect the passage to real life without becoming generic. Show how the passage shapes the reader's understanding of God and life today.

Use the finalized intro/list/outro rhythm here too. Start with 2-3 sentences connecting the passage to life, add several emoji application lines, then close with 2-3 sentences that help the reader remember why the passage matters.

## Quality Test

Before saving any Study Notes, ask:

"Did this section teach the user something new?"

If the answer is no, rewrite it.
