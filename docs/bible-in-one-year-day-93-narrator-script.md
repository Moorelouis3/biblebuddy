# Day 93 Narrator Script

## Opening

Welcome to Day 93 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are reading Second Kings 22 through 25.

The title for today is Josiah's Reform and Judah's Fall.

This day begins with a tender-hearted king and ends with Jerusalem burned.

Josiah hears the book of the law and humbles himself.

He renews covenant, removes idols, restores Passover, and leads one of Judah's greatest reforms.

But the judgment built up through generations does not disappear.

After Josiah, Judah declines quickly.

Babylon rises.

Jerusalem falls.

The temple is burned.

The people are carried away.

Day 93 teaches both the beauty of repentance and the seriousness of long rebellion.

## Second Kings 22

Second Kings 22 introduces Josiah.

He is eight years old when he begins to reign.

He does right in the sight of the LORD and walks in the way of David.

During temple repair, Hilkiah the priest finds the book of the law.

When Shaphan reads it before the king, Josiah tears his clothes.

That response matters.

Josiah does not treat God's word as old religious material.

He hears it as living truth.

He knows Judah has not obeyed.

He sends to inquire of the LORD.

Huldah the prophetess gives the word: judgment will come because Judah has forsaken the LORD and burned incense to other gods.

But Josiah's heart was tender.

He humbled himself.

He tore his clothes and wept.

The LORD heard him.

Josiah will be gathered to his grave in peace and will not see all the evil brought on the place.

## Second Kings 23

Second Kings 23 shows Josiah acting on the word.

He gathers the elders, priests, prophets, and people.

He reads the words of the book of the covenant.

He stands by a pillar and makes a covenant before the LORD to walk after Him with all the heart and soul.

Then reform becomes action.

Josiah removes vessels made for Baal, the grove, and the host of heaven.

He puts down idolatrous priests.

He breaks down houses connected with corrupt worship.

He defiles Topheth so no one can make a son or daughter pass through the fire.

He removes horses dedicated to the sun.

He breaks down altars.

He goes even to Bethel and fulfills the old word spoken against Jeroboam's altar.

Then Josiah keeps a Passover unlike any kept in the days of the judges or kings.

The chapter says there was no king like him, who turned to the LORD with all his heart, soul, and might, according to the law of Moses.

Yet the LORD does not turn from the fierceness of His great wrath because of Manasseh's provocations.

Josiah's reform is real.

Judah's long guilt is also real.

After Josiah dies, Jehoahaz and Jehoiakim do evil.

The decline resumes.

## Second Kings 24

Second Kings 24 brings Babylon into the story.

Nebuchadnezzar comes up.

Jehoiakim becomes his servant and then rebels.

Bands of Chaldees, Syrians, Moabites, and Ammonites come against Judah according to the word of the LORD.

The chapter says this comes because of Manasseh's sins and the innocent blood he shed.

Jehoiachin reigns after Jehoiakim.

Nebuchadnezzar besieges Jerusalem.

Jehoiachin goes out to him, and Babylon takes the king, his mother, servants, princes, officers, treasures, and vessels.

Craftsmen and mighty men are carried away.

Only the poorest sort remain.

Mattaniah is made king and renamed Zedekiah.

He does evil and rebels against Babylon.

The chapter shows exile beginning before the final destruction.

Judah is being emptied piece by piece.

## Second Kings 25

Second Kings 25 is the fall of Jerusalem.

Nebuchadnezzar besieges the city until famine prevails.

The city is broken up.

Zedekiah flees, but he is caught.

His sons are killed before his eyes.

Then his eyes are put out, and he is carried to Babylon.

Nebuzaradan burns the house of the LORD, the king's house, and the houses of Jerusalem.

The walls are broken down.

Temple pillars, brass, vessels, and sacred objects are taken.

Priests and leaders are executed.

Judah is carried away out of the land.

Gedaliah is appointed over the remaining people, but he is murdered.

Fear drives the remnant toward Egypt.

The book ends with a small note of mercy.

Evil-merodach king of Babylon lifts Jehoiachin out of prison, speaks kindly to him, changes his prison garments, and gives him a continual allowance.

It is not full restoration.

But it is a lamp in the darkness.

The line of David is not erased.

## Full Lesson Review

Day 93 begins with rediscovered Scripture and ends with exile.

Josiah shows what tender-hearted repentance looks like.

He hears, humbles himself, and acts.

Judah's later kings return to evil.

Babylon carries the people away.

The temple burns.

Jerusalem falls.

But the final mercy toward Jehoiachin whispers that God has not forgotten David's line.

## Slow Walk

Do not rush past Josiah.

He cannot undo generations of sin, but he can respond faithfully in his own day.

That matters.

The fact that judgment is coming does not make obedience pointless.

Josiah still reads the word publicly.

He still renews covenant.

He still tears down idols.

He still keeps Passover.

Faithfulness matters even in a late hour.

Then do not rush past Jerusalem's fall.

The burned temple is one of the saddest scenes in the Old Testament.

The place of worship is destroyed because the people refused the God they worshiped there.

The ending with Jehoiachin is quiet, but important.

Judgment has fallen, yet hope has not died.

## Final Guided Recap

Before the closing, gather the whole fall of Judah.

Second Kings 22 begins with the word of God being found.

Josiah's heart is tender.

He does not argue with Scripture.

He lets it expose the condition of the nation.

That is the beginning of true reform.

Second Kings 23 shows reform becoming action.

Josiah reads the covenant publicly.

He removes idols.

He defiles false worship sites.

He keeps Passover.

He turns to the LORD with heart, soul, and might.

The chapter honors him deeply, yet it also says the LORD's wrath remains because Judah's rebellion has been long and severe.

Second Kings 24 shows Babylon tightening its grip.

Kings do evil.

Treasures are carried away.

Leaders and craftsmen are exiled.

Judah is being emptied before the final destruction.

Second Kings 25 brings the terrible end.

Jerusalem is besieged.

The king is blinded.

The temple is burned.

The walls are broken.

The people are carried away.

The book could have ended in total darkness, but it closes with Jehoiachin lifted from prison.

That small mercy matters.

It tells the reader that David's line is humbled, but not forgotten.

Day 93 teaches that repentance is beautiful, judgment is serious, and hope can survive even after the city burns.

## Closing

As you finish Day 93, remember Josiah's tender heart.

The right response to God's word is not defensiveness.

It is humility, repentance, and obedience.
