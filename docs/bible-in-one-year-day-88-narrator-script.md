# Day 88 Narrator Script

## Opening

Welcome to Day 88 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are reading Second Kings 2 through 5.

The title for today is Elisha's Ministry Begins.

This day moves from Elijah to Elisha.

Elijah is taken up by a whirlwind into heaven.

Elisha receives the mantle and begins walking in prophetic ministry.

The chapters show water healed, judgment at Bethel, kings delivered, a widow provided for, a promised child restored to life, poisoned food healed, bread multiplied, Naaman washed clean, and Gehazi judged for greed.

The day teaches that the LORD's power does not end with one servant.

Elijah departs, but the God of Elijah remains.

## Second Kings 2

Second Kings 2 begins with the LORD about to take Elijah into heaven by a whirlwind.

Elijah and Elisha travel from Gilgal to Bethel, to Jericho, and to Jordan.

Again and again, Elijah tells Elisha to stay behind.

Again and again, Elisha says, As the LORD liveth, and as thy soul liveth, I will not leave thee.

The sons of the prophets know Elijah will be taken, but Elisha keeps following.

At the Jordan, Elijah takes his mantle, wraps it together, strikes the waters, and they divide.

Elijah and Elisha cross on dry ground.

Elijah asks what he should do for Elisha before he is taken.

Elisha asks for a double portion of Elijah's spirit.

This is inheritance language.

He is not asking to be twice as famous.

He is asking to carry the prophetic work as the true successor.

Then a chariot of fire and horses of fire separate them, and Elijah goes up by a whirlwind into heaven.

Elisha cries, My father, my father, the chariot of Israel, and the horsemen thereof.

He takes up Elijah's mantle and returns to the Jordan.

He asks, Where is the LORD God of Elijah?

When he strikes the water, it divides.

The sons of the prophets see that the spirit of Elijah rests on Elisha.

The chapter then shows Elisha healing the waters of Jericho with salt in a new cruse.

The power is not in salt as magic.

The LORD heals the waters through His prophet.

Then at Bethel, mockers despise Elisha, and judgment falls.

This is a severe scene, but it shows that rejecting the prophet of the LORD is serious.

## Second Kings 3

Second Kings 3 turns to Jehoram of Israel.

He removes the image of Baal that his father made, but still cleaves to the sins of Jeroboam.

That is partial reform.

Some evil is removed, but the deeper false worship remains.

Moab rebels against Israel.

Jehoram joins with Jehoshaphat of Judah and the king of Edom.

They march through the wilderness of Edom, but after seven days there is no water for the host or the cattle.

Jehoshaphat asks whether there is a prophet of the LORD.

Elisha is there.

Elisha speaks sharply to the king of Israel, asking why he should go to him.

But because of Jehoshaphat, Elisha seeks the LORD.

A minstrel plays, and the hand of the LORD comes upon Elisha.

The LORD says the valley will be full of ditches.

They will not see wind or rain, but the valley will be filled with water.

This is a light thing in the sight of the LORD; He will also deliver Moab.

In the morning, water comes by the way of Edom.

The Moabites see the water as blood and think the kings have destroyed one another.

They rush in and are defeated.

The chapter ends grimly with the king of Moab offering his son, and great indignation comes against Israel.

The day does not turn war into a simple adventure.

It keeps showing the darkness of nations and the need for the LORD.

## Second Kings 4

Second Kings 4 gathers several acts of mercy through Elisha.

A widow of the sons of the prophets cries out because a creditor is coming to take her sons as bondmen.

Elisha asks what she has in the house.

She has only a pot of oil.

He tells her to borrow vessels, not a few.

The oil keeps pouring until there are no more vessels.

She sells the oil, pays the debt, and lives with her children.

God's mercy reaches debt, food, family, and survival.

Then Elisha often passes through Shunem, where a great woman provides a chamber for him.

Elisha asks what can be done for her.

She has no child, and her husband is old.

Elisha promises a son.

The child is born, but later dies.

The woman goes to Elisha with grief and urgency.

Elisha comes, prays, stretches himself upon the child, and the child lives.

The chapter then shows poisoned food healed during famine.

A man brings bread of the firstfruits, and Elisha tells them to set it before the people.

Though it seems too little, they eat and leave thereof according to the word of the LORD.

This chapter is full of homes, tables, hunger, grief, and provision.

The LORD is not only God of kings and battles.

He is God over widows, children, food, debt, and life.

## Second Kings 5

Second Kings 5 tells the story of Naaman.

Naaman is captain of the host of Syria, a great man, honorable, and a mighty man in valor.

But he is a leper.

A little maid from Israel tells Naaman's wife that the prophet in Samaria could recover him.

That small witness matters.

Naaman comes with horses, chariot, silver, gold, and garments.

The king of Israel panics, but Elisha tells him to send Naaman so he may know there is a prophet in Israel.

Elisha does not come out with ceremony.

He sends a messenger telling Naaman to wash in Jordan seven times.

Naaman is angry.

He expected Elisha to come out, call on the LORD, strike his hand over the place, and recover the leper.

He thinks the rivers of Damascus are better than the waters of Israel.

His servants reason with him.

If the prophet had commanded some great thing, would he not have done it?

Naaman humbles himself, dips seven times in Jordan, and his flesh comes again like the flesh of a little child.

He confesses that there is no God in all the earth but in Israel.

Elisha refuses payment.

But Gehazi runs after Naaman and lies for silver and garments.

Elisha confronts him.

Naaman's leprosy clings to Gehazi.

The chapter contrasts humble obedience with greedy deception.

## Full Lesson Review

Day 88 shows Elisha's ministry beginning with the same God who worked through Elijah.

The Jordan divides.

Water is healed.

Kings are rescued.

A widow's oil multiplies.

A child is raised.

Food is healed.

Bread is multiplied.

A foreign commander is cleansed.

Greed is judged.

The LORD's power reaches public kingdoms and private rooms.

He works through prophets, widows, servants, and even a little captive girl.

## Slow Walk Through the Day

Day 88 is full of transitions and small mercies.

Elijah's departure could feel like the end of prophetic power.

But when Elisha takes up the mantle and the Jordan parts, the reader sees continuity.

The ministry is not built on Elijah's personality.

It belongs to the LORD.

The God who was with Elijah is with Elisha.

That is why the question, Where is the LORD God of Elijah, matters so much.

Elisha is not asking where Elijah is.

He is looking to the God who called Elijah.

The healing of Jericho's water shows that God can heal what is bitter or deadly at the source.

The judgment at Bethel shows that contempt for God's prophet is not harmless.

Both scenes introduce Elisha as a true prophet whose ministry brings blessing and judgment.

Second Kings 3 then places Elisha before kings.

Jehoram is not faithful, but Jehoshaphat still asks for the prophet of the LORD.

The valley full of ditches teaches that God can provide before people can see the normal signs of provision.

No wind.

No rain.

Yet water comes.

The LORD does not need visible conditions to be favorable before He can act.

Second Kings 4 brings the prophet into homes.

The widow's oil is one of the tenderest pictures of practical mercy.

The miracle answers debt.

It keeps sons from bondage.

It provides a way to live.

The Shunammite story shows hospitality, promise, grief, and restoration.

The woman who made room for the prophet is met by the God who gives life.

The poisoned pot and multiplied bread show the LORD sustaining His people during famine.

Second Kings 5 opens the story to a foreign commander.

Naaman has status, strength, and honor, but he also has leprosy.

The little maid has no power in worldly terms, but she knows where help can be found.

Naaman nearly misses mercy because the command seems too simple and too humbling.

Wash in Jordan seven times.

That is not impressive enough for his pride.

But the healing comes through humble obedience.

Gehazi then shows the opposite heart.

He sees grace and tries to turn it into profit.

The chapter ends by warning that holy ministry must not be used for greed.

## Prayerful Landing

Day 88 teaches that God's power meets people in many conditions.

A prophet loses his master.

A city has bad water.

Kings lack water.

A widow has debt.

A mother loses a child.

A pot is poisoned.

A crowd lacks bread.

A commander has leprosy.

A servant hides greed.

The LORD is not distant from any of it.

He works in public and private, in Israel and beyond Israel, through great prophets and unnamed servants.

The listener should leave this day humbled.

God's help may come through a mantle, a jar of oil, a word from a little girl, or a simple command to wash.

Do not despise the small path of obedience.

Naaman was cleansed when he stopped arguing with the simplicity of God's word.

## Final Guided Recap

Before the closing, gather the shape of Elisha's beginning.

Second Kings 2 shows succession.

Elijah is taken, but the LORD is not gone.

Elisha receives the mantle and crosses the Jordan by the power of God.

The sons of the prophets recognize that Elijah's spirit rests on Elisha.

Second Kings 3 shows the prophet speaking into royal crisis.

The kings lack water.

Elisha does not flatter the king of Israel.

Yet the LORD gives water and deliverance.

The chapter teaches that God can provide without the normal signs people expect.

Second Kings 4 shows mercy entering ordinary homes.

The widow's oil answers debt.

The Shunammite's son is given and restored.

The poisoned pot is healed.

The bread is multiplied.

The chapter says plainly that God's power is not limited to palaces, armies, or public altars.

Second Kings 5 shows grace crossing national lines.

Naaman is great, but unclean.

The little maid is lowly, but she points him toward help.

Elisha's instruction is simple.

Naaman's pride almost rejects it.

When he obeys, he is cleansed.

Then Gehazi's greed warns the listener not to turn God's mercy into a chance for gain.

Day 88 is therefore a day of transferred ministry, humble obedience, practical mercy, and holy warning.

The God of Elijah is still acting through Elisha.

## Closing

As you finish Day 88, remember Naaman in the Jordan.

The way of healing was not impressive to his pride.

But when he humbled himself and obeyed the word, he was made clean.
