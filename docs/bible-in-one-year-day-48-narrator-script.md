# Bible in One Year - Day 48 Narrator Script

**Reading:** Deuteronomy 2-5

## Opening

Welcome to Day 48 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are reading Deuteronomy chapters 2 through 5.

The theme is remembering the journey and the covenant.

Moses is speaking to the new generation.

He wants them to remember where God led them, what God gave them, and how God spoke to them.

## Deuteronomy 2 verses 1 through 37

**Scripture**

Moses remembers Israel turning into the wilderness by the way of the Red Sea.

God tells them not to meddle with Edom, Moab, or Ammon, because He gave those lands to others.

Then Sihon king of Heshbon refuses passage and fights Israel.

The LORD gives Sihon into Israel's hand.

**Narrator**

This chapter teaches restraint and victory.

Israel cannot take whatever land they want.

God gave some lands to other peoples.

That means obedience sometimes means not taking.

But when Sihon resists, the LORD gives victory.

There are lands Israel must not seize.

There is a battle God gives.

There is a journey God directs.

The LORD is not only powerful in battle.

He is also Lord over boundaries.

Deuteronomy 2 is important because it teaches Israel that victory does not mean taking everything.

The LORD tells Israel not to meddle with Edom, Moab, or Ammon.

Those lands were given to other peoples.

That means Israel must respect what God has assigned.

This is a major lesson in restraint.

The promised land does not give Israel permission to become greedy.

God's people must obey both when He says take and when He says do not take.

The journey is directed by the LORD, not by appetite.

Then Sihon refuses peaceful passage.

That situation is different.

Israel asks to pass through, but Sihon comes out against them.

The LORD gives victory.

So the chapter holds two truths together.

Sometimes obedience means restraint.

Sometimes obedience means battle.

In both cases, the LORD is directing the road.

This helps beginners see that conquest in Deuteronomy is not random land hunger.

God rules over boundaries, timing, and judgment.

## Deuteronomy 3 verses 1 through 29

**Scripture**

Moses remembers victory over Og king of Bashan.

The land east of Jordan is given to Reuben, Gad, and half Manasseh.

Moses commands Joshua to remember what the LORD has done and not fear.

Moses pleads to enter the land, but the LORD tells him to go up Pisgah and see it with his eyes.

**Narrator**

Og was a terrifying enemy.

But the LORD gave victory.

Moses uses that memory to strengthen Joshua.

What God has done becomes courage for what God will do.

Moses also tells his own painful story.

He wants to enter the land, but the LORD says no.

He may see it, but Joshua will lead the people in.

The mission continues, even when Moses' role changes.

Og king of Bashan represents another frightening obstacle.

He is connected with the remnant of giants, and his territory is strong.

But Moses tells Joshua to remember what the LORD has already done.

Memory becomes courage.

The victories over Sihon and Og are not only military stories.

They are lessons for the next leader.

Joshua will face battles across the Jordan.

Moses wants him to know that the LORD who gave victory before can give victory again.

The land east of Jordan is also important.

Reuben, Gad, and half Manasseh receive inheritance there, but they must still help their brothers.

That keeps shared responsibility alive.

Moses' own request to enter the land is deeply human.

He longs to see the good land.

But the LORD says no.

Moses may see it from Pisgah, but Joshua will lead the people in.

That teaches that God's mission is bigger than even Moses.

The servant changes.

The promise continues.

Moses' disappointment does not mean God's plan has failed.

It means Moses must trust the LORD even with the boundary placed on his own life.

## Deuteronomy 4 verses 1 through 24

**Scripture**

Moses calls Israel to hearken to the statutes and judgments of the LORD.

They must not add to or take away from God's word.

They must remember Horeb, where they heard God's voice but saw no form.

They must not make graven images.

The LORD is a consuming fire, even a jealous God.

**Narrator**

Moses connects memory to obedience.

Israel heard God's voice.

They did not see a shape.

That matters because they must not make an image and pretend it represents God.

God is not like the idols of the nations.

He cannot be reduced to metal, wood, stone, sun, moon, or stars.

The phrase consuming fire teaches holiness and seriousness.

God's covenant love is not casual.

Deuteronomy 4 is a major teaching chapter because Moses presses memory into obedience.

He says Israel must not add to God's word or take away from it.

That means God's command is not raw material for Israel to reshape.

They must receive it.

They must guard it.

They must obey it.

Moses points back to Horeb, where Israel heard God's voice from the fire.

They heard words, but they saw no form.

That detail is central.

Because they saw no form, they must not make an image of God.

No carved shape can represent the LORD.

No male or female form, animal, bird, creeping thing, fish, sun, moon, or stars can capture Him.

God is not part of creation.

He is the Creator and covenant LORD.

The phrase consuming fire teaches that God's holiness is serious.

The phrase jealous God teaches that His covenant love demands exclusive loyalty.

Jealous here does not mean petty insecurity.

It means the LORD will not share His people's worship with idols.

He loves them too faithfully to treat idolatry as harmless.

## Deuteronomy 4 verses 25 through 49

**Scripture**

Moses warns that if Israel corrupts itself with idols, they will be scattered among the nations.

But if they seek the LORD with all their heart and soul, they will find Him.

Moses says the LORD is God in heaven above and on earth beneath; there is none else.

Moses also sets apart cities of refuge east of Jordan.

**Narrator**

This warning reaches far into Israel's future.

Moses speaks of idolatry, exile, and mercy.

If Israel turns away, scattering will come.

But exile will not make God impossible to seek.

If they seek Him with all the heart and soul, He will be found.

This shows both warning and mercy.

God is holy enough to judge idolatry and merciful enough to receive the repentant.

The second half of Deuteronomy 4 looks far ahead.

Moses warns that if Israel corrupts itself with idols, exile will come.

That is a frightening warning because Israel has not even entered the land yet.

Moses is already warning them about losing it through idolatry.

But the warning includes mercy.

If they seek the LORD from there with all their heart and soul, they will find Him.

That phrase from there is important.

Even in exile, God is not unreachable.

Even after judgment, repentance is not meaningless.

The LORD is merciful.

He will not forget the covenant with their fathers.

Moses then asks them to consider how unique their story is.

Has any people heard the voice of God from the fire and lived?

Has any god taken a nation out of another nation with signs, wonders, war, mighty hand, stretched out arm, and great terrors?

The answer is no.

The LORD is God in heaven above and on earth beneath.

There is none else.

This is not abstract theology.

It is the reason Israel must obey.

The God who rescued them is the only God.

## Deuteronomy 5 verses 1 through 33

**Scripture**

Moses calls all Israel to hear the statutes and judgments.

He repeats the Ten Commandments.

He reminds Israel how they heard the voice of the LORD from the fire.

The people feared and asked Moses to hear for them.

The LORD desired that they would have a heart to fear Him and keep His commandments always.

**Narrator**

Deuteronomy 5 gives the covenant words to the new generation.

They must not think Horeb only belonged to their parents.

These commandments are for them too.

The law begins with grace.

I am the LORD thy God, which brought thee out of the land of Egypt.

Obedience comes after rescue.

God wants more than rule-keeping.

He says, O that there were such an heart in them.

The covenant aims at the heart.

Deuteronomy 5 repeats the Ten Commandments for the new generation.

That matters because the covenant is not only a memory from their parents' past.

Moses says the LORD made this covenant with us.

The new generation must stand inside the covenant themselves.

The commandments begin with grace.

I am the LORD thy God, which brought thee out of the land of Egypt.

God's rescue comes before the commands.

Obedience is not Israel earning rescue.

Obedience is Israel responding to the God who rescued them.

The commands shape love for God and love for neighbor.

No other gods.

No idols.

Honor God's name.

Keep the Sabbath.

Honor father and mother.

Do not murder.

Do not commit adultery.

Do not steal.

Do not bear false witness.

Do not covet.

These commands are not random restrictions.

They form a holy community under the LORD.

The people's fear at the mountain shows that God's voice is overwhelming.

They ask Moses to stand between them and the LORD.

God says, O that there were such an heart in them.

That line shows the goal.

God wants a heart that fears Him and keeps His commandments always.

Before we close Day 48, hold the full movement together.

Deuteronomy 2 teaches that God directs the road and the boundaries.

Deuteronomy 3 teaches that past victory gives courage for future obedience.

Deuteronomy 4 teaches that memory must become loyalty and idolatry must be rejected.

Deuteronomy 5 repeats the covenant words and presses them into the heart.

This day is about remembering rightly.

Israel must remember where God said no.

They must remember where God gave victory.

They must remember Horeb.

They must remember that they saw no form.

They must remember that the LORD alone is God.

They must remember the commandments.

Memory is not nostalgia.

Memory is training for obedience.

For a beginner, Day 48 teaches that God leads, limits, warns, rescues, speaks, and commands.

The proper response is not casual religion.

The proper response is careful obedience from the heart.

The new generation is being taught to live from memory, not from pride.

They are not entering the land as self-made people.

They are entering as people who heard the voice of God and must keep His covenant.

One more way to understand Day 48 is through the phrase take heed.

Moses keeps urging Israel to pay careful attention.

Take heed to the journey.

Take heed to the boundaries God gave.

Take heed to the victories God provided.

Take heed to the fact that they saw no form at Horeb.

Take heed to the covenant words.

This repeated concern shows that forgetfulness is not a small problem.

Forgetfulness can become idolatry.

Forgetfulness can become pride.

Forgetfulness can become disobedience.

Moses knows the new generation will face pressures their parents did not face in the same way.

They will live in cities.

They will farm land.

They will encounter local worship practices.

They will be tempted to think visually, like the nations around them.

That is why the warning about images is so strong.

Israel heard the voice of God, but saw no form.

The LORD chose to reveal Himself by His word.

So Israel must not replace the living God with an image.

An idol always makes God smaller than He is.

It gives people something they can control, carry, decorate, and manipulate.

But the LORD is God in heaven above and on earth beneath.

There is none else.

This truth gives Deuteronomy 4 its force.

The God who rescued Israel cannot be reduced to creation.

He rules creation.

He speaks from fire.

He enters covenant.

He judges idolatry.

He receives the repentant who seek Him with all the heart.

Then Deuteronomy 5 repeats the commandments because memory must become concrete obedience.

It is not enough to say, "We remember Horeb."

Israel must live by the words spoken there.

The Ten Commandments shape worship, time, family, life, marriage, property, truth, and desire.

They reach from public behavior down into the heart that covets.

That is why God's statement about the heart is so important.

O that there were such an heart in them.

The LORD is not looking for mechanical rule-keeping disconnected from love and reverence.

He wants a heart that fears Him and keeps His commands.

Day 48 therefore moves from road memory to covenant heart.

The road shows God's guidance.

The battles show God's power.

Horeb shows God's holiness.

The warnings show God's jealousy.

The commandments show God's will.

The heart shows the real target.

For a beginner, this day teaches that biblical memory is active.

It does something.

It leads to worship without idols.

It leads to obedience without editing God's word.

It leads to repentance when judgment comes.

It leads to courage because God has already acted.

It leads to humility because the LORD alone is God.

Moses wants Israel to carry all of that into the land.

If they remember wrongly, they will live wrongly.

If they remember rightly, they will have wisdom for obedience.

That is why Day 48 matters.

The new generation is being taught to think, worship, and obey from the story God has already written among them.

Before we close, let us gather the details one more time.

Deuteronomy 2 teaches restraint.

That may surprise us.

Israel is on the edge of the promised land, and God is giving them victory.

But God also tells them where not to fight.

Edom, Moab, and Ammon are not given to Israel.

Those lands belong to other peoples because of earlier family lines connected to Esau and Lot.

That teaches Israel that God's promise to them does not cancel God's rule over everyone else.

The LORD is not a tribal god with power over only one people.

He rules over the map.

He gives land.

He sets limits.

He commands Israel to respect what He has not given them.

For a beginner, this is a helpful way to understand holy war in the Old Testament.

Israel is not allowed to attack anyone they want.

They move only where the LORD commands.

They must not confuse desire with permission.

They must not confuse strength with right.

Then the story of Sihon shows the opposite situation.

Israel asks to pass through peacefully.

Sihon refuses.

His heart is hardened.

The LORD gives him into Israel's hand.

That victory teaches the people that obedience to God's command brings them forward.

It also warns the surrounding nations that the LORD is acting for Israel.

Deuteronomy 3 continues with Og of Bashan.

Og sounds terrifying.

His kingdom is strong.

His cities are fortified.

His bed is described in a way that makes him feel larger than ordinary enemies.

But the point is not to make Og impressive by himself.

The point is to show that no impressive enemy is too great for the LORD.

Moses wants the new generation to remember this before they face Canaan.

They have already seen fortified cities fall.

They have already seen powerful kings defeated.

They have already seen God keep His word.

Then Moses says he pleaded to enter the land.

This is a tender moment.

Moses has led Israel through so much.

He has prayed for them.

He has carried their complaints.

He has watched a generation fall in the wilderness.

But he will not cross Jordan.

The LORD tells him not to speak of it again.

That feels hard.

It reminds us that Moses is deeply loved by God, but he is still accountable before God.

Leadership does not remove responsibility.

Closeness to God does not make disobedience unimportant.

Yet God also shows mercy.

Moses is allowed to see the land.

Joshua will lead the people in.

The work continues.

The promise continues.

God's plan is bigger than one servant, even a servant as great as Moses.

Deuteronomy 4 then becomes one of the strongest teaching chapters in the book.

Moses says, hear and do.

Those two words belong together.

Israel is not supposed to hear God's word as information only.

They are to receive it and live by it.

Moses also says they must not add to the word or take away from it.

That teaches reverence.

God's people are not editors over God's commands.

They are listeners under God's authority.

Then Moses tells them to teach their children.

This is important because the covenant must not stop with one generation.

Children need to know what their parents saw, heard, and learned.

The story of Horeb must become family memory.

Faith is passed on through words, teaching, repetition, and daily life.

The warning about idols is also very important.

At Horeb, Israel heard God's voice but saw no form.

That means they must not make a form and call it God.

An idol shrinks God down into something people can see, carry, control, or decorate.

But the LORD is not like that.

He speaks.

He commands.

He reveals Himself by His word.

He cannot be reduced to an image.

When Moses calls the LORD a consuming fire and a jealous God, he is not saying God is unstable or petty.

He is saying God's holiness is real.

He will not share His people's worship with false gods.

His jealousy is covenant love refusing to let idolatry destroy the people He rescued.

Then Moses looks ahead to exile.

He knows idolatry will bring scattering if Israel turns away.

But he also teaches mercy.

If they seek the LORD with all their heart and soul, they will find Him.

That sentence matters because judgment is not the last word for the repentant.

The LORD is merciful.

He remembers covenant.

He hears those who seek Him.

Finally, Deuteronomy 5 repeats the Ten Commandments.

The commandments do not begin with Israel's achievement.

They begin with God's rescue.

The LORD brought them out of Egypt.

Then He gave His commands.

Grace comes before obedience.

Rescue comes before covenant instruction.

That order matters for the whole Bible.

God does not tell Israel to obey so they can become rescued.

He rescues them and then teaches them how rescued people must live.

Day 48 is therefore about memory becoming obedience.

Remember the road.

Remember the victories.

Remember the limits.

Remember Horeb.

Remember the voice.

Remember the warning against idols.

Remember mercy.

Remember the commandments.

If Israel remembers rightly, they can enter the land with wisdom.

If they forget, the land itself will become dangerous to them.

Before we close, notice how Deuteronomy 2 through 5 trains Israel to remember several different kinds of grace.

There is guiding grace.

The LORD directed their steps through the wilderness.

Even when the journey was long, the road was not outside His sight.

There is restraining grace.

God told Israel not to attack Edom, Moab, or Ammon.

That means obedience sometimes looks like stopping, waiting, and respecting what God has given to someone else.

There is conquering grace.

When the LORD gave Sihon and Og into Israel's hand, the victories were not proof that Israel was naturally stronger.

They were proof that the LORD was faithful to bring His people forward when He chose.

There is warning grace.

At Horeb, the people heard God's voice but saw no form.

That warning protected them from making idols and shrinking the living God down into something human hands could manage.

And there is covenant grace.

The Ten Commandments begin with rescue.

The LORD names Himself as the God who brought Israel out of Egypt before He commands them how to live.

That order helps beginners understand the whole day.

God is not saying, obey Me so I might rescue you.

He is saying, I rescued you, so live as My covenant people.

The new generation needs to remember this because they are about to live in houses they did not build and eat from fields they did not plant.

Comfort can make people forget what hardship once taught them.

Victory can make people forget who fought for them.

Land can make people forget the Giver of the land.

That is why Moses keeps pressing memory into the heart.

Remember the wilderness.

Remember the boundaries.

Remember the victories.

Remember the voice.

Remember the covenant.

Remember mercy if you turn back and seek the LORD.

Day 48 teaches that biblical memory is not nostalgia.

It is spiritual training.

It teaches Israel how to see the present correctly.

The land ahead is not an independent future.

It is the next chapter of the same faithful God who led them through the past.

## Closing

That is Day 48.

Deuteronomy 2 through 5 teaches Israel to remember rightly.

God directed the road.

God gave victory.

God warned against idols.

God spoke His covenant words.

Memory is meant to become obedience.
