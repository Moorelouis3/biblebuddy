# Bible In One Year - Day 21 Narrator Script

## Day 21 - Genesis Ends With Hope

**Reading:** Genesis 49 and 50  
**Bible text:** King James Version, Genesis 49-50  
**Audio style:** Narrator teaching with Scripture reading  

## Opening

Welcome to Day 21 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are reading Genesis 49 and 50.

And today, we finish the book of Genesis.

That matters.

Genesis began with God creating the heavens and the earth.

It began with light breaking into darkness.

It began with a world that was good.

Then sin entered.

Shame entered.

Death entered.

Families fractured.

Brothers killed brothers.

Nations scattered.

And yet God kept speaking promise.

He promised that the seed of the woman would crush the serpent.

He called Abraham.

He promised land, descendants, and blessing for all families of the earth.

He carried that promise through Isaac.

Then through Jacob.

Then through Joseph's suffering in Egypt.

Now Genesis ends in a surprising way.

Not with everyone back in Canaan.

Not with a throne.

Not with a completed nation.

Not with every wound perfectly healed.

Genesis ends with final words, death, burial, lingering guilt, forgiveness, and a coffin in Egypt.

But it does not end without hope.

That is the key.

Genesis 49 and 50 teach us that God's promise can outlive grief.

It can outlive human failure.

It can outlive death.

And it can even sit inside Egypt, waiting for the day God will surely visit His people.

## Genesis 49:1-7

**Scripture**

And Jacob called unto his sons, and said, Gather yourselves together, that I may tell you that which shall befall you in the last days.

Gather yourselves together, and hear, ye sons of Jacob; and hearken unto Israel your father.

Reuben, thou art my firstborn, my might, and the beginning of my strength.

Unstable as water, thou shalt not excel; because thou wentest up to thy father's bed.

Simeon and Levi are brethren; instruments of cruelty are in their habitations.

Cursed be their anger, for it was fierce; and their wrath, for it was cruel.

**Narrator**

Genesis 49 is not a soft goodbye speech.

Picture Jacob as an old man near death.

His sons gather around him.

These are not just sons anymore.

They are the roots of the future tribes of Israel.

So Jacob's words are personal, but they are also prophetic.

He is speaking about character, consequence, and future direction.

He begins with Reuben.

Reuben was the firstborn.

In that culture, the firstborn normally carried special honor, leadership, and inheritance privilege.

Jacob even calls him the beginning of his strength.

But then comes the painful line.

Unstable as water, thou shalt not excel.

Reuben's position was strong, but his character was not.

Earlier in Genesis, Reuben slept with Bilhah, his father's concubine.

That was not only sexual sin.

In the ancient world, that kind of act could also be a power move inside the family.

Jacob never forgot it.

Privilege could not protect Reuben from the consequences of instability.

Then Jacob turns to Simeon and Levi.

He remembers their violence at Shechem.

Dinah had been terribly wronged, but Simeon and Levi answered with deception and slaughter.

Jacob does not bless their anger.

He curses it.

That is important.

The Bible does not say every angry response becomes righteous just because the original wrong was real.

Simeon and Levi were angry about something serious.

But their wrath became cruel.

Genesis is teaching that legacy is shaped by character.

Firstborn position matters.

Family outrage matters.

But character matters too.

## Genesis 49:8-12

**Scripture**

Judah, thou art he whom thy brethren shall praise: thy hand shall be in the neck of thine enemies; thy father's children shall bow down before thee.

Judah is a lion's whelp: from the prey, my son, thou art gone up.

The sceptre shall not depart from Judah, nor a lawgiver from between his feet, until Shiloh come.

Binding his foal unto the vine, and his ass's colt unto the choice vine; he washed his garments in wine, and his clothes in the blood of grapes.

**Narrator**

Then Jacob speaks over Judah.

And the tone changes.

Judah's story has not been clean.

He suggested selling Joseph.

He left the family.

He failed Tamar and was exposed by her.

But Judah also changed.

He confessed, She hath been more righteous than I.

Later, he offered himself in Benjamin's place.

So when Jacob speaks over Judah, we are not hearing praise for a flawless man.

We are hearing grace moving through a changed man.

Judah will be praised by his brothers.

His brothers will bow.

He is pictured like a lion.

Strong.

Royal.

Settled in authority.

Then comes one of the most important lines in Genesis.

The scepter shall not depart from Judah.

A scepter is a symbol of rule.

This means kingship will be associated with Judah's line.

Later in the Bible, Israel's great king David will come from Judah.

And much later, the New Testament will present Jesus as the Lion of the tribe of Judah.

So Genesis is already pointing forward.

The promise is narrowing.

The seed promise from Genesis 3.

The blessing promise to Abraham.

The covenant line through Isaac and Jacob.

Now royal hope is being spoken over Judah.

And that is an aha moment.

The Bible's kingly storyline does not begin in 1 Samuel with David.

The roots are already here.

Genesis 49 is whispering that a ruler will come through Judah.

## Genesis 49:13-21

**Scripture**

Zebulun shall dwell at the haven of the sea; and he shall be for an haven of ships.

Issachar is a strong ass couching down between two burdens.

Dan shall judge his people, as one of the tribes of Israel.

I have waited for thy salvation, O LORD.

Gad, a troop shall overcome him: but he shall overcome at the last.

Out of Asher his bread shall be fat, and he shall yield royal dainties.

Naphtali is a hind let loose: he giveth goodly words.

**Narrator**

The next group of sons receives shorter sayings.

These can feel difficult because the language is poetic.

Jacob gives pictures more than explanations.

Zebulun is connected with the sea and ships.

Issachar is pictured as strong but burdened.

Dan is connected with judgment, but also with danger.

Gad will be attacked, but will overcome.

Asher is connected with rich food.

Naphtali is pictured with freedom and beautiful words.

These are tribal images.

Jacob is looking beyond the sons as individual men and toward the future shape of Israel.

But right in the middle of these sayings, Jacob suddenly prays.

I have waited for thy salvation, O LORD.

That line is easy to miss.

But it is one of the most beautiful interruptions in the chapter.

Jacob is speaking about tribes, futures, territory, strength, danger, and blessing.

Then he pauses and says, Lord, I am waiting for Your salvation.

That means the future of Israel is not ultimately secured by the tribes themselves.

Not by their strength.

Not by their land.

Not by their ability to fight.

Not by their wisdom.

Their hope is the Lord's salvation.

At the end of Jacob's life, after all the wrestling, scheming, fearing, losing, grieving, and receiving, he knows this:

God must save.

That prayer belongs in the middle of every family story.

## Genesis 49:22-28

**Scripture**

Joseph is a fruitful bough, even a fruitful bough by a well; whose branches run over the wall.

The archers have sorely grieved him, and shot at him, and hated him:

But his bow abode in strength, and the arms of his hands were made strong by the hands of the mighty God of Jacob.

Even by the God of thy father, who shall help thee; and by the Almighty, who shall bless thee.

All these are the twelve tribes of Israel.

**Narrator**

Then Jacob speaks over Joseph.

The image is fruitfulness.

Joseph is a fruitful bough by a well.

Picture a branch growing near water.

Healthy.

Alive.

Spreading beyond the wall.

That is Joseph's life.

But notice, fruitfulness did not mean Joseph avoided suffering.

Jacob says the archers attacked him.

They grieved him.

They shot at him.

They hated him.

That poetic image gathers Joseph's whole story.

His brothers hated him.

They stripped him.

They sold him.

Potiphar's wife falsely accused him.

Prison swallowed years of his life.

The cupbearer forgot him.

Joseph was fruitful, but he was also wounded.

That is important for regular people to understand.

Sometimes we think fruitfulness means life will feel easy.

But Joseph's fruit grew in the presence of attack.

Then Jacob tells us why Joseph endured.

His bow remained strong by the hands of the mighty God of Jacob.

Joseph was wise.

Joseph was faithful.

Joseph worked hard.

But Genesis does not make him self-made.

God strengthened him.

God helped him.

God blessed him.

The Shepherd of Israel carried him.

And then Genesis says these are the twelve tribes of Israel.

That sentence matters.

The family has become the seed of a nation.

Genesis began with one man made from dust.

Now it is looking at twelve tribes.

The promise is still moving.

## Genesis 49:29-33

**Scripture**

And he charged them, and said unto them, I am to be gathered unto my people: bury me with my fathers in the cave that is in the field of Ephron the Hittite,

In the cave that is in the field of Machpelah, which is before Mamre, in the land of Canaan.

There they buried Abraham and Sarah his wife; there they buried Isaac and Rebekah his wife; and there I buried Leah.

And when Jacob had made an end of commanding his sons, he gathered up his feet into the bed, and yielded up the ghost.

**Narrator**

After speaking over his sons, Jacob gives one final instruction.

Bury me with my fathers.

Again, Canaan is in view.

Jacob is dying in Egypt, but he does not want Egypt to define the ending.

He names the cave of Machpelah.

That cave goes back to Genesis 23, when Abraham bought a burial place after Sarah died.

It was only a small piece of land.

But it mattered because it was a real foothold in the land God promised.

Abraham and Sarah were buried there.

Isaac and Rebekah were buried there.

Leah was buried there.

And now Jacob wants to be buried there too.

That burial request is faith.

It says, I die before seeing the full promise, but I still believe the promise.

Then Jacob gathers his feet into the bed and dies.

It is a quiet line.

No dramatic speech after this.

No last-minute miracle.

Jacob dies.

But the promise does not.

That is one of the great lessons at the end of Genesis.

God's servants die.

God's word continues.

## Genesis 50:1-14

**Scripture**

And Joseph fell upon his father's face, and wept upon him, and kissed him.

And Joseph commanded his servants the physicians to embalm his father: and the physicians embalmed Israel.

And the Egyptians mourned for him threescore and ten days.

And Joseph went up to bury his father: and with him went up all the servants of Pharaoh, the elders of his house, and all the elders of the land of Egypt.

And his sons carried him into the land of Canaan, and buried him in the cave of the field of Machpelah.

**Narrator**

Genesis 50 opens with grief.

Joseph falls on his father's face.

He weeps.

He kisses him.

Joseph is governor of Egypt, but in this moment he is simply a son who has lost his father.

The Bible does not rush grief.

Jacob is embalmed according to Egyptian practice.

Egypt mourns him for seventy days.

That tells us Jacob is honored because of Joseph's position.

But then Joseph keeps his promise.

He asks Pharaoh for permission to bury Jacob in Canaan.

Pharaoh allows it.

And a huge funeral procession goes up.

Servants of Pharaoh.

Elders of Egypt.

Joseph's household.

His brothers.

Chariots.

Horsemen.

It is a very great company.

Try to picture that procession leaving Egypt and heading toward Canaan.

This is more than a funeral.

It is a public witness that Jacob belonged to a promise older than Egypt.

The family lays him in Machpelah.

They honor his final words.

They bury him where Abraham, Sarah, Isaac, Rebekah, and Leah were buried.

Egypt had preserved Jacob.

But Canaan still held the promise.

## Genesis 50:15-21

**Scripture**

And when Joseph's brethren saw that their father was dead, they said, Joseph will peradventure hate us, and will certainly requite us all the evil which we did unto him.

And they sent a messenger unto Joseph, saying, Thy father did command before he died, saying,

So shall ye say unto Joseph, Forgive, I pray thee now, the trespass of thy brethren, and their sin.

And Joseph wept when they spake unto him.

And his brethren also went and fell down before his face; and they said, Behold, we be thy servants.

And Joseph said unto them, Fear not: for am I in the place of God?

But as for you, ye thought evil against me; but God meant it unto good, to bring to pass, as it is this day, to save much people alive.

Now therefore fear ye not: I will nourish you, and your little ones.

**Narrator**

After Jacob dies, the brothers are afraid.

They wonder if Joseph's forgiveness only lasted while their father was alive.

Maybe now Joseph will repay them.

That fear tells us something painful.

Guilt can survive for a long time.

Even after mercy has been shown, guilt can keep whispering, You are not safe.

The brothers send a message asking Joseph to forgive them.

And Joseph weeps.

Why does he weep?

Probably because their fear hurts him.

He has provided for them.

He has embraced them.

He has spoken kindly to them.

But they still wonder if revenge is hidden under the surface.

Then Joseph gives one of the greatest responses in Genesis.

Am I in the place of God?

That is humility.

Joseph has power.

He could punish them.

He could make their lives miserable.

But he refuses the seat of final judgment.

Revenge would require Joseph to act as if God's throne belonged to him.

He will not do that.

Then comes the famous sentence.

Ye thought evil against me; but God meant it unto good.

Notice what Joseph does not say.

He does not say, It was not evil.

He does not say, It did not hurt.

He does not say, You had good intentions.

He says, You meant evil.

That is honest.

But he also says, God meant it unto good.

That is providence.

Human evil was real.

But it was not ultimate.

God was working above it, through it, and beyond it to preserve many people alive.

This line explains the whole Joseph story.

The pit was evil.

The sale was evil.

The lie to Jacob was evil.

The false accusation was evil.

The prison years were painful.

But God was not absent.

God meant it for good.

And Joseph comforts them.

He promises to nourish them and their little ones.

Forgiveness becomes provision again.

## Genesis 50:22-26

**Scripture**

And Joseph dwelt in Egypt, he, and his father's house: and Joseph lived an hundred and ten years.

And Joseph saw Ephraim's children of the third generation.

And Joseph said unto his brethren, I die: and God will surely visit you, and bring you out of this land unto the land which he sware to Abraham, to Isaac, and to Jacob.

And Joseph took an oath of the children of Israel, saying, God will surely visit you, and ye shall carry up my bones from hence.

So Joseph died, being an hundred and ten years old: and they embalmed him, and he was put in a coffin in Egypt.

**Narrator**

Genesis ends with Joseph's death.

He lives one hundred and ten years.

He sees generations after him.

The boy who was sold as a slave becomes an old man surrounded by descendants.

But like Jacob, Joseph knows Egypt is not the final destination.

He says, God will surely visit you.

That phrase is full of future hope.

It points forward to Exodus.

One day, God's people will be oppressed in Egypt.

They will cry out.

And God will remember His covenant.

God will visit them.

God will come down to deliver.

Joseph does not know every detail, but he trusts the promise.

Then he makes them swear to carry up his bones.

That is faith beyond death.

Joseph's bones become a witness.

Every generation that remembers his coffin is meant to remember: we are not staying here forever.

God promised.

God will visit.

God will bring us up.

Then Genesis ends with a coffin in Egypt.

That might sound dark.

But it is not hopeless.

The coffin is waiting.

The promise is waiting.

The people are waiting.

Genesis ends by making us look forward.

## Closing

Day 21 closes Genesis with a strange kind of hope.

Jacob dies.

Joseph dies.

The family remains in Egypt.

There is still no nation settled in the land.

There is still more waiting ahead.

But Genesis has taught us something by now.

God's promise does not die when His servants die.

God's plan is not defeated by human evil.

God's mercy can reach into guilty families.

God's providence can work above betrayal.

And God's future can be trusted even when the current chapter ends in a coffin.

That is why Genesis 50:20 matters so much.

Ye thought evil against me; but God meant it unto good.

That sentence does not make evil small.

It makes God big.

It tells us that human sin is real, but it is not sovereign.

Pain is real, but it is not ultimate.

Death is real, but it is not the end of God's promise.

Genesis began with creation.

It ends with a coffin.

But between creation and the coffin, God has been speaking promise.

And the last thing Joseph says is not, Egypt is all we have.

He says, God will surely visit you.

That is where Genesis leaves us.

Waiting for God to visit.

Waiting for deliverance.

Waiting for the promise to keep moving.

And as the Bible continues, that is exactly what God will do.
