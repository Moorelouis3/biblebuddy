# Bible In One Year - Day 15 Narrator Script

## Day 15 - Joseph's Testing Begins

**Reading:** Genesis 37 and 38  
**Bible text:** King James Version, Genesis 37-38  
**Goal:** Read the full Scripture in order, pause after natural sections, explain what is happening in clear modern language, and help the listener understand Joseph's dreams, the brothers' jealousy, the pit, Judah's failure, Tamar's courage, and God's hidden work inside a very broken family.

## Estimated Length

**Estimated script word count:** about 6,000-7,000 words  
**Estimated spoken time:** about 35-45 minutes at a calm narrator pace  
**Tone:** warm Bible teacher, emotionally honest, clear, steady, and conversational

## Production Direction

Day 14 ended with Jacob's family reckoning: violence in Genesis 34, renewal at Bethel in Genesis 35, and Esau's line becoming Edom in Genesis 36. Day 15 now begins the Joseph story, but it should not feel like a clean new chapter detached from everything before it.

Joseph is born into the family we have been watching: favoritism, rivalry, grief, deception, and unresolved pain. Genesis 37 begins Joseph's testing, and Genesis 38 shows Judah being exposed before Genesis returns to Joseph in Egypt.

## Opening

Yesterday, Genesis closed the Jacob and Esau section.

Jacob's family had faced grief, violence, renewal, loss, and the separation of Esau's line into Edom.

Now Genesis turns toward Joseph.

But Joseph does not step into a calm family.

He steps into the same household we have been watching for days.

A household marked by favoritism.

Rivalry.

Old wounds.

Different mothers.

Visible preference.

And brothers who already know what it feels like to compete for love.

That matters because Joseph's testing does not begin in Egypt.

It begins at home.

Welcome to Day 15 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are reading Genesis 37 and 38.

Genesis 37 begins Joseph's story with the robe, the dreams, the hatred of his brothers, the pit, the sale into Egypt, and Jacob's grief.

Genesis 38 feels like a detour, but it is not random. It turns toward Judah and Tamar and shows us God exposing the brother who helped sell Joseph.

Listen for three big ideas today.

First: favoritism can make family pain visible.

Second: people can fight against a dream, but they cannot cancel what God is carrying forward.

Third: God can work through hidden places, painful truth, and messy family lines.

Let's begin with Genesis chapter 37, verses 1 through 4.

## Genesis 37:1-4

**Scripture**

1. And Jacob dwelt in the land wherein his father was a stranger, in the land of Canaan.

2. These are the generations of Jacob. Joseph, being seventeen years old, was feeding the flock with his brethren; and the lad was with the sons of Bilhah, and with the sons of Zilpah, his father's wives: and Joseph brought unto his father their evil report.

3. Now Israel loved Joseph more than all his children, because he was the son of his old age: and he made him a coat of many colours.

4. And when his brethren saw that their father loved him more than all his brethren, they hated him, and could not speak peaceably unto him.

**Narrator**

Genesis 37 opens by placing Jacob in the land of Canaan.

He is in the land of promise, but the family is not at peace.

That is important.

You can be in the right place geographically and still have disorder inside the home.

Verse 2 says, "These are the generations of Jacob." That phrase signals a new movement in Genesis. The focus shifts from Jacob himself to what comes from Jacob, especially his sons.

Joseph is seventeen years old.

He is young, he is with the sons of Bilhah and Zilpah, and he brings a bad report of them to his father.

That detail starts the tension right away.

Then the text says Israel loved Joseph more than all his children.

Israel is Jacob. The new name is used, but the old family patterns are still present.

Jacob loves Joseph in a special way because Joseph is the son of his old age. Joseph is also Rachel's son, the child born after years of longing.

That does not make the favoritism right.

But it helps us understand why Jacob's love is so emotionally loaded.

Then Jacob makes Joseph a coat of many colors.

This is not just a nice jacket.

It is a visible symbol.

Every time Joseph wears it, the brothers see what they already feel: Dad loves him more.

Verse 4 says they hated him and could not speak peaceably to him.

That means the house is already broken before the pit ever appears.

The pit begins in the heart.

Now Joseph has dreams.

## Genesis 37:5-11

**Scripture**

5. And Joseph dreamed a dream, and he told it his brethren: and they hated him yet the more.

6. And he said unto them, Hear, I pray you, this dream which I have dreamed:

7. For, behold, we were binding sheaves in the field, and, lo, my sheaf arose, and also stood upright; and, behold, your sheaves stood round about, and made obeisance to my sheaf.

8. And his brethren said to him, Shalt thou indeed reign over us? or shalt thou indeed have dominion over us? And they hated him yet the more for his dreams, and for his words.

9. And he dreamed yet another dream, and told it his brethren, and said, Behold, I have dreamed a dream more; and, behold, the sun and the moon and the eleven stars made obeisance to me.

10. And he told it to his father, and to his brethren: and his father rebuked him, and said unto him, What is this dream that thou hast dreamed? Shall I and thy mother and thy brethren indeed come to bow down ourselves to thee to the earth?

11. And his brethren envied him; but his father observed the saying.

**Narrator**

Joseph dreams two dreams.

In the first dream, the brothers are binding sheaves in the field. Joseph's sheaf rises and stands upright, and the brothers' sheaves bow down to it.

The meaning is not hard to feel.

The brothers hear authority.

They hear Joseph over them.

They hear the younger favored brother being lifted above the rest.

So they ask, "Shalt thou indeed reign over us?"

They understand the direction of the dream, and they hate him even more.

Then Joseph dreams again.

This time the sun, moon, and eleven stars bow down to him.

Jacob rebukes him because now the dream seems to include the whole family.

But verse 11 gives us a subtle detail.

His brothers envy him, but his father observes the saying.

Jacob does not fully receive it, but he does not forget it either.

That is important because Joseph's dreams will not make sense for many chapters.

At this point, they just sound like more fuel on the family fire.

The dreams are from God, but Joseph does not yet have the maturity to carry them quietly.

That is a real lesson.

Not everything God shows a person should be announced the moment they see it.

Truth still needs wisdom.

Now Jacob sends Joseph to check on his brothers.

## Genesis 37:12-17

**Scripture**

12. And his brethren went to feed their father's flock in Shechem.

13. And Israel said unto Joseph, Do not thy brethren feed the flock in Shechem? come, and I will send thee unto them. And he said to him, Here am I.

14. And he said to him, Go, I pray thee, see whether it be well with thy brethren, and well with the flocks; and bring me word again. So he sent him out of the vale of Hebron, and he came to Shechem.

15. And a certain man found him, and, behold, he was wandering in the field: and the man asked him, saying, What seekest thou?

16. And he said, I seek my brethren: tell me, I pray thee, where they feed their flocks.

17. And the man said, They are departed hence; for I heard them say, Let us go to Dothan. And Joseph went after his brethren, and found them in Dothan.

**Narrator**

Jacob sends Joseph to Shechem.

That place should ring a bell.

Shechem was connected to the painful events of Genesis 34. So even the location carries tension.

Joseph goes to check on his brothers and the flocks.

When he does not find them, he keeps searching.

A man finds him wandering in the field and tells him they went to Dothan.

So Joseph follows.

On the surface, this feels ordinary.

A father sends a son.

A son checks on his brothers.

He cannot find them, so he asks for directions.

But because we already know the brothers hate him, the scene feels dangerous.

Joseph is walking toward people who cannot speak peaceably to him.

He is obeying his father, but obedience is leading him toward a test he does not see coming.

Now the brothers see him from a distance.

## Genesis 37:18-28

**Scripture**

18. And when they saw him afar off, even before he came near unto them, they conspired against him to slay him.

19. And they said one to another, Behold, this dreamer cometh.

20. Come now therefore, and let us slay him, and cast him into some pit, and we will say, Some evil beast hath devoured him: and we shall see what will become of his dreams.

21. And Reuben heard it, and he delivered him out of their hands; and said, Let us not kill him.

22. And Reuben said unto them, Shed no blood, but cast him into this pit that is in the wilderness, and lay no hand upon him; that he might rid him out of their hands, to deliver him to his father again.

23. And it came to pass, when Joseph was come unto his brethren, that they stript Joseph out of his coat, his coat of many colours that was on him;

24. And they took him, and cast him into a pit: and the pit was empty, there was no water in it.

25. And they sat down to eat bread: and they lifted up their eyes and looked, and, behold, a company of Ishmeelites came from Gilead with their camels bearing spicery and balm and myrrh, going to carry it down to Egypt.

26. And Judah said unto his brethren, What profit is it if we slay our brother, and conceal his blood?

27. Come, and let us sell him to the Ishmeelites, and let not our hand be upon him; for he is our brother and our flesh. And his brethren were content.

28. Then there passed by Midianites merchantmen; and they drew and lifted up Joseph out of the pit, and sold Joseph to the Ishmeelites for twenty pieces of silver: and they brought Joseph into Egypt.

**Narrator**

The brothers see Joseph before he reaches them.

And before he even speaks, they conspire to kill him.

That shows how deep the hatred has become.

They call him "this dreamer."

They are not only angry at Joseph. They are angry at what his dreams seem to mean.

They say, "We shall see what will become of his dreams."

That sentence is one of the keys to the whole Joseph story.

The brothers think they can destroy the dreams by destroying Joseph.

But they cannot.

They can strip the robe.

They can throw him in a pit.

They can sell him.

They can lie to Jacob.

But they cannot cancel what God is carrying forward.

Reuben tries to stop the murder. He suggests throwing Joseph into the pit, planning to rescue him later.

That is better than murder, but it is still weak leadership. He does not confront the evil directly.

Then Joseph arrives, and they strip off the robe.

The robe was the visible sign of favor.

So they attack the symbol first.

Then they throw him into a pit.

The text says the pit was empty and there was no water in it.

That means Joseph is trapped, helpless, and exposed.

Then comes one of the coldest details.

They sit down to eat bread.

Joseph is in the pit, and they are eating.

Then traders pass by on the way to Egypt.

Judah speaks.

He says there is no profit in killing Joseph. Instead, they can sell him.

That word "profit" is chilling.

Judah turns his brother's suffering into a transaction.

Joseph is sold for twenty pieces of silver and taken toward Egypt.

From Joseph's view, everything is falling apart.

But from the larger story, Egypt is exactly where God will one day use him to preserve life.

Joseph does not know that yet.

All he knows is betrayal.

Now the brothers must face the empty pit and the lie they are about to tell.

## Genesis 37:29-36

**Scripture**

29. And Reuben returned unto the pit; and, behold, Joseph was not in the pit; and he rent his clothes.

30. And he returned unto his brethren, and said, The child is not; and I, whither shall I go?

31. And they took Joseph's coat, and killed a kid of the goats, and dipped the coat in the blood;

32. And they sent the coat of many colours, and they brought it to their father; and said, This have we found: know now whether it be thy son's coat or no.

33. And he knew it, and said, It is my son's coat; an evil beast hath devoured him; Joseph is without doubt rent in pieces.

34. And Jacob rent his clothes, and put sackcloth upon his loins, and mourned for his son many days.

35. And all his sons and all his daughters rose up to comfort him; but he refused to be comforted; and he said, For I will go down into the grave unto my son mourning. Thus his father wept for him.

36. And the Midianites sold him into Egypt unto Potiphar, an officer of Pharaoh's, and captain of the guard.

**Narrator**

Reuben returns to the pit and Joseph is gone.

He tears his clothes because the plan to rescue Joseph has collapsed.

Then the brothers create the cover story.

They take Joseph's robe, kill a young goat, dip the robe in blood, and bring it to Jacob.

This should sound familiar.

Jacob once deceived Isaac with goat skins and clothing.

Now Jacob is deceived by his sons with a goat and a garment.

Genesis is showing the painful echo of deception across generations.

The brothers do not even directly say Joseph is dead.

They let Jacob draw the conclusion.

They say, "Know now whether it be thy son's coat or no."

Jacob recognizes the robe and believes Joseph has been torn by a beast.

He mourns many days and refuses to be comforted.

The sons who created the grief now stand around trying to comfort him.

That is the weight of hidden sin.

It forces people to live in rooms full of pain they helped create.

But verse 36 gives us a split screen.

Jacob thinks Joseph is dead.

The brothers are living inside a lie.

But Joseph is alive in Egypt.

He is sold to Potiphar, an officer of Pharaoh and captain of the guard.

Genesis 37 ends in grief, but not in hopelessness.

Now Genesis does something surprising.

Instead of following Joseph immediately, it turns to Judah.

## Genesis 38:1-11

**Scripture**

1. And it came to pass at that time, that Judah went down from his brethren, and turned in to a certain Adullamite, whose name was Hirah.

2. And Judah saw there a daughter of a certain Canaanite, whose name was Shuah; and he took her, and went in unto her.

3. And she conceived, and bare a son; and he called his name Er.

4. And she conceived again, and bare a son; and she called his name Onan.

5. And she yet again conceived, and bare a son; and called his name Shelah: and he was at Chezib, when she bare him.

6. And Judah took a wife for Er his firstborn, whose name was Tamar.

7. And Er, Judah's firstborn, was wicked in the sight of the LORD; and the LORD slew him.

8. And Judah said unto Onan, Go in unto thy brother's wife, and marry her, and raise up seed to thy brother.

9. And Onan knew that the seed should not be his; and it came to pass, when he went in unto his brother's wife, that he spilled it on the ground, lest that he should give seed to his brother.

10. And the thing which he did displeased the LORD: wherefore he slew him also.

11. Then said Judah to Tamar his daughter in law, Remain a widow at thy father's house, till Shelah my son be grown: for he said, Lest peradventure he die also, as his brethren did. And Tamar went and dwelt in her father's house.

**Narrator**

Genesis 38 begins with Judah going down from his brothers.

That is more than geography.

Judah has just helped sell Joseph.

Now he separates from the family and starts building his own household.

He marries a Canaanite woman and has three sons: Er, Onan, and Shelah.

Then Tamar enters the story as the wife of Er.

Er is wicked in the sight of the Lord, and the Lord puts him to death.

Then Judah tells Onan to fulfill the duty of raising up offspring for his brother.

This is connected to what later becomes known as levirate responsibility. The idea was that a brother could preserve the dead brother's line by giving the widow a child who would carry the dead brother's name and inheritance.

That mattered because widowhood in the ancient world could leave a woman extremely vulnerable.

Tamar needed protection, future, and justice inside the family she had married into.

But Onan refuses.

He takes from Tamar while refusing to give her the future connected to the duty.

The Lord sees this as wicked.

Then Judah sends Tamar away to her father's house and tells her to wait until Shelah grows up.

But the text tells us Judah is afraid Shelah will die too.

So Tamar is left waiting.

She is trapped by a promise Judah does not really intend to keep.

Genesis is showing us Judah's failure before it shows us Judah's transformation later.

Now time passes.

## Genesis 38:12-23

**Scripture**

12. And in process of time the daughter of Shuah Judah's wife died; and Judah was comforted, and went up unto his sheepshearers to Timnath, he and his friend Hirah the Adullamite.

13. And it was told Tamar, saying, Behold thy father in law goeth up to Timnath to shear his sheep.

14. And she put her widow's garments off from her, and covered her with a vail, and wrapped herself, and sat in an open place, which is by the way to Timnath; for she saw that Shelah was grown, and she was not given unto him to wife.

15. When Judah saw her, he thought her to be an harlot; because she had covered her face.

16. And he turned unto her by the way, and said, Go to, I pray thee, let me come in unto thee; for he knew not that she was his daughter in law. And she said, What wilt thou give me, that thou mayest come in unto me?

17. And he said, I will send thee a kid from the flock. And she said, Wilt thou give me a pledge, till thou send it?

18. And he said, What pledge shall I give thee? And she said, Thy signet, and thy bracelets, and thy staff that is in thine hand. And he gave it her, and came in unto her, and she conceived by him.

19. And she arose, and went away, and laid by her vail from her, and put on the garments of her widowhood.

20. And Judah sent the kid by the hand of his friend the Adullamite, to receive his pledge from the woman's hand: but he found her not.

21. Then he asked the men of that place, saying, Where is the harlot, that was openly by the way side? And they said, There was no harlot in this place.

22. And he returned to Judah, and said, I cannot find her; and also the men of the place said, that there was no harlot in this place.

23. And Judah said, Let her take it to her, lest we be shamed: behold, I sent this kid, and thou hast not found her.

**Narrator**

Time passes.

Judah's wife dies.

Judah goes up to Timnath for sheep shearing, and Tamar hears about it.

Tamar sees the truth clearly: Shelah has grown, and Judah has still not given him to her.

That means Judah has left her in a dead-end situation.

So Tamar removes her widow's garments, covers herself with a veil, and sits by the road.

This is a morally tangled scene.

Genesis is not pretending everything here is clean.

But it is showing why Tamar acts.

She is not chasing drama. She has been denied justice by the man responsible to protect the family line.

Judah sees her and thinks she is a prostitute because her face is covered.

He does not recognize Tamar.

He offers to send a young goat from the flock.

Tamar asks for a pledge until he sends it.

And she asks for his signet, his cord, and his staff.

Those items are personal identity markers.

In modern language, it is like asking for the thing that proves exactly who he is.

Judah gives them to her, and Tamar conceives by him.

Then she leaves, removes the veil, and puts on her widow's garments again.

Judah later sends the goat and tries to recover his pledge, but Tamar cannot be found.

The men of the place say there was no prostitute there.

Judah decides to let the pledge go because he does not want public shame.

That is important.

Judah is concerned about shame, but he still has not truly faced truth.

The truth is coming.

## Genesis 38:24-30

**Scripture**

24. And it came to pass about three months after, that it was told Judah, saying, Tamar thy daughter in law hath played the harlot; and also, behold, she is with child by whoredom. And Judah said, Bring her forth, and let her be burnt.

25. When she was brought forth, she sent to her father in law, saying, By the man, whose these are, am I with child: and she said, Discern, I pray thee, whose are these, the signet, and bracelets, and staff.

26. And Judah acknowledged them, and said, She hath been more righteous than I; because that I gave her not to Shelah my son. And he knew her again no more.

27. And it came to pass in the time of her travail, that, behold, twins were in her womb.

28. And it came to pass, when she travailed, that the one put out his hand: and the midwife took and bound upon his hand a scarlet thread, saying, This came out first,

29. And it came to pass, as he drew back his hand, that, behold, his brother came out: and she said, How hast thou broken forth? this breach be upon thee: therefore his name was called Pharez.

30. And afterward came out his brother, that had the scarlet thread upon his hand: and his name was called Zarah.

**Narrator**

Three months later, Tamar is found to be pregnant.

Judah's response is severe.

He says, "Bring her forth, and let her be burnt."

That is hypocrisy.

Judah recently acted sexually with someone he thought was a prostitute, but now he is ready to condemn Tamar harshly.

Then Tamar sends the pledge.

She does not give a long speech.

She simply says she is pregnant by the man who owns these items.

Then she asks Judah to discern whose they are.

That word "discern" is powerful in the larger story.

In Genesis 37, Judah and his brothers sent Joseph's robe to Jacob and asked him to recognize it.

Now Tamar sends Judah's own items and asks him to recognize them.

The deceiver is confronted by evidence.

Judah acknowledges them.

Then he says, "She hath been more righteous than I."

That does not mean everything in the chapter is morally simple.

It means Judah finally stops defending himself.

He admits that he failed to give Tamar to Shelah.

That confession matters because Judah will later become the brother who offers himself for Benjamin in Genesis 44.

The Judah who sells Joseph is not the final Judah.

The Judah who fails Tamar is not the final Judah.

This exposure becomes part of his transformation.

Tamar gives birth to twins, Perez and Zerah.

Perez matters deeply because later the line of David will come through Perez. And in Matthew 1, Tamar is named in the genealogy of Jesus.

That means Genesis 38 is not a random scandal chapter.

It is part of the promise line.

God is working in a place that looks messy, shameful, and hidden.

## Closing

Day 15 begins Joseph's testing.

But it also shows us the family Joseph comes from.

Joseph is favored.

His brothers hate him.

The robe becomes a target.

The dreams make the hatred worse.

Joseph goes looking for his brothers and walks straight into betrayal.

They strip him.

They throw him into a pit.

They sell him.

They deceive Jacob.

And Jacob believes Joseph is dead.

But Joseph is alive in Egypt.

That is the quiet hope at the end of Genesis 37.

The family thinks the story has ended in grief, but God is still moving the story forward.

Then Genesis 38 turns to Judah.

Judah goes down from his brothers.

His household becomes broken.

Tamar is denied justice.

Judah hides from responsibility.

Then Tamar's evidence exposes him, and Judah finally tells the truth.

That matters because Judah will not stay the same.

God is not only preserving Joseph in Egypt.

God is also beginning to confront Judah at home.

If you carry one thing from Genesis 37 and 38, carry this:

God can keep His purpose alive even when people strip the coat, hide the truth, sell the innocent, and bury a family under lies.

The pit is real.

The betrayal is real.

The hypocrisy is real.

But none of it is stronger than God's hidden providence.

Tomorrow, Day 16 brings us back to Joseph in Egypt.

Joseph will enter Potiphar's house.

He will be tested by temptation.

He will do what is right and still suffer for it.

But Day 15 has already prepared us to see the pattern:

Joseph's road looks like it is going down.

But God is carrying him toward a purpose no one can see yet.
