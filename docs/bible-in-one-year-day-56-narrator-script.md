# Bible in One Year - Day 56 Narrator Script

**Reading:** Deuteronomy 34; Joshua 1-3

## Opening

Welcome to Day 56 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are reading Deuteronomy chapter 34 and Joshua chapters 1 through 3.

The theme is Moses dies and Joshua leads.

This is one of the biggest transitions in the Old Testament.

Moses has been the central leader from Egypt to the edge of the promised land.

He stood before Pharaoh.

He led Israel through the sea.

He received the law.

He interceded for the people.

He taught the covenant.

But now Moses dies.

The promise does not die with him.

Joshua is called to lead, Rahab shows faith inside Jericho, and Israel crosses the Jordan by the power of God.

Day 56 teaches that God's work is bigger than one servant.

Leaders change.

The LORD remains faithful.

## Deuteronomy 34 verses 1 through 12

**Scripture**

Moses goes up from the plains of Moab to Mount Nebo.

The LORD shows him the land promised to Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob.

Moses dies in the land of Moab.

The LORD buries him.

Israel mourns Moses for thirty days.

Joshua is full of the spirit of wisdom because Moses laid his hands on him.

The chapter says there arose not a prophet since in Israel like unto Moses, whom the LORD knew face to face.

**Narrator**

Deuteronomy 34 is tender and serious.

Moses sees the land, but he does not enter it.

The LORD shows him the promise from a mountain.

That moment is both mercy and judgment.

It is judgment because Moses cannot cross Jordan.

It is mercy because the LORD lets him see what He promised.

The land is real.

The promise is real.

Moses' longing is real.

But God's word is also real.

Moses dies in Moab, and the LORD buries him.

That detail is unusual and deeply personal.

The servant of the LORD is not abandoned at death.

God Himself cares for Moses.

Israel mourns for thirty days.

That grief is right.

Moses mattered.

He was not a disposable leader.

He had carried a heavy calling for many years.

But the story does not end with grief.

Joshua is full of the spirit of wisdom.

Moses had laid his hands on him.

The people listen to Joshua.

This teaches transition.

God honors Moses, but He also prepares Joshua.

The chapter ends by saying no prophet arose like Moses, whom the LORD knew face to face.

Moses is unique.

The signs in Egypt, the mighty hand, the great terror, the face-to-face knowledge of the LORD: all of this marks Moses as a towering servant.

Yet even Moses is not the source of the promise.

The LORD is.

That is why Joshua can begin.

The reader should feel the weight of Moses' death without thinking God's plan has failed.

Deuteronomy closes with loss.

Joshua opens with continuation.

That is the movement of Day 56.

## Joshua 1 verses 1 through 18

**Scripture**

After Moses dies, the LORD speaks to Joshua.

He tells him to arise and go over Jordan.

Every place the sole of his foot treads will be given to Israel.

The LORD commands Joshua to be strong and of a good courage.

Joshua must meditate in the book of the law day and night.

Joshua commands the officers to prepare the people.

The tribes east of Jordan promise to help their brothers cross and fight.

**Narrator**

Joshua begins after the death of Moses.

That opening matters.

Joshua is not leading in an easy moment.

He begins with grief behind him and the Jordan in front of him.

The LORD speaks first.

That is important.

Joshua's leadership begins with God's word, not Joshua's ambition.

The LORD says, Moses my servant is dead.

Now therefore arise.

That sentence is honest.

Moses is dead.

The next step is still commanded.

God does not ask Joshua to pretend there has been no loss.

He tells him to move forward because the promise remains.

The LORD promises the land.

Every place the sole of your foot shall tread upon, that have I given unto you.

The wording is strong.

The land is given by God.

Joshua must step forward in obedience, but the gift comes from the LORD.

Then comes the repeated command: be strong and of a good courage.

Joshua will need courage because leadership is heavy.

He will face rivers, walls, battles, sin in the camp, deception from enemies, and the burden of dividing the land.

But his courage is not based on self-confidence.

The LORD says, I will be with thee.

I will not fail thee, nor forsake thee.

That is the foundation.

Biblical courage is not pretending danger is small.

It is trusting that the LORD is greater than the danger.

The LORD also commands Joshua to stay close to the law.

This book of the law shall not depart out of thy mouth.

Joshua must meditate therein day and night.

He must observe to do according to all that is written.

That means Joshua is not free to lead by personality, pressure, or military instinct alone.

He must lead under Scripture.

The leader who carries Israel into the land must be governed by the word of God.

That is a major lesson.

Victory in Joshua is tied to obedience.

The chapter also shows community responsibility.

The Reubenites, Gadites, and half tribe of Manasseh already have inheritance east of Jordan.

But they must not leave their brothers alone.

They promise to cross over armed and help until the LORD gives rest to all Israel.

This teaches that inheritance does not cancel responsibility.

They have received, so they must serve.

Day 56 therefore shows courage, Scripture, leadership, and shared obedience.

Joshua is not Moses, but the LORD is still the LORD.

## Joshua 2 verses 1 through 24

**Scripture**

Joshua sends two spies secretly to Jericho.

They come to the house of Rahab.

The king of Jericho hears that spies have come.

Rahab hides the men and sends the king's messengers away.

Rahab confesses that the LORD has given Israel the land.

She remembers the Red Sea and the defeat of Sihon and Og.

She asks for kindness for herself and her family.

The spies promise safety if the scarlet cord is in the window and her family stays in the house.

**Narrator**

Joshua 2 takes us inside Jericho before the walls fall.

The surprise is Rahab.

She is a Canaanite woman inside a judged city.

Yet she has heard what the LORD has done.

She says, I know that the LORD hath given you the land.

That confession is remarkable.

Many in Israel struggled to trust God's promise when they saw strong cities.

Rahab is inside one of those cities, and she sees the truth.

She remembers the Red Sea.

She remembers Sihon and Og.

She says the hearts of the people melted.

Fear has filled Jericho.

But Rahab's fear moves toward faith.

She does not only know Israel is coming.

She confesses the LORD.

The LORD your God, he is God in heaven above, and in earth beneath.

That is strong theology from inside Jericho.

Rahab hides the spies.

Then she asks for mercy.

She asks that her father, mother, brethren, sisters, and all that they have may be delivered from death.

Her faith is not private only.

She pleads for her household.

The scarlet cord becomes the sign.

When judgment comes, the house marked by the cord will be spared.

For beginners, this is important.

Joshua is a book of conquest, but it also shows mercy.

Rahab is not saved because Jericho is harmless.

She is saved because she turns toward the LORD and receives mercy.

The spies return to Joshua and say, Truly the LORD hath delivered into our hands all the land.

Their report is the opposite of the fearful report in Numbers.

The earlier spies saw giants and spread fear.

These spies see Jericho trembling and return with confidence.

Joshua 2 therefore prepares the crossing and the conquest by showing faith in an unexpected place.

Mercy is already at work before judgment arrives.

## Joshua 3 verses 1 through 17

**Scripture**

Israel comes to the Jordan.

The officers command the people to follow the ark of the covenant.

Joshua tells the people to sanctify themselves, for the LORD will do wonders.

The priests carry the ark into the Jordan.

When their feet touch the water, the waters stop.

The priests stand firm on dry ground in the middle of Jordan until all Israel passes over.

**Narrator**

Joshua 3 brings Israel to the river.

The Jordan is not a small detail.

It stands between the people and the land.

The first generation crossed the Red Sea coming out of Egypt.

Now the new generation crosses the Jordan entering Canaan.

The ark of the covenant goes before them.

That means God's presence leads the way.

Israel does not rush ahead on its own.

They must watch the ark and follow.

Joshua tells them to sanctify themselves.

The LORD will do wonders among them.

This is not only a travel moment.

It is a holy moment.

The priests step into the water before the waters stop.

That detail matters.

Faith is shown in obedient movement.

They carry the ark toward the obstacle because the LORD has spoken.

Then the waters stand up.

The priests stand firm on dry ground.

All Israel passes over.

This is a new exodus-style moment.

God is showing that He is with Joshua as He was with Moses.

The miracle is not only about getting across a river.

It confirms God's presence with the new leader and the new generation.

Joshua is magnified in the sight of Israel, but the glory belongs to the LORD.

The people enter the land by God's power.

They do not begin with self-confidence.

They begin by following the ark through a path God opens.

Day 56 moves from Moses' death to Jordan's crossing.

That is a huge movement.

The servant dies.

The LORD remains.

Joshua is called.

Rahab believes.

The ark leads.

The waters stop.

The promise continues.

## Closing

That is Day 56.

Deuteronomy 34 and Joshua 1 through 3 teach leadership transition, courage, faith, mercy, and God's continuing promise.

Moses dies, but the LORD does not leave His people.

Joshua leads, but the LORD goes before him.

Rahab is spared because mercy reaches inside Jericho.

Israel crosses because the LORD opens the way.

The promise continues because God is faithful.

## Full Lesson Review

Before we finish, let us walk slowly through the movement of this day one more time.

Deuteronomy 34 does not treat Moses like a disposable leader.

It honors him.

The chapter lets us feel the weight of his life.

Moses climbed Nebo.

He saw the land.

He saw Gilead.

He saw Dan.

He saw Naphtali.

He saw Ephraim and Manasseh.

He saw Judah.

He saw the plain of Jericho.

The LORD let Moses see the promise with his eyes, even though Moses would not enter it with his feet.

That matters because the Bible is honest about both grace and consequence.

Moses was beloved by God.

Moses was faithful in his calling.

Moses spoke with the LORD in a way that marked him as unique.

Yet Moses also faced the consequence of his earlier disobedience.

The promised land was real.

The promise was still sure.

But Moses' own part in the journey ended before crossing Jordan.

That is not a small moment.

It teaches us that God can honor a servant and still be holy.

It teaches us that God can continue a promise without depending on one human leader forever.

It teaches us that the LORD's plan is larger than even the greatest servant.

Then the text says Moses died there in the land of Moab, according to the word of the LORD.

Even his death is held inside God's word.

Moses does not disappear from God's sight.

The LORD buries him.

Israel weeps for him.

Joshua is filled with the spirit of wisdom.

The people listen to Joshua.

So the story turns, but it does not break.

That is the important thing to hear.

The story turns.

It does not break.

Joshua 1 opens with the words, Now after the death of Moses the servant of the LORD.

The death is real.

The grief is real.

The transition is real.

But the LORD speaks again.

God does not leave Israel in silence.

He calls Joshua.

He says, Moses my servant is dead; now therefore arise.

That phrase is strong because it holds sorrow and obedience together.

God does not pretend Moses did not die.

He names it.

But God also tells Joshua to arise.

The next faithful step does not erase grief.

It obeys God inside grief.

Joshua is not told to become Moses.

He is told to lead under the same LORD.

The promise is repeated.

Every place that the sole of your foot shall tread upon, that have I given unto you.

This is not Joshua's ambition.

This is God's gift.

The land is not won because Joshua is impressive.

The land is received because the LORD is faithful.

That is why the command to be strong and of a good courage is not motivational noise.

It is covenant courage.

Joshua can be strong because the LORD will be with him.

Joshua can be courageous because the LORD will not fail him.

Joshua can lead because the LORD has already spoken.

But courage is tied to the book of the law.

The LORD tells Joshua not to let the law depart out of his mouth.

He is to meditate therein day and night.

This means Joshua's strength is not just battlefield confidence.

It is obedience shaped by God's word.

The leader of God's people must be ruled by God's instruction.

Then Joshua commands the officers.

The people must prepare victuals.

They are about to cross Jordan.

The promise is no longer only something heard in the wilderness.

It is now something they must step toward.

Joshua also speaks to Reuben, Gad, and the half tribe of Manasseh.

They already have inheritance east of Jordan, but they must still help their brothers.

That detail matters.

God's promise does not make them private consumers of blessing.

They must stand with the whole people until the LORD gives rest to their brethren.

Then the people answer Joshua.

All that thou commandest us we will do.

Whithersoever thou sendest us, we will go.

They ask that the LORD be with Joshua as He was with Moses.

So Joshua 1 gives us leadership, courage, Scripture, unity, and obedience.

Joshua 2 then takes us inside Jericho.

The next major figure is Rahab.

This is one of the most beautiful turns in the early conquest story.

Rahab lives in the city that is under judgment.

She is not an Israelite.

She is not introduced as powerful or respected.

Yet she has heard what the LORD has done.

She knows about the Red Sea.

She knows about Sihon and Og.

She knows the fear of Israel has fallen on Jericho.

Most importantly, she confesses the truth.

The LORD your God, he is God in heaven above, and in earth beneath.

That confession is the center of her story.

Rahab is not saved because Jericho is safe.

Rahab is saved because she trusts the God who is judging Jericho.

She hides the spies.

She asks for mercy.

She asks that her father, mother, brothers, sisters, and all that they have be spared.

The scarlet cord becomes a sign.

It marks the house where mercy will be found when judgment comes.

So Day 56 does not only teach courage from Joshua.

It teaches faith from Rahab.

It teaches that the LORD is feared by nations, yet merciful to those who trust Him.

It teaches that covenant mercy can reach into a doomed city and pull a family out.

Then Joshua 3 brings the people to Jordan.

They lodge before they pass over.

The officers move through the host.

They tell the people to follow the ark of the covenant.

The ark is not decoration.

The ark represents the presence and covenant rule of the LORD.

Israel is not following a random military banner.

They are following the sign of God's presence.

The priests bear the ark.

The people are told to sanctify themselves.

Tomorrow the LORD will do wonders among them.

That line prepares the heart.

Crossing Jordan is not treated as mere travel.

It is holy movement under the LORD.

The waters do not stop until the priests' feet touch the brim of the water.

That is important.

Faith moves at God's command before the way looks open.

The priests step forward.

The waters stand.

The people pass over on dry ground.

The God who opened the Red Sea now opens the Jordan.

The new generation learns that the same LORD is with them.

Joshua is magnified in the sight of Israel, but the glory belongs to God.

The crossing says that the promise is continuing.

The LORD who led through Moses now leads through Joshua.

The LORD who saved at the sea now brings them into the land.

The LORD who judged Egypt now rules over Jordan.

Day 56 therefore gives us a complete transition.

Moses dies.

Joshua is commissioned.

Rahab believes.

Israel follows the ark.

Jordan opens.

The promise moves forward.

When you listen to this lesson, do not only hear history.

Hear the shape of faithful life.

There will be endings.

There will be grief.

There will be commands that feel too large.

There will be moments when obedience requires courage.

There will be people like Rahab who seem far away but are being drawn by the fame of the LORD.

There will be rivers that cannot be crossed unless God makes a way.

And through all of it, the LORD remains faithful.

His servants change.

His word remains.

His people tremble.

His presence leads.

His mercy reaches.

His promise continues.

## Final Meditation

Let this day settle in your mind as a doorway lesson.

Israel is standing at the edge of a new season.

The wilderness has been long.

The graves have been many.

The lessons have been hard.

Now the land is close enough to see and cross into.

But the first thing the reader must learn is that the LORD is the true constant.

Moses was not the constant.

Joshua is not the constant.

The Jordan is not the constant.

Israel's confidence is not the constant.

The LORD is the constant.

That is why the death of Moses does not end the story.

That is why the fear surrounding Joshua does not cancel the call.

That is why Rahab can come under mercy from inside Jericho.

That is why the priests can step toward water with the ark before them.

The story keeps saying, look at the LORD.

Look at His word to Moses.

Look at His word to Joshua.

Look at His fame reaching Rahab.

Look at His presence before the people.

Look at His power over Jordan.

The people are not being asked to manufacture a future.

They are being asked to follow the God who has already made the promise.

This is also why Joshua's courage is so different from pride.

Pride says, I am enough.

Covenant courage says, the LORD is with me, and His word must guide me.

Pride rushes ahead.

Covenant courage meditates on the law day and night.

Pride looks for applause.

Covenant courage obeys when the next step is costly.

Joshua needs that kind of courage.

Israel needs that kind of courage.

Every reader needs to understand that kind of courage.

The chapter is not simply saying, be brave.

It is saying, be strong because the LORD has spoken.

Be courageous because the LORD is present.

Be obedient because the law of the LORD must shape the life of His people.

Rahab's story adds another layer.

The LORD's power does not only terrify enemies.

It awakens faith.

Rahab hears and believes.

She asks for mercy before Jericho falls.

She places her family under the sign she has been given.

So Day 56 teaches courage without arrogance and mercy without softness toward sin.

Jericho is still under judgment.

Rahab is still brought out by mercy.

Both are true.

Then the Jordan crossing gives the day its final picture.

The priests stand in the riverbed until all the people pass over.

The ark stays central.

The presence of the LORD is not a quick symbol at the beginning.

The presence of the LORD holds the way open.

That is the image to carry from this day.

The LORD brings His people through transition by His word, His mercy, and His presence.
