# Day 77 Narrator Script

## Opening

Welcome to Day 77 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are reading Second Samuel 4 through 7.

The title for today is David's Throne and God's Promise.

This day moves from the collapse of Saul's house to the covenant promise God gives to David.

Ishbosheth is murdered.

David refuses to reward murder done in his name.

All Israel comes to David at Hebron.

David is anointed king over Israel.

Jerusalem is captured and becomes the city of David.

The ark is brought toward Jerusalem, but Uzzah dies when he touches it.

David learns again that worship must honor God's holiness.

Then the ark is brought with joy.

Finally David wants to build a house for the LORD, but the LORD promises to build David a house.

This is one of the most important covenant moments in the Old Testament.

God promises David a throne, a kingdom, and a house that will reach beyond David's own lifetime.

## Second Samuel 4

Second Samuel 4 begins with Ishbosheth hearing that Abner is dead in Hebron.

His hands become feeble, and all the Israelites are troubled.

The death of Abner weakens Saul's remaining house.

Two men, Baanah and Rechab, captains of bands, come into Ishbosheth's house in the heat of the day while he lies on his bed at noon.

They smite him, kill him, behead him, and carry his head away.

They bring the head to David at Hebron.

They think they are bringing good news.

They say the LORD has avenged David of Saul and of his seed.

But David will not receive murder as the LORD's gift.

He reminds them what happened to the man who said he killed Saul.

That man thought he brought good tidings, but David took hold of him and slew him in Ziklag.

How much more, David says, when wicked men have slain a righteous person in his own house upon his bed?

David commands his young men, and they execute the murderers.

Then Ishbosheth's head is buried in Abner's sepulchre in Hebron.

This chapter is short, but it matters.

David refuses to build his kingdom on assassination.

He will not call wickedness useful just because it removes a rival.

## Second Samuel 5

Second Samuel 5 opens with all the tribes of Israel coming to David at Hebron.

They say, Behold, we are thy bone and thy flesh.

They remember that even when Saul was king, David led Israel out and brought them in.

They also remember the word of the LORD: Thou shalt feed my people Israel, and thou shalt be a captain over Israel.

The elders come to David.

David makes a league with them before the LORD.

They anoint David king over Israel.

David is thirty years old when he begins to reign, and he reigns forty years.

Then David goes to Jerusalem against the Jebusites.

The Jebusites mock him, saying the blind and the lame will keep him out.

But David takes the stronghold of Zion.

It becomes the city of David.

David dwells in the fort and builds round about.

The text says David went on and grew great, and the LORD God of hosts was with him.

That sentence is the key.

David's greatness is not detached from God's presence.

Hiram king of Tyre sends cedar trees, carpenters, and masons, and they build David a house.

David perceives that the LORD has established him king over Israel and exalted his kingdom for His people Israel's sake.

David understands the purpose of his throne.

It is not merely for David.

It is for God's people.

Then the Philistines hear that David has been anointed king over Israel, and they come to seek him.

David inquires of the LORD.

The LORD tells him to go up, and He will deliver the Philistines into David's hand.

David defeats them and says the LORD has broken forth upon his enemies like the breach of waters.

The Philistines come again.

David inquires again.

This time the LORD gives a different strategy: fetch a compass behind them and come upon them over against the mulberry trees.

When David hears the sound of a going in the tops of the mulberry trees, he is to bestir himself, because then the LORD will go out before him.

David does as the LORD commands.

That phrase matters.

David asks.

David listens.

David obeys.

The king's strength is shown in dependence on God.

## Second Samuel 6

Second Samuel 6 is about bringing the ark of God.

David gathers chosen men of Israel and goes to bring up the ark whose name is called by the name of the LORD of hosts that dwells between the cherubims.

They set the ark upon a new cart.

Uzzah and Ahio drive the cart.

David and all the house of Israel play before the LORD with music.

But when they come to Nachon's threshingfloor, the oxen shake the ark.

Uzzah puts forth his hand to the ark and takes hold of it.

The anger of the LORD is kindled against Uzzah, and God smites him there for his error.

Uzzah dies by the ark of God.

David is displeased and afraid.

He says, How shall the ark of the LORD come to me?

So David will not remove the ark to himself into the city of David.

The ark is carried aside into the house of Obededom the Gittite.

The LORD blesses Obededom and all his household.

When David hears that the LORD has blessed Obededom because of the ark, David brings up the ark with gladness.

This time the movement is marked by sacrifice.

When those bearing the ark have gone six paces, David sacrifices oxen and fatlings.

David dances before the LORD with all his might.

David is girded with a linen ephod.

The ark comes into the city of David with shouting and trumpet sound.

Michal, Saul's daughter, looks through a window and despises David in her heart.

They set the ark in its place in the tent David pitched for it.

David offers burnt offerings and peace offerings.

He blesses the people in the name of the LORD.

He gives food to all the people.

But Michal confronts David sarcastically.

David answers that it was before the LORD, who chose him before her father and all his house.

David says he will yet be more vile in his own sight.

The chapter ends with Michal having no child unto the day of her death.

This chapter holds joy and holiness together.

The ark is not a prop for worship excitement.

The LORD is holy.

But once David approaches with reverence, the ark's arrival becomes a day of gladness, sacrifice, blessing, and humble worship.

## Second Samuel 7

Second Samuel 7 is one of the mountain peaks of the Bible's kingdom story.

David sits in his house, and the LORD has given him rest from his enemies.

David says to Nathan the prophet that he dwells in a house of cedar, but the ark of God dwells within curtains.

Nathan first tells David to do all that is in his heart.

But that night the word of the LORD comes to Nathan.

The LORD asks whether David will build Him a house to dwell in.

God reminds David that He has not dwelt in a house since bringing Israel out of Egypt.

He has walked in a tent and tabernacle.

He never asked the tribes why they had not built Him a house of cedar.

Then God turns the promise around.

He took David from the sheepcote, from following the sheep, to be ruler over Israel.

He has been with David wherever he went.

He has cut off David's enemies.

He has made David a great name.

He will appoint a place for Israel and plant them.

He will give rest.

Then comes the central promise: the LORD tells David that He will make David a house.

When David's days are fulfilled, God will set up David's seed after him.

That son will build a house for God's name.

God will establish the throne of his kingdom forever.

God says, I will be his father, and he shall be my son.

If he commits iniquity, God will chasten him, but His mercy shall not depart from him as it departed from Saul.

David's house, kingdom, and throne shall be established forever.

This promise reaches first to Solomon.

But it also points beyond Solomon.

The promise of a forever throne prepares the way for the Son of David, the Messiah.

David goes in and sits before the LORD.

He asks, Who am I, O Lord GOD?

What is my house, that thou hast brought me hitherto?

David is humbled by grace.

He praises God's greatness.

He remembers God's redemption of Israel.

He asks God to do as He has spoken.

David's prayer is not proud.

It is full of wonder that God would make such a promise.

## Full Lesson Review

Day 77 teaches that David's kingdom is established by God's promise, not by murder, ambition, or human strength.

In chapter 4, David rejects the murder of Ishbosheth.

In chapter 5, all Israel recognizes David, and Jerusalem becomes the city of David.

In chapter 6, David learns that the ark must be approached with reverence, not carelessness.

In chapter 7, David wants to build a house for God, but God promises to build David a house.

The whole day moves from throne to worship to covenant.

David is king, but the LORD is the true giver of the kingdom.

David has a city, but the ark reminds Israel that God's presence is central.

David has a house, but God's promise is greater than David's plan.

## Key Connections

First, David refuses wicked shortcuts.

He will not call murder a blessing.

Second, David keeps inquiring of the LORD.

Even established kings still need guidance.

Third, worship requires both joy and reverence.

Uzzah's death warns against careless handling of holy things.

David's dancing shows worship with whole-hearted gladness.

Fourth, the covenant with David points forward.

The forever throne prepares the reader for the greater Son of David.

## Guided Listening Review

Day 77 should be heard as a mercy-filled correction to human ambition.

In chapter 4, two men think David's throne can be advanced by murder.

David says no.

That refusal protects the meaning of David's kingdom.

If David rewards men who murder Ishbosheth in his bed, then David's throne looks no different from the violent power games around him.

But David calls the murder wicked.

He gives Ishbosheth burial honor.

He will not build the kingdom by approving secret bloodshed.

Then all Israel comes to David.

They say David is their bone and flesh.

They remember that David led them even when Saul was king.

Most importantly, they remember the LORD's word that David would feed His people.

That phrase turns kingship into shepherding.

David is not called to consume the people.

He is called to feed them.

Jerusalem then becomes the city of David.

This matters because the kingdom gains a center.

But the story immediately moves from city to ark.

A capital city is not enough.

God's presence must be central.

Second Samuel 6 is therefore not an interruption.

It is a warning.

David can have a throne and still need to learn reverence.

The new cart may look impressive, but the ark is holy.

Uzzah's death makes the whole celebration stop.

When the ark later comes with sacrifice and joy, the reader sees the right combination.

The LORD is not approached casually.

The LORD is also not approached coldly.

True worship has reverence and gladness together.

Then chapter 7 lifts the story higher.

David wants to build for God.

God promises to build for David.

That reversal is grace.

David has a house of cedar, but God gives a house of promise.

David has a throne in Israel, but God speaks of a throne established forever.

The reader should feel the size of that promise.

It is bigger than David.

It is bigger than Solomon.

It stretches forward to the Messiah, the Son of David whose kingdom does not end.

Day 77 is about more than David becoming secure.

It is about God anchoring the hope of His people in a promise only He can fulfill.

## Chapter Takeaways

From Second Samuel 4, remember that David refuses murder disguised as loyalty.

The men who kill Ishbosheth use religious language, but David judges the act by righteousness.

Useful sin is still sin.

From Second Samuel 5, remember that David's reign is described with shepherd language.

He is called to feed God's people.

The city, the battles, and the victories must serve that calling.

From Second Samuel 6, remember that worship is not safe when it becomes careless.

The ark is holy because the LORD is holy.

David learns fear, then returns with sacrifice and joy.

The reader needs both parts.

Reverence without joy becomes cold.

Joy without reverence becomes careless.

From Second Samuel 7, remember that God's promise is larger than David's plan.

David wants to build a temple.

God promises a dynasty.

David wants to honor God with a house.

God honors David with a covenant.

That does not make David greater than God.

It makes grace greater than David's imagination.

The chapter is a foundation stone for the rest of the Bible.

When later prophets speak about David's throne, they are standing on this promise.

When the New Testament speaks of Jesus as Son of David, this promise is in the background.

So Day 77 is not only about ancient Israel's monarchy.

It is about the line of hope that leads toward Christ.

## Slow Recap

If you remember one movement from Day 77, remember this: the king is established, but God remains the builder.

David can receive the tribes.

David can capture Jerusalem.

David can defeat Philistines.

David can bring the ark with music and sacrifice.

David can desire to build a house for the LORD.

But David cannot create the covenant promise for himself.

The promise must come from God.

That keeps the whole chapter full of grace.

David is not the source of Israel's hope.

The LORD is.

David is a recipient before he is a builder.

David is blessed before he blesses.

David is taken from the sheepcote before he sits in a cedar house.

David's name is made great because God chooses to make it great.

That is why David's prayer is humble.

He does not say, I deserve this.

He says, Who am I?

The right response to covenant grace is wonder.

The ark scene also belongs in that same lesson.

God is gracious, but He is not common.

His presence is blessing, but His holiness is real.

David must learn to rejoice in a way that still trembles before the LORD.

That is the balance Day 77 gives us: joy, reverence, promise, and humble gratitude.

## Final Meditation

The throne comes before the covenant promise, but the throne does not explain itself.

Jerusalem becomes David's city, but Jerusalem is not meant to be merely a political prize.

The ark comes to Jerusalem, but the ark cannot be handled as decoration for David's success.

The LORD must define the kingdom.

The LORD must define worship.

The LORD must define David's future.

That is why David's desire to build is answered by God's promise to build.

God is not dependent on David's cedar house.

David is dependent on God's covenant word.

The day teaches us to put grace before achievement.

David's best plans are still smaller than God's promise.

His desire is good, but God's covenant is greater.

The hope of Israel rests on what God says He will do, not on what David can construct for God.

## Closing

As you finish Day 77, remember the order.

God establishes the king.

God guards His holiness.

God gives the covenant promise.

David receives more than he asked for.

He wanted to build a house for the LORD.

The LORD promised to build David a house that would carry hope far beyond David's lifetime.
