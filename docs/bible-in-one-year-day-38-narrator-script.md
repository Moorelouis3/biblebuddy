# Bible in One Year - Day 38 Narrator Script

**Reading:** Leviticus 25-27; Numbers 1

## Opening

Welcome to Day 38 of Bible in One Year.

Today we close Leviticus and begin Numbers.

We are reading Leviticus chapters 25 through 27, and Numbers chapter 1.

The theme is Jubilee, covenant, and Israel counted.

Leviticus ends by reminding us that the land, the people, the vows, the blessings, and the warnings all belong to the LORD.

Then Numbers begins by counting Israel for the journey ahead.

## Leviticus 25 verses 1 through 22

**Scripture**

The LORD speaks to Moses in Mount Sinai.

The land must keep a Sabbath unto the LORD.

For six years Israel may sow and prune, but in the seventh year the land must rest.

After seven sabbaths of years comes the fiftieth year, the Jubilee.

Liberty is proclaimed throughout the land, and every man returns to his possession and to his family.

**Narrator**

The Sabbath year teaches that the land belongs to God.

Israel may work the land, but they do not own it as if God has disappeared.

The Jubilee goes even deeper.

It protects freedom.

It protects inheritance.

It protects families from permanent loss.

Liberty is not just a nice idea here.

It is built into Israel's life because the LORD is the true owner and redeemer.

The people must learn to trust God even when obedience interrupts normal production.

Slow down over the phrase Sabbath unto the LORD.

This is not simply a farming technique.

The land rests because the land belongs to God.

Israel is being taught that even soil, harvest, labor, and income live under the LORD's rule.

For six years the people work.

They sow.

They prune.

They gather.

But in the seventh year, they stop in obedience.

That pause teaches trust.

It says, "We live because God provides, not because our hands never stop moving."

The Jubilee takes that same idea and stretches it across a whole society.

Debt does not get to become forever.

Lost family land does not get to disappear forever.

Poverty does not get to swallow a family line forever.

The word liberty means release.

In this chapter, liberty is not just a feeling.

It has a shape.

People return home.

Families recover what was slipping away.

The land reminds Israel that God is Redeemer, not just Owner.

So when the trumpet sounds in the fiftieth year, it is not merely announcing a date.

It is announcing that God built mercy into Israel's calendar.

This matters for beginners because Leviticus can feel like a book of rules.

But here the rules are protecting people from permanent ruin.

God cares about worship.

God also cares about land, debt, work, family, and the poor.

Holiness is not locked inside the tabernacle.

It reaches into the economy of the whole community.

## Leviticus 25 verses 23 through 55

**Scripture**

The LORD says the land shall not be sold forever, because the land is His.

Israel are strangers and sojourners with Him.

If a brother becomes poor, the people must not take advantage of him.

Relatives may redeem land.

Poor brothers must be helped.

Servants from Israel must not be ruled with rigor, because the children of Israel are the LORD's servants whom He brought out of Egypt.

**Narrator**

This section explains redemption in everyday life.

Redemption means buying back what was in danger of being lost.

A family member could redeem land.

A poor brother could be helped.

A servant could eventually go free.

The reason is repeated.

Israel belongs to the LORD.

They are not free to treat one another like Egypt treated them.

God's rescue from bondage must shape how they handle land, money, debt, power, and poor neighbors.

The phrase the land shall not be sold forever is very important.

Israel may sell the use of land for a time, but they cannot treat the land as if God has lost His claim on it.

The land is Mine means God remains the true owner.

That changes how people use power.

If a rich person buys land from a poor person, the rich person must remember that the land is not ultimately his kingdom.

If a poor brother falls into trouble, the community must not turn his weakness into a trap.

This is why the redeemer matters.

A redeemer is a near relative who steps in to buy back what is in danger of being lost.

The word carries rescue inside it.

It is about family responsibility.

It is about mercy becoming practical.

It is about not letting poverty erase a brother's future.

The command not to rule with rigor also points backward to Egypt.

Israel knows what harsh bondage feels like.

They were crushed under Pharaoh.

Now God says, do not become little Pharaohs to one another.

That is a powerful connection.

The rescued people must not copy the oppressor.

They must let redemption shape their treatment of servants, debtors, neighbors, and families in trouble.

## Leviticus 26 verses 1 through 13

**Scripture**

The LORD commands Israel not to make idols.

They must keep His Sabbaths and reverence His sanctuary.

If they walk in His statutes and keep His commandments, He will give rain, harvest, peace, safety, victory, fruitfulness, and His presence.

The LORD says He will walk among them and be their God, and they shall be His people.

**Narrator**

This is covenant blessing.

The blessing is not only about crops and safety.

The greatest promise is God's presence.

He says He will walk among them.

That language takes us back toward Eden.

God wants His people to live with Him.

Rain, harvest, peace, and victory are real blessings.

But they are not the center.

The center is relationship with the LORD.

The phrase I will walk among you is one of the most beautiful lines in this section.

Walking among His people means God is not distant from the camp.

He is present with them.

He is near enough to dwell among them.

That takes us back toward Eden, where God was present with humanity before sin broke fellowship.

Leviticus has spent many chapters showing how a holy God can live among a sinful people.

Sacrifices, priests, cleansing, holy days, and commands all lead toward this goal.

God wants a people who live with Him.

The blessings are real.

Rain matters.

Harvest matters.

Peace matters.

Safety matters.

But the deepest blessing is not the gift without the Giver.

The deepest blessing is the LORD Himself.

When God says I will be your God, and ye shall be my people, He is using covenant language.

He is saying, "You belong to Me, and I give Myself to you."

That is why obedience is not just rule-keeping.

Obedience guards fellowship.

It teaches Israel to live as the people of the God who dwells among them.

## Leviticus 26 verses 14 through 46

**Scripture**

The LORD warns Israel what will happen if they will not listen.

Discipline will increase if they continue in rebellion.

The land will enjoy its Sabbaths while they are in their enemies' land.

Yet if they confess their iniquity, the LORD will remember His covenant with Jacob, Isaac, Abraham, and the land.

**Narrator**

This chapter is heavy because covenant rebellion is serious.

God is not threatening randomly.

He is warning His people before they walk into ruin.

The repeated pattern is refusal to listen, then stronger discipline.

But mercy is still present.

If the people confess, God remembers His covenant.

That matters.

Israel's hope is not that they will never fail.

Their hope is that the LORD remembers His promise.

The warnings in Leviticus 26 are severe because covenant relationship is serious.

God is not describing a small mistake.

He is warning against stubborn rebellion.

The repeated idea is, if ye will not hearken.

To hearken means to listen with obedience.

Israel's danger is not that they accidentally misunderstand one small detail.

The danger is that they refuse God's voice again and again.

That is why the discipline increases.

The chapter shows a downward road.

Refusing to listen leads to harder consequences.

Harder consequences reveal the seriousness of sin.

Yet even here, the chapter does not end with God forgetting His people.

If they confess their iniquity, God remembers His covenant.

Confession means they stop defending their rebellion.

They agree with God about their sin.

They humble their uncircumcised hearts.

That phrase means the heart itself must stop being hard and resistant.

The hope is not Israel's perfect record.

The hope is the LORD's covenant faithfulness.

God remembers Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, and the land.

He disciplines, but He does not become faithless.

## Leviticus 27 verses 1 through 34

**Scripture**

Leviticus ends with vows, valuations, dedicated people, animals, houses, fields, devoted things, and tithes.

The chapter teaches how dedicated things may be valued or redeemed.

It closes by saying these are the commandments which the LORD commanded Moses for the children of Israel in Mount Sinai.

**Narrator**

This chapter can feel like a strange ending.

But it fits Leviticus.

It teaches that what is promised to the LORD must be treated honestly.

Vows are not games.

Dedicated things are not casual.

The tithe is holy unto the LORD.

Leviticus ends by reminding Israel that holiness reaches promises, property, animals, fields, money, and worship.

If it belongs to the LORD, it must be handled as holy.

Leviticus 27 may feel like a list of values, animals, fields, and payments, but the big idea is simple.

Words spoken to God matter.

If someone vows something to the LORD, it cannot be treated like casual talk.

A vow is a serious promise.

A dedicated thing is something set apart for God.

This chapter teaches Israel not to play games with devotion.

If a person says something belongs to the LORD, then it must be handled with honesty.

That is why the chapter talks about valuation.

The priest helps determine value so that dedication is not driven by emotion one moment and dishonesty the next.

The tithe is also called holy unto the LORD.

That means giving is part of holiness.

It is not merely a financial habit.

It recognizes that increase, animals, land, harvest, and life come from God.

Leviticus ends here because holiness has now touched almost everything.

Sacrifice is holy.

Priests are holy.

Food laws are holy.

The calendar is holy.

The land is holy.

Vows and gifts are holy.

The whole life of Israel is being brought under the LORD's claim.

## Numbers 1 verses 1 through 54

**Scripture**

The LORD speaks to Moses in the wilderness of Sinai, in the tabernacle of the congregation.

Israel is numbered by tribes, families, fathers' houses, and names.

The men twenty years old and upward, able to go forth to war, are counted.

The Levites are not numbered with the armies.

They are appointed over the tabernacle of testimony, its vessels, and its service.

**Narrator**

Numbers begins with a census.

That means the people are counted.

But this is not just bookkeeping.

Israel is being ordered for the journey.

The tribes are named.

The families are remembered.

The fighting men are counted.

The Levites are set apart for the tabernacle.

The people who came out of Egypt are becoming an organized camp in the wilderness.

God is preparing them to move forward with His presence at the center.

Numbers begins in the wilderness of Sinai, but the people are not wandering aimlessly yet.

They are being counted and arranged.

The phrase by their families, by the house of their fathers shows that God sees the people in ordered groups.

They are not just a mass of bodies.

They have tribes.

They have households.

They have names.

The census counts men from twenty years old and upward who are able to go forth to war.

That tells us the journey ahead will involve danger.

Israel is being prepared as a camp that can move, defend, and obey.

But the Levites are different.

They are not counted with the armies because their assignment is the tabernacle.

They guard the holy things.

They carry the pieces.

They camp around the dwelling place of God.

This keeps the center clear.

Israel's strength is not only military.

Their life depends on the presence of the LORD in their midst.

So Day 38 ends with a transition.

Leviticus taught Israel how to live near a holy God.

Numbers now shows that this holy people must move through the wilderness in order.

One more thread ties this whole day together.

God keeps teaching Israel that nothing is disconnected from Him.

The seventh year belongs to Him.

The fiftieth year belongs to Him.

The land belongs to Him.

The poor brother belongs to Him.

The servant belongs to Him.

The harvest belongs to Him.

The vow belongs to Him.

The tithe belongs to Him.

The tribes belong to Him.

Even the count of fighting men belongs under His command.

That is why this reading is not random.

It moves from ownership to order.

In Leviticus 25, God says the land is Mine.

In Leviticus 26, God says I will walk among you.

In Leviticus 27, God says what is holy must be handled as holy.

In Numbers 1, God counts the people who will travel with His presence.

For a beginner, this helps the whole day make sense.

God is not only interested in religious moments.

He is forming a complete way of life.

Israel must learn how to rest.

They must learn how to release.

They must learn how to redeem.

They must learn how to confess.

They must learn how to give.

They must learn how to be counted and ordered for the road ahead.

The wilderness journey will test whether they really trust the God who gave these commands.

Will they trust Him when land must rest?

Will they trust Him when debts must be released?

Will they trust Him when obedience feels costly?

Will they trust Him when the camp has to move?

That is why the day matters.

It is not just closing one Bible book and opening another.

It is showing that the same LORD who rescued Israel from Egypt now claims every part of their future.

He does not rescue them and then leave them to invent life on their own.

He teaches them how to live as His people.

When you hear these chapters, keep three words together.

Rest.

Release.

Readiness.

Rest appears in the Sabbath year.

The land stops producing by Israel's labor for a season, and the people learn that God can sustain them.

Release appears in the Jubilee.

People, land, debt, and family inheritance are not allowed to be trapped forever under loss.

Readiness appears in Numbers 1.

The people are counted because the wilderness road is ahead, and the camp must be prepared to move.

Those three ideas make Day 38 easier to follow.

God gives rest because His people are not slaves anymore.

God commands release because He redeemed them from bondage.

God orders readiness because His people are going somewhere with Him.

This is why Leviticus and Numbers belong together.

Leviticus does not end with abstract religion.

It ends with holy life in a real land, with real families, real poverty, real vows, and real consequences.

Numbers does not begin with boring statistics.

It begins with the redeemed people being counted for a real journey.

Every name and every number says that God is forming Israel into a people who can move with His presence.

So as the day closes, do not only remember the word census.

Remember that the God who counts His people also commands their rest, protects their inheritance, warns them against rebellion, and keeps His covenant promise.

The journey is about to continue, but the LORD has not changed.

Let us walk the big picture one more time before we close.

Leviticus 25 begins with the land resting.

That teaches Israel that work is good, but work is not God.

The field does not have to be squeezed every year as if survival depends only on human effort.

The LORD can provide.

The Sabbath year trains the people to trust Him with time, food, and future harvest.

Then the Jubilee teaches release.

If a family has lost land, the story is not over.

If a person has fallen into poverty, the story is not over.

If debt has pressed hard on a household, the story is not over.

The fiftieth year announces that God does not want permanent bondage to become normal inside His redeemed people.

That is a major Bible theme.

God brings people out of slavery, and then He commands His people not to build a society that creates new forms of slavery among brothers.

Leviticus 26 then places a choice before Israel.

If they walk in God's statutes, blessing follows.

If they refuse to listen, discipline follows.

This is covenant language.

The LORD is not bargaining like a merchant.

He is teaching His people that life with Him has a real moral shape.

Obedience leads toward life, peace, fruitfulness, and fellowship.

Rebellion leads toward loss, fear, exile, and brokenness.

But even the warning contains mercy.

If they confess, God remembers.

That line matters because the rest of the Old Testament will show Israel failing in many ways.

Their hope will never be that they were flawless.

Their hope will be that the LORD remembers His covenant.

Leviticus 27 closes the book with vows and holy things.

That may seem small after blessings and curses, but it is not small.

It teaches that ordinary promises made to God matter.

What a person dedicates must be handled honestly.

What belongs to the LORD must not be treated as casual.

Then Numbers 1 opens a new stage.

The people are counted.

The tribes are named.

The fighting men are numbered.

The Levites are set apart for the tabernacle.

This is the beginning of a journeying people.

The LORD who gave laws at Sinai is now preparing the camp to move.

So Day 38 is not merely information.

It is formation.

God is forming trust through Sabbath.

He is forming mercy through Jubilee.

He is forming obedience through covenant warning.

He is forming honesty through vows.

He is forming order through the census.

And He is forming a people who must learn to live every part of life before Him.

## Closing

That is Day 38.

Leviticus closes by teaching that the land, freedom, vows, blessing, warning, and holy things belong to the LORD.

Numbers opens by showing Israel counted and ordered for the wilderness journey.

The same God who rescued them is now arranging their life.

He owns the land.

He remembers His covenant.

He receives what is holy.

He counts His people.

And He prepares them to move.

## Extra Teaching Reflection

Before we leave Day 38, slow down and hold these chapters together.

Leviticus 25 begins with rest.

Numbers 1 begins with movement.

That order matters.

God does not first teach Israel to rush.

He teaches them to trust.

The land gets a Sabbath before the camp begins its march.

The people learn that obedience can look like stopping.

Stopping work.

Stopping accumulation.

Stopping the cycle where debt, loss, and poverty swallow a family forever.

The Sabbath year and the Jubilee both teach Israel that the LORD is not only Lord of the sanctuary.

He is Lord of time.

He is Lord of property.

He is Lord of economics.

He is Lord of freedom.

He is Lord of family inheritance.

That is why the phrase, for the land is mine, is so important.

Israel must not look at Canaan the way Egypt looked at labor.

Egypt used people.

Egypt crushed the weak.

Egypt built wealth on bondage.

But the LORD brought Israel out of Egypt so they would not become Egypt in another land.

So when a brother becomes poor, Israel is not allowed to say, that is not my problem.

When a family loses land, Israel is not allowed to say, now it is gone forever.

When a person is sold into service, Israel is not allowed to treat him like an object.

The Jubilee keeps interrupting human greed with divine mercy.

Then Leviticus 26 shows why this kind of life matters.

The blessings and warnings are not random.

They are covenant realities.

If Israel walks with the LORD, the land becomes a place of fruitfulness, peace, and fellowship.

If Israel walks contrary to the LORD, the land becomes desolate, the cities become waste, and the people are scattered.

This is not because God is unstable.

It is because covenant life cannot be separated from the holy God who gives it.

The warning is heavy.

But it is not hopeless.

The LORD says that if they confess their iniquity, He will remember His covenant.

That phrase is one of the most important doors in the chapter.

God already knows Israel will fail.

The rest of the Old Testament will prove it.

But failure will not erase God's memory.

Exile will not erase God's covenant.

Judgment will not mean the LORD has forgotten Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob.

Then Leviticus 27 closes with vows, valuations, firstborn animals, devoted things, and tithes.

That may feel like a strange ending.

But it fits.

Leviticus is teaching Israel that holy things must not be treated casually.

If a person dedicates something to the LORD, that promise has weight.

If something is already the LORD's, the person cannot pretend to give it as if it were his own.

If the tithe belongs to the LORD, then even increase from the land is received under God's claim.

So Leviticus ends with a simple truth.

God is holy.

What belongs to Him must be handled honestly.

Then Numbers begins.

The book opens in the wilderness of Sinai.

The people are not home yet.

They are rescued, but still on the road.

They have worship instructions.

They have covenant laws.

They have the tabernacle.

Now they need order for the journey.

That is why the census matters.

Numbers 1 is not a boring list to skip.

It shows that the LORD knows His people by tribe, family, name, and responsibility.

The camp is not chaos.

The people are not a nameless crowd.

Each tribe has a place.

Each family line matters.

Each leader stands with Moses.

Each fighting man is counted.

And the Levites are set apart because the tabernacle must stay central.

That is the bridge from Leviticus into Numbers.

Leviticus teaches how a holy God dwells among His people.

Numbers teaches how that people moves with a holy God in the wilderness.

So when you hear all the counting, do not only hear numbers.

Hear preparation.

Hear order.

Hear identity.

Hear a rescued people being arranged around God's presence.

Day 38 is asking a simple question.

If every part of life belongs to the LORD, what would it look like to trust Him with time, money, promises, land, freedom, family, worship, confession, and the road ahead?

Israel will struggle with that question.

So will we.

But the LORD is patient enough to teach His people one step at a time.
