# Day 90 Narrator Script

## Opening

Welcome to Day 90 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are reading Second Kings 10 through 13.

The title for today is Jehu's Reform and Israel's Decline.

This day continues the judgment on Ahab's house.

Jehu destroys Ahab's sons, Jezebel's connections, and the worshipers of Baal.

But Jehu does not depart from the sins of Jeroboam.

Judah is nearly swallowed by Athaliah's violence, but Joash is hidden and later crowned.

The temple is repaired under Jehoiada's influence.

Israel continues to suffer under Syria, and Elisha's final scenes show both prophetic power and the sadness of partial obedience.

The day teaches that removing one evil is not the same as walking wholly with the LORD.

## Second Kings 10

Second Kings 10 begins with Ahab's seventy sons in Samaria.

Jehu writes letters to the rulers, elders, and those who brought up Ahab's children.

He challenges them to set the best of Ahab's sons on the throne and fight for their master's house.

They are afraid and submit to Jehu.

Jehu then commands them to send the heads of Ahab's sons.

The judgment is brutal and complete.

Jehu says the word of the LORD spoken against Ahab's house has not fallen to the earth.

The chapter wants the reader to see fulfillment.

The judgment announced by Elijah is being carried out.

Jehu also kills the brethren of Ahaziah king of Judah when he meets them.

Then he meets Jehonadab the son of Rechab and asks whether his heart is right.

Jehonadab joins him.

Jehu gathers all the worshipers of Baal through deception, fills Baal's house, and destroys them.

He breaks down the image of Baal and the house of Baal.

So Jehu destroys Baal out of Israel.

But the chapter turns with a painful word: howbeit.

Jehu does not depart from the sins of Jeroboam, the golden calves in Bethel and Dan.

He removed Baal, but he kept false worship that served Israel's political system.

The LORD grants that Jehu's sons will sit on Israel's throne to the fourth generation, but Jehu takes no heed to walk in the law of the LORD with all his heart.

In those days, the LORD begins to cut Israel short.

## Second Kings 11

Second Kings 11 turns to Judah.

When Athaliah sees that her son Ahaziah is dead, she destroys the royal seed.

This is a terrifying moment.

The line of David seems under attack.

But Jehosheba takes Joash, the son of Ahaziah, and hides him with his nurse in the bedchamber.

He is hidden in the house of the LORD six years while Athaliah reigns.

The Davidic line survives through a hidden child.

In the seventh year, Jehoiada the priest gathers captains, guards, and runners.

He shows them the king's son.

They guard the house and bring Joash out.

They put the crown on him, give him the testimony, make him king, anoint him, clap their hands, and say, God save the king.

Athaliah hears the noise and cries, Treason, Treason.

But her own reign was the violent usurpation.

She is taken out and killed.

Jehoiada makes a covenant between the LORD and the king and the people, that they should be the LORD's people.

The people break down the house of Baal.

The city is quiet, and Joash sits on the throne.

The chapter is a rescue of David's line and a renewal of covenant worship.

## Second Kings 12

Second Kings 12 describes Joash, also called Jehoash.

He begins to reign at seven years old and does right in the sight of the LORD all the days wherein Jehoiada the priest instructs him.

That sentence matters.

Joash's faithfulness is closely tied to faithful instruction.

The high places are still not taken away, but Joash cares about repairing the house of the LORD.

At first, the priests do not repair the breaches of the house.

Joash calls Jehoiada and the priests and changes the process.

A chest is set by the altar, and the money brought into the house of the LORD is gathered there.

When enough money is collected, it is given to the workers.

The carpenters, builders, masons, and hewers repair the temple.

They deal faithfully.

This is an important detail.

Temple repair requires honest stewardship.

Worship is not only songs and sacrifices.

It includes faithful handling of resources and repair of what has been neglected.

Later, Hazael king of Syria threatens Jerusalem.

Joash takes holy things dedicated by former kings and his own hallowed things and gold from the house of the LORD and sends them to Hazael.

The chapter ends with Joash being killed by his servants.

The reign began with rescue and repair, but it ends with weakness and conspiracy.

## Second Kings 13

Second Kings 13 returns to Israel.

Jehoahaz, son of Jehu, reigns and does evil, following the sins of Jeroboam.

The anger of the LORD is kindled against Israel, and He delivers them into the hand of Hazael and Ben-hadad.

Jehoahaz seeks the LORD, and the LORD hears him because He sees Israel's oppression.

The LORD gives Israel a savior, but they still do not depart from the sins of Jeroboam.

The grove remains in Samaria.

Israel is severely weakened.

Then Joash, son of Jehoahaz, reigns and also does evil.

Elisha becomes sick with the illness from which he will die.

Joash comes down and weeps over him, saying, O my father, my father, the chariot of Israel, and the horsemen thereof.

Elisha tells him to shoot an arrow out the window.

It is the arrow of the LORD's deliverance from Syria.

Then Elisha tells him to strike the ground with arrows.

Joash strikes three times and stops.

Elisha is angry because he should have struck five or six times.

He will defeat Syria only three times.

Even near death, Elisha's prophetic word matters.

After Elisha dies, a dead man is cast into his sepulchre and revives when he touches Elisha's bones.

The chapter ends by remembering that the LORD is gracious to Israel because of His covenant with Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob.

He will not destroy them or cast them from His presence yet.

## Full Lesson Review

Day 90 shows reform, preservation, repair, decline, and mercy.

Jehu removes Baal but keeps Jeroboam's calves.

Athaliah attacks David's line, but Joash is hidden and crowned.

Jehoiada leads Judah back into covenant.

Joash repairs the temple, but his story ends painfully.

Israel remains trapped in Jeroboam's sin, yet the LORD still hears distress and remembers covenant mercy.

Elisha dies, but the God who worked through him still rules life and deliverance.

The main lesson is that partial reform is not enough.

The LORD calls His people to walk with Him fully.

## Slow Walk Through the Day

Day 90 is a lesson in the difference between zeal and whole-hearted obedience.

Jehu has zeal against Ahab's house and against Baal.

He carries out real judgment.

He destroys much that needed to be destroyed.

But the Bible does not let the reader confuse that with full faithfulness.

Jehu keeps the golden calves.

That means he removes one false worship system while preserving another.

He will not surrender the political religion that helped hold the northern kingdom together.

That is why the line says he took no heed to walk in the law of the LORD with all his heart.

The issue is the heart.

Partial reform can look impressive and still leave the deepest disobedience untouched.

Second Kings 11 then shows the LORD preserving David's line through a hidden child.

Athaliah tries to destroy the royal seed.

If the story were only human politics, the promise could seem finished.

But Joash is hidden in the house of the LORD.

The covenant line survives by God's providence through courageous people.

Jehosheba hides him.

Jehoiada organizes his coronation.

The people renew covenant.

Baal's house is broken down.

The city becomes quiet.

This chapter is a bright mercy in a violent period.

Second Kings 12 shows repair and weakness together.

Joash does right while Jehoiada instructs him.

That shows how important faithful teaching is.

The temple repair also matters.

The breaches of the house of the LORD are not just construction problems.

They show neglect in worship.

Repairing the temple means honoring the place where God's name is worshiped.

The workers deal faithfully, which reminds the listener that ordinary honesty is part of worship.

Yet Joash's later response to Hazael shows fear and compromise.

The chapter ends sadly with conspiracy.

Second Kings 13 returns to Israel's long sickness.

Jehoahaz does evil, Israel suffers, and then he seeks the LORD.

The LORD hears because He sees oppression.

That mercy is striking.

Israel has not earned rescue.

But the LORD remembers covenant and shows compassion.

Still, Israel does not depart from Jeroboam's sins.

Elisha's final scene with the arrows teaches the sadness of partial response.

Joash receives a prophetic sign of deliverance, but he stops striking too soon.

The victory will be real, but limited.

That fits the whole day.

Partial obedience leads to partial reform, partial deliverance, and continuing decline.

## Prayerful Landing

Day 90 asks the listener to examine unfinished obedience.

What has been removed?

What has been kept?

Jehu removed Baal but kept the calves.

Joash repaired the temple but did not finish as strongly as he began.

Israel sought help in distress but did not turn fully from sin.

The LORD's mercy is still present.

He preserves Joash.

He restores covenant order in Judah.

He hears Israel under oppression.

He remembers Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob.

But mercy should lead to whole-hearted return, not comfortable half-measures.

The listener should leave this day asking for a heart that does not stop at visible reform.

The LORD is worthy of the hidden places too.

He is worthy of the whole heart.

## Final Guided Recap

Before the closing, gather the lesson of partial reform.

Second Kings 10 shows Jehu carrying out judgment on Ahab's house and Baal worship.

Much of what he does fulfills the word of the LORD.

But the chapter refuses to praise him without qualification.

He keeps the calves of Jeroboam.

That means his zeal has a boundary.

He will destroy what threatens one kind of faithfulness, but he will not surrender what protects his kingdom system.

Second Kings 11 shows the LORD preserving David's line when Athaliah tries to destroy it.

Joash is hidden in the house of the LORD.

The covenant promise survives in a child no one in power is supposed to see.

Jehoiada's courage and order bring the king into public view at the right time.

Second Kings 12 shows the temple repaired.

Money is gathered.

Workers deal faithfully.

The breaches are fixed.

This is practical worship.

Yet Joash's reign still ends with weakness and conspiracy, reminding the reader that a good beginning must be guarded.

Second Kings 13 shows Israel suffering, crying out, receiving mercy, and still refusing full repentance.

The LORD hears because He is gracious.

The LORD remembers covenant.

But Israel's half-heartedness keeps bearing bitter fruit.

Elisha's final arrow sign leaves the same lesson in another form.

Stopping too soon matters.

Day 90 therefore teaches that the LORD deserves more than impressive partial obedience.

He calls for the whole heart, the whole law, and the whole way.

## Closing

As you finish Day 90, remember the word howbeit in Jehu's story.

He removed much evil, but he did not give the LORD his whole heart.

BibleBuddy is training us to notice that difference.

God is not asking for partial loyalty dressed up as success.

He calls His people to walk in His ways with the whole heart.
