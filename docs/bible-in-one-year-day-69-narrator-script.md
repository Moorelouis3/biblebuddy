# Day 69 Narrator Script

## Opening

Welcome to Day 69 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are reading First Samuel 3 through 6.

The title for today is Samuel Hears God and the Ark Is Taken.

This day is about God's word and God's holiness.

Samuel hears the LORD in a time when the word of the LORD is rare.

Israel loses a battle and treats the ark like a religious weapon.

The ark is captured by the Philistines.

But then the LORD humiliates Dagon and afflicts the Philistine cities.

The ark returns, but Israel must still learn that the LORD is holy.

So the lesson is clear.

God is not weak when His people are defeated.

God is not a symbol that people can manage.

God is the living LORD who speaks, judges, rules, and must be treated as holy.

## First Samuel 3

First Samuel 3 begins with a spiritual description.

The child Samuel ministered unto the LORD before Eli.

The word of the LORD was precious in those days.

There was no open vision.

Precious here means rare.

The chapter is showing a dark time.

Eli's eyesight is growing dim.

The lamp of God has not yet gone out.

Samuel is lying down in the house of the LORD, where the ark of God is.

Then the LORD calls Samuel.

Samuel thinks Eli is calling.

He runs to Eli.

Eli says he did not call.

This happens again.

And again.

Samuel does not yet know the LORD in the sense of recognizing His prophetic voice.

Eli finally perceives that the LORD has called the child.

He tells Samuel what to say.

Speak, LORD; for thy servant heareth.

That is one of the great responses in Scripture.

It is humble.

It is open.

It is servant-hearted.

Samuel is not telling God what to say.

He is listening.

The LORD comes and stands and calls as at other times.

Samuel answers.

But the first message Samuel receives is heavy.

The LORD says He will do a thing in Israel at which both the ears of everyone that hears it shall tingle.

He confirms judgment on Eli's house.

Eli's sons made themselves vile, and Eli restrained them not.

Samuel lies until morning.

He is afraid to tell Eli.

But Eli calls him and asks for the word.

Samuel tells him every whit.

He hides nothing.

That is the beginning of prophetic faithfulness.

A prophet must speak the LORD's word, even when it is painful.

The chapter ends with hope.

Samuel grows.

The LORD is with him.

The LORD lets none of his words fall to the ground.

All Israel from Dan even to Beersheba knows Samuel is established to be a prophet of the LORD.

The LORD appears again in Shiloh.

He reveals Himself to Samuel by the word of the LORD.

This chapter marks a turning point.

God's word is no longer rare in the same way.

Samuel becomes a faithful messenger in a corrupt priestly house.

## First Samuel 4

First Samuel 4 moves from Samuel's call to national crisis.

Israel goes out against the Philistines.

Israel is defeated.

The elders ask why the LORD has smitten them.

But instead of repentance, they decide to fetch the ark of the covenant from Shiloh.

They say it may save them out of the hand of their enemies.

That wording matters.

They are treating the ark like a battle tool.

The ark was the sign of the LORD's covenant presence.

But Israel is acting as if the object can be used without humble obedience to the LORD Himself.

Hophni and Phinehas are there with the ark.

That should trouble the reader.

These are the corrupt sons already under judgment.

When the ark comes into the camp, Israel shouts with a great shout.

The Philistines are afraid.

They know about the God who smote Egypt.

But fear of a symbol is not the same as Israel's true faith.

The battle happens.

Israel is defeated again.

Thirty thousand footmen fall.

The ark of God is taken.

Hophni and Phinehas die.

The word of judgment comes true.

When Eli hears that the ark of God is taken, he falls backward, breaks his neck, and dies.

His daughter in law goes into labor.

She names the child Ichabod, saying, The glory is departed from Israel.

The ark of God is taken.

First Samuel 4 is one of Israel's most shocking defeats.

But the chapter is not saying the LORD is weak.

It is saying the LORD will not be manipulated.

Holy things cannot be used as substitutes for repentance, obedience, and true trust.

## First Samuel 5

First Samuel 5 shows the other side of the ark's capture.

The Philistines take the ark of God and bring it into the house of Dagon.

They set it by Dagon.

From a Philistine viewpoint, this may look like victory.

Their god has defeated Israel's God.

But the next morning, Dagon is fallen upon his face to the earth before the ark of the LORD.

They set Dagon back in his place.

The next morning, Dagon is fallen again.

This time his head and hands are cut off upon the threshold.

Only the stump of Dagon is left.

The message is unmistakable.

The LORD is not defeated.

Dagon cannot stand before Him.

The idol must be picked up by its worshipers.

The living God needs no one to pick Him up.

Then the hand of the LORD is heavy upon the people of Ashdod.

They are afflicted.

The ark is moved to Gath.

The hand of the LORD is against that city too.

The ark is sent to Ekron.

The people cry out because they fear death and destruction.

The Philistines pass the ark from city to city, but they cannot escape the LORD's hand.

First Samuel 5 teaches that God can defend His own glory in enemy territory.

Israel was defeated.

The ark was captured.

But the LORD was never captured.

The LORD was never weak.

The LORD was never subject to Dagon.

He is holy in Israel and holy among the Philistines.

## First Samuel 6

First Samuel 6 shows the Philistines deciding to send the ark back.

The ark has been in the country of the Philistines seven months.

They ask their priests and diviners what they should do.

They are told not to send it empty.

They send a trespass offering.

They remember Egypt and Pharaoh.

That is important.

The fame of the LORD's judgment on Egypt still echoes among the nations.

They place the ark on a new cart with milch cows that have never been yoked.

The calves are taken home from them.

This creates a test.

If the cows go straight toward Bethshemesh, the Philistines will know the LORD has done this great evil to them.

The cows go straight.

They do not turn aside to the right hand or to the left.

The lords of the Philistines follow after them.

The ark comes to Bethshemesh.

The people are reaping wheat harvest in the valley.

They lift up their eyes and rejoice to see the ark.

The cart becomes wood for sacrifice.

The cows are offered.

There is joy.

But the chapter also includes holy fear.

Some men of Bethshemesh look into the ark.

The LORD smites them.

The people say, Who is able to stand before this holy LORD God?

That question is the right question.

The ark's return is good news, but God's holiness has not changed.

Israel cannot treat the ark lightly just because it has come home.

The LORD is not a weapon for battle.

The LORD is not an idol to be moved around.

The LORD is not a curiosity to be opened and inspected.

He is holy.

## Full Lesson Review

Day 69 teaches two truths together.

First, the LORD speaks.

Second, the LORD is holy.

Samuel learns to listen.

Israel fails to listen.

Samuel receives a hard word and speaks it faithfully.

Israel tries to use the ark without repentance.

Samuel becomes known from Dan to Beersheba as a prophet.

Hophni and Phinehas die under the judgment already spoken.

The word of the LORD stands.

The holiness of the LORD stands.

The ark story teaches that God's presence cannot be controlled by His people or captured by His enemies.

Israel cannot use the ark as magic.

The Philistines cannot place the ark under Dagon.

Dagon falls.

Philistine cities suffer.

The cows go straight home.

Bethshemesh rejoices and then trembles.

The LORD is ruling the whole story.

That is why this day matters so much.

It corrects two wrong ideas.

One wrong idea says, if God's people lose, God must be weak.

First Samuel 5 says no.

The LORD can humble idols in their own temples.

The other wrong idea says, if we have religious symbols, God must bless us.

First Samuel 4 says no.

Holy things cannot replace humble obedience.

The right response is Samuel's response.

Speak, LORD; for thy servant heareth.

The right question is Bethshemesh's question.

Who is able to stand before this holy LORD God?

Together, those lines teach reverence.

God's people must listen.

God's people must tremble.

God's people must trust the living LORD, not use Him.

## Closing

That is Day 69.

First Samuel 3 through 6 shows Samuel hearing the LORD, Israel misusing the ark, the ark being taken, Dagon falling, and the ark returning with holy fear.

The LORD's word is faithful.

The LORD's holiness is serious.

The LORD's glory cannot be managed by people or defeated by idols.

The right posture is humble listening before the holy God.

## Final Meditation

Day 69 is a lesson in reverence.

The first kind of reverence is listening reverence.

Samuel hears the LORD and must learn how to answer.

He does not begin as an expert prophet.

He is a child serving in a troubled house.

He needs Eli to help him understand that the LORD is calling.

That detail should encourage the reader.

God can begin His work in someone who is still learning how to listen.

Samuel's response is simple and deep.

Speak, LORD; for thy servant heareth.

That sentence is not only for children.

It is the posture of every faithful servant.

God speaks.

The servant listens.

God gives the word.

The servant receives it.

But the word Samuel receives is not easy.

It is judgment on Eli's house.

So the chapter also teaches that listening to God is not only listening for comfort.

Sometimes the word of the LORD is heavy.

Sometimes it exposes sin.

Sometimes it confirms judgment.

Samuel must speak every whit.

He must not edit the message because it is painful.

That is prophetic faithfulness.

The second kind of reverence is worship reverence.

Israel in chapter 4 has lost this.

They know the ark is holy.

They know it belongs to the covenant presence of the LORD.

But they bring it into battle as if the object itself can force victory.

This is not faith.

It is religious control.

They do not fall on their faces in repentance.

They do not ask how the corruption of Eli's sons has affected the nation.

They do not seek the LORD Himself.

They fetch the ark.

The difference is enormous.

Seeking the LORD is not the same as using the symbols of the LORD.

Trusting God is not the same as trusting a religious object.

This warning still matters.

People can treat religious practices, words, buildings, traditions, or symbols as if they can replace humble obedience.

First Samuel 4 says no.

The LORD will not be used that way.

Then chapter 5 gives reverence to the nations by force.

Dagon falls.

The idol's head and hands are broken.

The Philistines may not understand Israel's covenant, but they learn that the God of Israel is not captured.

He rules in Ashdod.

He rules in Gath.

He rules in Ekron.

His hand is heavy.

His glory is not dependent on Israel's military success.

That is a hard but comforting truth.

God's people may fail badly.

God's leaders may be corrupt.

God's ark may be mishandled.

But God Himself is not weak.

God Himself is not corrupt.

God Himself is not defeated.

Chapter 6 then shows that even joy must be holy.

Bethshemesh rejoices when the ark returns.

That joy is right.

But curiosity without reverence becomes deadly.

The men who look into the ark are struck.

The people ask who is able to stand before this holy LORD God.

That question belongs with Samuel's answer.

Speak, LORD; for thy servant heareth.

Who is able to stand before this holy LORD God?

Together, they teach us how to approach God.

Listen humbly.

Stand reverently.

Receive His word.

Do not manage His presence.

Do not reduce Him to a symbol.

Do not treat holiness as common.

Day 69 also prepares the reader for Samuel's role in the next chapters.

Israel needs more than military confidence.

Israel needs the word of the LORD.

Israel needs a faithful prophet.

Israel needs leaders who fear God more than they fear people.

Samuel is being raised for that work.

The lamp has not gone out.

The LORD is speaking again in Shiloh.

And even when the ark is taken, the LORD is still ruling.

That is the hope inside the severity of this day.
