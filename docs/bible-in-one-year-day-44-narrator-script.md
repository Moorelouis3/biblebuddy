# Bible in One Year - Day 44 Narrator Script

**Reading:** Numbers 22-25

## Opening

Welcome to Day 44 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are reading Numbers chapters 22 through 25.

The theme is Balaam, blessing, and compromise.

Moab wants Israel cursed.

Balak hires Balaam.

But God turns attempted curses into blessing.

Then, after outside attack fails, Israel falls into compromise at Baalpeor.

## Numbers 22 verses 1 through 21

**Scripture**

Israel camps in the plains of Moab.

Balak, king of Moab, sees what Israel has done to the Amorites and is afraid.

He sends messengers to Balaam, asking him to curse Israel.

God tells Balaam not to go with them and not to curse the people, because they are blessed.

Balak sends more honorable princes and promises great honor.

Balaam says he cannot go beyond the word of the LORD.

**Narrator**

Balak wants spiritual power against God's people.

He thinks if Balaam curses Israel, Moab can defeat them.

But God's first word is clear.

Do not curse them.

They are blessed.

That phrase matters.

Israel's blessing does not depend on Balak's opinion or Balaam's fee.

God has already spoken over His people.

No hired curse can undo the LORD's covenant purpose.

The phrase they are blessed is the anchor for this whole story.

Balak looks at Israel and sees a threat.

He sees a people too strong for him.

He sees what Israel did to the Amorites.

He wants spiritual power to weaken them before battle ever begins.

That is why he sends for Balaam.

In the ancient world, people often believed a curse could shape the outcome of conflict.

Balak wants Balaam to speak words that will damage Israel.

But the LORD's word comes first.

God says Balaam must not curse the people, because they are blessed.

That means Israel's identity is not decided by Moab's fear.

It is not decided by Balaam's reputation.

It is not decided by money, honor, or political pressure.

It is decided by the LORD's covenant promise.

This connects back to Abraham.

God promised to bless Abraham's seed and to bless those who bless him.

Balak is trying to stand against that blessing.

The story begins by showing that God's blessing cannot be purchased away by an enemy king.

That is why Balaam's answer sounds right on the surface.

He says he cannot go beyond the word of the LORD.

But the rest of the story shows his heart is not simple.

He keeps listening when he should already know God's word.

He is drawn toward honor and reward.

Bible Buddy readers should notice the danger.

Someone can use the language of obedience while still being tempted by profit.

The LORD's word is clear.

The question is whether Balaam will be content with that word.

## Numbers 22 verses 22 through 41

**Scripture**

Balaam goes, and God's anger is kindled.

The angel of the LORD stands in the way.

Balaam's donkey sees the angel and turns aside, but Balaam does not see.

The donkey speaks to Balaam.

Then the LORD opens Balaam's eyes, and he sees the angel standing with a drawn sword.

Balaam admits he has sinned.

The angel tells him to go, but only to speak the word God gives him.

**Narrator**

This scene is unforgettable because Balaam is supposed to be the spiritual expert.

But the donkey sees what Balaam cannot see.

That is the point.

Balaam has sight, but he is spiritually blind.

?? The donkey sees danger.

?? The angel blocks the way.

?? Balaam's eyes are opened.

God controls the road.

God controls the prophet.

God even opens the mouth of the donkey.

Balaam will not be allowed to use spiritual language against God's will.

The donkey scene is funny at first, but it is also deeply serious.

Balaam is supposed to be the man with spiritual insight.

Yet the donkey sees the angel of the LORD before he does.

That reversal is the point.

Balaam has a reputation, but his heart is blind.

The donkey turns aside, crushes Balaam's foot, and finally lies down because danger is in the road.

Balaam responds with anger instead of humility.

Then the LORD opens the donkey's mouth.

That detail shows God's total control over the scene.

The LORD can open a donkey's mouth.

The LORD can open Balaam's eyes.

The LORD can block the road with an angel.

The LORD can control what Balaam is allowed to speak.

The drawn sword matters too.

It shows that Balaam's path is dangerous.

He is not simply taking a harmless business trip.

His heart is moving toward a mission that could oppose God's people.

?? Balaam's eyes are opened.

This teaches that spiritual danger may be real even when a person does not recognize it.

The animal is not wiser by nature than Balaam.

God is exposing Balaam's blindness.

The angel's command is clear.

Balaam may go, but he must speak only the word God gives.

That means Balaam's mouth is under the LORD's authority.

Balak may pay.

Balaam may want honor.

But God rules the road and the message.

## Numbers 23 verses 1 through 30

**Scripture**

Balaam tells Balak to build seven altars and prepare offerings.

But when Balaam speaks, he blesses Israel instead of cursing them.

Balak is angry and takes Balaam to another place.

Balaam speaks again and says God is not a man that He should lie, neither the son of man that He should repent.

Balak tries yet another place, hoping the curse will come from there.

**Narrator**

Balak keeps changing locations.

But changing the view cannot change God's word.

Balaam can only speak what the LORD puts in his mouth.

This chapter teaches God's faithfulness.

God is not a man that He should lie.

He does not bless Israel one moment and abandon His promise the next.

Balak wants manipulation.

God gives truth.

Balak wants curse.

God gives blessing.

Numbers 23 repeats the conflict between Balak's desire and God's word.

Balak keeps hoping a different location will produce a different result.

He takes Balaam from place to place as if the problem is the view.

But the problem is not the hilltop.

The problem for Balak is that God has blessed Israel.

Balaam builds altars and offers sacrifices, but he cannot manufacture the curse Balak wants.

The phrase God is not a man, that he should lie is one of the strongest lines in this chapter.

People change.

People manipulate.

People promise and then fail.

People speak out of fear, greed, or pressure.

God is not like that.

When the LORD blesses, He does not become unstable because a king is angry.

When He speaks, He does not need to repent as if He made a mistake.

That gives the blessing weight.

Balak is frustrated because he thinks spiritual words can be controlled like a weapon.

But the LORD's word cannot be hired.

The phrase He hath blessed; and I cannot reverse it explains Balaam's limitation.

Balaam is not the source of blessing.

He cannot reverse what he did not create.

This chapter teaches beginners that God's promise is stronger than human schemes.

The enemy can change angles.

The enemy can offer money.

The enemy can demand different words.

But God's covenant faithfulness does not move.

## Numbers 24 verses 1 through 25

**Scripture**

Balaam sees that it pleases the LORD to bless Israel.

The Spirit of God comes upon him.

He sees Israel's tents and speaks blessing.

He says, How goodly are thy tents, O Jacob.

Later he speaks of a Star coming out of Jacob and a Sceptre rising out of Israel.

Balaam then speaks against other nations and returns to his place.

**Narrator**

Numbers 24 lifts the blessing higher.

Balaam sees Israel's tents and the Spirit of God comes upon him.

The blessing is not only about present protection.

It points forward.

A star will come out of Jacob.

A sceptre will rise out of Israel.

? Star

?? Sceptre

??? Tents of Jacob

This points toward kingship and victory in God's larger plan.

Balak wanted Israel weakened.

God speaks of Israel's future strength.

Numbers 24 is where the blessing rises into prophecy.

Balaam sees that it pleases the LORD to bless Israel.

That phrase matters because blessing is not accidental here.

God delights to speak good over the people He has chosen.

The Spirit of God comes upon Balaam, and Balaam sees Israel's tents spread out below.

How goodly are thy tents, O Jacob is a blessing over the camp itself.

The tents are not impressive because Israel is rich or settled in a palace.

They are beautiful because God is with His people.

The camp that looked threatening to Moab is seen from God's perspective as blessed and ordered.

Then the prophecy stretches forward.

A Star comes out of Jacob.

A Sceptre rises out of Israel.

The star points to royal hope.

The sceptre points to rule and kingship.

This is bigger than Balaam and Balak.

It points toward Israel's future kingly line.

Christians also hear this as part of the long road that leads toward Christ, the true King from Israel.

Balak wanted a curse in the present.

God gave a blessing that reaches into the future.

That is the beautiful reversal.

The enemy's attempt to harm Israel becomes the stage where God announces Israel's hope.

The nations named later in the chapter show that God is not only ruling Israel's camp.

He rules the destiny of nations.

Balaam may be a compromised messenger, but the word God puts in his mouth is still true.

God can speak His blessing even through a deeply flawed instrument.

## Numbers 25 verses 1 through 18

**Scripture**

Israel stays in Shittim, and the people begin to commit whoredom with the daughters of Moab.

They are called to the sacrifices of their gods and join themselves to Baalpeor.

The anger of the LORD is kindled against Israel.

A plague breaks out.

Phinehas, the son of Eleazar, acts with zeal for the LORD and turns away wrath.

The LORD gives him a covenant of peace.

**Narrator**

This is the tragic turn.

The curses failed.

But compromise succeeds in harming Israel from within.

The people join themselves to Baalpeor.

That phrase means more than a mistake.

It shows attachment, loyalty, and spiritual betrayal.

?? Covenant unfaithfulness

?? False worship

?? Deadly compromise

Phinehas acts with zeal for the LORD.

His action stops the plague, and God gives him a covenant of peace.

The chapter teaches that God's blessing is real, but His people must not play with idolatry.

Numbers 25 is painful because it shows a different kind of danger.

Balak could not curse Israel from the outside.

Balaam could not reverse God's blessing with his mouth.

But Israel can still be wounded by compromise from within.

The people begin to commit whoredom with the daughters of Moab.

This is sexual sin, but it is also spiritual betrayal.

The text says they were called to the sacrifices of their gods.

That means the sin moves from desire into worship.

Israel joins itself to Baalpeor.

Joined is a strong word.

It means attachment.

It means loyalty.

It means Israel is binding itself to a false god while belonging to the LORD.

That is why the LORD's anger burns.

This is not a small mistake.

?? Covenant unfaithfulness

The plague shows that idolatry brings death into the camp.

Phinehas acts with zeal for the LORD.

Zeal means he cares intensely for God's honor and covenant holiness.

His action is severe because the situation is severe.

The covenant of peace given to him shows that his zeal turned away wrath from Israel.

This chapter teaches a hard but necessary lesson.

God's blessing protects Israel from curses.

But blessing is not permission to flirt with idolatry.

The people who cannot be cursed by Balak must still guard their own hearts.

Outside opposition failed.

Inside compromise brought disaster.

Numbers 24 lifts the story into future hope with the Star and the Sceptre.

Numbers 25 then shows that the greatest danger is not always the enemy speaking curses outside the camp.

That is why this day is so important.

Israel's obedience still matters because idolatry is deadly.

Day 44 is not only about Balaam.

## Final Teaching Reflection

Day 44 is powerful because it shows two kinds of danger.

First, there is danger from outside.

Balak sees Israel and is afraid.

He sends for Balaam because he wants Israel cursed.

From the outside, Moab tries to use spiritual words against God's people.

But the LORD rules Balaam's mouth.

Balaam may want reward.

Balak may want control.

But no curse can overturn God's blessing.

Again and again, the words that come out are not what Balak wants.

Israel is blessed because God has chosen to bless them.

But then comes the second danger.

Numbers 25 shows danger from inside.

Israel joins itself to Baal-peor.

The people fall into idolatry and immorality.

The plague begins.

Phinehas acts with zeal, and the plague is stopped.

For a beginner, Day 44 teaches that God's people can be protected from outside curses and still be wounded by inward compromise.

That is a serious lesson.

Balak cannot force God to abandon Israel.

Balaam cannot speak beyond what the LORD allows.

But Israel can still be tempted to betray the covenant.

The problem is not that God's blessing is weak.

The problem is that human hearts can turn aside.

This day also teaches that God's sovereignty is bigger than human schemes.

God can turn intended curses into blessing.

God can make even a compromised prophet speak truth.

God can preserve His promise when enemies gather.

But holiness still matters.

The camp cannot treat idolatry as harmless.

The blessing of God is not permission to live carelessly.

Day 44 teaches reverence, warning, and confidence at the same time.

No outside enemy can cancel God's word.

But God's people must still guard their hearts before Him.

That is the heart of Day 44.
