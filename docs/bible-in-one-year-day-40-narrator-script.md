# Bible in One Year - Day 40 Narrator Script

**Reading:** Numbers 6-9

## Opening

Welcome to Day 40 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are reading Numbers chapters 6 through 9.

The theme is blessing, dedication, and Passover.

This reading moves from personal dedication to priestly blessing, from altar offerings to Levite service, and from Passover memory to God's guiding cloud.

Israel is learning how to live as a people blessed, ordered, cleansed, remembered, and led by the LORD.

## Numbers 6 verses 1 through 21

**Scripture**

The LORD gives instructions for the Nazarite vow.

A man or woman may separate themselves unto the LORD.

During the vow, the Nazarite avoids wine and strong drink, does not cut the hair, and does not come near a dead body.

At the end of the days of separation, offerings are brought before the LORD.

**Narrator**

Nazarite means someone specially separated.

This vow is voluntary.

It is a way for a person to set apart a season of life for the LORD.

The outward signs make the dedication visible.

No wine.

No haircut.

No contact with death.

Those details show separation, focus, and seriousness.

The vow reminds us that belonging to God is not only national.

Individual Israelites may also respond to God with special dedication.

The phrase separate themselves unto the LORD explains the Nazarite vow.

This is not separation for pride.

It is separation for devotion.

The person is setting apart a period of life to be specially marked for God.

That is why the signs are visible.

Wine and strong drink are avoided.

The hair is left uncut.

Contact with death is avoided.

Each sign teaches something about dedication.

Avoiding wine points to self-control and focus.

Uncut hair becomes a public sign that the vow is still active.

Avoiding death shows that the vow is connected to life and holiness before the LORD.

The vow could be taken by a man or a woman.

That detail matters.

This special act of dedication was not limited to priests.

Ordinary Israelites could choose a season of deeper consecration.

At the end, offerings were brought because the vow was not just personal emotion.

It was worship.

The person who separated himself or herself to the LORD came back to the sanctuary with gifts, sacrifice, and gratitude.

## Numbers 6 verses 22 through 27

**Scripture**

The LORD tells Moses to teach Aaron and his sons how to bless the children of Israel.

The blessing says, The LORD bless thee, and keep thee.

The LORD make His face shine upon thee, and be gracious unto thee.

The LORD lift up His countenance upon thee, and give thee peace.

The LORD says they shall put His name upon the children of Israel, and He will bless them.

**Narrator**

This is the priestly blessing.

It is not a wish.

It is a blessing spoken by God's command.

The LORD blesses and keeps.

He makes His face shine.

He is gracious.

He lifts up His countenance.

He gives peace.

The phrase put My name upon the children of Israel is beautiful.

God's people are marked by His name.

They live under His care, His grace, and His peace.

The priestly blessing is one of the most important blessings in the Old Testament.

It begins with the LORD bless thee, and keep thee.

To bless means God gives favor and good from Himself.

To keep means God guards and watches over His people.

Then the blessing says the LORD make His face shine upon thee.

That picture is warm and personal.

It is the opposite of God turning away in anger.

His shining face means favor, nearness, and kindness.

Be gracious unto thee means the people need mercy they have not earned.

Lift up His countenance means God looks toward His people with attention and care.

Give thee peace means more than a quiet feeling.

Peace means wholeness, well-being, and life rightly ordered under God.

The final line says the priests shall put My name upon the children of Israel.

That means the blessing marks the people as belonging to the LORD.

They do not carry their own name as the center.

They carry His.

The blessing is commanded by God, spoken by priests, and fulfilled by the LORD Himself.

## Numbers 7 verses 1 through 89

**Scripture**

When Moses finishes setting up the tabernacle, the leaders of Israel bring offerings.

Each tribe brings its offering for the dedication of the altar.

The chapter repeats the offerings tribe by tribe.

At the end, Moses enters the tabernacle and hears the voice of the LORD speaking from above the mercy seat, from between the two cherubims.

**Narrator**

Numbers 7 is long because every tribe is named.

The repetition can feel slow.

But the repetition is part of the message.

Every tribe has a place before the LORD.

Every tribe brings an offering.

Every gift is remembered.

No tribe disappears into the crowd.

The chapter ends with Moses hearing God's voice from above the mercy seat.

The altar is dedicated, the gifts are recorded, and the LORD continues to speak among His people.

Numbers 7 repeats the tribal offerings over and over.

A modern reader may wonder why the Bible does not simply summarize them.

But the repetition is part of the honor.

Each leader is named.

Each tribe is remembered.

Each gift is recorded.

Nobody is swallowed by the group.

The offerings are similar, but the people are not ignored.

That teaches us something about how God receives worship.

He is not bored by faithful obedience.

He does not need novelty in order to notice a gift.

The same silver charger, silver bowl, golden spoon, animals, and sacrifices are repeated because each tribe brings its offering before the LORD.

The altar dedication belongs to all Israel.

The chapter also connects giving with God's presence.

At the end, Moses enters the tabernacle and hears the voice of the LORD from above the mercy seat.

That is the same mercy seat connected to atonement.

The gifts are not the final center.

The center is the God who speaks from the place of mercy.

The long list leads us back to the voice of the LORD among His people.

## Numbers 8 verses 1 through 26

**Scripture**

The LORD gives instruction about the lampstand.

The seven lamps give light over against the candlestick.

Then the Levites are cleansed.

They are sprinkled, shaved, washed, and brought before the tabernacle.

The children of Israel put their hands upon the Levites, and Aaron offers them before the LORD for service.

**Narrator**

The lampstand gives light in the holy place.

Then the Levites are prepared for service.

Their cleansing shows that serving near holy things requires preparation.

The laying on of hands shows that the Levites represent Israel in tabernacle service.

They are offered to the LORD as a living gift from the people.

The chapter also gives ages for Levitical service.

God orders both the beginning and the ending of their work.

Numbers 8 begins with light.

The lamps shine over against the candlestick.

That detail reminds us that God's house is not dark.

Light belongs in the holy place.

Then the chapter moves to the Levites.

They are cleansed before they serve.

The washing, shaving, sprinkling, and offerings show that service near holy things requires preparation.

The Levites are not casual volunteers walking into sacred space on their own terms.

They are presented before the LORD.

The children of Israel lay hands on them.

That action shows identification.

The Levites represent the people in tabernacle service.

Then Aaron offers them before the LORD.

The language is striking because the Levites themselves become like a living offering.

They are given to God for service.

The chapter also gives ages for their work.

They begin at a certain time.

They stop heavy service at a certain time.

Even service has order, limits, and care.

God does not only command work.

He also orders the seasons of work.

## Numbers 9 verses 1 through 14

**Scripture**

The LORD commands Israel to keep Passover in the wilderness of Sinai.

They keep it according to all that the LORD commanded Moses.

Some men are unclean by a dead body and cannot keep Passover at the appointed time.

The LORD gives a second-month Passover for those who are unclean or on a far journey.

**Narrator**

Passover remembers rescue from Egypt.

Even in the wilderness, Israel must not forget the night God delivered them.

But this passage also shows mercy.

Some men want to keep Passover but are unclean.

They ask what they should do.

Moses brings the question to the LORD.

God provides a second-month Passover.

Holiness is not ignored.

Mercy is not ignored either.

The LORD makes a way for His people to remember redemption rightly.

The Passover section matters because Israel is now in the wilderness.

They are no longer standing in Egypt on the night of deliverance.

But they must still remember the rescue.

Passover keeps redemption in front of the people.

Every year, Israel is reminded that they were spared by God's mercy and brought out by God's power.

The men who are unclean by a dead body create an important question.

They want to keep the Passover, but they cannot do it at the appointed time without ignoring holiness.

Moses does not guess.

He brings the question to the LORD.

That detail is important.

Good leadership does not invent answers when God's word is needed.

The LORD gives a second-month Passover for those who are unclean or on a far journey.

This shows both holiness and mercy.

The appointed time matters.

Cleanliness matters.

Remembering redemption matters.

And God provides a way for people with a real barrier to still participate rightly.

The passage teaches that God does not treat holiness and mercy as enemies.

## Numbers 9 verses 15 through 23

**Scripture**

On the day the tabernacle is reared up, the cloud covers it.

At evening there is the appearance of fire until morning.

When the cloud is taken up, Israel journeys.

When the cloud rests, Israel camps.

Whether for days, months, or a longer season, Israel keeps the charge of the LORD at His commandment.

**Narrator**

This is one of the clearest pictures of guidance in the wilderness.

The cloud shows God's presence.

The fire shines at night.

Israel does not move because they feel restless.

They move when the LORD moves.

They do not stay because the place is comfortable.

They stay when the LORD stays.

That teaches trust.

Sometimes obedience means moving.

Sometimes obedience means waiting.

Either way, the people follow the presence of God.

The cloud over the tabernacle is a visible sign of God's guidance.

By day, it appears as cloud.

By night, it appears as fire.

That means Israel is not left to navigate the wilderness by human instinct alone.

The command is simple, but not always easy.

When the cloud rises, they journey.

When the cloud rests, they camp.

Sometimes the cloud stays only a short time.

Sometimes it remains many days.

Sometimes the people have to move.

Sometimes they have to wait.

Both require obedience.

Moving can be hard because it disrupts comfort.

Waiting can be hard because it tests patience.

But Numbers 9 repeats that Israel moved and camped at the commandment of the LORD.

That phrase is the key.

The cloud is not magic.

It is the sign of the LORD's command.

The people are learning to be led.

They are learning that the wilderness journey cannot be survived by impulse.

They need God's presence ahead of them, over them, and among them.

Day 40 has a beautiful flow if you step back and look at it.

It begins with personal dedication.

Then it moves to priestly blessing.

Then it records tribal offerings.

Then it prepares the Levites.

Then it remembers Passover.

Then it ends with the cloud and fire guiding the camp.

That means the day moves from the individual to the whole community, and then from worship to journey.

The Nazarite vow shows that one person can set apart a season for the LORD.

The priestly blessing shows that the whole people need God's favor, grace, and peace.

The offerings in Numbers 7 show that every tribe has a place before the altar.

The cleansing of the Levites shows that service near the holy things requires preparation.

The Passover shows that Israel must never forget redemption.

The cloud shows that the redeemed people must keep following God's presence.

This is why the reading belongs together.

Dedication without blessing would become self-effort.

Blessing without worship would become shallow.

Offerings without cleansing would ignore holiness.

Passover without guidance would remember the past but miss the present leading of God.

The cloud without Passover would guide a people who had forgotten why they were free.

God gives all of it together.

He receives dedication.

He speaks blessing.

He remembers each tribe.

He cleanses His servants.

He preserves the memory of rescue.

He leads by His presence.

For beginners, this is a helpful way to understand Numbers.

The book is not only about complaints in the wilderness.

It is also about God patiently forming a people who must learn worship, order, memory, and trust.

The camp cannot move well if it forgets who blesses it.

The camp cannot worship well if it ignores holiness.

The camp cannot remember redemption well if it treats Passover casually.

The camp cannot survive the wilderness if it moves without the cloud.

So Day 40 teaches a complete picture of life with God.

Set yourself apart for Him.

Receive blessing from Him.

Bring gifts before Him.

Serve Him with clean hands.

Remember His redemption.

Follow His presence.

That is simple enough for a child to understand, but deep enough to carry the whole journey.

Israel will soon face fear, hunger, conflict, and unbelief.

But before those stories unfold, Numbers shows us what God has already given them.

He has given blessing.

He has given worship.

He has given order.

He has given memory.

He has given guidance.

The question for Israel will be whether they trust the God who has been so faithful to lead them.

One final phrase to hold onto is at the commandment of the LORD.

That phrase appears at the end of the cloud section, but it explains the whole day.

The Nazarite vow is not self-made spirituality.

It is dedication within God's instruction.

The priestly blessing is not Aaron inventing encouraging words.

It is blessing spoken at God's command.

The tribal offerings are not random generosity.

They are brought to dedicate the altar before the LORD.

The Levites are not serving by personal ambition.

They are cleansed, presented, and assigned by God's order.

Passover is not a private memory project.

It is kept according to what the LORD commanded.

The cloud does not invite Israel to guess.

It teaches them to move and rest at the LORD's command.

That gives Day 40 a strong center.

God's people are blessed by His word, ordered by His word, cleansed by His word, and led by His word.

They are not left alone in the wilderness.

The LORD who brought them out of Egypt is still speaking, blessing, receiving, cleansing, remembering, and guiding.

That is why the day should feel encouraging.

The wilderness is real.

But so is God's presence.

Before we close Day 40, listen to how each section prepares Israel for life with God.

Numbers 6 begins with the Nazarite vow.

That teaches personal dedication.

A person may choose to set apart a season for the LORD in a visible way.

The vow has boundaries because devotion needs shape.

It is not only a feeling inside the heart.

It affects habits, appearance, and contact with death.

Then the chapter gives the priestly blessing.

This teaches that God's people do not live by dedication alone.

They need God's blessing spoken over them.

The LORD bless thee, and keep thee.

That means God gives and guards.

The LORD make His face shine upon thee.

That means His favor and kindness are turned toward His people.

The LORD give thee peace.

That means wholeness, safety, and life under His care.

Numbers 7 then slows down over the offerings of the tribes.

The repetition is not wasted space.

It honors every tribe's place before the LORD.

Every offering is remembered.

Every leader is named.

Every tribe participates in the dedication of the altar.

This teaches that worship is both personal and communal.

No tribe is invisible.

No faithful gift is forgotten.

Numbers 8 turns to the lampstand and the Levites.

The light shines in the holy place, and the servants are cleansed for holy work.

That order matters.

Light, cleansing, presentation, and service belong together.

People who serve near holy things must be prepared by God, not merely eager to help.

Numbers 9 brings Passover back into the wilderness.

Israel must remember redemption even after the night in Egypt is past.

The question from the unclean men shows a sincere desire to obey.

They do not want to be left out of remembering God's rescue.

The LORD provides a second-month Passover, showing mercy without ignoring holiness.

Finally, the cloud and fire lead the people.

This is the perfect ending for the day.

After dedication, blessing, offerings, cleansing, and Passover, Israel still needs guidance.

They cannot move themselves into the promised land by enthusiasm.

They must follow the presence of God.

When He moves, they move.

When He rests, they rest.

That pattern is simple, but it is deeply challenging.

It teaches patience when God says wait.

It teaches courage when God says move.

It teaches dependence every single day.

Day 40 is therefore not random.

It is a complete picture of a people being prepared to travel with God.

They are dedicated.

They are blessed.

They bring gifts.

They are cleansed for service.

They remember redemption.

They follow the cloud.

That is Bible Buddy's big takeaway for this lesson.

The wilderness is not empty when the LORD is present.

The road may be difficult, but God's people are not abandoned.

They are blessed, ordered, remembered, and led.

## Closing

That is Day 40.

Numbers 6 through 9 gives us dedication, blessing, offerings, Levite service, Passover, and the cloud.

The LORD blesses His people.

He receives their gifts.

He prepares His servants.

He preserves the memory of redemption.

And He guides the camp step by step.

The wilderness journey will not be easy.

But Israel is not alone.

The LORD is with them, and His presence leads the way.

## Final Teaching Reflection

Before we close Day 40, hold the whole movement together.

Numbers 6 begins with separation.

Numbers 6 also gives blessing.

Numbers 7 shows leaders bringing offerings.

Numbers 8 sets apart the Levites.

Numbers 9 returns to Passover and then shows the cloud leading the camp.

That is not random.

The day is showing what a prepared people need before the journey continues.

They need consecration.

They need blessing.

They need worship.

They need servants for the tabernacle.

They need memory of rescue.

They need guidance from the LORD.

The Nazarite vow teaches that devotion can touch ordinary things like hair, grapes, mourning, and time.

The priestly blessing teaches that Israel cannot bless itself.

The offerings teach that worship is costly and ordered.

The Levites teach that service near holy things is a gift and a responsibility.

The Passover teaches that the people must not forget the night God brought them out of Egypt.

The cloud teaches that guidance is not a theory.

When the cloud moves, Israel moves.

When the cloud stays, Israel stays.

For a beginner, Day 40 helps answer a simple question.

How does God's rescued people keep walking with Him?

They do not walk by impulse.

They do not walk by ambition.

They do not walk by copying Egypt.

They walk by blessing, worship, service, remembrance, and the visible guidance of God.

That makes this reading deeply comforting.

The wilderness is still dangerous.

The people are still weak.

Complaints will come soon.

But before those failures appear, the LORD gives His people everything they need to walk with Him.

He blesses them.

He receives their offerings.

He orders their servants.

He reminds them of redemption.

He leads them by His presence.

That is the heart of Day 40.

One last thread ties the day together.

The LORD does not only command Israel from far away.

He blesses them with His name.

He receives their worship.

He sets apart servants for the holy place.

He makes room for people who missed Passover but still long to obey.

He leads by the cloud so the people do not have to invent their own path.

That is a deeply personal picture of guidance.

God's people still have to obey.

They still have to wait.

They still have to move when He says move.

But they are not abandoned to guesswork.

The same God who saved them is the God who blesses them, orders them, feeds their memory, and leads them forward.

That is why Day 40 feels like preparation.

The road ahead will test Israel.

But the LORD has already shown them how to walk.
