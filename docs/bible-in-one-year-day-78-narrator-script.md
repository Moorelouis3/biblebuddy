# Day 78 Narrator Script

## Opening

Welcome to Day 78 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are reading Second Samuel 8 through 11.

The title for today is David's Victories and David's Sin.

This day begins with David strong, victorious, and kind.

He defeats enemies.

He reigns with justice.

He shows covenant kindness to Mephibosheth, Jonathan's son.

He defeats the Ammonites and Syrians.

But then Second Samuel 11 tells one of the most sobering stories in Scripture.

David sees Bathsheba.

David takes her.

David tries to cover his sin.

David arranges Uriah's death.

The man after God's own heart falls into adultery, deceit, and bloodguilt.

This day must be read carefully.

The Bible does not hide David's sin.

Grace does not mean pretending God's servants never fall.

The chapter ends with this sentence: the thing that David had done displeased the LORD.

## Second Samuel 8

Second Samuel 8 summarizes David's victories.

David smites the Philistines and subdues them.

He smites Moab.

He defeats Hadadezer.

The Syrians come to help Hadadezer, and David slays thousands of Syrians.

David puts garrisons in Syria of Damascus, and the Syrians become servants to David.

The chapter repeatedly says the LORD preserves David wherever he goes.

That sentence keeps the reader from treating David's victories as mere military genius.

David is strong because the LORD is preserving him.

David takes shields of gold and brings them to Jerusalem.

He takes brass in great abundance.

Toi king of Hamath sends Joram to salute David and bless him because David fought against Hadadezer.

David dedicates the silver, gold, and brass to the LORD, along with spoil from the nations.

David makes himself a name after smiting the Syrians.

He reigns over all Israel.

The text says David executes judgment and justice unto all his people.

That phrase matters.

The king is not only a warrior.

He must rule justly.

Joab is over the host.

Jehoshaphat is recorder.

Zadok and Ahimelech are priests.

Seraiah is scribe.

Benaiah is over the Cherethites and Pelethites.

David's sons are chief rulers.

The kingdom looks strong and ordered.

But strength in chapter 8 does not make David immune to sin in chapter 11.

That contrast is part of the warning.

## Second Samuel 9

Second Samuel 9 is one of the tenderest chapters in David's reign.

David asks, Is there yet any that is left of the house of Saul, that I may show him kindness for Jonathan's sake?

This is covenant memory.

David remembers Jonathan.

He does not use Saul's fallen house as a target.

He asks how he can show kindness.

Ziba, a servant from Saul's house, is brought to David.

Ziba tells David that Jonathan has a son who is lame on his feet.

His name is Mephibosheth.

David sends and fetches him from Lo-debar.

Mephibosheth comes, falls on his face, and does reverence.

David says, Fear not.

He promises to show kindness for Jonathan's sake.

He restores Saul's land to Mephibosheth.

He tells him he will eat bread at David's table continually.

Mephibosheth bows and asks, What is thy servant, that thou shouldest look upon such a dead dog as I am?

His words show humility and fear.

He knows he has no claim to royal favor.

David calls Ziba and orders him, his sons, and his servants to work the land for Mephibosheth.

But Mephibosheth will eat at David's table as one of the king's sons.

The chapter ends with Mephibosheth dwelling in Jerusalem and eating continually at the king's table.

He is lame on both his feet.

The detail is repeated so the reader remembers his vulnerability.

David's kindness is not sentimental.

It restores, feeds, protects, and honors.

## Second Samuel 10

Second Samuel 10 begins when the king of the children of Ammon dies, and Hanun his son reigns in his stead.

David says he will show kindness to Hanun as Hanun's father showed kindness to him.

David sends servants to comfort Hanun concerning his father.

But the princes of Ammon suspect David's motives.

They ask whether David truly honors Hanun's father, or whether he sent servants to search the city and overthrow it.

Hanun takes David's servants, shaves off half their beards, cuts off their garments in the middle, and sends them away.

This is public humiliation.

David hears and tells the men to stay at Jericho until their beards grow, then return.

David cares for the shame done to his servants.

The Ammonites realize they stink before David.

They hire Syrians for battle.

Joab sees the battle is against him before and behind.

He chooses men to face the Syrians and puts the rest under Abishai against Ammon.

Joab says, Be of good courage, and let us play the men for our people and for the cities of our God.

The LORD do that which seemeth Him good.

That is a strong battle confession.

They must act courageously, but the outcome belongs to the LORD.

The Syrians flee before Joab.

The Ammonites flee before Abishai.

Later the Syrians gather again, and David himself leads Israel.

David slays the Syrians, and they make peace with Israel and serve them.

The chapter shows another victory, but it also sets up the next chapter.

In chapter 11, when kings go forth to battle, David stays behind.

## Second Samuel 11

Second Samuel 11 begins with timing.

At the time when kings go forth to battle, David sends Joab, his servants, and all Israel.

They destroy the children of Ammon and besiege Rabbah.

But David tarries still at Jerusalem.

That sentence is quiet but dangerous.

The king who has led battles is now absent from the field.

One evening David rises from his bed and walks upon the roof of the king's house.

From the roof he sees a woman washing herself.

The woman is very beautiful to look upon.

David sends and inquires after the woman.

He is told, Is not this Bathsheba, the daughter of Eliam, the wife of Uriah the Hittite?

That answer gives David boundaries.

She is named.

She is someone's daughter.

She is someone's wife.

David sends messengers and takes her.

She comes in unto him, and he lies with her.

Then she returns to her house.

The woman conceives and sends word to David: I am with child.

David now begins covering sin instead of confessing it.

He sends for Uriah from the battle.

He asks about Joab, the people, and the war.

Then he tells Uriah to go down to his house.

David wants Uriah to sleep at home so the pregnancy can be hidden.

But Uriah sleeps at the door of the king's house with the servants and does not go down.

David asks why.

Uriah says the ark, Israel, Judah, Joab, and the servants are in tents and open fields.

How can he go to his house, eat, drink, and lie with his wife?

Uriah's integrity exposes David.

The Hittite soldier is acting with more covenant loyalty than Israel's king.

David tries again.

He makes Uriah drunk.

Still Uriah does not go down to his house.

In the morning, David writes a letter to Joab and sends it by Uriah's hand.

The letter says to set Uriah in the forefront of the hottest battle and retire from him so he may be smitten and die.

Uriah carries his own death warrant.

Joab obeys the strategy.

Uriah dies.

Other servants die with him.

Joab sends news to David and prepares the messenger in case David is angry about the battle tactics.

When David hears that Uriah is dead, he tells the messenger to say to Joab, Let not this thing displease thee, for the sword devours one as well as another.

That sentence is cold.

David speaks as if Uriah's death is ordinary war loss.

Bathsheba mourns for her husband.

When the mourning is past, David sends and fetches her to his house.

She becomes his wife and bears him a son.

Then the chapter ends with the truth David cannot cover:

The thing that David had done displeased the LORD.

## Full Lesson Review

Day 78 is deliberately shocking.

The first chapters show David strong and kind.

The LORD preserves David.

David rules with justice.

David shows kindness to Mephibosheth.

David's army wins battles.

Then David sins terribly.

The warning is not that earlier goodness was fake.

The warning is that real past faithfulness does not make a person safe from present temptation.

David sees.

David sends.

David takes.

David covers.

David kills.

And the LORD sees.

## Key Connections

First, covenant kindness in chapter 9 shows what David's power should do.

It should protect the vulnerable and keep promises.

Second, Uriah's loyalty in chapter 11 exposes David's failure.

Uriah will not enjoy comfort while the ark and army are in the field.

David abuses comfort while others fight.

Third, hidden sin is never hidden from the LORD.

David can manage messages, servants, and military orders, but he cannot manage God's sight.

## Guided Listening Review

Day 78 needs to be heard with both gratitude and warning.

Do not skip the good chapters before David's sin.

Second Samuel 8 shows the LORD preserving David.

Second Samuel 9 shows David using power to show kindness.

Second Samuel 10 shows courage in battle and trust that the LORD will do what seems good to Him.

Those chapters are real.

David's sin in chapter 11 does not mean every earlier act was false.

But those earlier chapters make the fall even more sobering.

A person can be used by God and still need to guard the heart.

A person can show kindness one day and abuse power another day.

A person can speak of the LORD in battle and then act as if the LORD does not see in private.

That is why the pacing of chapter 11 matters.

David sees.

David sends.

David inquires.

David sends again.

David takes.

Sin moves through decisions.

The text does not present David as accidentally swept away without agency.

The warning signs are clear.

Bathsheba is the wife of Uriah.

Uriah is one of David's loyal fighting men.

The army is in the field.

The king is at home.

When Bathsheba sends word that she is with child, David chooses cover instead of confession.

Uriah's conduct is one of the strongest contrasts in the chapter.

He will not go home to his wife while the ark and the army remain in tents.

David, who should be the model of covenant loyalty, is exposed by the loyalty of a Hittite.

Then David's cover becomes murder.

The faithful man carries the command for his own death.

Joab obeys.

Other men die too.

Sin spreads beyond the original act.

By the end, David can bring Bathsheba into his house, but he cannot make the sin disappear.

The LORD is displeased.

That final line is mercy because it tells the truth.

If God did not speak, David's cover might appear successful.

But God's sight is the real court of judgment.

## Chapter Takeaways

From Second Samuel 8, remember that victory belongs to the LORD.

The chapter says the LORD preserves David wherever he goes.

That keeps military success from becoming self-glory.

From Second Samuel 9, remember that covenant kindness becomes practical mercy.

David does not only say he loved Jonathan.

He seeks Jonathan's remaining son.

He restores land.

He gives Mephibosheth a place at the king's table.

Grace has a seat, a table, a household, and provision.

From Second Samuel 10, remember that suspicion can turn kindness into conflict.

David sends comfort.

Hanun's counselors reinterpret comfort as threat.

Their fear and pride create shame and war.

From Second Samuel 11, remember that sin often begins before the most visible act.

David is not where kings normally are.

He sees and keeps looking.

He inquires and ignores the boundary.

He sends and takes.

Then he covers and kills.

This chapter warns the reader not to treat temptation as harmless because nothing public has happened yet.

The private movement of the heart matters.

The misuse of power matters.

The attempt to cover sin matters.

And the LORD's displeasure matters most of all.

The chapter ends without Nathan's confrontation because the reader needs to sit inside the exposure.

David has hidden the matter from people.

He has not hidden it from God.

## Slow Recap

If you remember one movement from Day 78, remember this: the same king who shows covenant kindness can still fall when he stops walking carefully.

That is why this day must not be flattened.

David is not only the villain of chapter 11.

He is also the king who showed kindness to Mephibosheth.

He is also the ruler whom the LORD preserved.

He is also the man through whom Israel received victories.

But none of that makes chapter 11 less evil.

The Bible is honest enough to hold the whole truth together.

Past obedience does not excuse present sin.

Past kindness does not cancel present abuse.

Past victories do not make a person untouchable.

The reader should also notice how power works in the chapter.

David sends servants.

David sends for Bathsheba.

David sends for Uriah.

David sends a letter to Joab.

David keeps moving other people with royal authority.

But he is not using authority to feed God's people.

He is using authority to take, hide, and destroy.

That is why the contrast with chapter 9 is so sharp.

In chapter 9, power lifts up a vulnerable man and brings him to the table.

In chapter 11, power takes another man's wife and sends that man to death.

Power reveals the heart.

The LORD sees both uses of power.

The final sentence is therefore the mercy of truth.

David may be king, but he is not judge over God.

The LORD weighs the king.

The LORD is displeased.

The next step must come from God's word.

## Final Meditation

Second Samuel 11 should be read without softening the verbs.

David sends.

David takes.

David lies.

David writes.

David kills by another man's sword.

The Bible does not protect David from the truth.

That honesty is part of Scripture's mercy to us.

If David's sin were hidden by the narrator, readers might think God's chosen servants are protected by reputation.

But the LORD exposes what is evil, even when the sinner is the king.

No title, calling, past usefulness, or public worship makes sin safe.

The Bible is not propaganda for David.

It is revelation from God.

So let the chapter make you sober, watchful, and grateful that the LORD sees what people try to bury.

## Closing

As you finish Day 78, do not rush past the grief of Second Samuel 11.

The Bible tells the truth about David because God tells the truth about sin.

The next day will bring Nathan's confrontation.

For now, sit with the final sentence.

The thing David had done displeased the LORD.
