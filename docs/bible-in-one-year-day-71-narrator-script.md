# Day 71 Narrator Script

## Opening

Welcome to Day 71 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are reading First Samuel 11 through 14.

The title for today is Saul's Rise and Early Failure.

This day begins with Saul looking like the king Israel hoped for.

He rescues Jabeshgilead.

He shows mercy after victory.

The kingdom is renewed before the LORD at Gilgal.

But the same reading also shows the crack in Saul's heart.

When pressure rises, Saul does not wait well.

When the army scatters, Saul reaches for a sacrifice he was not authorized to offer.

When the battle turns, Jonathan trusts the LORD, but Saul burdens the people with a rash oath.

So Day 71 is not simple.

It is not only a victory story.

It is not only a failure story.

It teaches us how quickly public success can hide private weakness.

It also teaches us that the LORD can save by many or by few.

The true question is not whether Saul can win a battle.

The deeper question is whether Saul will listen to the LORD when obedience is costly, slow, and humbling.

## First Samuel 11

First Samuel 11 opens with danger.

Nahash the Ammonite comes up and encamps against Jabeshgilead.

The men of Jabesh ask for a covenant.

They are not asking from strength.

They are asking because they feel trapped.

Nahash answers with cruelty.

He says he will make a covenant with them on one condition: that he may thrust out all their right eyes and lay it for a reproach upon all Israel.

That demand is more than military conquest.

It is humiliation.

It is shame.

It is meant to make Israel look helpless.

It would damage the men physically and dishonor the nation publicly.

The elders of Jabesh ask for seven days so they can send messengers through Israel.

If no one saves them, they will come out to Nahash.

That is how desperate they are.

The messengers come to Gibeah of Saul and tell the news.

All the people lift up their voices and weep.

Then Saul comes after the herd out of the field.

This detail matters.

Saul has been chosen king, but he is still connected to ordinary life.

He asks why the people are weeping.

When he hears the words of the men of Jabesh, the Spirit of God comes upon Saul, and his anger is kindled greatly.

This is Saul's best early moment.

The anger is not selfish rage.

It is stirred by the humiliation of God's people.

Saul takes a yoke of oxen, cuts them in pieces, and sends the pieces through Israel by messengers.

He warns that the oxen of anyone who does not come after Saul and Samuel will be treated this way.

The fear of the LORD falls on the people, and they come out with one consent.

Notice the wording.

It is not only fear of Saul.

The fear of the LORD falls on them.

The rescue is being framed under God's authority.

Saul numbers the people.

He sends word to Jabeshgilead that they will have help by the time the sun is hot.

The people of Jabesh tell Nahash they will come out tomorrow, but Saul divides the army into three companies and attacks in the morning watch.

The Ammonites are scattered.

The rescue is complete.

The people then ask Samuel, Who said, Shall Saul reign over us?

They want the doubters put to death.

But Saul says no one will be put to death that day, because the LORD has wrought salvation in Israel.

That sentence is beautiful.

Saul does not use victory as a chance for revenge.

He gives the credit to the LORD.

Samuel then calls the people to Gilgal.

There they make Saul king before the LORD.

They offer sacrifices of peace offerings.

Saul and all the men of Israel rejoice greatly.

The chapter ends with rescue, worship, mercy, and joy.

If First Samuel 11 were the only chapter we had about Saul, we might expect a strong future.

But the Bible is honest.

A good beginning does not guarantee a faithful heart.

## First Samuel 12

First Samuel 12 is Samuel's address to Israel.

Samuel says he has hearkened to their voice and made a king over them.

He is old and grayheaded.

His sons are with them.

Then he asks the people to witness against him before the LORD and before His anointed.

Whose ox has he taken?

Whose ass has he taken?

Whom has he defrauded?

Whom has he oppressed?

From whose hand has he received any bribe to blind his eyes?

Samuel is not bragging.

He is showing the people what faithful leadership looks like.

He can stand before them with clean hands.

The people answer that he has not defrauded them or oppressed them.

Samuel says the LORD is witness.

Then Samuel turns from his integrity to God's faithfulness.

He reminds them that the LORD advanced Moses and Aaron and brought their fathers out of Egypt.

He tells them to stand still so he may reason with them before the LORD of all the righteous acts of the LORD.

Samuel walks them through Israel's repeated pattern.

The fathers forgot the LORD.

The LORD sold them into the hand of enemies.

They cried to the LORD.

They confessed that they had sinned because they forsook the LORD and served Baalim and Ashtaroth.

The LORD sent deliverers.

He delivered them out of the hand of their enemies.

Again and again, Israel's story is a story of need, sin, mercy, and rescue.

Then Samuel names the recent issue.

When Nahash came against them, they said, Nay; but a king shall reign over us.

Yet the LORD their God was their king.

This is the heart of the warning.

Israel has received a king, but they must not forget the true King.

Samuel says that if they fear the LORD, serve Him, obey His voice, and do not rebel against the commandment of the LORD, then both they and their king will continue following the LORD.

But if they do wickedly, they and their king will be consumed.

Then Samuel gives a sign.

It is wheat harvest.

He calls upon the LORD.

The LORD sends thunder and rain.

The people greatly fear the LORD and Samuel.

They ask Samuel to pray for them, because they have added to all their sins this evil, to ask for a king.

Samuel does not tell them to despair.

He says, Fear not.

They have done all this wickedness, yet they must not turn aside from following the LORD.

They must serve the LORD with all their heart.

They must not go after vain things which cannot profit nor deliver.

The LORD will not forsake His people for His great name's sake.

Samuel also says it would be sin for him to cease praying for them.

He will teach them the good and right way.

The chapter ends with a serious call: fear the LORD, serve Him in truth with all your heart, and consider how great things He has done for you.

Samuel's warning is firm, but it is not hopeless.

The people have sinned.

The king has been given.

Now the question is whether king and people will walk under the LORD.

## First Samuel 13

First Samuel 13 shows the first major failure in Saul's reign.

Jonathan smites the garrison of the Philistines in Geba.

The Philistines hear of it.

Saul blows the trumpet throughout the land, saying, Let the Hebrews hear.

Israel gathers after Saul to Gilgal.

The Philistines gather to fight with Israel.

They have chariots, horsemen, and people as the sand by the sea in multitude.

The men of Israel see that they are in a strait.

The people are distressed.

They hide themselves in caves, thickets, rocks, high places, and pits.

Some cross Jordan.

Saul is still in Gilgal, and all the people follow him trembling.

This is the pressure point.

Saul tarries seven days according to the set time Samuel had appointed.

But Samuel does not come to Gilgal, and the people are scattered from Saul.

Saul says, Bring hither a burnt offering to me, and peace offerings.

He offers the burnt offering.

As soon as he finishes, Samuel comes.

That timing is painful.

Saul waited, but he did not wait faithfully.

He waited until obedience felt too risky.

Samuel asks, What hast thou done?

Saul explains the pressure.

The people were scattered.

Samuel did not come within the days appointed.

The Philistines gathered.

Saul says he forced himself therefore and offered a burnt offering.

But Samuel says, Thou hast done foolishly.

Saul has not kept the commandment of the LORD.

If he had, the LORD would have established his kingdom upon Israel for ever.

But now his kingdom shall not continue.

The LORD has sought Him a man after His own heart.

That phrase points forward to David.

It does not mean David will never sin.

It means God is seeking a king whose heart is shaped by reverence, repentance, and trust, not by panic and image.

The chapter then shows Israel's weakness.

Saul has about six hundred men.

There is no smith found throughout all the land of Israel, because the Philistines do not want the Hebrews to make swords or spears.

The Israelites must go down to the Philistines to sharpen agricultural tools.

On the day of battle, there is neither sword nor spear found in the hand of any of the people with Saul and Jonathan.

Only Saul and Jonathan have weapons.

This detail helps us feel how impossible the situation looks.

Israel is outnumbered.

Israel is under-equipped.

Israel is afraid.

But the point is not that Saul needed to invent his own way.

The point is that Saul needed to trust the LORD.

## First Samuel 14

First Samuel 14 contrasts Saul's fearful leadership with Jonathan's faith.

Jonathan says to the young man who bears his armor, Come, and let us go over to the Philistines' garrison.

He does not tell his father.

Saul is sitting under a pomegranate tree.

The people do not know Jonathan is gone.

Jonathan and his armor bearer pass between two sharp rocks.

The path is difficult.

The odds are not in their favor.

But Jonathan says, It may be that the LORD will work for us, for there is no restraint to the LORD to save by many or by few.

That is one of the clearest faith statements in these chapters.

Jonathan is not pretending he controls the outcome.

He says, It may be.

But he knows the LORD is not limited by numbers.

His armor bearer answers with loyalty: Do all that is in thine heart.

Jonathan sets a sign.

If the Philistines tell them to wait, they will stand still.

If the Philistines say, Come up unto us, they will go up, because the LORD has delivered them into Israel's hand.

The Philistines mock them and call them up.

Jonathan climbs on his hands and feet, and his armor bearer follows.

Jonathan strikes them, and his armor bearer slays after him.

Then trembling falls on the host.

The earth quakes.

It is a very great trembling.

Saul's watchmen see the multitude melting away.

Saul asks who has gone from them, and Jonathan and his armor bearer are missing.

The battle grows.

Every man's sword is against his fellow.

The LORD saves Israel that day.

Again the Bible tells us where salvation comes from.

Not from Saul's control.

Not from Israel's numbers.

Not from impressive weapons.

The LORD saves.

But Saul complicates the victory.

He adjures the people with an oath, saying, Cursed be the man that eateth any food until evening, that I may be avenged on mine enemies.

The people are faint.

They pass through a wood, and honey is on the ground.

No one eats because they fear the oath.

Jonathan has not heard his father charge the people.

He puts the end of his rod into the honeycomb, eats, and his eyes are enlightened.

When he is told about the oath, he says his father has troubled the land.

The people would have been stronger if they had eaten.

After the battle, the faint people fly upon the spoil and eat with the blood.

Saul is told that the people sin against the LORD by eating with blood.

He builds an altar to the LORD.

Then he wants to pursue the Philistines by night.

The priest says, Let us draw near hither unto God.

Saul asks counsel of God, but God does not answer him that day.

Saul concludes there is sin in the camp.

The lot falls on Jonathan.

Jonathan admits he tasted a little honey.

Saul says Jonathan must surely die.

But the people rescue Jonathan.

They say, Shall Jonathan die, who hath wrought this great salvation in Israel?

God forbid.

As the LORD liveth, there shall not one hair of his head fall to the ground, for he hath wrought with God this day.

The people rescue Jonathan.

That ending exposes Saul's foolishness.

Jonathan trusted the LORD and brought deliverance.

Saul's oath nearly destroyed the very son through whom God worked.

The chapter closes by summarizing Saul's wars and his family.

There is strength in Saul's reign.

There is real conflict.

There are victories.

But there is also a deep problem in how Saul listens, waits, leads, and responds to God.

## Full Lesson Review

Day 71 teaches us to read Saul carefully.

The Bible does not flatten him into one moment.

In chapter 11, Saul looks courageous, merciful, and God-empowered.

He rescues Jabeshgilead.

He refuses revenge.

He gives credit to the LORD.

In chapter 12, Samuel reminds Israel that the LORD has always been their rescuer.

Israel may now have a king, but they must still fear the LORD, serve Him, and obey His voice.

In chapter 13, Saul fails under pressure.

He cannot wait faithfully.

He offers what he should not offer.

He uses religious action to solve a fear problem.

In chapter 14, Jonathan shows the kind of trust Saul lacks.

Jonathan knows the LORD can save by many or by few.

Saul, by contrast, burdens the people with a rash oath and nearly destroys Jonathan.

The whole day asks us to watch the heart beneath leadership.

Victory can look good from the outside.

But obedience is tested when the crowd scatters, the enemy gathers, the clock runs long, and fear rises.

That is where Saul begins to break.

It also asks us to see the LORD's mercy.

The LORD saves Jabeshgilead.

The LORD warns Israel through Samuel.

The LORD points ahead to a man after His own heart.

The LORD saves Israel through Jonathan.

Human leadership is unstable, but the LORD remains faithful.

## Guided Listening Review

Before we close, walk back through the movement slowly.

In Jabeshgilead, the people face shame they cannot remove by themselves.

Nahash does not merely want surrender.

He wants reproach.

He wants Israel marked by weakness.

That is why Saul's rescue feels so strong.

The Spirit of God comes upon him, and he gathers Israel.

But even there, the chapter makes sure we do not worship Saul's strength.

The people are rescued because the LORD has wrought salvation in Israel.

That line protects the whole story.

If the reader only sees Saul, the reader misses the point.

Saul is useful when God empowers him.

Saul is dangerous when he begins to act from fear, pride, or impulse.

Samuel's speech then slows the celebration down.

Israel has a king, but they must not rewrite their history.

The LORD brought them out of Egypt.

The LORD delivered them from enemies.

The LORD heard their cries.

The LORD was their King before Saul sat on any throne.

So the thunder and rain are mercy, not mere terror.

God shakes them awake so they do not treat monarchy as a replacement for covenant faithfulness.

When you hear Samuel say, Fear the LORD, serve Him, and obey His voice, hear the structure of life under God.

Israel's king does not move Israel beyond obedience.

A king makes obedience even more urgent, because his choices can pull the whole nation with him.

Then Saul's failure in chapter 13 shows what happens when pressure rules the heart.

The Philistines look impossible.

The people scatter.

Samuel delays.

Saul wants spiritual cover for a fearful decision.

He offers the burnt offering, but the offering does not make the act faithful.

Religious action cannot turn disobedience into trust.

That is why Samuel's question, What hast thou done, lands so hard.

It is the question a person needs when fear has already explained away obedience.

Then Jonathan's faith gives the contrast.

Jonathan does not have better odds.

He does not have a stronger army.

He does not even claim certainty about exactly what God will do.

He simply knows the LORD is free to save by many or by few.

That kind of faith is humble and bold at the same time.

It does not command God, but it trusts His power.

Saul's oath then shows the difference between zeal and wisdom.

The battle is moving, but Saul burdens tired men.

The honey on the ground becomes a picture of strength God has placed nearby, while Saul's rash command keeps the army from receiving it.

By the end, the people have to rescue Jonathan from Saul's own words.

So Day 71 leaves us with two questions.

Will we wait for the LORD when fear says to hurry?

And will we trust the LORD's power when our resources look small?

## Key Connections

There are three connections worth carrying forward.

First, Saul's early mercy matters.

When he refuses to kill the men who doubted him, he shows what kingship could look like under the LORD.

Power does not have to become revenge.

Victory does not have to become cruelty.

Second, Samuel's warning matters because it keeps Israel from confusing a gift with a replacement.

The king may lead battles, but the king is not the covenant Lord.

Israel still belongs to the LORD.

Their safety still depends on His mercy.

Their future still requires listening to His voice.

Third, Jonathan's faith matters because it shows the kind of trust Saul lacks.

Jonathan can move forward without controlling the outcome.

Saul moves forward only after trying to control the religious moment.

Jonathan says the LORD can save by many or by few.

Saul acts as if everything will collapse unless he takes matters into his own hands.

That difference will matter through the rest of First Samuel.

One more detail matters for the listener.

Do not separate Saul's public victories from Saul's private tests.

The Bible lets us see both because real leadership is tested in both places.

Saul can gather an army when the whole nation sees the crisis.

But can Saul obey when only waiting is required?

Saul can speak mercy after Jabeshgilead.

But can Saul submit when Samuel confronts him?

Saul can lead a battle.

But can he lead people without burdening them through rash words?

Those questions prepare us for everything that comes next.

## Closing

As you finish Day 71, remember this:

The LORD is not limited by the weakness of His people.

He can save by many.

He can save by few.

He can save when the army is trembling.

He can save when weapons are scarce.

He can save through one faithful step taken in trust.

But the same chapters warn us not to confuse religious action with obedience.

Saul offered a sacrifice, but he did not obey.

Saul made an oath, but he did not lead wisely.

The LORD wants more than impressive moments.

He wants a heart that listens.

He wants trust that waits.

He wants obedience that does not collapse when pressure rises.

Day 71 helps us see why Israel needs a better king.

And as the story moves forward, the LORD is already preparing him.
