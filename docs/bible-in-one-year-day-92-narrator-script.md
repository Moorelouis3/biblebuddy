# Day 92 Narrator Script

## Opening

Welcome to Day 92 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are reading Second Kings 18 through 21.

The title for today is Hezekiah's Faith and Manasseh's Evil.

This day gives one of the strongest contrasts in Kings.

Hezekiah trusts the LORD, removes high places, breaks images, and refuses Assyria's final claim over Jerusalem.

The Assyrian spokesman mocks trust, mocks Hezekiah, and tries to make the people afraid.

Hezekiah spreads the threatening letter before the LORD.

The LORD delivers Jerusalem.

Then Hezekiah becomes sick, is healed, and later shows Babylon his treasures.

After him, Manasseh reigns and fills Judah with deep evil.

Day 92 shows faith under pressure, pride after mercy, and the terrible damage of wicked leadership.

## Second Kings 18

Second Kings 18 introduces Hezekiah.

He does right in the sight of the LORD, according to all that David did.

He removes the high places, breaks images, cuts down groves, and breaks the bronze serpent because Israel had begun burning incense to it.

That detail matters.

Even something connected to a past act of God can become an idol if people misuse it.

Hezekiah trusts in the LORD God of Israel.

The text says that after him there was none like him among the kings of Judah, nor any before him.

He cleaves to the LORD and keeps His commandments.

Then Assyria enters the story.

Samaria had already fallen because Israel would not obey the voice of the LORD.

Now Sennacherib king of Assyria comes against Judah.

Hezekiah first gives tribute, including silver from the house of the LORD and gold from the temple doors.

Then the Assyrian officials come to Jerusalem.

Rabshakeh speaks loudly in the Jews' language.

He attacks confidence.

He asks what trust Jerusalem has.

He mocks Egypt.

He twists Hezekiah's reforms.

He claims the LORD sent Assyria.

He promises comfort if they surrender.

His speech is spiritual warfare through words.

The people obey Hezekiah and answer him not a word.

## Second Kings 19

Second Kings 19 begins with Hezekiah tearing his clothes, covering himself with sackcloth, and going into the house of the LORD.

That is the right direction.

He does not answer pride with pride.

He carries the crisis toward God.

Isaiah sends word that the LORD has heard the blasphemy and will act.

Then another threatening letter comes from Assyria.

Hezekiah spreads it before the LORD.

His prayer is one of the great prayers of the kings.

He confesses that the LORD alone is God.

He admits Assyria has destroyed nations and their gods.

But those gods were no gods, only the work of men's hands.

He asks the LORD to save Judah so all kingdoms of the earth may know that He alone is the LORD God.

Isaiah sends the answer.

The LORD has heard.

Sennacherib has reproached the Holy One of Israel.

The LORD says Assyria's rage and tumult have come up into His ears.

Jerusalem will be defended for the LORD's sake and for David's sake.

That night the angel of the LORD smites the Assyrian camp.

Sennacherib returns and later dies by the sword of his own sons.

The chapter shows that the LORD is not intimidated by empire.

## Second Kings 20

Second Kings 20 shows Hezekiah sick unto death.

Isaiah tells him to set his house in order.

Hezekiah turns his face to the wall and prays.

The LORD hears and adds fifteen years to his life.

A lump of figs is used, and the sign of the shadow going backward confirms the word.

This is mercy.

But after healing comes another test.

Messengers from Babylon come, and Hezekiah shows them all his treasures.

Isaiah asks what they saw.

Hezekiah says there was nothing in his house or dominion that he did not show them.

Isaiah announces that the day will come when all these treasures will be carried to Babylon, and Hezekiah's sons will be taken.

Hezekiah answers that the word of the LORD is good, because there will be peace and truth in his days.

That answer feels small after such a large warning.

Hezekiah had great faith under Assyrian threat, but here pride and short-sighted comfort appear.

## Second Kings 21

Second Kings 21 shows Manasseh.

He begins to reign at twelve years old and reigns fifty-five years.

He does evil in the sight of the LORD.

He rebuilds high places, raises altars for Baal, makes a grove, worships the host of heaven, and builds altars in the house of the LORD.

He makes his son pass through the fire.

He uses enchantments and familiar spirits.

He sets a graven image in the house of God.

The evil is not only outside the temple.

It invades the very place where the LORD had put His name.

The LORD speaks judgment.

He will stretch over Jerusalem the line of Samaria and the plummet of the house of Ahab.

He will wipe Jerusalem as a man wipes a dish.

Manasseh also sheds innocent blood very much.

Then Amon reigns after him and does evil, walking in his father's way.

His servants conspire against him and kill him.

The people make Josiah king.

The chapter leaves Judah in deep darkness, but Josiah's name quietly prepares the next day.

## Full Lesson Review

Day 92 begins with one of Judah's brightest kings and ends in one of Judah's darkest reigns.

Hezekiah trusts the LORD and faces Assyria with prayer.

The LORD defends Jerusalem.

Hezekiah receives healing but later exposes his treasures to Babylon.

Manasseh fills Jerusalem with idolatry and innocent blood.

Amon continues evil.

The lesson is not that one good king fixes the heart of a nation forever.

Every generation must listen to the LORD.

## Slow Walk

Pay attention to words in this day.

Rabshakeh uses words to create fear.

Hezekiah uses prayer to bring fear before God.

Isaiah brings the word of the LORD that interprets the crisis.

The Assyrian letter looks powerful, but Hezekiah lays it before the LORD.

That is the picture to remember.

Threats do not have to be answered first by panic.

They can be spread before God.

Also notice that danger does not only come from enemies outside the wall.

Manasseh shows danger inside worship, inside leadership, and inside the temple itself.

Judah needs rescue from Assyria, but Judah also needs rescue from its own rebellion.

## Final Guided Recap

Before the closing, hold the contrast together.

Second Kings 18 shows Hezekiah trusting the LORD.

He removes high places and breaks the bronze serpent because even a good memory can become an idol when people worship the object instead of the LORD.

He cleaves to the LORD.

Then Assyria tests that trust.

Rabshakeh speaks in a way meant to weaken courage.

He tries to make surrender sound reasonable and faith sound foolish.

Second Kings 19 shows Hezekiah carrying the crisis into prayer.

He tears his clothes.

He goes into the house of the LORD.

He spreads the letter before God.

He asks for deliverance so all kingdoms may know that the LORD alone is God.

The LORD answers, and Jerusalem is defended.

Second Kings 20 shows mercy and warning in Hezekiah's own life.

He is healed.

His life is extended.

But when Babylon comes, he shows the treasures.

The future exile is announced.

Second Kings 21 then plunges into Manasseh's evil.

The son of a faithful king becomes one of Judah's darkest rulers.

He brings idolatry into the temple and sheds innocent blood.

This day teaches that faithfulness must be received personally.

A godly father does not automatically make a godly son.

The LORD must be trusted, obeyed, and worshiped in each generation.

## Closing

As you finish Day 92, remember Hezekiah spreading the letter before the LORD.

Faith does not pretend the threat is small.

Faith brings the real threat to the living God.
