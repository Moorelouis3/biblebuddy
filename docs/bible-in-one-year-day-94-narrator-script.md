# Day 94 Narrator Script

## Opening

Welcome to Day 94 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are reading First Chronicles 1 through 4.

The title for today is The Family Line of God's People.

After the fall of Jerusalem, Chronicles begins again with names.

That may feel like a strange turn.

But it is deeply meaningful.

The people have experienced exile, loss, and broken kingship.

Chronicles begins by reminding them who they are.

The names reach from Adam through the nations, through Abraham, through Israel, through Judah, and toward David's line.

Genealogies are not filler here.

They are memory.

They tell God's people that their story did not begin with exile and does not end there.

## First Chronicles 1

First Chronicles 1 begins with Adam, Seth, Enosh.

The opening stretches all the way back to creation.

The chronicler is not starting Israel's story in a narrow way.

He places Israel inside the story of all humanity.

The chapter moves through Noah's sons, the nations, Shem's line, Abraham, Ishmael, Keturah's sons, Isaac, Esau, and Edom's chiefs and kings.

For a beginner, the main point is not to memorize every name at once.

The point is to see that God remembers generations.

The nations are known.

Family lines are not lost.

Abraham's family is placed inside a larger world, and Israel's story will narrow through promise.

The chapter shows that the covenant people come from a real human family line, not from myth or vague legend.

## First Chronicles 2

First Chronicles 2 turns to the sons of Israel.

Reuben, Simeon, Levi, Judah, and the other sons are named.

Then the focus narrows into Judah.

This matters because kingship and David's line come through Judah.

The chapter names Perez, Hezron, Ram, Amminadab, Nahshon, Salmon, Boaz, Obed, Jesse, and David.

That line is precious.

It connects Ruth's story to David.

It connects Judah's family to royal promise.

The chapter also includes painful memories, like Achar, who troubled Israel.

Chronicles is not pretending the family story is clean.

It remembers both honor and trouble.

There are many branches, sons, daughters, fathers, towns, scribes, and clans.

The effect is to rebuild identity name by name.

The people are being told: you belong to a remembered story.

## First Chronicles 3

First Chronicles 3 focuses on David's sons and the royal line.

It names sons born in Hebron and Jerusalem.

Then it traces Solomon's line through the kings of Judah.

This chapter is especially important after the exile.

The monarchy has fallen in visible power, but the line is still remembered.

Ahaz, Hezekiah, Manasseh, Josiah, Jehoiakim, Jeconiah, and others appear.

These names carry the weight of the books of Kings.

Some were faithful.

Some were wicked.

Some suffered exile.

But the family line is still written.

For people wondering whether God's promise to David has disappeared, this chapter says the line has not been forgotten.

The royal house may be humbled, but memory remains.

The covenant story is still alive.

## First Chronicles 4

First Chronicles 4 returns to Judah and then includes Simeon.

The chapter contains many family names, but also a small prayer from Jabez.

Jabez calls on the God of Israel and asks that God would bless him, enlarge his coast, keep His hand with him, and keep him from evil.

God grants what he requests.

In a long list of names, this prayer stands out because it shows personal dependence inside family memory.

The chapter also names craftsmen, linen workers, potters, rulers, cities, and settlements.

That matters because God's people are not only kings and priests.

They are families, workers, towns, and communities.

The Simeonite section shows movement, pasture, settlement, and victory over Amalekite survivors.

The chapter keeps rebuilding the map of belonging.

## Full Lesson Review

Day 94 teaches how to read genealogy after exile.

These chapters are not random lists.

They rebuild memory.

They connect Adam to Abraham, Abraham to Israel, Israel to Judah, Judah to David, and David to the post-exile people.

They show that God remembers names, lines, places, and promises.

They also show that the family story includes sin, sorrow, work, prayer, and hope.

## Slow Walk

When you hear many names, resist the urge to treat them as empty.

Every name represents a life, a family, a place in the story.

Chronicles is teaching a wounded people to remember.

Exile can make people feel erased.

Genealogy says, you are not erased.

God knows where you came from.

God knows the promise line.

God knows the families that seem small.

God knows the workers, towns, and clans.

Day 94 is slower than a battle chapter, but it is doing deep repair work.

It gives God's people roots after disaster.

## Final Guided Recap

Before the closing, remember why Chronicles begins this way.

The people have just heard the story of Jerusalem's fall.

Kings failed.

The temple burned.

The people went into exile.

Then Chronicles begins with Adam.

That beginning says the story is larger than the disaster.

First Chronicles 1 connects Israel to humanity, Noah, the nations, Abraham, Isaac, Ishmael, and Esau.

It shows that God remembers the wider family of the earth while narrowing the covenant story.

First Chronicles 2 narrows into Israel and Judah.

Judah matters because the ruler comes from Judah.

David's line is not an accident.

The family history moves toward him through remembered names.

First Chronicles 3 names David's sons and the royal line.

After exile, that list matters because the promise to David might look buried.

Chronicles writes the line down again.

First Chronicles 4 continues Judah's families and includes Jabez calling on the God of Israel.

In the middle of many names, prayer appears.

That reminds the listener that genealogy is not only public history.

It includes personal dependence on God.

Day 94 is therefore a restoration lesson.

God's people are being given memory, roots, and identity again.

## Closing

As you finish Day 94, remember that God keeps names.

The people may have lost land, temple, and kingly glory, but their story is still held before the LORD.
