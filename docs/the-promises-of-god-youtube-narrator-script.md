# The Promises of God - YouTube Narrator Script

## Opening

There is a difference between knowing Bible verses and knowing the promises of God.

A verse can be memorized.

A verse can be printed on a wall, saved on a phone, or repeated in a hard moment.

But a promise has to be trusted.

And that is where many believers quietly struggle.

They know certain verses are in the Bible, but they are not always sure what God has actually promised them.

They wonder, Did God promise me an easy life?

Did God promise that nothing painful would happen?

Did God promise that if I pray hard enough, every problem will disappear?

Did God promise that I will always understand what He is doing?

Those questions matter, because disappointment often begins when we expect promises God never made.

But Scripture is full of promises God did make.

And those promises are not shallow.

They are not slogans.

They are not spiritual hype.

They are windows into God's character.

So in this study, we are going to slow down and walk through seven promises God repeatedly gives to His people.

Not to make life sound easy.

Not to pretend pain is small.

But to help us become more confident in the God who speaks, keeps, and fulfills His word.

We are going to look at God's love, His peace, His provision, His response to prayer, His strength in weakness, His protection in battle, and His presence that does not leave.

And as we walk through these promises, listen for the larger truth underneath all of them.

God's greatest promise is not that life will always feel comfortable.

God's greatest promise is that He gives Himself to His people.

## Promise 1: God's Love Does Not Leave When You Fail

The first promise is this: God's love does not leave when you fail.

Romans 8:38 and 39 says:

For I am persuaded, that neither death, nor life, nor angels, nor principalities, nor powers, nor things present, nor things to come,

Nor height, nor depth, nor any other creature, shall be able to separate us from the love of God, which is in Christ Jesus our Lord.

Paul is not writing as someone who thinks life is gentle.

He knows suffering.

He knows weakness.

He knows accusation.

He knows what it feels like to be opposed, pressured, misunderstood, and afraid.

And still he says, I am persuaded.

That word matters.

Paul is not guessing.

He is not wishing.

He is convinced.

Then he begins listing everything that cannot separate God's people from God's love.

Death cannot do it.

Life cannot do it.

Powers cannot do it.

The present cannot do it.

The future cannot do it.

That is important because many people are not only afraid of death. They are afraid of life.

They are afraid of what might happen next.

They are afraid of what they have already done.

They are afraid of the pressure they are under right now.

They are afraid of the future they cannot control.

Paul takes all of that fear and says, None of it is strong enough to separate you from the love of God in Christ Jesus.

This does not mean sin is small.

It does not mean failure does not matter.

It means God's love is rooted deeper than your performance.

God's love begins in His character, not in your ability to keep yourself impressive.

One of the clearest examples of this is David.

David is called a man after God's own heart, but David's life also includes terrible failure.

He commits adultery with Bathsheba.

He tries to cover it.

He deceives.

He arranges the death of Uriah.

This is not a small mistake.

It is sin layered on top of sin.

And when David finally breaks before God in Psalm 51, listen to how he begins.

He does not say, God, please start loving me again.

He says, Have mercy upon me, O God, according to thy lovingkindness.

According unto the multitude of thy tender mercies blot out my transgressions.

David appeals to God's steadfast love.

Steadfast love means loyal love.

It means covenant love.

It means love that does not vanish the moment the person loved becomes difficult.

David does not minimize his sin.

He confesses it.

He grieves it.

He owns it.

But he brings his guilt to the mercy of God, because he knows God's love is not fragile.

That matters for ordinary believers.

Because many people think failure moves them outside God's reach.

They think, I prayed that prayer too many times.

I fell again.

I should know better by now.

God must be tired of me.

But Romans 8 does not say God's love holds only when you feel strong.

It says nothing can separate us from the love of God in Christ Jesus our Lord.

So the application is not, be careless.

The application is, come back.

Confess honestly.

Stop hiding.

Bring the real failure into the real mercy of God.

The love of God is not permission to stay in sin.

It is the power that makes returning possible.

## Promise 2: God Promises Peace, Not Explanations

The second promise is this: God promises peace, not always explanations.

Philippians 4:7 says:

And the peace of God, which passeth all understanding, shall keep your hearts and minds through Christ Jesus.

Most of us want explanations.

We want to know why the door closed.

Why the relationship broke.

Why the prayer seems delayed.

Why the pain lasted so long.

Why God allowed one thing and stopped another.

And sometimes God does give understanding.

Sometimes He shows us the lesson.

Sometimes we look back and see the wisdom.

But many times, God does not explain everything.

Instead, He gives peace.

Paul calls it the peace of God.

That means this peace comes from God and carries the character of God.

It is not the peace of perfect circumstances.

It is not the peace of having every answer.

It is not the peace of being in control.

It is the peace of belonging to the One who is in control.

Then Paul says this peace surpasses understanding.

That does not mean it is irrational.

It means it is bigger than what the situation can explain.

There are times when a believer can say, I do not understand this, but somehow God is holding me.

I do not like this, but I am not alone.

I do not have the answer, but I have not lost the Lord.

That is peace surpassing understanding.

Then Paul says this peace will guard your hearts and minds.

That word guard gives us the picture of a soldier standing watch over a city gate.

The city may still be under pressure.

The threat may still exist outside.

But there is a guard at the gate.

God's peace does not always remove the battle around you.

Sometimes it guards the heart inside you.

And remember where Paul is writing from.

Philippians is often called one of Paul's prison letters.

Paul is not writing about peace from a vacation.

He is writing about peace while confined.

He does not have complete freedom.

He does not know every detail of what will happen next.

And yet he can speak about joy, prayer, contentment, and peace.

That teaches us something very important.

Peace is not denial.

Peace is not pretending the problem is not real.

Peace is trust in God while the problem is real.

Peace is stability when circumstances are unstable.

Peace is the heart learning to rest under God's care before the mind understands every detail.

So when you pray, do not only ask God for explanations.

You can ask for wisdom.

You can ask for clarity.

But also ask for the peace that guards you while you wait.

Because sometimes the promise is not, I will tell you everything.

Sometimes the promise is, I will keep your heart with My peace.

## Promise 3: God Promises Provision, Not Excess

The third promise is this: God promises provision, not excess.

In Matthew 6:31 and 32, Jesus says:

Therefore take no thought, saying, What shall we eat? or, What shall we drink? or, Wherewithal shall we be clothed?

For your heavenly Father knoweth that ye have need of all these things.

Jesus is speaking to real human anxiety.

Food.

Drink.

Clothing.

Daily needs.

He does not shame people for having needs.

He says, Your Father knows.

That is one of the most comforting truths in this passage.

Before you explain the need, God knows.

Before you find the words, God knows.

Before you solve the problem, God knows.

But we have to be careful.

Provision and excess are not the same thing.

Provision means God gives what is needed.

Excess means having more than enough to feel untouchable.

Most of us would prefer excess because excess feels like control.

If I have enough stored up, enough figured out, enough guaranteed, then maybe I do not have to feel dependent.

But God often teaches His people trust through daily provision.

That is what we see in Exodus 16.

Israel is in the wilderness.

They have been delivered from Egypt, but they are not yet in the promised land.

They are hungry.

They complain.

And God gives manna.

Manna was bread from heaven.

It appeared in the morning.

The people gathered it.

But they were not allowed to hoard it.

If they gathered more than they needed and tried to keep it overnight, it spoiled.

Except before the Sabbath, when God allowed enough for two days.

That rhythm taught Israel something.

God could provide today.

And they would have to trust Him again tomorrow.

Daily bread is not only about food.

It is about dependence.

God was forming a people who would learn that life is sustained by His hand, not by their ability to stockpile their way out of trust.

This is why Jesus teaches us to pray, Give us this day our daily bread.

Not yearly bread.

Not lifetime bread.

Daily bread.

That does not mean planning is wrong.

Wisdom plans.

Scripture praises diligence.

But planning becomes unhealthy when it becomes our replacement for trusting God.

God's provision often comes one day at a time.

Enough strength for today.

Enough mercy for today.

Enough wisdom for today.

Enough bread for today.

So if you are in a season where you do not see the full supply yet, do not assume God has failed.

Look for today's manna.

Look for the grace He has placed in front of you right now.

God may not give excess that removes dependence.

But He promises to be a Father who knows what His children need.

## Promise 4: God Promises That Prayer Is Not Ignored

The fourth promise is this: prayer is not ignored.

Jeremiah 33:3 says:

Call unto me, and I will answer thee, and shew thee great and mighty things, which thou knowest not.

This is a beautiful promise, but it is not a shallow one.

Because many believers have prayed and waited.

They have prayed through tears.

They have prayed for healing.

They have prayed for family.

They have prayed for direction.

They have prayed for a door to open.

And sometimes heaven feels quiet.

So we need to say this carefully.

Silence is not absence.

Delay is not rejection.

And an answer different from what we expected is not proof that God did not listen.

The Bible gives us many examples of waiting prayer.

Abraham waited years for the promised son.

God had spoken, but the fulfillment did not arrive quickly.

Hannah prayed in deep anguish for a child.

Her lips moved, her grief was visible, and the priest Eli misunderstood her.

But God saw her.

Daniel prayed, and in Daniel 10 we learn that there was spiritual conflict connected to the answer.

From Daniel's view, there was delay.

From heaven's view, something was happening.

These stories teach us not to measure God's attention by our timeline.

God is not impulsive.

He does not answer like someone pressured into action.

He responds with wisdom.

Sometimes God says yes.

Sometimes God says no.

Sometimes God says wait.

Sometimes God answers in a way we would not have known to request.

And that can be hard.

Because we often come to prayer with a clear picture of what we think faithfulness should look like.

But prayer is not only how we move requests toward God.

Prayer is also how God forms trust in us.

When Jeremiah 33:3 says, Call unto me, and I will answer thee, it does not mean God becomes our servant.

It means God invites His people to speak to Him with confidence that He hears and responds.

The answer may reveal things we did not know.

The timing may stretch us.

The result may humble us.

But prayer is not wasted breath.

The believer is never speaking into emptiness.

So keep praying.

Not because prayer controls God.

But because prayer brings you before the God who listens with wisdom, mercy, and perfect sight.

## Promise 5: God Promises Strength in Weakness

The fifth promise is this: God promises strength in weakness.

Second Corinthians 12:9 says:

And he said unto me, My grace is sufficient for thee: for my strength is made perfect in weakness.

Paul had what he called a thorn in the flesh.

Scripture does not tell us exactly what it was.

And maybe that is part of why the passage has helped so many people.

Because the thorn can meet many kinds of weakness.

Physical weakness.

Emotional pressure.

Ongoing limitation.

Pain that does not quickly leave.

A burden that keeps a person dependent.

Paul did not pretend to enjoy it.

He pleaded with the Lord three times that it might depart from him.

That means Paul asked for removal.

And God said no.

But God's no was not abandonment.

God gave a promise:

My grace is sufficient for thee.

Sufficient means enough.

Not decorative.

Not barely present.

Enough.

God did not give Paul the answer Paul first wanted.

But He gave Paul the grace Paul needed.

Then God says, My strength is made perfect in weakness.

Modern culture often worships self-confidence.

Be strong.

Trust yourself.

Believe in your own power.

Never admit weakness.

But Scripture teaches something deeper.

God-dependence.

Weakness is not valuable because pain is good in itself.

Weakness is valuable when it becomes a place where God's power is displayed.

Weakness creates space for dependence.

It reminds us we are not self-sufficient.

It makes room for prayer.

It teaches us to receive instead of only perform.

Paul learns that strength is not pretending to be strong.

Strength is trusting God when you are not.

That is why he can later say he will glory in weaknesses, so that the power of Christ may rest upon him.

This does not mean we should seek suffering.

It means we do not have to fear being weak in God's hands.

Your limitation does not disqualify you from grace.

Your weakness may become the very place where you learn that God's grace is enough.

So if you are tired, limited, or carrying something you wish God would remove, you can still pray honestly.

Paul did.

But also listen for the promise underneath the answer.

My grace is sufficient for thee.

## Promise 6: God Promises Protection, Not a Battle-Free Life

The sixth promise is this: God promises protection, not a battle-free life.

Isaiah 54:17 says:

No weapon that is formed against thee shall prosper.

Notice what the verse does not say.

It does not say no weapon will be formed.

It says no weapon formed against you shall prosper.

That distinction matters.

Many people assume God's protection means no pressure, no battle, no attack, no hardship, and no opposition.

But the Bible does not show God's people living battle-free lives.

Daniel still goes into the lions' den.

Shadrach, Meshach, and Abednego still stand before the fiery furnace.

David still faces Goliath.

Paul still experiences persecution, imprisonment, shipwreck, and danger.

The weapon forms.

The pressure comes.

The threat is real.

But God limits the outcome.

Daniel enters the den, but the lions' mouths are shut.

The three Hebrew men are thrown into the fire, but the fire does not consume them.

David stands before a giant, but the giant does not get the final word.

Paul suffers deeply, but the gospel keeps moving.

This teaches us that God's protection is often bigger than comfort.

Sometimes God protects purpose more than ease.

Sometimes He allows the battle, but does not allow the battle to destroy what He has called you to become.

That can be difficult to accept because we naturally want protection to mean avoidance.

Keep me from the fire.

Keep me from the den.

Keep me from the valley.

And sometimes God does.

But other times He protects us in the fire, in the den, and in the valley.

Protection does not mean nothing touches your life.

It means nothing can overrule God's authority over your life.

No weapon can become more powerful than the God who holds you.

No opposition can cancel His purpose.

No accusation can outrank His verdict.

No battle can write the final chapter without His permission.

So do not measure God's protection only by whether the battle exists.

Look also at what the battle cannot do.

It cannot separate you from God's love.

It cannot remove His presence.

It cannot defeat His final purpose.

Weapons may form.

But they do not get to be sovereign.

God does.

## Promise 7: God Promises His Presence Will Not Leave

The seventh promise is one of the greatest promises in all of Scripture: God promises His presence will not leave.

Deuteronomy 31:6 says:

Be strong and of a good courage, fear not, nor be afraid of them: for the Lord thy God, he it is that doth go with thee; he will not fail thee, nor forsake thee.

And Jesus says in Matthew 28:20:

Lo, I am with you alway, even unto the end of the world.

The promise of God's presence runs through the whole Bible.

God does not merely send help from a distance.

He goes with His people.

That is what Moses needed.

In Exodus 33, Moses essentially says that if God's presence does not go with them, he does not want to move forward.

Moses knows that the promised land without God's presence would not be enough.

The gift is not greater than the Giver.

Then Joshua receives the promise of God's presence as he prepares to lead after Moses.

Joshua is stepping into a role that must have felt overwhelming.

Moses is gone.

The people are about to enter the land.

There will be battles ahead.

And God's word to Joshua is not, This will be easy.

It is, I will be with thee.

David knew this too.

Psalm 23 does not say there is no valley.

It says, Yea, though I walk through the valley of the shadow of death, I will fear no evil: for thou art with me.

The comfort is not that the valley is imaginary.

The comfort is that God is present inside it.

The disciples receive the same promise from Jesus.

After the resurrection, Jesus sends them into the world.

That mission would not be safe or simple.

They would face rejection, confusion, persecution, and suffering.

But Jesus anchors the mission in His presence:

I am with you always.

Paul's life also shows this promise.

In Acts, the Lord appears to Paul and encourages him not to be afraid.

In Second Timothy, near the end of Paul's life, Paul says that at his first defense no man stood with him, but the Lord stood with him.

That sentence is deeply moving.

People were absent.

The Lord was not.

This is important because God's presence is covenantal, not merely emotional.

That means God's presence is rooted in His commitment, not only in our ability to feel Him.

There will be days when you feel close to God.

There will also be days when you feel dry, tired, distracted, or afraid.

But the promise does not depend on your emotional weather.

He will not leave you nor forsake you.

This does not mean feelings do not matter.

God cares about the heart.

The Psalms are full of honest emotion.

But feelings are not the foundation of the promise.

God's word is.

So when you cannot feel Him, you can still trust what He has spoken.

When the room feels quiet, He has not stopped being present.

When the road feels lonely, He has not abandoned the covenant.

When obedience feels costly, He has not sent you alone.

This promise may be the one that holds all the others together.

God's love is personal because He is present.

His peace guards us because He is present.

His provision comes from a Father who is present.

Prayer matters because we are calling on the God who is present.

Strength comes in weakness because Christ's power rests on us.

Protection matters because the Lord is with His people in the battle.

The greatest gift is not only what God gives.

The greatest gift is God Himself.

## Closing

So when we talk about the promises of God, we need to be honest.

God does not promise an easy life.

Jesus never promised that.

In John 16:33, Jesus says:

In the world ye shall have tribulation: but be of good cheer; I have overcome the world.

That is a very different kind of promise.

Jesus does not deny trouble.

He names it.

But He also names His victory.

Many people become disappointed with God because they expect promises He never made.

They expect no pain.

No waiting.

No weakness.

No battles.

No confusion.

No valleys.

But Scripture gives us better promises than a trouble-free life.

God promises love that does not leave when we fail.

He promises peace that guards the heart when explanations are missing.

He promises provision from a Father who knows what we need.

He promises that prayer is not ignored.

He promises strength in weakness.

He promises protection in the battle, even when weapons form.

And He promises His presence will not leave.

These promises do not make life shallow.

They make faith steady.

They do not remove every question.

They teach us where to stand while we carry the questions.

They do not turn God into a machine that gives us everything we demand.

They reveal Him as Father, Savior, Shepherd, Helper, Defender, and Lord.

So if you are holding onto a promise today, hold it carefully.

Do not twist it into something God did not say.

But do not let go of what He has said.

The God who promises is faithful.

The God who speaks is steady.

The God who calls you is present.

And the greatest promise He gives is not comfort.

The greatest promise He gives is Himself.
