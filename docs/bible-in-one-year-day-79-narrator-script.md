# Day 79 Narrator Script

## Opening

Welcome to Day 79 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are reading Second Samuel 12 through 15.

The title for today is Consequences in David's House.

This day begins with Nathan confronting David.

David confesses, I have sinned against the LORD.

The LORD puts away David's sin, but consequences enter David's house.

The child born to Bathsheba dies.

Solomon is born.

Then the story moves into family devastation.

Amnon violates Tamar.

Absalom murders Amnon.

Absalom is brought back but remains estranged.

Then Absalom steals the hearts of Israel and begins rebellion against David.

This day is painful because sin does not stay private.

Forgiveness is real, but consequences can still be deep.

## Second Samuel 12

Second Samuel 12 opens with the LORD sending Nathan to David.

That is mercy.

David has covered sin, but God sends a prophet to uncover it.

Nathan tells a story.

There are two men in one city, one rich and one poor.

The rich man has exceeding many flocks and herds.

The poor man has nothing except one little ewe lamb.

The lamb grows up with him and with his children.

It eats from his meat, drinks from his cup, lies in his bosom, and is like a daughter to him.

A traveler comes to the rich man.

The rich man refuses to take from his own flock.

He takes the poor man's lamb and prepares it for the traveler.

David's anger is greatly kindled.

He says the man who did this shall surely die and restore the lamb fourfold.

Then Nathan says, Thou art the man.

Those words pierce the cover David built.

Nathan speaks the word of the LORD.

God anointed David king.

God delivered him from Saul.

God gave him his master's house.

If that had been too little, God would have given more.

Why did David despise the commandment of the LORD?

He killed Uriah with the sword.

He took Uriah's wife.

The sword will not depart from David's house.

David says, I have sinned against the LORD.

Nathan says the LORD has put away David's sin; he shall not die.

But because David gave great occasion to the enemies of the LORD to blaspheme, the child will die.

The child becomes very sick.

David fasts and lies all night upon the earth.

The elders try to raise him, but he will not eat.

On the seventh day the child dies.

The servants are afraid to tell David, thinking he may harm himself.

David perceives the child is dead.

He rises, washes, anoints himself, changes clothes, goes into the house of the LORD, and worships.

Then he eats.

David says while the child was alive he fasted and wept, hoping the LORD might be gracious.

But now the child is dead.

David says, I shall go to him, but he shall not return to me.

David comforts Bathsheba.

She bears a son named Solomon.

The LORD loves him and sends by Nathan to call his name Jedidiah.

The chapter then returns to Rabbah.

Joab captures the royal city and calls David to finish the victory.

David takes the crown and completes the conquest.

The chapter holds confession, forgiveness, death, worship, birth, and victory together.

It is complex because sin's consequences do not erase God's mercy, and God's mercy does not pretend sin was light.

## Second Samuel 13

Second Samuel 13 shows the sword entering David's house.

Amnon, David's son, loves Tamar, Absalom's sister.

But his desire is not holy love.

He is so vexed that he falls sick.

Jonadab, a subtle man, counsels him to pretend sickness and ask David to send Tamar to make cakes before him.

David sends Tamar.

Amnon uses the plan to isolate her.

When she comes near, he takes hold of her and says, Come lie with me, my sister.

Tamar pleads with him.

She says no such thing ought to be done in Israel.

She names it folly.

She asks where she could carry her shame.

But Amnon will not hearken to her voice.

He forces her.

Then Amnon hates her exceedingly.

The hatred after the act is greater than the love with which he had loved her.

He tells her to arise and be gone.

Tamar says sending her away is another wrong.

He will not listen.

He commands his servant to put her out and bolt the door.

Tamar puts ashes on her head, rends her garment of divers colours, lays her hand on her head, and goes on crying.

Absalom tells her to hold her peace, but he hates Amnon.

David hears and is very wroth.

But the chapter does not show David bringing justice for Tamar.

Two full years pass.

Absalom arranges a sheep-shearing feast and has Amnon killed.

At first David hears that all the king's sons are dead, but Jonadab says only Amnon is dead.

Absalom flees to Geshur.

David mourns for his son every day.

This chapter is heartbreaking.

Tamar's voice is clear, wise, and ignored.

Amnon's sin is violent.

Absalom's revenge is calculated.

David's house is being torn by the consequences Nathan warned about.

## Second Samuel 14

Second Samuel 14 shows Joab trying to bring Absalom back.

Joab perceives the king's heart is toward Absalom.

He sends for a wise woman of Tekoah and gives her a story to tell.

She comes to David as a mourner and says she is a widow with two sons.

The sons fought, one killed the other, and now the family wants to execute the remaining son.

She pleads that her coal not be quenched.

David promises protection.

Then she turns the story toward David.

She asks why the king has thought such a thing against the people of God, since he does not fetch home again his banished.

She says we must needs die and are as water spilt on the ground.

Yet God devises means so that His banished be not expelled from Him.

David recognizes Joab's hand in the matter.

Joab admits it.

David allows Absalom to return to Jerusalem, but says he shall not see the king's face.

Absalom dwells two full years in Jerusalem and does not see David's face.

The chapter describes Absalom's beauty.

In all Israel there is none so praised as Absalom for beauty.

From the sole of his foot to the crown of his head, there is no blemish.

This outward beauty will become dangerous because the people are drawn to appearance.

Absalom wants Joab to come, but Joab will not.

Absalom sets Joab's field on fire to force a meeting.

He says if there is iniquity in him, let the king kill him.

Joab tells David.

David calls Absalom.

Absalom bows, and David kisses him.

But the reconciliation feels thin.

Absalom is back, but his heart is not healed.

David's household is not whole.

## Second Samuel 15

Second Samuel 15 shows Absalom's rebellion forming.

Absalom prepares chariots, horses, and fifty men to run before him.

He rises early and stands beside the way of the gate.

When people come to the king for judgment, Absalom speaks to them.

He says their matters are good and right, but no man is deputed by the king to hear them.

Then he says, Oh that I were made judge in the land.

He presents himself as the one who would give justice.

When men come near to do obeisance, he puts forth his hand, takes them, and kisses them.

So Absalom steals the hearts of the men of Israel.

That phrase is key.

He does not win hearts by covenant faithfulness.

He steals them through image, flattery, and political performance.

After forty years, Absalom asks David to let him go to Hebron to pay a vow.

David sends him in peace.

But Absalom sends spies through the tribes, saying that when they hear the trumpet, they should say Absalom reigns in Hebron.

The conspiracy is strong.

The people increase continually with Absalom.

David hears that the hearts of Israel are after Absalom.

He tells his servants to arise and flee, lest Absalom overtake them and smite the city with the edge of the sword.

David leaves Jerusalem.

This is a terrible reversal.

The king who captured Jerusalem now flees it.

The priests bring the ark, but David tells Zadok to carry the ark back into the city.

If David finds favor in the eyes of the LORD, God will bring him back.

If not, David says, Let Him do to me as seemeth good unto Him.

That is submission under discipline.

David goes up by the ascent of Mount Olivet, weeping, barefoot, and with his head covered.

The people weep with him.

Then David hears that Ahithophel is among the conspirators with Absalom.

David prays that the LORD would turn Ahithophel's counsel into foolishness.

Hushai meets David, and David sends him back to Jerusalem to defeat Ahithophel's counsel.

The chapter ends with Absalom coming into Jerusalem.

## Full Lesson Review

Day 79 shows that forgiveness is real, and consequences are also real.

David confesses his sin.

The LORD puts away his sin.

But the sword enters David's house.

Tamar is violated.

Amnon is murdered.

Absalom flees.

Absalom returns without true restoration.

Absalom steals Israel's hearts.

David flees Jerusalem.

This is not random tragedy.

The story is showing the bitter fruit of sin inside the royal house.

## Key Connections

First, Nathan's confrontation is mercy.

Without the word of the LORD, David would remain hidden in sin.

Second, Tamar's words matter.

The Bible lets her name the evil done to her.

Third, Absalom's beauty and charm do not equal righteousness.

He can look impressive while acting treacherously.

Fourth, David's flight shows humility under God's hand.

He sends the ark back and leaves his future with the LORD.

## Guided Listening Review

Day 79 is a day to read slowly because every chapter carries pain.

Nathan's confrontation is sharp, but it is also rescue.

David had power to silence people around him.

He had already used messengers, servants, Joab, and battle reports to hide what he did.

But David cannot silence the LORD.

When Nathan says, Thou art the man, the story stops hiding.

That is what faithful rebuke does.

It brings the sinner into truth.

David's confession is short.

I have sinned against the LORD.

He does not give a speech of excuses.

He does not blame Bathsheba.

He does not blame the pressure of kingship.

He names sin before God.

That confession matters, but the chapter also teaches that confession does not erase every consequence.

The child dies.

The sword enters the house.

Trouble rises from within David's family.

Second Samuel 13 is especially important because Tamar's voice must not be ignored.

She names the evil clearly.

She appeals to what ought not be done in Israel.

She speaks about shame, folly, and consequence.

Amnon refuses to listen.

The Bible lets the reader hear Tamar before showing the silence and failures around her.

David is angry, but the story does not show him bringing justice.

Absalom waits and then takes vengeance.

That vengeance does not heal Tamar.

It adds more blood to David's house.

Second Samuel 14 shows a kind of return without full reconciliation.

Absalom comes back to Jerusalem, but he does not see David's face for two years.

When they finally meet, the kiss does not resolve the heart.

Unresolved sin and shallow reconciliation can become future rebellion.

Then chapter 15 shows Absalom's skill.

He looks impressive.

He sounds compassionate.

He stands near the gate.

He tells people what they want to hear.

He steals hearts slowly.

The rebellion does not begin with swords.

It begins with image, flattery, and distrust.

When David leaves Jerusalem barefoot and weeping, the reader should feel the consequence of the earlier word from Nathan.

The kingdom is still God's promise, but David is walking through discipline.

Even there, David entrusts himself to the LORD.

## Chapter Takeaways

From Second Samuel 12, remember that confession is necessary and mercy is real.

David says, I have sinned against the LORD.

Nathan says the LORD has put away his sin.

But mercy does not make the sin small.

From Second Samuel 13, remember Tamar's suffering.

The Bible does not erase her voice.

She speaks wisely and clearly, and the wrong done to her is shown as shameful and evil.

Amnon's desire is exposed as selfishness, not love.

Absalom's revenge is exposed as violence, not healing.

From Second Samuel 14, remember that return is not always reconciliation.

Absalom comes back to Jerusalem, but distance remains.

The outward problem is managed, but the inward fracture remains dangerous.

From Second Samuel 15, remember that rebellion can wear a charming face.

Absalom rises early.

He stands at the gate.

He speaks smoothly.

He kisses hands.

He steals hearts.

The reader should learn that not every person who talks about justice is righteous.

Absalom uses the language of concern to gather power for himself.

David's flight is therefore more than political crisis.

It is the bitter fruit of a house where sin, silence, revenge, and ambition have been growing.

Yet David sends the ark back.

He does not use God's presence as a lucky object.

He entrusts his return to the favor of the LORD.

## Slow Recap

If you remember one movement from Day 79, remember this: forgiven sin can still leave painful consequences.

That is hard, but it is important.

The LORD puts away David's sin.

David does not die under the judgment he deserves.

That is mercy.

But the chapters that follow show the sword moving through David's house.

That is consequence.

The Bible puts both truths together because both are true.

God's mercy is real.

God's holiness is real.

Sin's damage is real.

Tamar's story must sit heavily with the reader.

She speaks with clarity, but Amnon refuses to listen.

David is angry, but his anger does not become justice for her.

Absalom acts, but his action is revenge, not righteousness.

The family breaks further.

Then Absalom's return shows another danger.

Proximity is not the same thing as reconciliation.

Absalom can live in Jerusalem while rebellion grows in his heart.

David can kiss him while the fracture remains unresolved.

That prepares the reader for the gate scene.

Absalom's rebellion is not sudden.

It is cultivated.

He performs concern.

He gathers sympathy.

He steals hearts.

The day ends with David leaving the city weeping, and that image should stay with us.

The king is forgiven, but he is walking through the bitter harvest of sin.

## Final Meditation

Absalom's rebellion is the visible unraveling of a house that has not dealt rightly with sin.

The reader should not detach it from Tamar's suffering, Amnon's violence, David's failure to bring justice, and the shallow repair of chapter 14.

The story is connected.

Sin grows when it is hidden.

Bitterness grows when justice is absent.

Ambition grows when charm is rewarded.

Absalom understands appearances.

He knows how to stand in the right place, say the right words, and make people feel seen.

But he is not seeking the LORD's will.

He is seeking a throne.

The day warns us that charisma is not character, beauty is not holiness, and public sympathy is not righteousness.

The true king must be measured by faithfulness before God.

## Closing

As you finish Day 79, remember that God's forgiveness should never make us casual about sin.

David is forgiven, but his house is wounded.

The LORD is merciful, but He is not mocked.

The story is preparing us to long for a King whose house is not corrupted by sin.
