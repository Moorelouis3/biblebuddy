# Bible In One Year - Day 11 Exact WEB Narrator Script

## Day 11 - Jacob Meets God at Bethel

**Reading:** Genesis 28 and 29  
**Bible text:** World English Bible, Genesis 28-29  
**Goal:** Read the full Scripture in order, pause after natural sections, explain what is happening in clear modern language, and help the listener follow Jacob's story while driving, walking, working, or listening in the background.

## Estimated Length

**Estimated script word count:** about 5,200-5,800 words  
**Estimated spoken time:** about 30-36 minutes at a calm narrator pace  
**Estimated audio runtime with light pauses/music bed:** about 34-40 minutes

This follows the Day 10 style: Scripture first, then a short explanation. The narrator should sound like a warm story guide, not like a preacher giving a sermon.

## Production Direction

Treat this as an audio Bible drama and story companion, but do not say that style out loud in the script. Start by carrying the emotional weight of Day 10 into Day 11. Jacob has the blessing, but the family is broken. He is not walking away as a clean hero. He is leaving home with fear behind him, uncertainty ahead of him, and God's promise about to meet him on the road.

Read the WEB Scripture with reverence. Then explain what is happening in plain modern language that a sixth grader can follow without talking down to them. Keep the explanations conversational, connected, and emotionally alive. Give useful nuggets: words that need explaining, cultural context, family pressure, repeated patterns, and how the scene connects to real life.

## Opening

Jacob has the blessing now.

But he does not have peace.

Day 10 ended with a family torn open. Isaac wanted to bless Esau. Rebekah wanted the blessing for Jacob. Jacob put on Esau's clothes, covered his skin, walked into his blind father's tent, and lied his way into the blessing.

And when Esau found out, the grief was loud.

He cried bitterly. He hated Jacob. And he planned to kill him.

So now Jacob is leaving home.

That matters, because Jacob is not leaving as a brave explorer. He is leaving because the house is no longer safe. He is carrying the blessing of Abraham, but he is also carrying the consequences of deception. He is chosen, but he is not mature. He has promise over him, but he has pain behind him.

And that is where Genesis 28 begins.

Jacob is about to discover something that will change his whole life: the God of Abraham and Isaac is not only the God of his father and grandfather.

God is going to meet Jacob himself.

Not when Jacob is settled.

Not when Jacob has cleaned everything up.

Not when Jacob finally becomes wise.

God meets him on the road, at night, alone, with a stone for a pillow.

Welcome to Day 11 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are reading Genesis 28 and 29.

Genesis 28 shows Jacob leaving home, dreaming at Bethel, and hearing God's promise directly. Genesis 29 brings him to Haran, where he meets Rachel, enters Laban's house, works seven years for love, and then gets deceived by a man even craftier than he is.

So listen for three big ideas today.

First: God's promise can meet a person in the middle of consequences.

Second: a holy encounter does not erase the need for growth.

Third: God sees the person everyone else overlooks.

Let's begin with Genesis chapter 28, verses 1 through 5.

## Genesis 28:1-5

**Scripture**

1. Isaac called Jacob, blessed him, and commanded him, "You shall not take a wife of the daughters of Canaan.

2. Arise, go to Paddan Aram, to the house of Bethuel your mother's father. Take a wife from there from the daughters of Laban, your mother's brother.

3. May God Almighty bless you, and make you fruitful, and multiply you, that you may be a company of peoples,

4. and give you the blessing of Abraham, to you and to your offspring with you, that you may inherit the land where you travel, which God gave to Abraham."

5. Isaac sent Jacob away. He went to Paddan Aram to Laban, son of Bethuel the Syrian, the brother of Rebekah, Jacob's and Esau's mother.

**Narrator**

Isaac sends Jacob away, but this is more than travel advice.

He tells Jacob not to marry a woman from Canaan. That connects back to Abraham's concern for Isaac in Genesis 24. The covenant family is not supposed to blend into the surrounding culture and lose its identity. Jacob is being sent back toward the family line connected to Rebekah.

But notice the emotional weight.

Jacob is blessed again. Isaac speaks the blessing of Abraham over him: fruitfulness, multiplication, a company of peoples, and inheritance of the land.

So there is no confusion now. Isaac recognizes that the covenant blessing is resting on Jacob.

But Jacob is also leaving because home has become dangerous. Esau is angry enough to kill him. Rebekah is trying to protect him. Isaac sends him away with blessing, but the family is still fractured.

That is important for understanding Jacob.

He is not just the chosen son. He is also the fleeing son.

He is not just carrying promise. He is carrying consequences.

And sometimes life feels like that. You can be moving under God's mercy and still be dealing with the fallout of your own choices. Genesis does not flatten that tension. It lets both be true.

Jacob is blessed.

And Jacob has to leave.

Now Genesis turns briefly back to Esau.

Genesis chapter 28, verses 6 through 9.

## Genesis 28:6-9

**Scripture**

6. Now Esau saw that Isaac had blessed Jacob and sent him away to Paddan Aram, to take him a wife from there, and that as he blessed him he gave him a command, saying, "You shall not take a wife of the daughters of Canaan;"

7. and that Jacob obeyed his father and his mother, and was gone to Paddan Aram.

8. Esau saw that the daughters of Canaan didn't please Isaac, his father.

9. So Esau went to Ishmael, and took, in addition to the wives that he had, Mahalath the daughter of Ishmael, Abraham's son, the sister of Nebaioth, to be his wife.

**Narrator**

Esau is watching.

He sees that Jacob has been blessed. He sees that Jacob has been told not to marry Canaanite women. And he realizes his own Canaanite wives have displeased his parents.

So Esau tries to respond.

He marries Mahalath, a daughter from Ishmael's line.

At first, that might look like Esau is trying to fix the problem. But Genesis phrases it carefully. He takes her in addition to the wives he already had. He is adding another marriage, not really healing the deeper issue.

This is Esau reacting to the outside of the problem without understanding the heart of it.

That happens in real life too. A person can see that something is wrong, make a quick religious-looking adjustment, and still not understand what trust and obedience actually require.

Esau is close to the covenant family, but he keeps missing the weight of the covenant.

And while Esau is trying to respond from home, Jacob is out on the road.

Now the story slows down.

Genesis chapter 28, verses 10 through 15.

## Genesis 28:10-15

**Scripture**

10. Jacob went out from Beersheba, and went toward Haran.

11. He came to a certain place, and stayed there all night, because the sun had set. He took one of the stones of the place, and put it under his head, and lay down in that place to sleep.

12. He dreamed and saw a stairway set upon the earth, and its top reached to heaven. Behold, the angels of God were ascending and descending on it.

13. Behold, Yahweh stood above it, and said, "I am Yahweh, the God of Abraham your father, and the God of Isaac. I will give the land you lie on to you and to your offspring.

14. Your offspring will be as the dust of the earth, and you will spread abroad to the west, and to the east, and to the north, and to the south. In you and in your offspring, all the families of the earth will be blessed.

15. Behold, I am with you, and will keep you, wherever you go, and will bring you again into this land. For I will not leave you until I have done that which I have spoken of to you."

**Narrator**

This is one of the great turning points in Jacob's life.

He leaves Beersheba and goes toward Haran. That is a long journey. He is leaving the land of promise and heading toward family territory far away.

Then night comes.

The text says he takes a stone and puts it under his head. That detail makes the scene feel lonely. Jacob is not in a warm house. He is not surrounded by servants. He is not sleeping in comfort. He is exposed on the road.

And there, he dreams.

He sees a stairway set on the earth, reaching to heaven, with angels of God ascending and descending.

The point is not just that Jacob sees something strange. The point is connection. Heaven and earth are not sealed off from each other. God is not distant from Jacob's road. The place Jacob thought was just a random campsite is actually a place where God is present and active.

Then Yahweh speaks.

He identifies Himself as the God of Abraham and Isaac. That matters because Jacob has grown up inside the covenant family, but now the promise is being spoken directly to him.

God promises land.

God promises offspring.

God promises blessing to all families of the earth through Jacob's offspring.

Those are Abrahamic promise words. Jacob is now hearing the same covenant future that shaped Abraham and Isaac.

But verse 15 is deeply personal.

God says, I am with you. I will keep you wherever you go. I will bring you back. I will not leave you until I have done what I promised.

Jacob is leaving home, but he is not leaving God's reach.

That is the comfort in this scene.

Jacob does not meet God because he finally became impressive. He meets God while he is afraid, unfinished, and on the run. The promise is not resting on Jacob's perfect character. The promise is resting on God's faithful word.

Now Jacob wakes up.

Genesis chapter 28, verses 16 through 22.

## Genesis 28:16-22

**Scripture**

16. Jacob awakened out of his sleep, and he said, "Surely Yahweh is in this place, and I didn't know it."

17. He was afraid, and said, "How awesome this place is! This is none other than God's house, and this is the gate of heaven."

18. Jacob rose up early in the morning, and took the stone that he had put under his head, and set it up for a pillar, and poured oil on its top.

19. He called the name of that place Bethel, but the name of the city was Luz at the first.

20. Jacob vowed a vow, saying, "If God will be with me, and will keep me in this way that I go, and will give me bread to eat, and clothing to put on,

21. so that I come again to my father's house in peace, and Yahweh will be my God,

22. then this stone, which I have set up for a pillar, will be God's house. Of all that you will give me I will surely give a tenth to you."

**Narrator**

Jacob wakes up and says, Yahweh is in this place, and I did not know it.

That sentence is honest.

Jacob did not arrive at Bethel expecting worship. He just needed somewhere to sleep. But God was there before Jacob realized it.

Bethel means house of God.

Jacob names the place because the place has become holy to him. The stone that was under his head becomes a pillar. He pours oil on it, marking it as set apart.

But listen carefully to Jacob's vow.

He says if God will be with me, if God will keep me, if God will give me bread and clothing, if I come back in peace, then Yahweh will be my God.

Jacob is responding, but he is still Jacob.

His faith is awakening, but it is not fully mature. He is not Abraham calmly building an altar after decades of walking with God. He is a scared man on the road, starting to realize that God has come close to him.

That is why this scene feels so real.

Some people meet God and still have a lot of growing to do.

Jacob's vow is not the finish line. It is the beginning of a long transformation.

And that matters for us.

A real encounter with God does not mean every fear disappears in one moment. It means God has stepped into the story. The road ahead may still be long, but now Jacob knows he is not alone on it.

Genesis 28 ends with Jacob naming the place Bethel.

Genesis 29 begins with him walking forward.

Genesis chapter 29, verses 1 through 8.

## Genesis 29:1-8

**Scripture**

1. Then Jacob went on his journey, and came to the land of the children of the east.

2. He looked, and saw a well in the field, and saw three flocks of sheep lying there by it. For out of that well they watered the flocks. The stone on the well's mouth was large.

3. There all the flocks were gathered. They rolled the stone from the well's mouth, and watered the sheep, and put the stone back on the well's mouth in its place.

4. Jacob said to them, "My relatives, where are you from?"

They said, "We are from Haran."

5. He said to them, "Do you know Laban, the son of Nahor?"

They said, "We know him."

6. He said to them, "Is it well with him?"

They said, "It is well. See, Rachel, his daughter, is coming with the sheep."

7. He said, "Behold, it is still the middle of the day, not time to gather the livestock together. Water the sheep, and go and feed them."

8. They said, "We can't, until all the flocks are gathered together, and they roll the stone from the well's mouth. Then we will water the sheep."

**Narrator**

Jacob arrives in the east and sees a well.

If you have been following Genesis, wells should feel familiar.

Back in Genesis 24, Abraham's servant found Rebekah at a well. That scene led to Isaac's marriage. Now Jacob, Rebekah's son, comes to a well and is about to meet Rachel.

In the ancient world, wells were life centers. People, animals, families, and travelers gathered around water. A well was not just a background object. It was a place where survival, community, and sometimes major story turns happened.

Jacob sees flocks waiting because a large stone covers the well. The shepherds say they cannot water the sheep until all the flocks are gathered and the stone is moved.

Then Rachel appears.

The story is setting the scene with hope.

Jacob has left home in fear. He met God on the road. Now he reaches the right area, finds people who know Laban, and sees Rachel coming with the sheep.

For a moment, it looks like the road is opening beautifully.

Genesis chapter 29, verses 9 through 14.

## Genesis 29:9-14

**Scripture**

9. While he was yet speaking with them, Rachel came with her father's sheep, for she kept them.

10. When Jacob saw Rachel the daughter of Laban, his mother's brother, and the sheep of Laban, his mother's brother, Jacob went near, and rolled the stone from the well's mouth, and watered the flock of Laban his mother's brother.

11. Jacob kissed Rachel, and lifted up his voice, and wept.

12. Jacob told Rachel that he was her father's relative, and that he was Rebekah's son. She ran and told her father.

13. When Laban heard the news of Jacob, his sister's son, he ran to meet Jacob, and embraced him, and kissed him, and brought him to his house. Jacob told Laban all these things.

14. Laban said to him, "Surely you are my bone and my flesh." Jacob stayed with him for a month.

**Narrator**

Rachel comes with her father's sheep.

Jacob sees her, and the scene bursts with emotion.

He rolls the stone away from the well and waters Laban's flock. This is a strong, dramatic action. The shepherds had been waiting for the group to gather, but Jacob moves the stone himself.

Then he kisses Rachel and weeps.

That does not mean romance is fully developed in one second. In family greeting culture, a kiss could be a greeting. But the weeping shows the emotional release. Jacob has been far from home, under pressure, and uncertain. Now he has found his mother's family.

Rachel runs to tell Laban.

Laban runs to meet Jacob, embraces him, kisses him, and brings him into the house.

At first, this feels like safety.

Laban says, you are my bone and my flesh.

That phrase means family closeness. Jacob has arrived among his relatives. But if you remember Laban from Genesis 24, you know he is not a simple background character. He is interested, calculating, and very aware of advantage.

Jacob has entered family territory.

But family territory is not always safe territory.

Genesis chapter 29, verses 15 through 20.

## Genesis 29:15-20

**Scripture**

15. Laban said to Jacob, "Because you are my relative, should you therefore serve me for nothing? Tell me, what will your wages be?"

16. Laban had two daughters. The name of the elder was Leah, and the name of the younger was Rachel.

17. Leah's eyes were weak, but Rachel was beautiful in form and attractive.

18. Jacob loved Rachel. He said, "I will serve you seven years for Rachel, your younger daughter."

19. Laban said, "It is better that I give her to you, than that I should give her to another man. Stay with me."

20. Jacob served seven years for Rachel. They seemed to him but a few days, for the love he had for her.

**Narrator**

Laban asks about wages.

Jacob has been staying with him for a month, and now Laban turns the family arrangement into a labor arrangement.

Then Genesis introduces Leah and Rachel.

Leah is the older daughter. Rachel is the younger. That detail will matter a lot, because the older and younger theme has been running through Jacob's life from the beginning. Jacob was the younger brother who received what Esau, the older brother, expected.

Now Jacob is in a house with an older daughter and a younger daughter.

And Jacob loves Rachel.

He offers to serve seven years for her.

That was serious. In that world, marriage involved family arrangements, bride price, labor, and household responsibility. Jacob does not arrive with wealth like Abraham's servant did in Genesis 24. So he offers labor.

The text says the seven years seemed like only a few days because of his love for Rachel.

That is one of the tenderest lines in Genesis.

But tenderness does not mean the situation is simple.

Laban's answer is vague. He does not clearly say, yes, Rachel will be yours at the end of seven years. He says it is better that I give her to you than to another man.

That sounds agreeable.

But Laban is leaving himself room.

And Jacob, who has used ambiguity and deception before, is about to be caught in someone else's ambiguity.

Genesis chapter 29, verses 21 through 25.

## Genesis 29:21-25

**Scripture**

21. Jacob said to Laban, "Give me my wife, for my days are fulfilled, that I may go in to her."

22. Laban gathered together all the men of the place, and made a feast.

23. In the evening, he took Leah his daughter, and brought her to Jacob. He went in to her.

24. Laban gave Zilpah his servant to his daughter Leah for a servant.

25. In the morning, behold, it was Leah! He said to Laban, "What is this you have done to me? Didn't I serve with you for Rachel? Why then have you deceived me?"

**Narrator**

This is the painful reversal.

Jacob finishes the seven years and asks for his wife.

Laban gathers the men of the place and makes a feast. This is public. This is formal. Everyone is involved.

But in the evening, Laban brings Leah to Jacob instead of Rachel.

The next morning, Jacob realizes what happened.

And the line hits hard: behold, it was Leah.

Jacob asks, why have you deceived me?

That question echoes through his whole story.

Jacob deceived Isaac in Genesis 27. He used clothing, darkness, family closeness, and his father's limited sight to take the blessing. Now Laban uses a feast, evening darkness, family custom, and Jacob's desire to deceive him.

Genesis is not saying Jacob deserved cruelty in some cheap way. Leah is also being used here. Rachel is wounded too. Laban's deception creates pain for everyone.

But the story is showing that deception has a way of coming back around.

Jacob is finally feeling what it is like to be on the receiving end of manipulation.

And notice Leah.

She is not treated like a full person by Laban. She is used as part of his plan. Jacob did not choose her. Rachel loses the wedding night she expected. The whole household is being shaped by Laban's control.

Sin rarely damages only one person.

It spreads through the room.

Genesis chapter 29, verses 26 through 30.

## Genesis 29:26-30

**Scripture**

26. Laban said, "It is not done so in our place, to give the younger before the firstborn.

27. Fulfill the week of this one, and we will give you the other also for the service which you will serve with me for seven more years."

28. Jacob did so, and fulfilled her week. He gave him Rachel his daughter as wife.

29. Laban gave Bilhah, his servant, to his daughter Rachel to be her servant.

30. He went in also to Rachel, and he loved also Rachel more than Leah, and served with him seven more years.

**Narrator**

Laban's explanation is sharp.

He says, it is not done so in our place, to give the younger before the firstborn.

That lands directly on Jacob's life.

Jacob, the younger, had taken the blessing connected to the older. Now Laban says, in our place, the older comes first.

Laban is not morally pure here. He is using custom to defend deception. But the words still sting because they touch the wound in Jacob's own story.

Jacob fulfills Leah's week, then receives Rachel also, and serves seven more years.

So now the family has two sisters married to the same man, one loved more than the other.

This is not presented as a healthy model. Genesis is showing us the brokenness that will shape Jacob's household for years.

Polygamy in these stories usually brings rivalry, grief, jealousy, and pain. The Bible records it honestly. It does not romanticize the damage.

Jacob loves Rachel more than Leah.

That one sentence becomes a doorway into deep sorrow.

And God sees it.

Genesis chapter 29, verses 31 through 35.

## Genesis 29:31-35

**Scripture**

31. Yahweh saw that Leah was hated, and he opened her womb, but Rachel was barren.

32. Leah conceived, and bore a son, and she named him Reuben. For she said, "Because Yahweh has looked at my affliction; for now my husband will love me."

33. She conceived again, and bore a son, and said, "Because Yahweh has heard that I am hated, he has therefore given me this son also." She named him Simeon.

34. She conceived again, and bore a son. She said, "Now this time my husband will be joined to me, because I have borne him three sons." Therefore his name was called Levi.

35. She conceived again, and bore a son. She said, "This time I will praise Yahweh." Therefore she named him Judah. Then she stopped bearing.

**Narrator**

This ending is heavy and beautiful at the same time.

The text says Yahweh saw that Leah was hated.

That word does not necessarily mean Jacob felt active violence toward her. It means she was unloved, rejected, or treated as second compared with Rachel. In the emotional world of this household, Leah is the unwanted wife.

But Yahweh sees her.

That matters.

Laban used Leah.

Jacob preferred Rachel.

The household measured Leah by whether she was loved.

But God saw her affliction.

Leah gives birth to Reuben and hopes Jacob will love her. She gives birth to Simeon and says Yahweh has heard that she is hated. She gives birth to Levi and hopes her husband will be joined to her.

You can hear the ache in the names.

She is not only having children. She is longing to be seen, loved, and valued.

Then Judah is born.

And Leah says, this time I will praise Yahweh.

Judah's birth is a major thread in the Bible.

From Judah will come the royal line. Later, King David will come from Judah. And much later, Jesus the Messiah will be called the Lion of the tribe of Judah.

That means God is doing something astonishing.

The line of kings will come through Leah, the woman who was overlooked and unloved.

God is building the future through the person the household did not honor.

That is not a small detail.

Genesis 29 ends by showing us that God's mercy sees into places human love has failed.

## Closing

Day 11 began with Jacob leaving home.

He had the blessing, but he did not have peace. He had promise, but he also had consequences.

Then God met him on the road.

At Bethel, Jacob learned that heaven was nearer than he knew. God promised presence, protection, return, land, descendants, and blessing for the nations.

But the holy encounter did not magically make Jacob mature.

He still had a road to walk.

He still entered Laban's house.

He still had to learn what deception feels like from the other side.

And inside that painful family system, God saw Leah.

That is one of the strongest messages of Day 11.

God meets Jacob when he is afraid and unfinished.

God sees Leah when she is unloved and overlooked.

God keeps His promise even when the family carrying the promise is full of weakness.

So if you carry nothing else from Genesis 28 and 29, carry this:

God is not limited to clean moments, settled places, or impressive people.

He can meet you on the road.

He can speak promise into fear.

He can keep working while you are still growing.

And He can see the pain that everyone else treats like background noise.

Tomorrow, Day 12 follows Jacob deeper into Laban's house. The family will grow, but the pain will grow too. Rachel and Leah will struggle, children will be born, labor will become conflict, and eventually God will begin turning Jacob back toward home.

For today, remember Bethel.

Jacob thought he was sleeping in an ordinary place.

But God was there.

And Jacob did not know it.

## Reusable Day Script Framework

Use this pattern for future Bible in One Year audio scripts:

1. Open by carrying the unresolved story tension from the previous day into the current day.
2. Welcome the listener only after the connection is emotionally clear.
3. Name the chapters and the main movements of the day.
4. Give three simple listening themes.
5. Read the Scripture in natural sections.
6. After each section, explain what happened, define important words or customs, connect repeated Bible patterns, and make the emotional pressure clear.
7. Keep the tone conversational, modern, and serious without sounding preachy.
8. Close by naming what the day teaches, then point toward tomorrow's story.
