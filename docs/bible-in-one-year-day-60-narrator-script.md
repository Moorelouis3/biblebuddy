# Bible in One Year - Day 60 Narrator Script

**Reading:** Joshua 16-19

## Opening

Welcome to Day 60 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are reading Joshua chapters 16 through 19.

The theme is inheritance for the tribes.

These chapters continue the distribution of the promised land.

They describe Ephraim, Manasseh, Benjamin, Simeon, Zebulun, Issachar, Asher, Naphtali, Dan, and Joshua's own inheritance.

There are many borders and city names.

But the point is not empty geography.

The land promised to the fathers is becoming ordered life for the tribes of Israel.

The inheritance is being measured, named, and received.

Day 60 helps us read the land lists as faithfulness, responsibility, and unfinished obedience.

## Joshua 16 verses 1 through 10

**Scripture**

Joshua 16 describes the lot for the children of Joseph.

It gives the borders for Ephraim.

The chapter ends by saying they did not drive out the Canaanites who dwelt in Gezer, but the Canaanites dwelt among Ephraim and served under tribute.

**Narrator**

Joshua 16 gives inheritance to the children of Joseph, especially Ephraim.

Joseph's family had become two tribes through Ephraim and Manasseh.

That connects this chapter back to Genesis.

Jacob blessed Joseph's sons long before Israel entered the land.

Now that blessing is taking geographical shape.

The borders are described carefully.

Again, this is not vague promise language.

God is giving a real portion to a real tribe.

The land has edges.

The inheritance can be walked.

Families can live there.

But the chapter ends with a warning.

Ephraim did not drive out the Canaanites in Gezer.

Instead, the Canaanites dwelt among them and served under tribute.

That may sound practical, but it signals incomplete obedience.

The LORD had warned Israel about leaving the nations and their worship in the land.

Turning enemies into forced labor may look useful, but it is not the same as full obedience.

This ending teaches us to read the inheritance chapters carefully.

The land is gift.

The promise is real.

But Israel's response is still mixed.

Blessing can be received incompletely.

Obedience can be compromised for convenience.

Joshua 16 therefore gives both gratitude and warning.

Ephraim receives inheritance.

Ephraim also leaves a problem in the land.

## Joshua 17 verses 1 through 18

**Scripture**

Joshua 17 describes the inheritance of Manasseh.

The daughters of Zelophehad receive inheritance among their father's brethren, as the LORD commanded Moses.

The children of Joseph say their portion is too small because they are a great people.

Joshua tells them to go up to the wood country and clear it.

They complain about the Canaanites with chariots of iron.

Joshua tells them they are a great people and shall drive out the Canaanites.

**Narrator**

Joshua 17 continues with Manasseh.

One of the most important details is the daughters of Zelophehad.

Their story began earlier in the Torah.

Their father had no sons, and they asked for inheritance so his name would not disappear.

The LORD had commanded Moses that they should receive an inheritance.

Now Joshua shows that command being honored in the land.

That matters.

The promise reaches family-level details.

God's word about these daughters is not forgotten when the land is divided.

The inheritance is given among their father's brethren.

This shows careful justice and covenant memory.

Then the children of Joseph complain.

They say they are a great people and need more than one lot.

Joshua's response is direct.

If you are a great people, go up to the wood country and clear land for yourselves.

They answer that the mountain is not enough and the Canaanites have chariots of iron.

That sounds like fear mixed with frustration.

Joshua does not simply give them an easier answer.

He says they are a great people and will drive out the Canaanites, though they have iron chariots.

This section teaches responsibility.

Inheritance is gift, but the tribe must still act in faith.

The presence of difficulty does not cancel the promise.

The Canaanites have chariots of iron.

But Israel has the LORD's promise.

Joshua 17 therefore places two stories side by side.

The daughters of Zelophehad show careful fulfillment of God's word.

The children of Joseph show the need for courage and responsibility inside the inheritance.

The land is not only received.

It must be occupied with faithfulness.

## Joshua 18 verses 1 through 28

**Scripture**

The whole congregation gathers at Shiloh and sets up the tabernacle.

There remain seven tribes that have not yet received inheritance.

Joshua asks how long they will be slack to go possess the land.

He sends three men from each tribe to describe the land.

The land is divided by lot before the LORD at Shiloh.

Benjamin receives its inheritance with borders and cities listed.

**Narrator**

Joshua 18 begins at Shiloh.

The tabernacle of the congregation is set up there.

That detail matters because worship is at the center of Israel's life in the land.

The land is not only about farms and borders.

The presence and worship of the LORD matter most.

Then Joshua confronts the seven remaining tribes.

How long are ye slack to go to possess the land?

That question is sharp.

The LORD has given the land, but the tribes are slow to take responsibility.

This teaches that receiving God's promise does not mean passivity.

The people must move forward in obedience.

Joshua sends men to walk through the land and describe it.

The inheritance is then divided by lot before the LORD.

That phrase is important.

The division is not merely political negotiation.

It happens before God.

Benjamin's inheritance is then described.

Borders are named.

Cities are listed.

Jericho, Bethel, Gibeon, Mizpeh, and many others appear.

For a beginner, these names may feel like a map we do not know.

But in the story, the names mean settlement.

They mean families will live in particular places.

They mean the promise is being organized.

Joshua 18 teaches worship, responsibility, and order.

The tabernacle is set up.

The slow tribes are challenged.

The land is described.

The lot is cast before the LORD.

Benjamin receives a portion.

## Joshua 19 verses 1 through 51

**Scripture**

Joshua 19 gives inheritances to Simeon, Zebulun, Issachar, Asher, Naphtali, and Dan.

Simeon's inheritance is within the inheritance of Judah because Judah's portion was too much.

Dan's territory is described, and Dan also goes up to fight Leshem.

After the tribes receive inheritance, the children of Israel give Joshua an inheritance at Timnath-serah.

The chapter says these are the inheritances Eleazar the priest, Joshua, and the heads of the fathers divided by lot in Shiloh before the LORD.

**Narrator**

Joshua 19 continues the land distribution.

Simeon receives cities within Judah's inheritance.

That detail shows adjustment and order.

Judah's portion was too much for them, so Simeon receives inheritance within it.

The land is being arranged for actual tribal life.

Then Zebulun, Issachar, Asher, Naphtali, and Dan receive portions.

Again, many city names appear.

These lists can feel long, but they are not meaningless.

Each name represents the promise becoming local.

The covenant is not floating above the ground.

It is touching towns, borders, families, and future generations.

Dan's inheritance includes tension.

The children of Dan go up and fight against Leshem, take it, and call it Dan after their father.

This shows the ongoing work of occupying and ordering the inheritance.

Then Joshua receives his inheritance.

That is a beautiful detail.

Joshua has led the people through the Jordan, through battles, and through the distribution of land.

Only after the tribes receive their inheritance does Joshua receive his own.

He is given Timnath-serah in mount Ephraim.

He builds the city and dwells there.

This shows humble leadership.

Joshua does not use his position to take first.

He serves the distribution, then receives his portion.

The chapter ends by naming Eleazar the priest, Joshua, and the heads of the fathers.

They divide the inheritance by lot in Shiloh before the LORD.

That ending ties the whole section together.

Priest.

Leader.

Tribal heads.

Shiloh.

Before the LORD.

The land is distributed with worship, leadership, and community order together.

Day 60 therefore teaches that the promised land becomes ordered life.

Ephraim receives inheritance but leaves Gezer unresolved.

Manasseh receives inheritance, and the daughters of Zelophehad are honored.

Joseph's sons are called to act with courage.

The tabernacle is set up at Shiloh.

Benjamin and the remaining tribes receive their portions.

Joshua receives last.

The promise becomes a map, but it is more than a map.

It is the LORD giving His people a place to live as His covenant people.

## Closing

That is Day 60.

Joshua 16 through 19 teaches inheritance for the tribes.

The land is divided with borders, cities, lots, leaders, and worship before the LORD.

The daughters of Zelophehad remind us that God's justice reaches family details.

The complaint of Joseph's sons reminds us that inheritance still calls for courage.

Shiloh reminds us that worship belongs at the center.

Joshua receiving last reminds us that faithful leadership serves before taking.

The promised land is becoming ordered life for the people of God.

## Full Lesson Review

Day 60 continues the inheritance section, and the main thing to hear is that the promised land is being made specific.

Joshua 16 begins with the children of Joseph.

Ephraim receives an inheritance.

The borders are described.

The cities are named.

But the chapter ends with an unresolved note.

They drave not out the Canaanites that dwelt in Gezer.

The Canaanites dwell among the Ephraimites unto this day, and serve under tribute.

That line is easy to pass over, but it matters.

Ephraim receives land, but possession is incomplete.

The Canaanites remain.

Tribute may look practical, but the text is showing compromise in the inheritance.

Israel is receiving the promise, yet not every tribe finishes the command.

Joshua 17 widens the story to Manasseh.

The chapter remembers Machir, the firstborn of Manasseh, a man of war.

Then it gives special attention to the daughters of Zelophehad.

Their names are listed.

Mahlah.

Noah.

Hoglah.

Milcah.

Tirzah.

They come before Eleazar the priest, Joshua the son of Nun, and the princes.

They remind them that the LORD commanded Moses to give them an inheritance among their brethren.

This is not a side issue.

It shows covenant justice reaching into family inheritance.

The daughters do not disappear because their father had no sons.

The LORD's earlier command is remembered.

They receive an inheritance among the brethren of their father.

That matters because the land promise is not only about military conquest.

It is also about right ordering.

It is about honoring what the LORD has commanded.

It is about making room for families according to God's justice.

Joshua 17 also shows the children of Joseph complaining.

They ask why they have been given only one lot and one portion, seeing they are a great people.

Joshua tells them that if they are a great people, they should go up to the wood country and cut down for themselves there.

They respond that the hill is not enough, and the Canaanites have chariots of iron.

Joshua answers with courage.

Thou art a great people, and hast great power.

Thou shalt not have one lot only.

The mountain shall be thine.

Though the Canaanites have iron chariots, and though they be strong, thou shalt drive them out.

That exchange is important.

Joseph's sons want more inheritance, but Joshua calls them to act in faith with what they have been given.

The obstacle is real.

The chariots are real.

The strength of the Canaanites is real.

But the promise calls for courage.

Joshua 18 then gathers the congregation at Shiloh.

The tabernacle of the congregation is set up there.

This detail is central.

The land distribution is not merely administrative.

Worship is placed at the center of Israel's life in the land.

The tabernacle stands among them.

The LORD's presence and worship shape the inheritance.

Yet seven tribes still have not received their inheritance.

Joshua asks, How long are ye slack to go to possess the land, which the LORD God of your fathers hath given you.

That question should land with force.

The land is given.

But they are slack to possess it.

Again, Joshua holds together gift and responsibility.

He sends men to walk through the land and describe it according to inheritance.

They write it in a book.

They return to Joshua.

Joshua casts lots for them in Shiloh before the LORD.

That phrase matters.

Before the LORD.

The land is not divided by manipulation.

The lots are cast before the LORD.

Benjamin receives a portion.

Its borders and cities are named.

Jericho.

Bethhoglah.

Bethel.

Gibeon.

Ramah.

Mizpeh.

Jerusalem is mentioned within Benjamin's border descriptions.

Again, this is real geography for real covenant life.

Joshua 19 continues with Simeon, Zebulun, Issachar, Asher, Naphtali, and Dan.

Each tribe receives cities and borders.

Simeon's inheritance is within the inheritance of Judah because Judah's portion was too much for them.

That detail shows adjustment inside the distribution.

Zebulun receives its cities.

Issachar receives its cities.

Asher receives coastal and northern places.

Naphtali receives its portion.

Dan receives its lot, but the coast of the children of Dan goes out too little for them, and they fight against Leshem.

Even in these lists, the story keeps showing that inheritance must be possessed.

Then comes Joshua's inheritance.

When they had made an end of dividing the land for inheritance by their coasts, the children of Israel gave an inheritance to Joshua the son of Nun among them.

That order is beautiful.

Joshua receives last.

The leader who led the people into the land does not seize first place.

He serves the distribution of the tribes, and then receives what the LORD commanded.

They give him Timnath-serah in mount Ephraim.

He builds the city and dwells therein.

The chapter closes by naming Eleazar the priest, Joshua the son of Nun, and the heads of the fathers of the tribes.

They divide inheritance by lot in Shiloh before the LORD, at the door of the tabernacle of the congregation.

So they made an end of dividing the country.

That final sentence is large.

The division of the land reaches a major completion.

Day 60 therefore teaches several connected truths.

First, inheritance is a gift from the LORD.

The land is not random.

The borders are not meaningless.

The cities are not filler.

They are the shape of God's promise entering daily life.

Second, inheritance requires faithful possession.

Ephraim leaves Gezer unresolved.

Joseph's sons must face chariots of iron.

Seven tribes are slack to possess the land.

Dan must fight for space.

The promise does not remove the call to obey.

Third, inheritance must be ordered by God's justice.

The daughters of Zelophehad receive what the LORD commanded.

Their names are remembered because the LORD's instruction matters in family detail.

Fourth, worship belongs at the center.

Shiloh and the tabernacle are not background details.

Israel's life in the land must gather around the presence and worship of the LORD.

Fifth, leadership is service.

Joshua receives last.

He does not use leadership to take first.

He leads the people into their inheritance and then receives his own.

For the listener, Day 60 is a call to trust God's ordered faithfulness.

God is not vague with His promises.

He brings them down into places, families, borders, and responsibilities.

He remembers daughters who might otherwise be overlooked.

He confronts tribes that hesitate.

He calls strong people to act with courage.

He centers worship before administration.

He honors servant leadership.

So when these chapters name borders and cities, hear more than geography.

Hear the LORD teaching His people how to live in the gift He has given.

Hear the LORD turning promise into ordered life.

Hear the LORD calling His people not to be slack, not to compromise, not to complain without acting, and not to forget worship.

The land is being divided.

The tribes are being placed.

The tabernacle is set up.

Joshua receives last.

The promised life is taking shape before the LORD.

## Final Meditation

Day 60 asks the reader to care about ordered faithfulness.

The chapters are full of borders, portions, lots, complaints, assignments, and cities.

That may not feel dramatic at first.

But this is what promise looks like when it moves into daily life.

The LORD did not only bring Israel through the sea.

He did not only feed Israel in the wilderness.

He did not only bring Israel across Jordan.

He also placed tribes in inheritance.

He cared about who received what.

He cared about the daughters of Zelophehad.

He cared about Shiloh.

He cared about the tribes that were slow to possess.

He cared about Joshua receiving last.

This teaches us that God's faithfulness reaches details.

Ephraim's unresolved Canaanites show that compromise can remain inside a gift if obedience is incomplete.

Manasseh's inheritance shows that families matter inside covenant promises.

The daughters of Zelophehad show that God's command protects people who might otherwise be forgotten.

The complaint from Joseph's sons shows that wanting more must be joined to courageous obedience.

The tabernacle at Shiloh shows that worship cannot be pushed to the edge of life.

The seven tribes still waiting show that delay can become disobedience when God has already given the land.

Joshua's inheritance at the end shows leadership shaped by service.

Those are not small lessons.

They are the texture of covenant life.

Day 60 also helps us understand the difference between receiving and possessing.

Receiving says, the LORD has given.

Possessing says, we must faithfully walk in what the LORD has given.

Both matter.

If we only speak of receiving, we may become passive.

If we only speak of possessing, we may forget grace.

Joshua holds them together.

The land is the LORD's gift.

The tribes must still go up, describe it, receive it, and live in it.

The lots are cast before the LORD.

The tabernacle stands before the people.

The final inheritance is not treated like a human land grab.

It is received in the presence of God.

That is the image to carry away.

The people of God should not organize life as if God is absent.

Their maps, homes, families, leaders, and future must stand before the LORD.

So Day 60 is not simply about where tribes lived.

It is about the LORD forming His people into a worshiping, ordered, responsible community in the land He promised.

The promise becomes place.

The place becomes responsibility.

Responsibility must remain before the LORD.

That is the heart of this day.

One final thing should be clear from Joshua 16 through 19.

The inheritance chapters are not trying to entertain the reader with constant surprise.

They are teaching stability.

They show the LORD placing His people.

They show leadership acting before the LORD.

They show families and tribes receiving what was assigned.

They show warnings where obedience is incomplete.

They show worship in the center at Shiloh.

This is what covenant promise looks like when it becomes a home.

Not every line is dramatic, but every line belongs to the story of God keeping His word.

The listener should hear these chapters as the sound of settlement under the LORD.

The wandering people are being placed.

The promise is being mapped.

The tribes are being named.

The tabernacle is standing.

The leader is serving.

The unfinished work is still being named.

That is a full and honest picture of life before God.

The chapter group also reminds us that inheritance can reveal character.

Some tribes receive and still leave work unfinished.

Some ask for more, and Joshua calls them to act with courage.

Some have waited for justice, like the daughters of Zelophehad, and the LORD's command gives them a place.

Some are slow, and Joshua asks why they are slack.

Joshua himself waits until the tribes receive their portions before receiving his own.

So the land distribution is not spiritually neutral.

It reveals whether people will trust, obey, serve, and remember the LORD.

The same gift can expose faith or hesitation.

The same promise can call one person to courage and another away from complaint.

The same inheritance can become worship when it is received before the LORD, or compromise when obedience stops halfway.

That is why Day 60 has so much value.

It teaches that the promised life must be received with a faithful heart.

The LORD gives the land.

His people must live in it as His people.
