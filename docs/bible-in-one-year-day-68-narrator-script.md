# Day 68 Narrator Script

## Opening

Welcome to Day 68 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are reading Ruth 3 and 4, then First Samuel 1 and 2.

The title for today is Redemption and Samuel's Birth.

This day moves from Ruth asking for redemption to Hannah praying for a son.

At first, those may feel like two separate family stories.

But they belong together in the Bible's larger movement.

Ruth shows the LORD preserving the line that will lead to David.

First Samuel shows the LORD raising Samuel, the prophet who will stand at the turning point between the judges and the kings.

Both stories begin quietly.

Both stories involve vulnerable women.

Both stories include grief, prayer, faith, and God's hidden work.

And both stories teach that the LORD often begins public renewal in places that look small to everyone else.

## Ruth 3

Ruth 3 begins with Naomi seeking rest for Ruth.

Rest in Ruth does not mean laziness.

It means settled security.

It means a protected future.

It means Ruth no longer living as a vulnerable widow gleaning in the fields without a household of her own.

Naomi identifies Boaz as their kindred.

He is a near kinsman.

That matters because Ruth is not only a love story.

It is a redemption story.

Family land, family name, widowhood, and covenant kindness are all involved.

Naomi tells Ruth to go to the threshing floor.

Modern readers may find the scene strange.

But the key is Ruth's request.

When Boaz wakes and asks who she is, Ruth says, I am Ruth thine handmaid: spread therefore thy skirt over thine handmaid; for thou art a near kinsman.

She is asking Boaz to cover, protect, and redeem.

She is not manipulating him.

She is making a bold and humble appeal based on his family role.

Boaz responds with blessing.

He calls her daughter.

He recognizes her kindness.

He says she has not followed young men, whether poor or rich.

He promises to do all that she requires, but he also names a complication.

There is a nearer kinsman than he.

That detail matters.

Boaz will not skip righteousness to get what he wants.

He protects Ruth.

He protects the legal order.

He protects the honor of the situation.

He tells Ruth to stay until morning.

He sends her home with six measures of barley.

Naomi understands the sign.

She says Boaz will not be in rest until he has finished the thing this day.

Ruth 3 teaches courageous faith and righteous restraint together.

Ruth is bold.

Boaz is honorable.

Naomi is hoping.

The LORD is quietly moving the story toward redemption.

## Ruth 4

Ruth 4 brings the matter to the gate.

The gate was the public place where legal matters were witnessed.

Boaz sits there.

The nearer kinsman comes by.

Boaz gathers ten men of the elders.

This redemption will not be hidden.

It will be handled publicly and rightly.

At first, the nearer kinsman is willing to redeem the land.

But when Boaz explains that the matter includes Ruth the Moabitess, the widow of Mahlon, he refuses.

He says he cannot redeem it for himself, lest he mar his own inheritance.

So Boaz redeems.

The custom with the shoe confirms the transfer.

The people and elders become witnesses.

Boaz takes Ruth.

The LORD gives her conception.

She bears a son.

The women speak to Naomi.

They say, Blessed be the LORD, which hath not left thee this day without a kinsman.

They say the child will be a restorer of life and a nourisher of old age.

That is beautiful because Naomi once said she came home empty.

Now a child is placed in her bosom.

The women say there is a son born to Naomi.

His name is Obed.

He becomes the father of Jesse, the father of David.

This ending is huge.

Ruth's small story is not small in God's plan.

A famine, a grieving widow, a loyal Moabite woman, a field, a redeemer, a public gate, and a child all become part of the line leading to David.

The book ends with genealogy because God is building a future.

Ordinary faithfulness is being woven into royal promise.

## First Samuel 1

First Samuel begins with another family story and another grieving woman.

Elkanah has two wives, Peninnah and Hannah.

Peninnah has children.

Hannah has no children.

Peninnah provokes Hannah sore, year by year.

Hannah weeps and does not eat.

Elkanah loves Hannah, but he cannot remove her grief.

The chapter shows pain honestly.

Hannah's sorrow is not dismissed.

Her longing is not mocked by God.

She goes to the house of the LORD in Shiloh.

She is in bitterness of soul.

She prays unto the LORD and weeps sore.

She vows that if the LORD gives her a son, she will give him unto the LORD all the days of his life.

Eli sees her lips moving and misunderstands her.

He thinks she is drunken.

Hannah answers with humility.

She says she is a woman of a sorrowful spirit.

She says she has poured out her soul before the LORD.

That phrase is one of the great pictures of prayer in Scripture.

Prayer is not always neat.

Prayer can be grief poured out before God.

Eli tells her to go in peace.

Her countenance is no more sad.

The answer is not visible yet, but her burden has been placed before the LORD.

The LORD remembers Hannah.

She bears a son and calls his name Samuel, because she asked him of the LORD.

Then Hannah keeps her vow.

When Samuel is weaned, she brings him to the house of the LORD.

She says, For this child I prayed.

Then she says she has lent him to the LORD.

Samuel's life begins in prayer, promise, and surrender.

First Samuel 1 teaches that God can begin a national turning point through one woman's anguish and faith.

## First Samuel 2

First Samuel 2 begins with Hannah's prayer.

Her horn is exalted in the LORD.

She rejoices in God's salvation.

She says there is none holy as the LORD.

Her song is full of reversals.

The bows of the mighty are broken.

They that stumbled are girded with strength.

The full hire themselves out for bread.

The hungry cease from hunger.

The barren bears seven.

The LORD kills and makes alive.

The LORD brings low and lifts up.

This song is not only personal thanksgiving.

It reveals how Hannah understands God.

The LORD raises the lowly.

The LORD humbles the proud.

The LORD guards the feet of His saints.

The LORD will give strength unto His king.

That last line matters because Israel does not yet have a king.

Hannah's song points forward.

Then the chapter sets Samuel's faithful growth beside the corruption of Eli's sons.

Hophni and Phinehas are sons of Belial.

They know not the LORD.

They abuse the sacrifices.

They make men abhor the offering of the LORD.

This is spiritual leadership doing spiritual damage.

Religious position does not mean faithful obedience.

Meanwhile, Samuel ministers before the LORD.

His mother makes him a little coat and brings it year by year.

The detail is tender.

Samuel is dedicated to the LORD, but Hannah's love remains visible.

The LORD visits Hannah, and she has more children.

Samuel grows before the LORD.

Eli warns his sons, but they do not listen.

Then a man of God comes to Eli with judgment.

The LORD says, Them that honour me I will honour, and they that despise me shall be lightly esteemed.

God promises to raise up a faithful priest who will do according to what is in His heart and mind.

First Samuel 2 therefore gives both hope and warning.

Samuel is growing.

Eli's house is under judgment.

God will not ignore corrupt worship.

God will provide faithful service.

## Full Lesson Review

Day 68 shows redemption and renewal beginning quietly.

Ruth does not look powerful.

She is a Moabite widow.

Yet she becomes part of the line of David.

Hannah does not look powerful.

She is a grieving woman praying in bitterness of soul.

Yet her son Samuel will become one of the most important prophets in Israel's history.

Boaz's kindness at the gate matters.

Hannah's prayer at Shiloh matters.

Naomi's restoration matters.

Samuel's little coat matters.

God works through details that people might overlook.

This day also teaches that true faithfulness is practical.

Boaz handles redemption publicly and rightly.

Ruth acts with courage and humility.

Hannah keeps her vow after the LORD answers.

Samuel ministers before the LORD as a child.

Faith is not only emotion.

It becomes action.

It becomes obedience.

It becomes generosity.

It becomes prayer.

It becomes surrender.

The contrast with Eli's sons is important.

They have religious position, but not faithful hearts.

They are near holy things, but they despise the LORD's offering.

Samuel is small, but he is growing before the LORD.

So Day 68 asks the reader to see the difference between appearance and faithfulness.

The widow may be central to God's plan.

The praying woman may be the doorway to national renewal.

The child may become the prophet.

The priests may be under judgment.

The LORD sees rightly.

He raises the lowly.

He brings down the proud.

He prepares His future through faithful people.

## Closing

That is Day 68.

Ruth 3 and 4 show redemption through Boaz and the birth of Obed.

First Samuel 1 and 2 show Hannah's prayer, Samuel's birth, Hannah's song, and judgment on Eli's corrupt house.

The LORD is preparing David's line through Ruth.

The LORD is preparing prophetic leadership through Samuel.

Renewal begins quietly, but it is not small in the hands of God.

She asks for covering.

She entrusts herself to a righteous process.

Boaz's response matters because he has power in the situation.

He could exploit Ruth.

He could act secretly.

He could ignore the nearer kinsman.

Instead, he protects her, honors her, and moves toward public righteousness.

That is one of the quiet beauties of Ruth.

The story does not only celebrate affection.

It celebrates covenant integrity.

Redemption is handled with witnesses.

The vulnerable person is not erased.

Ruth the Moabitess is named.

The dead man's name is remembered.

Naomi's future is restored.

The child born to Ruth is also a comfort to Naomi.

This is redemption reaching backward and forward.

It honors the dead.

It protects the living.

It opens the future.

Then the genealogy reminds us that God is doing even more than the characters can see.

Boaz, Obed, Jesse, David.

Ruth's loyalty and Boaz's righteousness become part of the royal line.

The reader now understands that the answer to Judges is already growing in the family of Ruth and Boaz.

First Samuel begins in another house with another sorrow.

Hannah's grief is not the same as Naomi's, but both women know emptiness.

Naomi lost husband and sons.

Hannah longs for a son and is provoked year by year.

Hannah brings that grief before the LORD.

She does not turn it into bitterness against God.

She pours out her soul.

That teaches a deep lesson about prayer.

Prayer is not pretending to be calm when the soul is wounded.

Prayer is bringing the wound before the LORD.

Hannah's vow also shows surrender.

She asks for a son, but she does not clutch the gift as if it belongs only to her.

She gives Samuel to the LORD.

The child she prayed for becomes the servant God will use for Israel.

Then Hannah's song teaches how she interprets the answer.

She does not merely say, I got what I wanted.

She praises the LORD who raises the lowly and brings down the proud.

She sees her story inside God's larger way of ruling the world.

That is why her song can look ahead to the king.

Her personal joy becomes prophetic worship.

The contrast with Eli's sons sharpens the lesson.

Hophni and Phinehas have position.

Samuel has smallness.

They have office.

Samuel has growth before the LORD.

They are near sacrifices but do not know the LORD.

Samuel is a child, but he ministers before the LORD.

The chapter is teaching us how God sees.

He is not impressed by religious status that despises Him.

He honors those who honor Him.

He promises faithful service where corrupt service has failed.

This matters for the larger Bible story.

Israel is moving toward kingship.

But before the king, God raises a prophet.

Before public leadership, there is a praying mother.

Before national change, there is a child dedicated to the LORD.

Day 68 therefore tells us not to despise hidden beginnings.

A widow's request can belong to the line of David.

A mother's prayer can belong to the rise of Samuel.

A little coat brought year by year can be part of faithful love.

A song of praise can announce how God rules the proud and the lowly.

The LORD is working through covenant kindness, prayer, and faithful service.

He is preparing what Israel does not yet know it needs.
