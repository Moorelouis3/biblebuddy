# Bible in One Year - Day 54 Narrator Script

**Reading:** Deuteronomy 26-29

## Opening

Welcome to Day 54 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are reading Deuteronomy chapters 26 through 29.

The theme is blessing, curse, and covenant renewal.

Moses is bringing Israel to a serious covenant moment.

They are near the land.

They have heard laws about worship, justice, mercy, leadership, war, family, work, and daily life.

Now Moses calls them to remember the rescue story, write the law plainly, answer Amen to covenant warnings, hear blessing and curse, and renew the covenant in Moab.

This is a weighty day.

It is also a clarifying day.

God has spoken.

The people must respond.

## Deuteronomy 26 verses 1 through 19

**Scripture**

Moses commands Israel to bring firstfruits when they enter the land.

The worshiper confesses the story of Jacob, Egypt, affliction, deliverance, and the good land.

The third-year tithe is given for the Levite, stranger, fatherless, and widow.

Israel avouches the LORD to be their God, and the LORD avouches Israel to be His special people.

**Narrator**

Deuteronomy 26 begins with firstfruits.

When Israel receives the land, they must not treat the harvest as if it came from their own hand alone.

They bring the first of the fruit before the LORD.

That means worship comes before self-congratulation.

Then the worshiper speaks a confession.

A Syrian ready to perish was my father.

This points back to Jacob.

The family was small and vulnerable.

They went down into Egypt.

They became a great nation there, but Egypt afflicted them.

They cried to the LORD.

The LORD heard.

The LORD brought them out with a mighty hand.

The LORD brought them into a land flowing with milk and honey.

This is not only history.

It is worship memory.

The person holding fruit in the land must remember bondage in Egypt.

The basket of firstfruits tells the rescue story.

Then Moses returns to the third-year tithe.

The Levite, stranger, fatherless, and widow are fed.

Again, worship and mercy belong together.

The chapter ends with covenant language.

Israel has avouched the LORD to be their God.

That means they have declared, confessed, and owned Him as their God.

The LORD has avouched Israel to be His special people.

Covenant is relationship with obligation and privilege.

They are called to keep His commandments.

He sets them high in praise, name, and honor.

Day 54 begins by teaching that blessing must be received with confession, gratitude, mercy, and obedience.

## Deuteronomy 27 verses 1 through 26

**Scripture**

Moses commands Israel to set up great stones when they cross Jordan.

They must write the law plainly on them.

They must build an altar of whole stones and offer sacrifices.

The tribes are divided between Gerizim and Ebal.

The Levites speak covenant curses, and all the people answer Amen.

**Narrator**

Deuteronomy 27 looks ahead to a public covenant moment in the land.

The law must be written on stones.

Write them very plainly.

That phrase matters.

God's word is not meant to be hidden, vague, or available only to a few.

It is placed publicly before the people.

The stones stand as witness.

Israel enters the land under the written word of God.

Then an altar is built.

The stones must be whole.

No iron tool is lifted on them.

This connects obedience and worship.

The law is written, and sacrifices are offered.

Israel cannot separate hearing God's word from coming before Him.

Then the curses are spoken.

Many of them deal with hidden sins.

Secret idolatry.

Dishonoring parents.

Moving a neighbor's landmark.

Misleading the blind.

Perverting judgment for the stranger, fatherless, and widow.

Sexual sin.

Secret murder.

Bribes.

Not confirming all the words of the law.

After each curse, the people say Amen.

Amen means let it be so.

They are agreeing that the LORD's judgment is right.

This is serious because covenant obedience is public.

Israel is not allowed to pretend hidden sin does not matter.

The whole community hears the warnings.

The whole community answers.

The law is plain.

The altar is present.

The people are accountable.

## Deuteronomy 28 verses 1 through 68

**Scripture**

Moses announces blessings if Israel hearkens diligently to the voice of the LORD.

Blessing comes in the city, field, fruit, basket, store, coming in, going out, victory, work, and land.

Then Moses announces long curses if Israel refuses the LORD.

The curses include confusion, disease, defeat, famine, siege, exile, and return toward bondage.

**Narrator**

Deuteronomy 28 is one of the strongest chapters in the Torah.

It begins with blessing.

If thou shalt hearken diligently.

That phrase means listen carefully with obedience.

The blessings touch every part of life.

City and field.

Family and animals.

Basket and store.

Coming in and going out.

Enemies defeated.

Work established.

The LORD's name placed on His people.

The picture is full life under covenant blessing.

But the curses are much longer.

That length is meant to make the warning heavy.

Moses is not trying to entertain Israel.

He is warning them with spiritual seriousness.

If Israel refuses the LORD's voice, the life of the land will unravel.

The same places that could be blessed become places of curse.

City.

Field.

Basket.

Store.

Body.

Family.

Harvest.

Work.

Battle.

The chapter becomes painful to read because rebellion against God is not small.

The curses include siege and exile.

That means Moses is looking ahead to what covenant unfaithfulness can bring.

Later in the Bible, Israel's story will echo these warnings.

The point is not that God enjoys judgment.

The point is that covenant rejection destroys.

The LORD is life.

Turning from Him is not harmless.

For beginners, this chapter can feel overwhelming.

Read it slowly.

The blessing section shows what life under God's favor looks like.

The curse section shows how serious it is to reject His word after receiving His covenant.

Deuteronomy 28 is meant to make Israel tremble before choosing rebellion.

## Deuteronomy 29 verses 1 through 29

**Scripture**

Moses renews the covenant in Moab.

He reminds Israel of what they saw in Egypt, how God led them forty years, how their clothes and shoes did not wear out, and how Sihon and Og were defeated.

Everyone stands before the LORD to enter covenant.

Moses warns against secret idolatry and stubborn self-blessing.

The chapter ends by saying the secret things belong to the LORD, but the revealed things belong to Israel and their children forever, that they may do all the words of this law.

**Narrator**

Deuteronomy 29 renews the covenant in Moab.

Moses reminds the people of what they have seen.

Egypt.

Signs.

Wonders.

The wilderness.

Clothes that did not wax old.

Shoes that did not wear out.

Victory over Sihon and Og.

God has sustained them.

Yet Moses also says the LORD had not given them a heart to perceive, eyes to see, and ears to hear until that day.

That shows the deep need for spiritual understanding.

Seeing events is not the same as understanding them rightly.

The covenant includes everyone.

Captains, elders, officers, men, little ones, wives, the stranger, the hewer of wood, and the drawer of water.

No one stands outside the seriousness of this moment.

Then Moses warns against the person who hears the curse and blesses himself in his heart.

That phrase is chilling.

It describes someone who hears God's warning but privately tells himself he will be fine while he walks in stubbornness.

This is false peace.

It is the heart saying, judgment will not reach me.

Moses says the LORD will not spare that person.

Secret idolatry is not safe.

Stubbornness is not safe.

Religious self-assurance without obedience is not safe.

The chapter ends with one of the most famous lines in Deuteronomy.

The secret things belong unto the LORD our God.

But those things which are revealed belong unto us and to our children forever.

That means Israel does not know everything.

Some things remain with God.

But what God has revealed is enough for obedience.

They are not responsible for secret things.

They are responsible for revealed things.

God has spoken His law.

Now they must do it.

## Holding the Day Together

Before we close Day 54, hold the whole reading together.

Deuteronomy 26 teaches confession.

When Israel brings firstfruits, they must remember the rescue story.

The land is not a place for forgetting.

It is a place for worship.

Deuteronomy 27 teaches public covenant accountability.

The law is written plainly.

The altar is built.

The curses are spoken.

The people answer Amen.

Deuteronomy 28 teaches blessing and curse.

Obedience leads toward life under God's favor.

Disobedience leads toward unraveling, judgment, and exile.

Deuteronomy 29 renews the covenant.

Everyone stands before the LORD.

No one may hide secret idolatry behind religious confidence.

The revealed word must be obeyed.

This day is intense because covenant is serious.

God is not playing with Israel.

He has rescued them.

He has sustained them.

He has spoken to them.

He has warned them.

He has brought them near the land.

Now the people must not enter casually.

They must enter with memory, gratitude, obedience, reverence, and holy fear.

For beginners, Day 54 helps explain why the rest of the Old Testament keeps returning to covenant faithfulness and unfaithfulness.

The blessings and curses of Deuteronomy become a lens for Israel's future.

When later prophets warn the people, they are not inventing a new message.

They are calling Israel back to the covenant word already spoken.

There is one more layer to Day 54.

This reading teaches Israel how to stand inside the story without rewriting it.

Deuteronomy 26 gives them the words to say over the firstfruits.

That is important.

When people become comfortable, they often tell the story in a way that makes themselves look central.

They say, my strength got this.

My wisdom built this.

My work secured this.

But the firstfruits confession does not begin with Israel's strength.

It begins with weakness.

A vulnerable father.

Affliction in Egypt.

Crying out to the LORD.

Deliverance by God's hand.

The good land as gift.

The basket of fruit is held in hands that must tell the truth.

This harvest is grace.

This land is grace.

This life is grace.

Deuteronomy 27 then makes the word public.

Write it plainly.

That phrase matters for every generation.

God's people do not need a hidden covenant that only experts can see.

The words are placed before the people.

The curses are spoken aloud.

The people answer Amen.

That means covenant is not treated like background music.

It is heard.

It is answered.

It is owned.

Deuteronomy 28 then shows the two roads clearly.

Blessing is not vague.

It touches city, field, family, food, work, and security.

Curse is not vague either.

It touches the same places with unraveling and grief.

Moses is teaching that obedience and rebellion are not equal paths with equal endings.

One path receives life from the LORD.

The other path turns from life and meets destruction.

The length of the curse section is not accidental.

It presses seriousness into the listener.

Do not play with covenant rebellion.

Do not assume the land will protect you if you reject the God who gave it.

Do not think religious history can replace present obedience.

Then Deuteronomy 29 brings everyone into the covenant moment.

Leaders and children.

Men and women.

Native-born and stranger.

Woodcutters and water carriers.

No one is too important to stand under the covenant.

No one is too ordinary to be included.

The warning against secret self-blessing is one of the most searching parts of the day.

The person hears the warning but says in his heart, I shall have peace.

That is dangerous religion.

It uses covenant language while keeping a stubborn heart.

Moses says that is not safe.

God is not fooled by private rebellion wearing public confidence.

Finally, the secret things belong to the LORD.

That line gives humility.

Israel will not know everything.

They will not control everything.

They will not explain every hidden thing.

But the revealed things belong to them and their children.

That gives responsibility.

Do what God has shown.

Obey what He has spoken.

Teach what He has revealed.

For Bible Buddy readers, this is a strong place to pause.

We often want secret things before obeying revealed things.

We want answers to mysteries while neglecting clear commands.

Deuteronomy turns us around.

The hidden belongs to God.

The revealed is enough for faithfulness.

Day 54 therefore teaches memory, clarity, warning, humility, and obedience.
