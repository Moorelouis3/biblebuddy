# Day 83 Narrator Script

## Opening

Welcome to Day 83 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are reading First Kings 4 through 7.

The title for today is Solomon's Wisdom and Temple Preparations.

This day shows Solomon's kingdom in order and abundance.

His officials are named.

His wisdom spreads.

His provision is great.

Then Solomon prepares to build the temple.

Hiram of Tyre helps with timber and skilled workers.

The temple is built with careful measurements, costly materials, carved beauty, and holy purpose.

The day is full of details, and those details matter.

The temple is not thrown together.

It is built as a house for the name of the LORD.

## First Kings 4

First Kings 4 describes Solomon's kingdom administration.

Solomon reigns over all Israel.

The chapter names priests, scribes, recorders, officers, and governors.

The structure shows order.

The kingdom is not merely a throne with a crown.

It is a whole people governed through offices, provision, judgment, and responsibility.

Judah and Israel are many, as the sand by the sea in multitude.

They eat, drink, and make merry.

Solomon has dominion over the kingdoms from the river to the border of Egypt.

The chapter describes daily provisions for Solomon's household.

The amount is huge.

It shows abundance, but also the weight of royal administration.

Solomon has peace on all sides.

Judah and Israel dwell safely, every man under his vine and under his fig tree.

That image matters.

It pictures security, settled life, and blessing.

The chapter then describes Solomon's wisdom.

God gives him wisdom, understanding, and largeness of heart.

His wisdom exceeds the wisdom of all the children of the east country and all Egypt.

He speaks three thousand proverbs and his songs are a thousand and five.

He speaks of trees, beasts, birds, creeping things, and fish.

People from all kings of the earth come to hear his wisdom.

Solomon's wisdom is public, practical, poetic, and famous.

## First Kings 5

First Kings 5 begins with Hiram king of Tyre sending servants to Solomon because he loved David.

Solomon sends word to Hiram.

He says David could not build a house for the name of the LORD because of wars.

Now the LORD has given Solomon rest on every side.

There is neither adversary nor evil occurrent.

Solomon intends to build a house for the name of the LORD, as God spoke to David.

Hiram rejoices and blesses the LORD.

He sends cedar and fir timber from Lebanon.

Solomon provides food for Hiram's household.

The two make a league.

The chapter also describes labor.

Solomon raises a levy out of Israel.

There are burden bearers, hewers in the mountains, and rulers over the work.

Great stones, costly stones, and hewed stones are prepared to lay the foundation.

The temple project is spiritual, but it is also physical.

It requires wood, stone, labor, skill, and covenant cooperation.

## First Kings 6

First Kings 6 gives the building of the temple.

The chapter dates the work in relation to the Exodus.

In the four hundred and eightieth year after Israel came out of Egypt, Solomon begins to build the house of the LORD.

That connection matters.

The temple is tied to redemption history.

The God who brought Israel out of Egypt now has His house built in the land.

The chapter gives measurements for the house, porch, windows, chambers, and inner sanctuary.

The stones are made ready before being brought, so no hammer, axe, or tool of iron is heard in the house while it is built.

The LORD speaks to Solomon during the building.

If Solomon walks in God's statutes, executes His judgments, keeps His commandments, and walks in them, God will perform His word with David.

God will dwell among the children of Israel and not forsake His people.

The building is beautiful, but obedience still matters.

Gold covers the house.

Cherubims, palm trees, and open flowers are carved.

The oracle, the most holy place, is prepared.

The chapter ends by noting the years of building.

The temple is glorious, ordered, and deeply symbolic.

## First Kings 7

First Kings 7 begins with Solomon building his own house.

It takes thirteen years.

The chapter describes royal buildings and then returns to temple furnishings.

Hiram, a skilled worker in brass, is brought from Tyre.

He makes pillars, chapiters, networks, pomegranates, the molten sea, bases, lavers, pots, shovels, and basins.

The names of the two pillars are Jachin and Boaz.

The work is detailed because worship is embodied.

The temple has structure, beauty, weight, and craftsmanship.

Gold furnishings are also made: altar, table, candlesticks, flowers, lamps, tongs, bowls, snuffers, basins, spoons, censers, and hinges.

The chapter ends with Solomon bringing in the things David had dedicated: silver, gold, and vessels.

They are put among the treasures of the house of the LORD.

David's preparation and Solomon's building meet.

The temple is not merely Solomon's achievement.

It is part of the covenant story stretching from David to Solomon and from Exodus to the land.

## Full Lesson Review

Day 83 shows a kingdom at rest and a temple rising.

Solomon's wisdom orders the kingdom.

Hiram helps with materials.

Workers prepare costly stones and timber.

The temple is built according to careful design.

The LORD reminds Solomon that obedience matters more than architecture alone.

The furnishings show beauty and holiness.

The whole day teaches that God's worship is not casual.

It is prepared, costly, ordered, and centered on His presence.

## Key Connections

First, Solomon's wisdom is a gift from God and a blessing to the kingdom.

Second, rest from enemies creates space for temple building.

Third, the temple is connected to the Exodus, reminding Israel who redeemed them.

Fourth, the LORD's word to Solomon keeps obedience at the center.

## Slow Walk Through the Day

Start with the administration in First Kings 4.

The names and offices can feel like a list to skip, but they show something important.

Wisdom does not only appear in dramatic court cases.

Wisdom also orders daily life.

Solomon's kingdom has priests, scribes, recorders, officers, governors, and provision.

The people need justice.

The court needs memory.

The land needs organization.

The royal household needs food.

Peace must be managed.

Abundance must be stewarded.

That is why the chapter moves from officials to provision to safety to wisdom.

The picture of every man under his vine and under his fig tree is especially rich.

It describes settled blessing.

People are not constantly running from enemies.

They can sit, eat, work, and live in peace.

This is one of the beautiful glimpses of kingdom rest.

But the same chapter also hints at weight.

The daily provision is massive.

Royal greatness carries demands.

The listener should enjoy the blessing while staying awake to the pressure that such a system creates.

Solomon's wisdom is described broadly.

He speaks proverbs.

He writes songs.

He speaks about trees, beasts, birds, creeping things, and fish.

His wisdom touches creation.

This is not narrow cleverness.

It is wide understanding.

People from the nations come to hear him.

Solomon's wisdom becomes a witness beyond Israel.

Now move into First Kings 5.

Hiram loved David, and that relationship now helps Solomon.

David's faithfulness and alliances continue to bear fruit after his death.

Solomon says David could not build because of wars, but now the LORD has given rest.

The temple comes in a season of rest.

That matters.

Israel's worship is being built not as a war trophy, but as a house for the name of the LORD.

Hiram blesses the LORD when he hears Solomon's request.

Even a Gentile king recognizes something of God's hand in Solomon's wisdom.

The timber comes from Lebanon.

Food goes to Hiram's household.

Workers cut, carry, shape, and prepare.

Great stones and costly stones are laid for the foundation.

The temple is spiritual, but it is not imaginary.

It takes real material, real labor, real skill, real time, and real cost.

That is a key lesson for the listener.

Holy work is not less practical because it is holy.

Sometimes faithfulness looks like cedar logs, stonework, schedules, and skilled hands.

Now listen to First Kings 6.

The temple is dated four hundred and eighty years after the Exodus.

This connects the building to redemption.

The LORD brought Israel out of Egypt.

The LORD gave them the land.

Now the house is built in the land for His name.

The temple stands inside a story of rescue.

The measurements and rooms can feel technical, but they teach order.

God's house is not built carelessly.

The stones are prepared before they arrive, so no hammer or axe is heard in the house while it is being built.

That quiet detail gives the scene a sense of reverence.

Preparation happens outside.

The house itself is assembled with honor.

Then the LORD speaks to Solomon.

This is the theological center of the building chapter.

The LORD does not say, Since the building is beautiful, everything is now secure no matter how you live.

He says Solomon must walk in His statutes, execute His judgments, keep His commandments, and walk in them.

Only then does the promise concerning David stand in its proper covenant shape.

The house is glorious, but obedience is still required.

Gold, cherubims, palm trees, and open flowers all serve the worship of the LORD.

Beauty is not the enemy of holiness.

In the temple, beauty is ordered toward God's presence.

Now slow down with First Kings 7.

Solomon builds his own house for thirteen years.

The text spends time describing royal buildings and then temple furnishings.

The listener should notice both splendor and scale.

Solomon's world is becoming very large.

Hiram the craftsman works in brass.

The pillars, chapiters, networks, pomegranates, sea, bases, lavers, pots, shovels, and basins are all named.

Details matter because worship has shape.

The molten sea is not simply an object.

It belongs to the cleansing and service of the priests.

The pillars named Jachin and Boaz stand with meaning.

The house has strength, establishment, and beauty.

The gold furnishings also matter.

The altar, table, candlesticks, flowers, lamps, tongs, bowls, spoons, censers, and hinges show that every part of the space is set apart.

Then Solomon brings in the things David dedicated.

This connects father and son.

David prepared.

Solomon built.

The house belongs to the LORD.

The treasures are placed in the house of the LORD, not displayed as Solomon's private glory.

## Guided Listening Reflection

Day 83 teaches that wisdom builds.

It builds administration.

It builds peace.

It builds relationships.

It builds the temple.

But wisdom must stay submitted to the LORD.

The most important word in the temple chapters is not cedar, gold, stone, or brass.

The most important word is the LORD's warning to walk in His ways.

Without obedience, the building cannot protect the people from covenant unfaithfulness.

Listen also for the difference between beauty and pride.

The temple is beautiful because God is worthy.

The materials are costly because worship matters.

The craftsmanship is excellent because the house is for the name of the LORD.

But beauty can become dangerous if the heart starts trusting in the building instead of the God who meets His people.

That warning will matter soon in Solomon's story.

For now, Day 83 lets the listener stand in the wonder of preparation.

The kingdom has rest.

The workers are organized.

The stones are cut.

The cedar is brought.

The gold is placed.

The furnishings are made.

The story is moving toward dedication.

The listener should feel the weight of a long promise taking visible form.

The God who redeemed Israel from Egypt is giving Israel a house for His name in the land.

That is why the details matter.

They are not filler.

They are the sound of covenant history becoming architecture.

## Final Guided Recap

Before the closing, hold the whole day together.

First Kings 4 shows wisdom in public life.

Solomon's wisdom is not only private insight.

It orders officials, food, regions, peace, songs, sayings, and knowledge of creation.

The people dwell safely, and the nations come to hear.

That gives the listener a picture of kingdom blessing.

The vine and fig tree image is peaceful because it describes ordinary life under safety.

People can sit where they live.

They can enjoy fruit from what they tend.

They are not running from war.

First Kings 5 shows rest becoming preparation.

David wanted to build but could not because of war.

Solomon receives a season of rest, and that rest becomes responsibility.

He does not waste peace.

He uses it to build for the name of the LORD.

Hiram's help shows that God's purposes can include skilled work, international cooperation, and practical provision.

The cedar and stones are not boring details.

They are part of worship taking material form.

First Kings 6 shows the temple rising with reverence.

The date after the Exodus reminds the listener that this building stands inside God's rescue story.

The quiet stones remind the listener that holy work involves preparation.

The LORD's word to Solomon reminds the listener that obedience still matters most.

The building is glorious, but it is not magic.

The king must walk with God.

First Kings 7 shows craftsmanship filling the house with beauty and function.

The pillars, sea, bases, lavers, vessels, lamps, and gold furnishings all serve worship.

They are made with skill because the LORD is worthy.

David's dedicated treasures are brought in, connecting generations.

David prepared.

Solomon built.

The LORD receives the house.

So Day 83 is not merely about architecture.

It is about wisdom becoming order, rest becoming worship, beauty serving holiness, and covenant promise taking shape in the land.

## Prayerful Landing

Let this day teach patience with holy details.

The LORD includes names, measurements, materials, workers, rooms, pillars, vessels, and furnishings because worship is not vague.

God's people are embodied people.

They live in places.

They carry wood.

They shape stone.

They make offerings.

They organize work.

They prepare space.

The temple chapters slow the listener down because reverence often moves slower than convenience.

Solomon's builders do not rush into the house with noisy tools.

The stones are made ready before they arrive.

That image can stay with the heart.

The LORD prepares His people too.

He shapes what will serve His presence.

He orders what would otherwise be scattered.

He makes rest fruitful.

The beauty of the temple is not random luxury.

It is beauty ordered toward the name of the LORD.

At the same time, the LORD's word to Solomon keeps the listener grounded.

No amount of cedar, gold, stone, or craftsmanship can replace obedience.

The house is holy because the LORD puts His name there.

The king must still walk with Him.

That is the balance of Day 83.

Build carefully.

Worship reverently.

Receive wisdom gratefully.

And never let the beauty of the work replace love for the God the work is meant to honor.

## One More Listening Pass

There is one more way to hear Day 83.

It is a day about gifts being turned toward God.

Solomon has wisdom.

Hiram has timber and skilled workers.

Israel has laborers.

David has already dedicated treasures.

The land has stone.

The craftsmen have ability.

The kingdom has rest.

All of these gifts are gathered into one holy purpose.

That matters because the Bible does not treat spiritual life as detached from ordinary ability.

Wisdom, trade, labor, architecture, music, administration, and craftsmanship can all be brought under the LORD's purpose.

The question is not whether the gift looks religious at first glance.

The question is whether it is offered in obedience to the LORD.

The temple also teaches the listener to respect preparation.

The most visible moment will come later, when the ark enters and the glory fills the house.

But before that moment, there are years of unseen labor.

Trees must be cut.

Stones must be shaped.

Measurements must be followed.

Workers must be organized.

Furnishings must be formed.

Holy moments often rest on faithful preparation no one applauds while it is happening.

Day 83 honors that preparation.

It tells the listener that careful obedience before the public moment matters to God.

The temple is not yet filled with the cloud in this reading, but it is being made ready.

That is a faithful season too.

Sometimes the work of God in a life feels like preparation more than arrival.

The stones are being shaped.

The rooms are being ordered.

The vessels are being made.

The purpose is not lost just because the dedication has not happened yet.

The LORD is still moving the story toward His presence.

## Closing

As you finish Day 83, remember that the temple's beauty points beyond itself.

Gold, cedar, stone, pillars, and carved flowers all serve one purpose.

This is the house built for the name of the LORD.
