# Day 75 Narrator Script

## Opening

Welcome to Day 75 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are reading First Samuel 27 through 30.

The title for today is David in Exile.

This day follows David into one of the most complicated stretches of his life.

David is tired of running from Saul.

He decides to live among the Philistines.

Saul, meanwhile, faces terror because the Philistines gather for war and the LORD does not answer him.

Saul seeks a woman with a familiar spirit, even though he had removed such practices from the land.

David is nearly pulled into battle against Israel, but the Philistine lords reject him.

Then David returns to Ziklag and finds the city burned and the families taken.

At that lowest moment, David encourages himself in the LORD his God.

The day is about fear, compromise, judgment, grief, and recovery.

It does not pretend David's exile is neat.

It shows the pressure of waiting and the mercy of God in a hard place.

## First Samuel 27

First Samuel 27 begins with David speaking in his heart.

He says he shall now perish one day by the hand of Saul.

There is nothing better for him than to escape into the land of the Philistines.

Then Saul will despair of seeking him any more in the coast of Israel.

David has been preserved again and again.

He has spared Saul twice.

He has heard Saul admit wrong.

But David is weary.

He concludes that staying in Israel will end in death.

So David arises and passes over with six hundred men to Achish, the son of Maoch, king of Gath.

David dwells with Achish at Gath, he and his men, every man with his household.

David has two wives, Ahinoam and Abigail.

Saul is told David has fled to Gath, and Saul seeks no more again for him.

David's plan works in one sense.

Saul stops hunting him.

But it places David in morally dangerous territory.

David asks Achish for a place in some town in the country.

He says, Why should thy servant dwell in the royal city with thee?

Achish gives him Ziklag.

Ziklag becomes connected with the kings of Judah.

David dwells in the country of the Philistines a full year and four months.

Then David and his men go up and invade the Geshurites, Gezrites, and Amalekites.

These peoples had long inhabited the land.

David smites the land and leaves neither man nor woman alive.

He takes sheep, oxen, asses, camels, apparel, and returns to Achish.

When Achish asks where David made a road that day, David says against the south of Judah, or against the south of the Jerahmeelites, or against the south of the Kenites.

David is deceiving Achish.

He is attacking Israel's enemies while making Achish think he is attacking Israelite-related regions.

David saves neither man nor woman alive to bring tidings to Gath, lest they should tell what he has done.

Achish believes David.

He says David has made his people Israel utterly to abhor him, therefore David shall be his servant forever.

This chapter is uncomfortable.

David is preserved from Saul, but he is living by deception in Philistine territory.

The Bible does not pause to praise or fully condemn every detail.

It lets the tension sit.

David is the LORD's anointed, but he is also a weary man making dangerous choices in exile.

## First Samuel 28

First Samuel 28 begins with the Philistines gathering their armies for warfare against Israel.

Achish tells David he will go out with him to battle, David and his men.

David answers in a way that sounds loyal to Achish, but it is ambiguous.

Achish says he will make David keeper of his head forever.

Then the chapter reminds us Samuel is dead.

All Israel lamented him.

Saul has put away those that have familiar spirits and wizards out of the land.

The Philistines gather and pitch in Shunem.

Saul gathers Israel and pitches in Gilboa.

When Saul sees the host of the Philistines, he is afraid, and his heart greatly trembles.

Saul inquires of the LORD, but the LORD answers him not, neither by dreams, nor by Urim, nor by prophets.

This is the terror of being cut off from God's answer.

Saul has spent years rejecting God's word.

Now in crisis, he wants guidance, but no answer comes.

So Saul tells his servants to seek a woman with a familiar spirit.

He wants to go to her and inquire.

His servants say there is such a woman at Endor.

Saul disguises himself and goes by night.

The king who removed mediums now seeks one secretly.

He asks the woman to bring up Samuel.

The woman is afraid because Saul had cut off those with familiar spirits.

Saul swears by the LORD that no punishment will happen to her.

That is a tragic misuse of the LORD's name.

He invokes the LORD while doing what he should not do.

The woman sees Samuel and cries with a loud voice.

She realizes the visitor is Saul.

Saul asks what she sees.

She describes an old man covered with a mantle.

Saul perceives it is Samuel and stoops with his face to the ground.

Samuel asks why Saul has disquieted him.

Saul says he is sore distressed.

The Philistines make war against him.

God is departed from him and answers him no more.

Samuel answers that the LORD has departed from Saul and become his enemy.

The LORD has done as He spoke by Samuel.

The LORD has rent the kingdom out of Saul's hand and given it to David.

Because Saul did not obey the voice of the LORD concerning Amalek, the LORD has done this thing.

Samuel says the LORD will deliver Israel with Saul into the hand of the Philistines.

Tomorrow Saul and his sons will be with Samuel.

The LORD also will deliver the host of Israel into the hand of the Philistines.

Saul falls straightway all along on the earth and is sore afraid.

There is no strength in him, because he has eaten no bread all day and all night.

The woman and Saul's servants urge him to eat.

At first he refuses, but then he listens.

The woman kills a fat calf, makes unleavened bread, and sets it before Saul and his servants.

They eat and rise up and go away that night.

The chapter is dark.

Saul's story is nearing its end.

The man who would not obey God's word now hears judgment from the prophet he can no longer consult among the living.

Fear drives him where faith should never go.

## First Samuel 29

First Samuel 29 returns to David among the Philistines.

The Philistines gather all their armies to Aphek.

The Israelites pitch by a fountain in Jezreel.

The lords of the Philistines pass on by hundreds and thousands, and David and his men pass on in the rearward with Achish.

The princes of the Philistines ask, What do these Hebrews here?

Achish defends David.

He says David has been with him these days, or these years, and he has found no fault in him.

But the Philistine princes are wroth.

They tell Achish to make David return to the place appointed for him.

They do not want David going down with them to battle, lest he become an adversary in the battle.

They ask how David could reconcile himself to his master Saul, if not with the heads of Philistine men.

They remember the song: Saul slew his thousands, and David his ten thousands.

Achish calls David and says that as the LORD lives, David has been upright in his eyes.

But the lords do not favor him.

He tells David to return in peace so he does not displease the lords of the Philistines.

David asks what he has done and why he may not fight against the enemies of his lord the king.

Again his words are difficult.

Is David performing loyalty to Achish?

Is he relieved beneath the surface?

The text does not tell us everything inside David.

But the result is clear.

David is removed from the Philistine battle line before Israel is attacked.

Achish sends him away in the morning.

David and his men rise early to return to the land of the Philistines, and the Philistines go up to Jezreel.

This is mercy through rejection.

The Philistine commanders do not trust David.

Their suspicion prevents David from being trapped in battle against Israel and Saul.

God can deliver His servant through a closed door.

## First Samuel 30

First Samuel 30 begins when David and his men come to Ziklag on the third day.

The Amalekites have invaded the south and Ziklag.

They have smitten Ziklag and burned it with fire.

They have taken the women captive, and all who were there, small and great.

They did not kill any, but carried them away and went on their way.

David and his men come to the city, and behold, it is burned with fire.

Their wives, sons, and daughters are taken captive.

David and the people lift up their voice and weep until they have no more power to weep.

David's two wives are taken.

David is greatly distressed because the people speak of stoning him.

The soul of all the people is grieved, every man for his sons and daughters.

Then comes one of the most important lines in David's life:

But David encouraged himself in the LORD his God.

David has no easy human comfort in that moment.

The city is burned.

The families are gone.

The men are bitter.

His own life is in danger.

So David strengthens himself in the LORD.

Then David calls for Abiathar and the ephod.

He inquires of the LORD, saying, Shall I pursue after this troop?

Shall I overtake them?

The LORD answers, Pursue, for thou shalt surely overtake them, and without fail recover all.

David goes with six hundred men.

They come to the brook Besor.

Two hundred are so faint that they cannot go over.

David continues with four hundred.

They find an Egyptian in the field and bring him to David.

They give him bread, water, figs, and raisins.

His spirit comes again to him, because he had eaten no bread and drunk no water for three days and nights.

David asks to whom he belongs.

The Egyptian says he is a young man of Egypt, servant to an Amalekite.

His master left him because he fell sick.

He tells David they had invaded the south and burned Ziklag.

David asks if he can bring him down to the company.

The Egyptian asks David to swear by God that he will neither kill him nor deliver him into his master's hands.

David swears.

The Egyptian brings David down.

The Amalekites are spread abroad, eating, drinking, and dancing because of the great spoil.

David smites them from twilight even unto the evening of the next day.

Not a man escapes except four hundred young men who ride on camels and flee.

David recovers all that the Amalekites had carried away.

David rescues his two wives.

There is nothing lacking to them, neither small nor great, neither sons nor daughters, neither spoil, nor anything they had taken.

David recovers all.

That repeated idea matters.

The chapter began with total loss.

It ends with full recovery.

David takes the flocks and herds, and they say, This is David's spoil.

Then David comes to the two hundred men who were so faint they could not follow.

Some wicked men and men of Belial among those who went with David say the two hundred should not receive spoil, except each man's wife and children.

David refuses.

He says they shall not do so with that which the LORD has given them.

The LORD preserved them and delivered the company against them into their hand.

As his part is that goes down to the battle, so shall his part be that tarries by the stuff.

They shall part alike.

David makes it a statute and ordinance for Israel from that day forward.

Then David sends portions of the spoil to the elders of Judah, to his friends, saying it is a present from the spoil of the enemies of the LORD.

David's leadership after Ziklag is important.

He strengthens himself in God.

He asks the LORD.

He pursues with a promise.

He shows mercy to the abandoned Egyptian.

He recovers all.

He refuses selfishness among his men.

He shares with those too weary to fight.

He blesses Judah.

In a chapter that begins with ashes, David learns again that the LORD can restore.

## Full Lesson Review

Day 75 shows two men under pressure.

Saul is afraid because the Philistines gather and God does not answer him.

Instead of repenting and trusting God's word, Saul seeks forbidden spiritual help.

He goes to Endor by night.

He hears judgment.

His story is collapsing toward death.

David is also under pressure.

He is weary of Saul's pursuit and goes to Philistine territory.

He lives in Ziklag.

He uses deception with Achish.

He nearly gets pulled into a Philistine battle against Israel.

Then Ziklag is burned, and his own men speak of stoning him.

But David does what Saul does not do.

David strengthens himself in the LORD.

David inquires of the LORD.

David listens and acts.

The contrast is strong.

Saul asks for guidance after long rebellion, but the LORD does not answer him.

David, in distress, turns to the LORD his God and receives direction.

The day also teaches mercy in leadership.

David shares spoil with the weary.

He refuses to let strong men despise faint men.

He says the victory is what the LORD has given.

That theology shapes generosity.

If the LORD gave the victory, the spoil is not a trophy for the proud.

It is a gift to be shared rightly.

## Guided Listening Review

Day 75 should be heard with the weight of exhaustion.

David has been hunted for a long time.

He has spared Saul more than once.

He has lived in caves, wildernesses, holds, and enemy-shadowed places.

So when he says in his heart that he will perish one day by the hand of Saul, the sentence comes from weariness.

It is not a formal prayer.

It is an inward conclusion.

That matters because the chapter shows how fear can reason inside a tired heart.

David's move to Philistine territory gives him relief from Saul, but it also creates danger.

Achish thinks David has made himself hateful to Israel.

David lives by deception.

The reader is meant to feel the tension.

The LORD's anointed is safe from Saul, but he is now entangled with Israel's enemies.

Chapter 28 shows Saul under a different kind of pressure.

The Philistines gather.

Saul trembles.

He asks the LORD, but the LORD does not answer.

That silence is not random.

Saul has rejected God's word for years.

He has killed priests.

He has pursued David.

He has disobeyed concerning Amalek.

Now, instead of humbling himself, he seeks forbidden counsel.

The visit to Endor is a tragic picture of religious collapse.

Saul swears by the LORD while doing what the LORD forbids.

He wants information without obedience.

He wants relief without repentance.

The message he hears is judgment.

Chapter 29 then shows God protecting David through Philistine suspicion.

David is nearly in the wrong battle.

If he marches with the Philistines against Israel, every path looks dangerous.

But the Philistine lords reject him.

Their distrust becomes a mercy.

Sometimes God rescues through acceptance.

Sometimes God rescues through rejection.

David is sent away before the battle.

Then chapter 30 takes David to ashes.

Ziklag is burned.

The families are gone.

The men weep until strength is gone.

Then grief turns against David, and the people speak of stoning him.

This is one of the lowest points in David's story before he becomes king.

And there, David encourages himself in the LORD his God.

That line does not mean David feels cheerful.

It means David finds strength in the only place that can hold him.

He calls for the ephod.

He asks the LORD.

He receives a promise.

He pursues.

He shows kindness to the abandoned Egyptian.

He recovers all.

Then he shares the spoil with the faint men who stayed by the stuff.

That final decision shows the kind of king David is becoming.

He does not let victory make the strong despise the weak.

He says the LORD gave the victory, so the gift must be shared with mercy.

That is leadership shaped by grace after grief.

## Key Connections

Three contrasts make Day 75 especially important.

First, David speaks in his heart, while Saul seeks a forbidden voice.

David's inward fear leads him into a risky choice, but Saul's fear leads him into open rebellion.

Both men are pressured.

Both men are afraid.

But the direction of the heart matters.

Second, the Philistine rejection of David becomes protection.

David may have thought he needed Achish's favor to survive.

Yet Achish's commanders close the door.

That closed door keeps David from standing in battle against Israel.

God's mercy can arrive through embarrassment, rejection, or disappointment.

Third, Ziklag shows David's leadership after loss.

He does not have strength in himself.

He strengthens himself in the LORD.

He does not punish the weary men who cannot cross the brook.

He shares with them.

He does not treat recovery as private property.

He sends gifts to Judah.

The burned city becomes a place where David learns dependence, mercy, and generous rule.

That prepares the reader for the kingdom that is about to open after Saul's fall.

One more thing makes this day sobering.

Both Saul and David are close to a turning point, but they move differently in distress.

Saul's distress drives him toward a forbidden practice under cover of night.

David's distress, at its lowest point, drives him to strengthen himself in the LORD and ask for direction.

The difference is not that David has an easier life.

Ziklag is awful.

The grief is real.

The men's anger is real.

The loss is real.

But David still turns toward the LORD.

That is why the recovery at Ziklag is more than a rescue story.

It is a leadership story.

David receives mercy, then leads with mercy.

He recovers all, then shares with those who were too faint to continue.

He treats the victory as the LORD's gift, not his private achievement.

## Closing

As you finish Day 75, remember Ziklag.

David came home to ashes.

The people wept until they had no more power to weep.

David was greatly distressed.

But David encouraged himself in the LORD his God.

That line is not sentimental.

It comes from a burned city, stolen families, exhausted men, and a leader under threat.

Faith does not mean David never feels fear.

Faith means he turns to the LORD when fear has no human answer.

The LORD leads him to recover all.

The next day will bring Saul's fall and David's next step toward the kingdom.

But Day 75 leaves us with this contrast:

Fear without obedience drives Saul into darkness.

Distress turned toward God strengthens David to keep walking.
