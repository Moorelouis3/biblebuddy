# Bible In One Year - Day 26 Narrator Script

## Day 26 - Water, Battle, and the Ten Commandments

**Reading:** Exodus 17 through 20  
**Bible text:** King James Version, Exodus 17-20  
**Audio style:** Narrator teaching with Scripture reading  

## Opening

Welcome to Day 26 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are reading Exodus 17 through 20.

Israel has passed through the sea.

They have sung the song of victory.

They have tasted bitter water made sweet.

They have received manna from heaven.

And now the journey continues deeper into the wilderness.

But the wilderness is not wasted space.

It is not just the empty land between Egypt and the promised land.

The wilderness is where God begins forming a rescued people.

That is one of the big themes today.

God has brought Israel out of slavery.

But now He must teach them how to live as His people.

So in today's reading, Israel faces thirst again.

Then they face battle.

Then Moses faces exhaustion.

Then Israel arrives at Mount Sinai.

And finally, God speaks the Ten Commandments.

This is a major turning point.

The God who rescued Israel now gives them a way of life.

He provides water.

He gives victory.

He gives wisdom for leadership.

He gives covenant identity.

And He gives commands that show what life with Him is supposed to look like.

So as we move through Exodus 17 through 20, listen for this:

Rescue is not the end of the story.

Rescue leads to formation.

God saves His people, and then He teaches them how to belong to Him.

## Exodus 17:1-7

**Scripture**

And all the congregation of the children of Israel journeyed from the wilderness of Sin, after their journeys, according to the commandment of the LORD, and pitched in Rephidim.

And there was no water for the people to drink.

Wherefore the people did chide with Moses, and said, Give us water that we may drink.

And Moses cried unto the LORD, saying, What shall I do unto this people?

And the LORD said unto Moses, Go on before the people, and take with thee of the elders of Israel; and thy rod, wherewith thou smotest the river, take in thine hand, and go.

Behold, I will stand before thee there upon the rock in Horeb; and thou shalt smite the rock, and there shall come water out of it.

And he called the name of the place Massah, and Meribah, because they tempted the LORD, saying, Is the LORD among us, or not?

**Narrator**

Exodus 17 begins with a real need.

There is no water.

That is not a small problem in the wilderness.

This is not discomfort.

This is survival.

The people are thirsty, their children are thirsty, their animals need water, and the wilderness does not offer easy answers.

But the way Israel responds shows how quickly fear can turn rescue into accusation.

They do not simply ask for help.

They quarrel with Moses.

They ask why he brought them out of Egypt to kill them.

And then the chapter gives us the painful question underneath the complaint:

Is the LORD among us, or not?

That question should make us pause.

Israel has seen the plagues.

They have seen Passover.

They have seen the sea open.

They have seen manna in the morning.

They have seen cloud and fire.

But fear has a short memory.

Need can make yesterday's miracle feel far away.

So God tells Moses to take the staff.

The same staff connected to the Nile.

The same staff connected to judgment in Egypt.

The same staff connected to deliverance.

And now, in the wilderness, the staff is connected to provision.

Moses strikes the rock.

Water comes out.

And God provides for people whose hearts are not beautiful in the moment.

That is mercy.

The place is named Massah and Meribah.

Testing and quarreling.

The names remember Israel's failure, but the water remembers God's kindness.

## Exodus 17:8-16

**Scripture**

Then came Amalek, and fought with Israel in Rephidim.

And Moses said unto Joshua, Choose us out men, and go out, fight with Amalek.

So Joshua did as Moses had said to him, and fought with Amalek.

And Moses, Aaron, and Hur went up to the top of the hill.

And it came to pass, when Moses held up his hand, that Israel prevailed.

And when he let down his hand, Amalek prevailed.

But Moses' hands were heavy; and they took a stone, and put it under him, and he sat thereon.

And Aaron and Hur stayed up his hands, the one on the one side, and the other on the other side.

And Moses built an altar, and called the name of it Jehovah-nissi.

**Narrator**

Right after the water crisis, Israel faces attack.

Amalek comes and fights with Israel at Rephidim.

This is Israel's first battle after leaving Egypt.

They are not yet a settled nation.

They are not experienced warriors.

They are former slaves walking through dangerous land.

And here we meet Joshua as a military leader for the first time.

Moses tells him to choose men and fight Amalek.

Joshua fights below.

Moses stands above with the staff of God.

When Moses' hands are raised, Israel prevails.

When his hands fall, Amalek prevails.

This scene teaches dependence.

Joshua really fights.

The men really battle.

Moses really stands with the staff.

But the victory is not finally in human strength.

The raised hands point to dependence on God.

And then Moses gets tired.

That detail is deeply human.

Moses is called by God.

Moses has stood before Pharaoh.

Moses has lifted the staff over the sea.

But Moses is still a man with tired arms.

So Aaron and Hur give him a stone to sit on.

They stand beside him.

They hold up his hands.

And Israel prevails.

This is a beautiful picture of shared faithfulness.

God uses Joshua in the battle.

God uses Moses on the hill.

God uses Aaron and Hur in support.

Nobody carries the whole thing alone.

After the victory, Moses builds an altar and calls it Jehovah-nissi.

The LORD is my banner.

A banner was a sign under which people gathered and fought.

Moses is saying Israel's victory belongs under God's name.

Not human pride.

Not Moses.

Not Joshua.

The LORD is the banner over His people.

## Exodus 18:1-12

**Scripture**

When Jethro, the priest of Midian, Moses' father in law, heard of all that God had done for Moses, and for Israel his people, and that the LORD had brought Israel out of Egypt;

Then Jethro, Moses' father in law, came with his sons and his wife unto Moses into the wilderness.

And Moses told his father in law all that the LORD had done unto Pharaoh and to the Egyptians for Israel's sake, and all the travail that had come upon them by the way, and how the LORD delivered them.

And Jethro rejoiced for all the goodness which the LORD had done to Israel.

And Jethro said, Blessed be the LORD, who hath delivered you out of the hand of the Egyptians.

Now I know that the LORD is greater than all gods.

**Narrator**

Exodus 18 slows the story down.

After thirst and battle, we get a family reunion.

Jethro, Moses' father-in-law, hears what God has done.

He brings Moses' wife, Zipporah, and Moses' sons to him in the wilderness.

And Moses tells Jethro the story.

But notice what Moses includes.

He tells him all that the LORD did to Pharaoh and Egypt.

And he tells him all the hardship that came along the way.

That balance matters.

Moses does not only tell the miracle parts.

He does not clean up the story to make it sound easy.

He tells about deliverance and trouble.

Power and pressure.

Rescue and wilderness.

That is often what real testimony sounds like.

God delivered us.

And the road was hard.

Jethro rejoices.

He blesses the LORD.

And then he says, Now I know that the LORD is greater than all gods.

That line matters because Jethro is not an Israelite by birth.

He is a Midianite priest.

And yet Israel's deliverance becomes testimony to someone outside Israel.

God's work among His people is already declaring His greatness beyond their own camp.

The Exodus is not only for Israel to know.

It is a witness that the LORD is greater than all false powers.

## Exodus 18:13-27

**Scripture**

And it came to pass on the morrow, that Moses sat to judge the people.

And the people stood by Moses from the morning unto the evening.

And when Moses' father in law saw all that he did to the people, he said, What is this thing that thou doest to the people?

Thou wilt surely wear away, both thou, and this people that is with thee.

The thing that thou doest is not good.

Hearken now unto my voice, I will give thee counsel, and God shall be with thee.

Thou shalt provide out of all the people able men, such as fear God, men of truth, hating covetousness.

And let them judge the people at all seasons.

**Narrator**

The next day, Jethro watches Moses work.

Moses sits as judge, and the people stand around him from morning until evening.

The line probably sounds normal at first.

Moses is the leader.

The people need help.

They need decisions.

They need wisdom.

But Jethro sees the problem.

Moses is doing important work, but he is doing too much of it alone.

And Jethro says something very direct:

The thing that thou doest is not good.

That is strong.

But it is not cruel.

It is wisdom.

Jethro says Moses will wear himself out, and the people will wear out too.

This is important because sometimes faithful people assume exhaustion is proof of devotion.

But Jethro shows Moses that wise structure can be part of obedience.

He tells Moses to teach the people God's ways, then appoint capable leaders.

Able men.

Men who fear God.

Men of truth.

Men who hate dishonest gain.

That list matters.

Leadership among God's people is not only about skill.

It is about character.

Moses should handle the hardest matters, but others can carry smaller cases.

The burden must be shared.

This is practical wisdom, but it is also spiritual wisdom.

God's people need order.

Leaders need limits.

And good leadership trains others to carry responsibility.

## Exodus 19:1-8

**Scripture**

In the third month, when the children of Israel were gone forth out of the land of Egypt, the same day came they into the wilderness of Sinai.

And Moses went up unto God, and the LORD called unto him out of the mountain, saying,

Ye have seen what I did unto the Egyptians, and how I bare you on eagles' wings, and brought you unto myself.

Now therefore, if ye will obey my voice indeed, and keep my covenant, then ye shall be a peculiar treasure unto me above all people.

And ye shall be unto me a kingdom of priests, and an holy nation.

**Narrator**

Now Israel arrives at Sinai.

This is a huge moment.

Back at the burning bush, God told Moses that after he brought the people out of Egypt, they would serve God on this mountain.

Now that promise is being fulfilled.

The slaves are standing near the mountain of God.

And before God gives commandments, He reminds Israel what He has done.

You have seen what I did to the Egyptians.

I carried you on eagles' wings.

I brought you to Myself.

That order is important.

Grace comes before law.

Rescue comes before commandments.

God does not say, Obey Me so I might bring you out of Egypt.

He has already brought them out.

Now He teaches them how to live as His people.

And the phrase eagles' wings is tender and powerful.

It pictures God carrying and protecting His people.

He did not merely move them from one location to another.

He brought them to Himself.

Then He gives them identity:

A peculiar treasure.

A kingdom of priests.

A holy nation.

Peculiar treasure means treasured possession.

Israel belongs specially to the LORD.

A kingdom of priests means Israel is meant to represent God among the nations.

A holy nation means they are set apart for God's purpose.

So Sinai is not just about rules.

It is about relationship, identity, and mission.

## Exodus 19:9-25

**Scripture**

And the LORD said unto Moses, Go unto the people, and sanctify them to day and to morrow, and let them wash their clothes.

And be ready against the third day: for the third day the LORD will come down in the sight of all the people upon mount Sinai.

And it came to pass on the third day in the morning, that there were thunders and lightnings, and a thick cloud upon the mount.

And mount Sinai was altogether on a smoke, because the LORD descended upon it in fire.

And the whole mount quaked greatly.

**Narrator**

The people must prepare.

They wash their clothes.

They wait.

They keep boundaries around the mountain.

This teaches that meeting God is not casual.

The God who rescued Israel is also holy.

That is a theme we need to hold together.

God is merciful.

And God is holy.

God comes near.

And God must be approached on His terms.

On the third day, the mountain is covered with thunder, lightning, thick cloud, trumpet sound, smoke, fire, and shaking.

Try to imagine standing there.

You are a former slave.

You have seen Egypt fall.

You have crossed the sea.

You have eaten manna.

And now the mountain trembles because the living God has come down.

Israel is not meeting an idea.

They are not hearing a religious theory.

They are encountering the LORD.

The boundaries around the mountain are not because God is cruel.

They teach that sinful people cannot rush into holy presence on their own terms.

God is near, but He is never common.

He is gracious, but He is never small.

## Exodus 20:1-3

**Scripture**

And God spake all these words, saying,

I am the LORD thy God, which have brought thee out of the land of Egypt, out of the house of bondage.

Thou shalt have no other gods before me.

**Narrator**

Exodus 20 begins with God speaking.

The commandments are not Moses' personal ideas.

They come from the LORD Himself.

And before God gives the first command, He reminds Israel who He is.

I am the LORD thy God, which brought thee out of the land of Egypt, out of the house of bondage.

That sentence is the foundation of the Ten Commandments.

God begins with redemption.

He says, I rescued you.

I brought you out.

You were in bondage, and I delivered you.

Then He says, Thou shalt have no other gods before Me.

That matters deeply after Egypt.

Israel has just been delivered from a land filled with gods, idols, rituals, and divine claims.

The plagues showed that Egypt's gods could not stand before the LORD.

Now Israel must learn exclusive loyalty.

No other gods before Me.

This is not God being insecure.

This is covenant faithfulness.

The God who redeemed Israel has the right to Israel's worship.

The Savior is not one option among many.

He is the LORD.

## Exodus 20:4-11

**Scripture**

Thou shalt not make unto thee any graven image.

Thou shalt not bow down thyself to them, nor serve them.

Thou shalt not take the name of the LORD thy God in vain.

Remember the sabbath day, to keep it holy.

Six days shalt thou labour, and do all thy work:

But the seventh day is the sabbath of the LORD thy God.

For in six days the LORD made heaven and earth, the sea, and all that in them is, and rested the seventh day.

**Narrator**

The second commandment forbids making images for worship.

In the ancient world, idols were often treated like visible access points to divine power.

People made images, bowed to them, served them, and treated them as if the divine could be controlled or represented by human hands.

Israel must not do that.

The LORD cannot be reduced to something carved.

He is not shaped by human imagination.

He is the Creator.

Then God commands Israel not to take His name in vain.

This is bigger than using God's name as a careless phrase.

It includes carrying His name falsely, lightly, emptily, or hypocritically.

Israel bears the LORD's name as His covenant people.

So His name must be honored in speech, worship, and daily life.

Then comes the Sabbath command.

Remember the Sabbath day, to keep it holy.

Israel has already had a Sabbath lesson with manna.

Now Sabbath is placed inside the covenant commands.

And God roots it in creation.

Six days the LORD made heaven and earth.

The seventh day He rested.

That means rest is not laziness.

Rest is built into God's design.

For former slaves, this would be powerful.

Pharaoh demanded endless production.

The LORD commands holy rest.

Israel's weekly rhythm will say:

We do not belong to Pharaoh anymore.

We belong to the God who created, rescued, and provides.

## Exodus 20:12-17

**Scripture**

Honour thy father and thy mother.

Thou shalt not kill.

Thou shalt not commit adultery.

Thou shalt not steal.

Thou shalt not bear false witness against thy neighbour.

Thou shalt not covet thy neighbour's house.

Thou shalt not covet thy neighbour's wife, nor his servant, nor his maid, nor his ox, nor his ass, nor any thing that is thy neighbour's.

**Narrator**

The commandments now move from Israel's relationship with God into relationships with people.

Honor father and mother.

This protects family order and generational faithfulness.

In Israel, children needed to learn the story of redemption, the covenant, and the ways of the LORD.

Honoring parents was not only about politeness.

It helped protect the passing down of faith and wisdom.

Then God protects life:

You shall not murder.

Human life matters because people are made in God's image.

Then God protects marriage:

You shall not commit adultery.

Marriage is not treated as disposable.

Then God protects property:

You shall not steal.

Then God protects truth:

You shall not bear false witness.

A community cannot be healthy when lies destroy justice and trust.

And then the final command reaches inward:

You shall not covet.

Coveting is desire that grasps after what belongs to someone else.

That command is powerful because it shows God's law is not only about visible behavior.

God cares about the heart beneath the action.

You can avoid stealing with your hands while still stealing in your desires.

You can avoid adultery outwardly while still allowing the heart to reach for what is not yours.

The Ten Commandments are not shallow rules.

They reveal what love for God and neighbor should begin to look like.

## Exodus 20:18-26

**Scripture**

And all the people saw the thunderings, and the lightnings, and the noise of the trumpet, and the mountain smoking.

And when the people saw it, they removed, and stood afar off.

And they said unto Moses, Speak thou with us, and we will hear: but let not God speak with us, lest we die.

And Moses said unto the people, Fear not: for God is come to prove you, and that his fear may be before your faces, that ye sin not.

An altar of earth thou shalt make unto me, and shalt sacrifice thereon thy burnt offerings, and thy peace offerings.

**Narrator**

The people tremble.

They have heard God's voice.

They have seen the thunder, lightning, trumpet, and smoking mountain.

And they stand far off.

They ask Moses to speak to them because the direct encounter overwhelms them.

Then Moses says something that may sound confusing at first:

Fear not.

But also, God's fear should be before you.

How can both be true?

There is a fear that runs away from God in panic.

And there is a reverent fear that takes God seriously and turns away from sin.

Moses is saying God does not want them to live in terrified distance.

But He does want them to live with holy reverence.

The chapter ends with altar instructions.

The altar is simple.

Earth or uncut stones.

No flashy display.

No attempt to make worship impressive on human terms.

Israel's worship must be shaped by God's word.

That is a fitting ending to Day 26.

The God who rescued Israel now teaches Israel how to approach Him.

Not casually.

Not through idols.

Not through human show.

But through humble obedience.

## Closing

Day 26 moves Israel from survival lessons into covenant formation.

They need water, and God provides.

They face attack, and God gives victory.

Moses grows overwhelmed, and God provides wisdom through Jethro.

Israel arrives at Sinai, and God gives them identity.

They are His treasured possession.

A kingdom of priests.

A holy nation.

Then God speaks the Ten Commandments.

And the order matters.

God does not give commands so Israel can earn rescue.

He gives commands after rescue.

Obedience is not the ladder out of Egypt.

Obedience is the life of people God has already brought out.

That is why this day matters.

It shows us that God does not save people into aimlessness.

He saves people into belonging.

He forms them.

He teaches them.

He gives them rhythms, boundaries, worship, and wisdom.

For us, the lesson is still clear.

We may want God only to rescue us from crisis.

But God wants more than crisis relief.

He wants a people who trust Him when thirsty.

Depend on Him in battle.

Share burdens wisely.

Honor His holiness.

Love Him above every rival.

And love their neighbor in real, practical ways.

The God who says, I brought you out of the house of bondage, is the same God who says, Now walk with Me.

That is the movement of Day 26.

Rescue becomes relationship.

Deliverance becomes formation.

And the redeemed people begin learning the shape of life with God.
