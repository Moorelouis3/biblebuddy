# Bible in One Year - Day 58 Narrator Script

**Reading:** Joshua 8-11

## Opening

Welcome to Day 58 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are reading Joshua chapters 8 through 11.

The theme is conquest and covenant obedience.

After Achan's sin is judged, the LORD tells Joshua not to fear.

Ai falls.

Israel reads the law at Ebal and Gerizim.

The Gibeonites deceive Israel because Israel does not ask counsel of the LORD.

The southern kings attack, and the LORD fights for Israel.

Then northern kings gather, and Joshua continues obeying the LORD's command.

This day teaches that victory must stay tied to God's word.

Conquest is not self-confidence.

It is covenant obedience under the LORD's command.

## Joshua 8 verses 1 through 35

**Scripture**

The LORD tells Joshua not to fear.

He gives Ai into Joshua's hand.

Joshua sets an ambush behind the city.

Ai is taken and burned.

Afterward Joshua builds an altar on Mount Ebal.

He writes a copy of the law and reads all the words of the law, the blessings and curses, before all Israel.

**Narrator**

Joshua 8 begins with mercy after judgment.

Joshua 7 ended with Achan's sin exposed and judged.

Now the LORD says, Fear not, neither be thou dismayed.

That is a gracious command.

Israel's failure at Ai was real.

But after sin is dealt with, the LORD leads them forward again.

God does not let Joshua stay frozen in shame.

He gives new instruction.

This time Ai will fall.

The plan includes an ambush.

Israel will draw the men of Ai out of the city.

Then the hidden force will rise and take it.

The chapter shows strategy, but strategy is under God's word.

The LORD gives the command.

Joshua obeys.

The city falls.

Joshua stretches out the spear, and he does not draw it back until the work is complete.

The victory reverses the earlier defeat.

But the chapter does not end with military pride.

It ends with worship and Scripture.

Joshua builds an altar on Mount Ebal.

He offers burnt offerings and peace offerings.

He writes a copy of the law of Moses.

Then he reads all the words of the law.

Blessings and curses.

Men, women, little ones, and strangers all hear.

That ending is crucial.

Joshua 8 is not only about winning Ai.

It is about returning to the covenant word.

After battle, Israel does not celebrate itself.

Israel hears Scripture.

The people stand where Moses commanded them to stand.

Ebal and Gerizim connect Joshua back to Deuteronomy.

The conquest must be shaped by the covenant.

For Bible Buddy readers, that is the big lesson.

God's people need more than victory.

They need the word of God read over their lives.

They need worship after battle.

They need blessing and curse set before them.

Ai falls, but the law is read.

That order keeps Israel humble.

## Joshua 9 verses 1 through 27

**Scripture**

The kings of the land gather to fight against Joshua.

The Gibeonites act deceitfully.

They use old sacks, old bottles, old shoes, and dry moldy bread to pretend they are from far away.

Israel makes a league with them.

The chapter says the men took of their victuals and asked not counsel at the mouth of the LORD.

When the deception is discovered, Israel keeps the oath but makes the Gibeonites servants for the congregation and the altar of the LORD.

**Narrator**

Joshua 9 teaches that danger does not always look like battle.

At the start, many kings gather to fight.

That danger is obvious.

But the Gibeonites come with deception.

They do not attack with swords.

They come with old sacks, old bottles, old shoes, and dry bread.

They look harmless.

They sound reasonable.

They say they have come from a far country because of the name of the LORD.

Their words contain truth and deception mixed together.

They have heard of the LORD.

But they lie about where they are from.

Israel looks at the evidence.

The bread is dry.

The clothes look old.

The story seems plausible.

But the chapter gives the key sentence: they asked not counsel at the mouth of the LORD.

That is the lesson.

Israel relied on appearance and did not seek God's counsel.

The covenant people must not live by what merely looks wise.

They need the LORD's word.

When the deception is discovered, Israel faces another test.

They have sworn an oath.

They must not break it lightly.

So the Gibeonites live, but they become hewers of wood and drawers of water for the congregation and for the altar.

This chapter is humbling.

It shows that Joshua's generation can still make foolish decisions.

Jericho fell by obedience.

Ai fell after sin was judged.

Gibeon shows the danger of acting without asking the LORD.

For beginners, Joshua 9 is very practical.

Not every test looks frightening.

Some tests look sensible.

Some decisions feel urgent.

Some evidence seems obvious.

But God's people still need counsel from the LORD.

Faithfulness means asking God before acting, not after discovering the mistake.

## Joshua 10 verses 1 through 43

**Scripture**

Adoni-zedek king of Jerusalem hears that Gibeon has made peace with Israel.

Five Amorite kings gather against Gibeon.

Gibeon asks Joshua for help.

The LORD tells Joshua not to fear because He has delivered the enemies into his hand.

The LORD discomfits them, sends great hailstones, and gives victory.

Joshua speaks, and the sun stands still until Israel is avenged.

Joshua defeats the southern cities.

**Narrator**

Joshua 10 begins with the consequences of Gibeon's treaty.

Gibeon is now under threat because it made peace with Israel.

The five kings gather against it.

Joshua goes up from Gilgal with the men of war.

The LORD speaks again: Fear them not.

That phrase matters.

Joshua is not moving forward because the battle looks easy.

He moves because the LORD has spoken.

The LORD throws the enemies into confusion.

Israel fights.

The LORD sends hailstones.

More die from the hailstones than from Israel's sword.

That detail makes the theology clear.

The LORD is fighting for Israel.

Then comes the famous moment where the sun stands still.

Joshua speaks before the LORD, and the day is lengthened for battle.

The chapter says there was no day like that before it or after it, that the LORD hearkened unto the voice of a man.

For a beginner, the main point is not to get lost in curiosity and miss the message.

The text is showing extraordinary divine help.

The LORD fights for Israel.

Joshua's victory is not normal human power.

Then the five kings are found hiding in a cave.

They are brought out and judged.

Joshua tells Israel's captains to put their feet on the necks of the kings.

Then he says, Fear not, nor be dismayed, be strong and of good courage.

That command echoes Joshua 1.

The courage Joshua received is now taught to the leaders under him.

The chapter continues with city after city.

Makkedah.

Libnah.

Lachish.

Eglon.

Hebron.

Debir.

The repeated victories can feel intense, but the chapter keeps the focus on the LORD's command.

Joshua does as the LORD commanded Moses.

The southern campaign is not presented as Joshua's private ambition.

It is part of the LORD giving the land He promised.

Day 58 shows that battle, prayer, obedience, and divine help belong together in Joshua.

## Joshua 11 verses 1 through 23

**Scripture**

Jabin king of Hazor gathers northern kings and a great host with horses and chariots.

The LORD tells Joshua not to be afraid.

Joshua attacks suddenly.

He houghs their horses and burns their chariots as the LORD commanded.

Hazor is burned.

Joshua takes the land according to all that the LORD said to Moses.

The chapter ends by saying the land rested from war.

**Narrator**

Joshua 11 brings another large coalition.

The northern kings gather together.

Their army is described as very great.

They have horses and chariots.

That matters because horses and chariots represent military strength.

From a human point of view, this is frightening.

But the LORD says, Be not afraid because of them.

Again, courage comes from God's word.

Joshua attacks and obeys.

He houghs the horses and burns the chariots.

That may sound strange, but it prevents Israel from turning enemy military power into its own security.

Israel must trust the LORD, not chariots.

The chapter repeatedly says Joshua did what the LORD commanded.

He left nothing undone of all that the LORD commanded Moses.

That line is important.

Joshua is successful because he is obedient.

He is not inventing his mission.

He is carrying forward what the LORD spoke through Moses.

The chapter also mentions Anakims, the giant-like people who had terrified the earlier generation.

The first generation heard about giants and refused to enter.

Joshua's generation faces them under God's command.

That contrast is powerful.

Fear kept one generation out.

Faith-filled obedience carries the next generation forward.

Then the chapter ends: the land rested from war.

That does not mean every detail of settlement is finished.

But it does mark a major stage in the conquest.

The LORD has given victory across the land.

Day 58 therefore teaches covenant obedience in battle.

Ai falls after sin is dealt with.

The law is read after victory.

Gibeon warns against failing to seek counsel.

The LORD fights in the south.

The LORD gives courage in the north.

Joshua obeys all that the LORD commanded.

## Closing

That is Day 58.

Joshua 8 through 11 teaches conquest and covenant obedience.

The LORD restores Israel after Achan's sin.

Ai falls, but the law is read.

Gibeon exposes the danger of acting without God's counsel.

The southern and northern battles show the LORD fighting for His people.

Joshua's victories are not self-confidence.

They are obedience under the LORD's command.

## Full Lesson Review

Day 58 starts with restoration after failure.

That is important.

Joshua 7 ended with judgment in the valley of Achor.

Joshua 8 begins with the LORD speaking again to Joshua.

Fear not, neither be thou dismayed.

Those words matter because Israel has just been humbled.

They had lost men at Ai.

They had discovered hidden sin.

They had watched judgment fall inside the camp.

Now the LORD gives a new command.

Take all the people of war with thee, and arise, go up to Ai.

The LORD does not leave Israel forever under the shame of the earlier defeat.

Sin had to be exposed.

Judgment had to be dealt with.

But after that, the LORD leads them forward.

Ai is given into Joshua's hand.

This time the strategy includes an ambush.

Israel flees as if defeated.

The men of Ai are drawn out of the city.

Joshua stretches out the spear that is in his hand.

The ambush rises.

The city is taken.

The earlier defeat is answered by obedient victory.

That pattern is worth hearing carefully.

The Bible does not teach that failure has the final word when God's people repent and return to His command.

But it also does not teach cheap restoration.

Israel had to face the sin first.

Only then does Joshua 8 move forward.

After Ai falls, Joshua does something that might surprise a hurried reader.

He builds an altar in mount Ebal.

He writes a copy of the law of Moses.

He reads all the words of the law, the blessings and cursings.

All Israel is there.

The elders are there.

The officers are there.

The judges are there.

The strangers are there.

The women and little ones are there.

No word of all that Moses commanded is left unread.

That is the spiritual center of the chapter.

Victory does not lead Israel away from Scripture.

Victory brings Israel back under Scripture.

The conquest is covenantal.

The people must hear the law in the land.

The land is not a trophy.

It is the place where they must live under the LORD's word.

Joshua 9 then warns us through the Gibeonites.

The kings of the land gather to fight.

But Gibeon chooses deception.

They come with old sacks.

Old bottles.

Old shoes.

Old garments.

Dry and moldy bread.

They pretend to come from a far country.

They ask Israel to make a league with them.

The key line is that the men took of their victuals, and asked not counsel at the mouth of the LORD.

That phrase should slow every reader down.

Israel has just heard the law.

Israel has seen God give victory.

Yet in this moment they judge by appearance.

They look at the bread.

They look at the clothes.

They listen to the story.

They do not ask the LORD.

The result is a covenant with Gibeon that was made under false pretenses.

When the truth is discovered, Israel cannot simply break the oath.

The princes tell the congregation that they have sworn unto them by the LORD God of Israel.

So Gibeon is spared, but they become hewers of wood and drawers of water.

This chapter teaches that wisdom must seek the LORD, not just inspect evidence.

It also teaches that oaths made in the LORD's name are serious.

Joshua 10 then shows the southern campaign.

The Gibeonites are attacked because they made peace with Israel.

They send to Joshua, asking him not to slack his hand.

Joshua goes up from Gilgal.

The LORD says, Fear them not: for I have delivered them into thine hand.

Again, courage is rooted in God's promise.

The LORD discomfits the enemies before Israel.

Great stones fall from heaven.

More die from hailstones than from Israel's sword.

Then Joshua speaks in the sight of Israel.

Sun, stand thou still upon Gibeon; and thou, Moon, in the valley of Ajalon.

This is one of the most dramatic moments in the book.

The point is not that Joshua controls creation for his own glory.

The point is that the LORD fights for Israel.

The text says there was no day like that before it or after it, that the LORD hearkened unto the voice of a man: for the LORD fought for Israel.

That last phrase interprets the miracle.

The LORD fought for Israel.

Joshua 10 then lists city after city.

Makkedah.

Libnah.

Lachish.

Eglon.

Hebron.

Debir.

The repetition can feel heavy, but it is showing the sweep of victory.

The LORD delivers the southern region into Joshua's hand.

Joshua 11 moves to the northern campaign.

Jabin king of Hazor gathers a great host, even as the sand that is upon the sea shore in multitude.

They have horses and chariots.

From a human viewpoint, this looks overwhelming.

But the LORD speaks again.

Be not afraid because of them: for tomorrow about this time will I deliver them up all slain before Israel.

The size of the enemy does not change the certainty of God's word.

Joshua comes suddenly upon them.

The LORD delivers them.

Hazor is burned.

The northern coalition is broken.

The chapter then summarizes Joshua's obedience.

As the LORD commanded Moses his servant, so did Moses command Joshua, and so did Joshua.

He left nothing undone of all that the LORD commanded Moses.

That is one of the main lines of Day 58.

Joshua is not celebrated as an independent hero.

He is shown as an obedient servant under the word already given through Moses.

Day 58 therefore moves through restoration, Scripture, discernment, battle, miracle, and obedience.

Ai teaches that the LORD restores His people after sin is judged.

Mount Ebal teaches that victory must bow before the law.

Gibeon teaches that appearances can deceive when the LORD is not consulted.

The southern campaign teaches that the LORD fights for His people.

The northern campaign teaches that no coalition is too large for God's promise.

Joshua's obedience teaches that faithfulness means leaving nothing undone.

For the listener, the lesson is practical and deep.

When you have failed, do not pretend nothing happened.

Let the LORD expose and correct what is wrong.

Then listen when He calls you forward.

When God gives success, do not drift from His word.

Return to Scripture.

Read the blessings and warnings.

Remember who rules the land you stand in.

When decisions look obvious, do not live by appearances alone.

Ask counsel at the mouth of the LORD.

When opposition feels too large, remember that the LORD is not measuring the battle the way we do.

And when God commands, do not leave obedience half-finished.

Day 58 is conquest, yes.

But more than conquest, it is covenant obedience in motion.

## Final Meditation

Day 58 also teaches us how easily victory and danger can sit beside each other.

Ai becomes victory after hidden sin has been judged.

Mount Ebal becomes worship after the city falls.

Gibeon becomes a warning after the law has been read.

The southern battles become triumph because the LORD fights.

The northern battles become triumph because Joshua trusts the LORD's command.

This order matters.

Israel is never allowed to think that conquest is separate from covenant.

The land cannot be received rightly if the law is ignored.

The battles cannot be understood rightly if the LORD is treated as a tribal good luck charm.

Joshua 8 places the law in the center of the story.

All the words are read.

Blessings and cursings are read.

The people hear.

The strangers hear.

The women and little ones hear.

The whole congregation stands under the word of God.

That is the sound Israel must carry into the land.

Not only trumpet sound.

Not only battle shout.

The sound of the law read before the people.

Then Gibeon shows how quickly people can act without seeking the LORD.

The old bread and old garments seem convincing.

The story sounds reasonable.

The evidence looks clear.

But the missing thing is counsel from the mouth of the LORD.

That missing thing changes everything.

It is not enough to be experienced.

It is not enough to be practical.

It is not enough to inspect what lies before the eyes.

God's people need the LORD's counsel.

The battles that follow are full of strength, but they are also full of dependence.

The sun standing still is not a monument to Joshua's greatness.

It is a witness that the LORD fought for Israel.

The northern coalition is not defeated because its chariots were weak.

It is defeated because the LORD delivered them into Joshua's hand.

So Day 58 teaches a full pattern.

Deal honestly with sin.

Return to the word.

Seek the LORD's counsel.

Trust Him in overwhelming battles.

Obey what He has commanded.

If one piece is missing, the life of faith becomes distorted.

Power without Scripture becomes pride.

Decision without counsel becomes foolishness.

Victory without obedience becomes danger.

But when the LORD's people listen, seek, trust, and obey, conquest becomes covenant faithfulness instead of mere conquest.

Notice also how Day 58 keeps putting the mouth of the LORD above the sight of the eyes.

At Ai, the LORD says He has given the city.

At Ebal, the written law is read aloud.

At Gibeon, Israel fails because they do not ask counsel at the mouth of the LORD.

In the southern battle, the LORD says not to fear.

In the northern battle, the LORD again says not to fear.

Joshua's strength is not located in better eyesight.

It is located in hearing and obeying.

That is the heartbeat of this day.

God's people must not simply react to what they can see.

They must be governed by what the LORD has said.

The men of Gibeon looked convincing, but appearance was not enough.

The northern armies looked overwhelming, but appearance was not final.

The law looked like words on stones and scrolls, but those words governed the life of the people.

The sun and moon looked fixed in their normal courses, but even creation served the LORD's battle.

Day 58 teaches that the visible world must be read under the word of God.

That is how Joshua leads.

That is how Israel must live.

And that is why the final summary matters so much.

Joshua left nothing undone of all that the LORD commanded Moses.

That is the kind of obedience this day holds before us.

Carry one final thread through the whole day.

The LORD restores Israel after Ai, but He does not remove the need for obedience.

He gives strategy, but Joshua still must set the ambush.

He gives victory, but Joshua still builds the altar.

He gives the law, but Israel still must hear it.

He exposes the danger of Gibeon, but Israel must still honor the oath they swore.

He promises victory in the south, but Joshua still marches through the night.

He promises victory in the north, but Joshua still faces horses, chariots, and a multitude like sand.

This keeps the lesson balanced.

Grace does not make obedience unnecessary.

Obedience does not make grace unnecessary.

The LORD's promise gives the foundation.

The LORD's command gives the path.

Joshua's faith is seen in walking that path.

That is why this section belongs after the earlier failure at Ai.

Israel needs to learn how to move forward without pretending sin was harmless.

They also need to learn how to move forward without being paralyzed by the past.

The LORD speaks.

The LORD restores.

The LORD commands.

The LORD fights.

Joshua obeys.

That is the rhythm of Day 58.
