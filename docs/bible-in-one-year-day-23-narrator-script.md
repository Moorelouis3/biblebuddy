# Bible In One Year - Day 23 Narrator Script

## Day 23 - Pharaoh Resists God's Word

**Reading:** Exodus 5 through 8  
**Bible text:** King James Version, Exodus 5-8  
**Audio style:** Narrator teaching with Scripture reading  

## Opening

Welcome to Day 23 of Bible in One Year.

Today we are reading Exodus 5 through 8.

Yesterday, God called Moses from the burning bush.

God said He had seen Israel's affliction.

He had heard their cry.

He knew their sorrows.

And He had come down to deliver them.

That sounds like the moment everything should start getting better.

But Exodus 5 teaches us something very honest.

Sometimes obedience makes the conflict visible before it brings relief.

Moses obeys.

Aaron goes with him.

They speak God's word to Pharaoh.

And Pharaoh does not soften.

He hardens.

He mocks.

He increases the workload.

The people suffer more.

The Hebrew officers blame Moses.

And Moses turns back to God confused.

That is the emotional weight of today's reading.

God has truly spoken.

Moses has truly obeyed.

And yet the first result is heavier bondage.

But this is not God losing control.

This is Pharaoh's resistance being exposed.

Pharaoh asks the question that will frame the whole plague story:

Who is the LORD, that I should obey His voice?

Exodus 5 through 8 begins answering that question.

The LORD is the God whose staff swallows Egypt's staffs.

The LORD is the God who strikes the Nile.

The LORD is the God who fills Egypt with frogs.

The LORD is the God whose power the magicians eventually call the finger of God.

And the LORD is the God who can make a distinction between Egypt and Goshen.

So as we walk through these chapters, watch the pattern.

Pharaoh resists.

God speaks.

Pressure rises.

Pharaoh bargains.

Relief comes.

Pharaoh hardens again.

This is not just ancient politics.

This is a picture of what happens when human pride refuses God's word.

## Exodus 5:1-9

**Scripture**

And afterward Moses and Aaron went in, and told Pharaoh, Thus saith the LORD God of Israel, Let my people go, that they may hold a feast unto me in the wilderness.

And Pharaoh said, Who is the LORD, that I should obey his voice to let Israel go?

I know not the LORD, neither will I let Israel go.

And the king of Egypt said unto them, Wherefore do ye, Moses and Aaron, let the people from their works? get you unto your burdens.

Ye shall no more give the people straw to make brick, as heretofore: let them go and gather straw for themselves.

And the tale of the bricks, which they did make heretofore, ye shall lay upon them; ye shall not diminish ought thereof.

**Narrator**

Moses and Aaron finally stand before Pharaoh.

Imagine that room.

Egypt's ruler sits with power, wealth, guards, officials, and the confidence of empire around him.

Moses is not coming with an army.

Aaron is not carrying political leverage.

They come with a word.

Thus saith the LORD God of Israel.

Let my people go.

That phrase matters.

God calls Israel my people.

Pharaoh treats them like labor.

God calls them His people.

Pharaoh sees brickmakers.

God sees worshipers.

The goal is not only escape from work.

God says they are to hold a feast to Him in the wilderness.

Deliverance is about worship.

Freedom is not only leaving Pharaoh.

It is belonging to the LORD.

Pharaoh's answer is the heart of the conflict.

Who is the LORD, that I should obey His voice?

Pharaoh does not recognize God's authority.

He does not think Israel's God has a claim on him.

He does not see why Egypt should bend to a word from the LORD.

So he says, I know not the LORD, neither will I let Israel go.

That question will be answered.

Not by debate.

By judgment.

By signs.

By plague.

By deliverance.

Then Pharaoh increases the burden.

No more straw.

Same number of bricks.

That is cruelty designed to crush hope.

Straw helped bind bricks together.

Without straw, the work becomes harder, slower, and more exhausting.

But Pharaoh keeps the same quota.

He creates impossible conditions and then blames the people.

Exodus is showing us that oppressive power does not only say no.

It punishes people for hoping.

## Exodus 5:10-23

**Scripture**

And the taskmasters of the people went out, and their officers, and they spake to the people, saying, Thus saith Pharaoh, I will not give you straw.

Go ye, get you straw where ye can find it: yet not ought of your work shall be diminished.

And the officers of the children of Israel, which Pharaoh's taskmasters had set over them, were beaten.

And they met Moses and Aaron, who stood in the way, as they came forth from Pharaoh:

And they said unto them, The LORD look upon you, and judge; because ye have made our savour to be abhorred in the eyes of Pharaoh.

And Moses returned unto the LORD, and said, Lord, wherefore hast thou so evil entreated this people? why is it that thou hast sent me?

For since I came to Pharaoh to speak in thy name, he hath done evil to this people; neither hast thou delivered thy people at all.

**Narrator**

Now the order moves from Pharaoh's mouth into Israel's bodies.

The taskmasters tell the people to find their own straw.

The work will not be reduced.

The quota stays the same.

The people scatter through the land looking for stubble.

The Hebrew officers are beaten when the impossible demand is not met.

This is how Pharaoh's cruelty spreads.

He does not only increase work.

He creates pressure inside the community.

The officers feel the blows.

The people feel the exhaustion.

Moses and Aaron become the visible reason things got worse.

So the officers confront them.

They say, The LORD look upon you and judge.

That is heavy.

These are not lazy people resisting God.

These are crushed people reacting from pain.

Their suffering increased right after Moses spoke.

They do not yet understand the larger story.

All they can feel is the heavier burden.

Then Moses goes back to God.

And he asks hard questions.

Why have You brought trouble on this people?

Why did You send me?

Since I came to Pharaoh, he has done evil to this people.

You have not delivered Your people at all.

That last line is painful.

But it is also honest.

Moses does not hide his confusion.

He does not pretend obedience feels successful.

He brings the lament to God.

And that is important.

Faith is not refusing to ask.

Faith is taking the question to the LORD instead of walking away from Him.

Exodus 5 ends unresolved.

But the silence at the end of the chapter is not failure.

God is about to answer.

## Exodus 6:1-13

**Scripture**

Then the LORD said unto Moses, Now shalt thou see what I will do to Pharaoh.

And God spake unto Moses, and said unto him, I am the LORD:

And I have also heard the groaning of the children of Israel, whom the Egyptians keep in bondage; and I have remembered my covenant.

Wherefore say unto the children of Israel, I am the LORD, and I will bring you out from under the burdens of the Egyptians.

And I will rid you out of their bondage, and I will redeem you with a stretched out arm.

And I will take you to me for a people, and I will be to you a God.

And I will bring you in unto the land.

And Moses spake so unto the children of Israel: but they hearkened not unto Moses for anguish of spirit, and for cruel bondage.

**Narrator**

God answers Moses.

But notice how He answers.

He does not give Moses a full timeline.

He does not explain every delay.

He does not say, Here is why I allowed Pharaoh to make the bricks harder first.

Instead, God reveals who He is and what He will do.

I am the LORD.

That phrase repeats like an anchor.

Moses is discouraged.

Israel is crushed.

Pharaoh is defiant.

But God says, I am the LORD.

Then God says He has heard Israel's groaning and remembered His covenant.

That covenant goes back to Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob.

The Exodus is not random rescue.

It is covenant faithfulness.

Then come the great I will statements.

I will bring you out.

I will rid you out of bondage.

I will redeem you.

I will take you to Me for a people.

I will be to you a God.

I will bring you into the land.

I will give it to you.

The whole speech is built on God's action.

Not Israel's strength.

Not Moses' confidence.

Not Pharaoh's cooperation.

God's I will stands against Pharaoh's I will not.

But then comes a very human detail.

Moses speaks to Israel, but they do not listen because of anguish of spirit and cruel bondage.

That is important.

The promise is true.

But their suffering is so deep that they cannot take it in.

The Bible does not shame them for that.

It names the condition.

Cruel bondage can crush the spirit.

Sometimes people in pain cannot hear hope quickly.

God's promise remains true, but wounded people may need time before they can receive it.

## Exodus 6:14-30

**Scripture**

These be the heads of their fathers' houses.

These are that Aaron and Moses, to whom the LORD said, Bring out the children of Israel from the land of Egypt according to their armies.

These are they which spake to Pharaoh king of Egypt, to bring out the children of Israel from Egypt: these are that Moses and Aaron.

And Moses said before the LORD, Behold, I am of uncircumcised lips, and how shall Pharaoh hearken unto me?

**Narrator**

At first, the genealogy in Exodus 6 can feel like an interruption.

The story is tense.

Pharaoh is resisting.

Israel is suffering.

God is speaking.

And suddenly we get family names.

But the genealogy has a purpose.

It locates Moses and Aaron inside Israel.

They are not random spiritual figures who appear from nowhere.

They belong to the covenant people.

They come through the line of Levi.

That matters because Pharaoh has treated Israel like a labor force.

God treats them like named households.

Fathers.

Sons.

Generations.

People with memory.

People with identity.

People tied to promise.

The genealogy also slows us down before the plague conflict intensifies.

It grounds the miracles in real covenant history.

Then Moses repeats his concern.

I am of uncircumcised lips.

In plain language, Moses still feels unfit to speak.

That is striking.

God has called him.

God has promised him.

God has given Aaron.

And Moses still feels weak.

That should encourage regular people.

Calling does not always erase insecurity overnight.

Moses will move forward with weakness still present.

And God will still use him.

## Exodus 7:1-13

**Scripture**

And the LORD said unto Moses, See, I have made thee a god to Pharaoh: and Aaron thy brother shall be thy prophet.

Thou shalt speak all that I command thee.

And I will harden Pharaoh's heart, and multiply my signs and my wonders in the land of Egypt.

And the Egyptians shall know that I am the LORD.

And Aaron cast down his rod before Pharaoh, and before his servants, and it became a serpent.

Then Pharaoh also called the wise men and the sorcerers.

And they also did in like manner with their enchantments.

But Aaron's rod swallowed up their rods.

And he hardened Pharaoh's heart, that he hearkened not unto them.

**Narrator**

Now the confrontation resumes.

God tells Moses that Aaron will speak for him.

Pharaoh will resist.

Signs and wonders will multiply.

And Egypt will know that He is the LORD.

That is the answer to Pharaoh's question.

Who is the LORD?

Egypt is about to find out.

Aaron throws down the staff, and it becomes a serpent.

This sign matters because the staff represents God's authority with Moses and Aaron.

But Pharaoh's magicians imitate it.

That is also important.

Exodus does not pretend Egypt has no spiritual spectacle.

There are wise men, sorcerers, enchantments, and counterfeit signs.

Not everything impressive is from God.

Not every supernatural-looking display carries God's truth.

But then Aaron's staff swallows their staffs.

That is the point.

Egypt can imitate.

But Egypt cannot overcome.

The LORD's power swallows Pharaoh's power.

This is a preview of the whole Exodus story.

Pharaoh may look strong.

Egypt may look untouchable.

But the power of Egypt is about to be swallowed by the power of God.

Still, Pharaoh's heart is hardened.

He does not listen.

Clear signs do not automatically soften a proud heart.

## Exodus 7:14-25

**Scripture**

And the LORD said unto Moses, Pharaoh's heart is hardened, he refuseth to let the people go.

Get thee unto Pharaoh in the morning; lo, he goeth out unto the water.

Thus saith the LORD, In this thou shalt know that I am the LORD: behold, I will smite with the rod that is in mine hand upon the waters which are in the river, and they shall be turned to blood.

And the fish that is in the river shall die, and the river shall stink; and the Egyptians shall loathe to drink of the water of the river.

And all the waters that were in the river were turned to blood.

**Narrator**

The first plague strikes the Nile.

To understand this, we need to slow down.

The Nile was life to Egypt.

It watered the land.

It supported crops.

It provided fish.

It shaped transportation.

It stood at the center of Egypt's survival and imagination.

So when God strikes the Nile, He is striking one of Egypt's great securities.

The river turns to blood.

The fish die.

The river stinks.

The Egyptians cannot drink the water.

This is not symbolic only.

It touches daily life immediately.

People need water.

Families need water.

Animals need water.

Workers need water.

Egypt's life source becomes undrinkable.

There is also a deeper echo here.

Pharaoh used the Nile as a weapon against Hebrew sons.

Back in Exodus 1, he commanded that Hebrew baby boys be thrown into the river.

Now that same river is struck with blood.

The place of death becomes a place of judgment.

The magicians imitate the sign, but imitation does not help Egypt.

They can produce more blood-like water, but they cannot cleanse the Nile.

That is a key lesson in these early plagues.

Counterfeit power may imitate judgment.

But it cannot bring deliverance.

Pharaoh turns and goes into his house.

His heart remains hard.

## Exodus 8:1-15

**Scripture**

And the LORD spake unto Moses, Go unto Pharaoh, and say unto him, Thus saith the LORD, Let my people go, that they may serve me.

And if thou refuse to let them go, behold, I will smite all thy borders with frogs.

And the river shall bring forth frogs abundantly.

And the frogs shall come up both on thee, and upon thy people, and upon all thy servants.

Then Pharaoh called for Moses and Aaron, and said, Intreat the LORD, that he may take away the frogs from me, and from my people; and I will let the people go.

But when Pharaoh saw that there was respite, he hardened his heart.

**Narrator**

The second plague is frogs.

And it is invasive.

Frogs come into houses.

Bedrooms.

Beds.

Ovens.

Kneading troughs.

On people.

On servants.

On Pharaoh himself.

The plague almost feels absurd.

But that is part of its force.

Egypt cannot keep God's judgment outside.

It enters the royal house and the ordinary kitchen.

It disturbs sleep, food, work, and daily life.

Again, the magicians can bring up frogs too.

But that does not solve the problem.

Imagine that.

Egypt is covered in frogs, and the magicians prove their power by making more frogs.

They can imitate the sign, but they cannot remove the plague.

Then Pharaoh calls Moses and Aaron.

He asks them to plead with the LORD to remove the frogs.

That sounds like repentance at first.

But watch what happens.

Moses prays.

The frogs die.

Relief comes.

And when Pharaoh sees there is respite, he hardens his heart.

That is a pattern we need to notice.

Pressure can make a person sound spiritual.

Crisis can make someone ask for prayer.

Pain can make someone promise change.

But when relief comes, the heart is revealed.

Pharaoh wanted the frogs gone.

He did not want to surrender to God.

Relief is not the same as repentance.

## Exodus 8:16-19

**Scripture**

And the LORD said unto Moses, Say unto Aaron, Stretch out thy rod, and smite the dust of the land, that it may become lice throughout all the land of Egypt.

And they did so; for Aaron stretched out his hand with his rod, and smote the dust of the earth, and it became lice in man, and in beast.

And the magicians did so with their enchantments to bring forth lice, but they could not.

Then the magicians said unto Pharaoh, This is the finger of God.

And Pharaoh's heart was hardened, and he hearkened not unto them.

**Narrator**

The third plague comes without negotiation.

Aaron strikes the dust.

And lice, or gnats, come upon people and animals.

The dust itself becomes a plague.

That is creation-level imagery.

The God who made humanity from dust can command dust in judgment.

Now the magicians try again.

But this time, they cannot imitate it.

They reach their limit.

And they say to Pharaoh, This is the finger of God.

That is a major turning point.

Egypt's own religious experts admit this power is beyond them.

They recognize something divine is happening.

But Pharaoh still does not listen.

That is sobering.

Evidence is not always enough to soften someone who is determined to resist.

The magicians can see a limit.

Pharaoh refuses to bow.

Recognition is not the same as repentance.

You can admit that God is powerful and still refuse to obey Him.

Exodus keeps pressing that truth into the story.

## Exodus 8:20-32

**Scripture**

And the LORD said unto Moses, Rise up early in the morning, and stand before Pharaoh; lo, he cometh forth to the water.

And say unto him, Thus saith the LORD, Let my people go, that they may serve me.

Else, if thou wilt not let my people go, behold, I will send swarms of flies upon thee.

And I will sever in that day the land of Goshen, in which my people dwell, that no swarms of flies shall be there.

And I will put a division between my people and thy people.

And Pharaoh called for Moses and for Aaron, and said, Go ye, sacrifice to your God in the land.

And Pharaoh said, I will let you go, that ye may sacrifice to the LORD your God in the wilderness; only ye shall not go very far away.

And Pharaoh hardened his heart at this time also, neither would he let the people go.

**Narrator**

The fourth plague brings swarms.

But this plague adds something new.

God says He will make a distinction between Egypt and Goshen.

Goshen is where Israel lives.

The swarms will fill Egypt, but they will not fill Goshen.

That matters.

The plagues are not random disasters spinning out of control.

God rules with precision.

He knows where His people are.

He knows how to judge Egypt while preserving Israel.

And He says why He is doing it:

That thou mayest know that I am the LORD in the midst of the earth.

Pharaoh may rule Egypt politically, but the LORD rules in the midst of the earth.

Even in Pharaoh's territory, God is Lord.

Then Pharaoh begins bargaining.

At first he says Israel can sacrifice to God in the land.

In other words, worship here, under my control.

Moses refuses because God's command was to go.

Then Pharaoh says they may go, but not very far away.

That is compromise.

But compromise can still be resistance.

Pharaoh wants relief from the plague without full obedience to the word of God.

He wants enough religion to stop the pressure, but not enough obedience to release control.

Moses prays.

The swarms leave.

And Pharaoh hardens his heart again.

The pattern continues.

Pressure.

Negotiation.

Relief.

Resistance.

Pharaoh is not being slowly converted.

He is being slowly exposed.

## Closing

Day 23 is heavy because the rescue has begun, but freedom has not arrived yet.

Moses obeys, and Israel suffers more.

Pharaoh mocks God and increases the burden.

Moses laments.

God repeats His name.

Then the signs begin.

The staff swallows the staffs.

The Nile turns to blood.

Frogs fill the land.

Dust becomes lice.

Swarms strike Egypt while Goshen is protected.

Through all of it, Pharaoh's question is being answered.

Who is the LORD?

He is the God who claims His people.

He is the God who hears groaning.

He is the God who remembers covenant.

He is the God over the Nile, the dust, the animals, the land, and the false powers of Egypt.

He is the God who can distinguish between Egypt and Goshen.

And He is the God whose word Pharaoh cannot finally stop.

This day teaches us something important about resistance.

Resistance does not mean God has failed.

Harder circumstances do not mean God forgot His promise.

Delayed relief does not mean God is absent.

Sometimes God lets resistance expose itself so everyone can see what kind of power is really ruling.

Pharaoh wants control.

God wants worship.

Pharaoh says, I will not.

God says, I am the LORD.

And that is where the hope is.

The story is not anchored in Pharaoh's mood.

It is anchored in God's name.
